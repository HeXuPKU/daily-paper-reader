# EVEE: Interpretable variant effect prediction from genomic foundation model embeddings

Abstract 

Predicting the clinical significance of genetic variants re-mains a central challenge in genomic medicine, with most observed variants classified as variants of uncertain sig-nificance. Here we show that representations from Evo 2, a 7-billion-parameter genomic foundation model, support accurate and interpretable pathogenicity prediction across variant types from a single framework. An embedding-based classifier, or “probe”, trained on Evo 2 embed-dings achieves state-of-the-art performance across sin-gle nucleotide variant consequence types (0.997 overall AUROC on 833k ClinVar variants) and generalizes zero-shot to indels (0.991 AUROC), outperforming bioinformatic meta-predictors, protein models, and existing foundation model approaches. Performance is robust across con-servation levels and transfers to deep mutational scan-ning datasets for BRCA1, BRCA2, TP53, and LDLR. To make these predictions interpretable, we train supervised annotation probes to quantify predicted disruptions caused by each variant, then synthesize these disruption profiles into natural language explanations using a frontier rea-soning model. We provide pre-computed predictions and on-demand explanations for all 4.2 million ClinVar variants through the Evo Variant Effect Explorer (EVEE), an interac-tive web resource for the community. This work establishes that representations from genomic foundation models can serve as a unified substrate for both accurate variant effect prediction and mechanistic interpretation, reframing inter-pretability in computational genomics from a trade-off into a complementary product of learned biological structure.

Computational variant effect predictors have pro-liferated, spanning protein language models such as Al-phaMissense (Cheng et al., 2023) and EVE (Frazer et al., 2021), supervised sequence-to-activity models such as Al-phaGenome (Avsec et al., 2026) and Nucleotide Trans-former v3 (Boshar et al., 2025; Dalla-Torre et al., 2025), evolutionary DNA models such as Evo 2 (Brixi et al., 2026) and GPN-MSA (Benegas et al., 2023), compressed protein language models (Dinh et al., 2026), and clas-sical meta-predictors such as CADD (Schubach et al., 2024). However, these tools face complementary lim-itations: protein-based methods are restricted to mis-sense variants; AlphaGenome focuses on regulatory non-coding effects; and CADD, while genome-wide, integrates 100+ annotation features with crossed terms and rank transformations that obscure individual feature contribu-tions (Avsec et al., 2026; Schubach et al., 2024; Kircher et al., 2014). Critically, none provide human-readable explanations of why a variant is predicted pathogenic— a key requirement for clinical variant classification under ACMG/AMP guidelines (Richards et al., 2015), which de-mands categorized evidence rather than opaque scores. Genomic foundation models, large neural networks pre-trained on DNA sequences, learn representations that capture functional constraints across coding and non-coding regions (Dalla-Torre et al., 2025; Brixi et al., 2026; Feng et al., 2025). Evo 2 in particular has demon-strated zero-shot variant effect predictions and internal representations that correspond to biological features such as exon–intron boundaries and protein structural ele-ments (Brixi et al., 2026). However, systematic evaluation of embedding-based classifiers, or “probes”, and frame-works for generating mechanistic interpretation from these representations have been lacking. Our approach processes reference and alternate DNA sequences through Evo 2, computes the difference in per-position embeddings, and trains a covariance probe on the resulting representation to predict pathogenic-ity (Figure 1a). We evaluated pathogenicity classifica-tion on 833,970 ClinVar SNVs from genes shorter than 100 kb. Because pathogenic fractions vary dramati-cally across consequence types (nonsense: 98.3%; syn-onymous: 0.1%), aggregate metrics can be inflated by models that learn consequence-type priors rather than variant-level discrimination. We addressed this distri-butional confound by stratifying on each consequence type (Feng et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2025) (Figure 1b). An Evo 2 covariance probe achieved 0.997 overall AUROC, with strong performance across all consequence types: 0.971 for missense ( n=125,092), 0.961 for synonymous (n=292,369), 0.900 for nonsense ( n=36,050), 0.924 for splice ( n=21,683), 0.929 for UTR ( n=31,911), 0.984 for in-tronic ( n=254,737), and 0.969 for other non-coding vari-ants ( n=3,498). This covariance probe outperformed a lin-ear probe on Evo 2 mean-pooled embeddings, Evo 2 loss-based scoring, CADD, AlphaMissense, GPN-MSA, NTv3, and AlphaGenome composite scores across all variant consequence types. Results were confirmed on a decon-founded benchmark that balances pathogenic and benign labels within each consequence type (Supplementary Fig-ure S1), where the covariance probe (0.971 overall AU-ROC) was matched only by AlphaMissense for missense variants (0.972 AUROC) while exceeding all other meth-ods. Instead of mean-pooling over the embedding matrix X ∈

RL×d, the covariance probe is trained on the Gram ma-trix X⊤X ∈ Rd×d. This matrix captures the second-order structure of the embeddings, such as the correlations be-tween model dimensions or co-occurrence of sparse fea-tures along the sequence. Since the dimensionality of the matrix is large, in practice we compute a compressed version using linear down-projections (Figure 1a; Meth-ods) (Dooms et al., 2026). This allows for lightweight probe training that improves over logistic regression on mean-pooled embeddings. Since Evo 2 is an autoregressive model, we concatenate the embeddings for the sense and antisense directions along the gene to obtain bidirectional embeddings that are used for probe training throughout. Strikingly, the Evo 2 covariance probe generalized zero-shot to indels despite being trained exclusively on SNVs. On 73,961 ClinVar indels, it achieved 0.986 overall AU-ROC, with strong performance across size categories: 0.989 for 1-bp, 0.991 for 2–5 bp, 0.984 for 6–20 bp, and 0.916 for >20 bp indels, as well as 0.991 for insertions and 0.988 for deletions (Figure 1c). This zero-shot transfer outperformed CADD v1.7 InDel (0.980 overall) and sub-stantially exceeded NTv3 (0.828), demonstrating that rep-resentations learned from SNV training capture general principles of sequence disruption that extend to insertion– deletion variation. Performance was lower for frameshifts (0.770) due to severe class imbalance (99% pathogenic), though still substantially above NTv3 (0.602). The covariance probe also showed robust performance across conservation levels, maintaining high AUROC from fast-evolving to highly conserved sites, whereas CADD and GPN-MSA performance degraded at the extremes of the conservation spectrum (Figure 1d). This suggests that Evo 2 representations encode functional constraint information that is complementary to, rather than redun-dant with, phylogenetic conservation. UMAP visualization of reference-subtracted Evo 2 embeddings further con-firmed that the learned representations capture meaning-ful biological structure, revealing clear organization by both pathogenicity and variant consequence type (Figure 1e,f). To validate that Evo 2 representations capture biology beyond ClinVar-specific patterns, we evaluated transfer to deep mutational scanning (DMS) datasets for four well-characterized genes that were held out from probe training: BRCA1 (Findlay et al., 2018), BRCA2 (Sahu et al., 2025), TP53 (Funk et al., 2025), and LDLR (Tabet et al., 2025) (Figure 1g). We assessed correlation be-tween model predictions and continuous DMS functional scores (Spearman |ρ|), comparing Evo 2 mean-pooled and covariance-pooled probes trained on ClinVar labels against CADD, AlphaMissense, and Evo 2 loss-based scoring. We also evaluated probes trained directly on DMS data from the other three genes with both pooling strate-gies. Across all four genes, ClinVar-trained Evo 2 embedding probes outperformed loss-based scoring, confirming that learned representations encode functional information be-yond next-token likelihood. The covariance probe was competitive with AlphaMissense and CADD across genes and exceeded both on TP53 ( |ρ| ≈ 0.70 ), while CADD showed a particular advantage on BRCA2 ( |ρ| ≈ 0.46 vs. 0.30–0.37 for other methods). Covariance probes consis-tently outperformed their mean-pooled counterparts, mir-roring the gains observed on ClinVar and confirming that second-order embedding structure captures functional in-formation lost under mean pooling. In addition, we trained hold-one-gene-out probes using the DMS datasets that achieved performance comparable to the ClinVar-trained probes, indicating that clinical labels and experimental functional data capture overlapping biology. Having established that Evo 2 embeddings support accu-rate and generalizable pathogenicity prediction, we next asked whether we could make these predictions inter-pretable. Rather than relying on unsupervised decomposi-tion of model representations, we adopted a supervised approach: we trained annotation probes directly on the same Evo 2 reference-sequence embeddings to predict a comprehensive panel of known biological annotations, then quantified how each variant disrupts these predicted annotations relative to the reference sequence (Figure 2a). The annotation panel spans 251 properties organized across multiple categories: protein structural features, structural context, regulatory marks, protein domains, post-translational modifications, genomic region identity, and variant-level effects. Probes achieved strong per-formance: median AUC of 0.919 across a larger set of 357 annotation types on held-out reference positions (Fig-ure 2b), confirming that Evo 2 embeddings encode rich annotation information that can be reliably decoded. For each variant, we compute a disruption profile using two complementary probes trained on Evo 2’s internal rep-resentations. A token-level probe predicts biological anno-tations (protein domains, post-translational modifications, splice sites, secondary structure) at each genomic posi-tion, and we measure the change in each prediction be-tween the variant and reference sequences as a raw delta. The probe operates at per-position resolution: in addition to disruptions at the variant site itself, we identify signif-icant changes at up to five flanking positions, capturing long-range effects such as splice site disruption propa-gating across exon–intron boundaries or domain loss ex-tending tens of base pairs from the mutation. A sepa-rate sequence-level probe predicts amino acid biophysi-cal properties (BLOSUM62 substitution score, Grantham physicochemical distance, charge alteration, hydrophobic-ity and volume changes) and splice disruption probabil-ity, providing a complementary view of the variant’s effect at the protein level. At coding positions, the token-level probe also predicts amino acid identity, enabling direct comparison of the model’s predicted reference and variant residues. The resulting multi-scale disruption profile pro-vides a structured fingerprint of predicted molecular con-sequences, derived entirely from learned sequence repre-sentations rather than curated databases, and transparent at the individual-variant level. To synthesize disruption profiles into human-readable in-terpretations, we feed the top 10 disruptions (ranked by magnitude) along with variant metadata (gene name, con-sequence type, and HGVS protein/coding notation) into an LLM, which generates a contextualized natural-language explanation of the variant’s predicted pathogenicity (Fig-ure 2d). For a BRCA1 intronic variant (c.4987 −3C >G), the system detects complete loss of splice acceptor recogni-tion at the variant site, with intron-to-exon boundary predic-tion dropping from 1.00 to 0.00 ( ∆ = −1.00 ) and branch-point region prediction collapsing from 0.99 to 0.07 ( ∆ = 

−0.92 ). The polypyrimidine tract, which guides spliceo-some assembly upstream of the acceptor site, similarly drops from 0.98 to 0.12 ( ∆ = −0.86 ). Together, these three disruptions indicate that the model recognizes coordinated destruction of the splice acceptor machinery at the exon 15 boundary. RNA studies confirm that this variant produces aberrant, frameshifting transcripts due to weakened splice acceptor recognition (Brandão et al., 2011). The predicted pathogenicity of 71% is consistent with a loss-of-function allele in a haploinsufficiency-intolerant tumor suppressor gene. We evaluated interpretation quality using an LLM-as-judge approach against expert panel evidence from ClinVar. We selected 154 expert-reviewed ClinVar variants ( ≥3 stars; 98 benign, 56 pathogenic) and used the expert panel’s submission text as ground truth. Interpretations were scored on three axes: mechanism coverage (using a 5-level rubric assessing the specificity of correctly identi-fied molecular mechanisms), biological accuracy (consis-tency of biological claims with expert evidence), and speci-ficity (whether claims are specific to the variant rather than generic). We systematically varied the information provided to the interpreting LLM to quantify the contribu-tion of each context component (Figure 2c,e). Starting from genomic coordinates alone (composite score 1.47), adding gene name (+0.43), other context such as con-straint scores and gene function (+0.40), and HGVS pro-tein notation (+0.57) each improved quality, but the largest gain came from adding Evo 2 probe predictions (+1.02), yielding a composite score of 3.89/5 (mechanism=3.15, biological accuracy=4.27, specificity=4.15) with Claude Opus 4.6 as the interpreter. We provide all predictions, disruption profiles, and vari-ant interpretations through the Evo Variant Effect Explorer (EVEE), an interactive web application that allows users to browse pre-computed results for 4.2 million ClinVar vari-ants, inspect disruption profiles across annotation cate-gories, and examine LLM-synthesized interpretations. Our results establish Evo 2 embeddings as a unified foun-dation for variant effect prediction that addresses three persistent gaps in the current landscape. First, our ap-proach resolves the variant-type coverage gap: a single covariance probe achieves state-of-the-art performance across all SNV consequence types and indels, in con-trast to the numerous protein-specific tools restricted to missense or coding variants. As genomic foundation models continue to scale and diver-sify, the combination of accurate prediction, broad variant-type coverage, and structured interpretability demon-strated here provides a practical framework for AI-powered variant classification. We anticipate that integration with proteome-wide disease models (Orenbuch et al., 2025), regulatory variant data from massively parallel reporter as-says, and clinical cohort validation will further extend the utility of this approach toward complex and polygenic dis-ease contexts.

Genomic foundation model 

Embeddings. We used the 7B-parameter Evo 2 model (Brixi et al., 2026) to compute (i) per-position log-likelihood scores for variant effect scoring and (ii) intermediate-layer embeddings for representation analysis. Embeddings were extracted from layer 27, selected based on a layer sweep across all 32 blocks showing optimal pathogenicity classification at layers 24–27 (Supplementary Figure S3). 

Harvesting. We extract a fixed-size 65k bp window around each mutation site by evaluating 49k bp upstream context and capturing the 16k bp downstream effect. Our experiments show that accuracy increases with window size, indicating there is a long tail of low-range effects captured by Evo 2 (Supplementary Figure S4). The complete downstream is mean-pooled while only the top 256 diverging position’s embeddings are kept, measured by cosine similarity between reference and variant. Our experiments show this storage reduction strategy has no impact over keeping a trailing contiguous window after the mutation site for covariance probes (Supplementary Figure S5). 

Dataset. Both the reference and variant sequences are harvested in the sense and anti-sense direction. To handle indels, reference and variant sequences are aligned and any unmatched positions are discarded. The extraction used ∼20,000 H100-hours and the resulting dataset has shape 4,252 ,870 × 2 × 512 × 4,096 (variants, var/ref, positions, dimensions) stored as bf16, amounting to ∼34 TB. 

Baseline methods 

AlphaMissense. Pre-computed pathogenicity scores from the AlphaMissense hg38 genome-wide database (Zenodo); available for missense variants on MANE Select canonical transcripts only (Cheng et al., 2023). 

CADD. v1.7 PHRED-scaled scores from pre-computed whole-genome SNV files. Only indels <50 bp were scored (Schubach et al., 2024). 

GPN-MSA. Pre-computed scores were downloaded from HuggingFace (songlab/gpn-msa-hg38-scores) (Benegas et al., 2023). 

NTv3. Nucleotide Transformer v3 (650M parameters) embeddings were extracted from layer 11 (1,536-D) over 8,192 bp context windows centered on the variant. L2-regularized logistic regression trained on the element-wise difference of alternative and reference embeddings (alt − ref) using 5-fold gene-holdout cross-validation (Boshar et al., 2025; Dalla-Torre et al., 2025). 

AlphaGenome. Variant effect scores were computed using AlphaGenome (google/alphagenome-all-folds) following the published scoring methodology. Reference and alternative gene-body sequences were passed through the model to predict functional genomic tracks across 9 modalities. Scores were aggregated using CenterMask (log 2 fold-change within variant-centered windows) for chromatin and transcription initiation tracks, and GeneMask (max |ALT − REF | or log-fold change over the gene body) for expression and splicing tracks. The final score was the maximum across all modality-specific scores.

Covariance probe 

For each sequence (reference or alternate), Evo 2 produces an L × d matrix of token embeddings, where L is sequence length of the harvested embeddings and d is embedding dimension. We subtract these position-wise to get the difference 

X, encoding the variant’s effect. Standard mean pooling computes ¯x = (1 /L ) ∑ xi, yielding a d-dimensional summary. The covariance probe instead uses the Gram matrix C = X⊤X ∈ Rd×d, which captures pairwise interactions among embedding dimensions accumulated across all sequence positions. Pathogenicity is predicted using a linear classifier on this matrix. This probing setup can be interpreted as a linear attention mechanism that selectively aggregates positional information, rather than unconditionally summing. 

Dimensionality. Since the full Gram matrix is too large to use directly as a naive linear probe target, we use a low-dimensional projection E = ( XL )⊤(XR ) ∈ Rh×h where h = 64 (yielding an equally large embedding as mean pooling, 

h2 = d = 4096 ). The result is a low-dimensional linear probe using ∼500K parameters (rather than 16M). The embeddings for the forward and backward sequences are concatenated before inputting into the probe. 

Training. The covariance probe is trained with the Muon optimizer using a constant learning rate of 0.005, batch size of 512 × 8 (mini-batch × shards), weight decay of 0.1 for a single epoch. The labels are ClinVar P/LP versus B/LB, excluding 0-star. We use an 80/20 gene-level split; we do not perform model selection based on hyperparameters. The mean-pooled probe is trained with L2-regularized logistic regression using 5-fold gene-holdout cross-validation. The regularization strength C is selected via inner 5-fold stratified cross-validation over 12 values from 10 −4 to 10 1. Features are mean-centered and scaled to unit variance on the training fold.

Disruption scoring. For each variant, disruption was computed as the change in predicted annotation between alternate and reference sequences. Let fa be the probe prediction for annotation a; the disruption is ∆a = fa(Ealt ) − fa(Eref ).

LLM-based interpretation pipeline 

For each variant, the top-k disruptions (ranked by |∆|) were assembled alongside variant metadata (gene, transcript, consequence, genomic position, ClinVar status, gnomAD frequency, LOEUF score) into a structured prompt. Claude Son-net 4.6 (Anthropic, 2026) was used to synthesize a natural-language interpretation including: a one-sentence summary of the predicted molecular mechanism, a detailed explanation contextualizing the disruption profile against known biology, calibration information (percentage of ClinVar pathogenic variants at similar pathogenicity scores), and bullet-pointed key evidence. 

Evaluation. To evaluate the effect of contextual information on LLM-generated variant interpretations, we conducted a con-text stratification experiment across 154 expert-reviewed ClinVar variants (3+ stars). We defined a cumulative sequence of context configurations, each adding information to the previous level: (1) genomic coordinates only; (2) gene name; (3) gene name plus protein domain annotations, loss-of-function intolerance (LOEUF) scores, gnomAD allele frequen-cies, and gene function summaries; (4) HGVS protein and coding sequence notation; and (5) Evo 2 foundation model predictions including amino acid substitution probabilities, biophysical property scores (BLOSUM62, Grantham distance, charge, hydrophobicity, volume), splice disruption scores, and disruption profiles across nearby genomic positions. Inter-pretations were generated by three Claude model tiers (Haiku 4.5, Sonnet 4.6, and Opus 4.6) at temperature 0 with a maximum of 1,024 tokens. Each interpretation was evaluated by an LLM judge (Claude Opus 4.6) on three axes using a 1–5 Likert scale: mechanism coverage using a five-level specificity rubric ranging from gene-level identification (level 1) to multi-mechanism reasoning with biophysical detail (level 5); and biological accuracy and specificity by comparison against expert-submitted ClinVar evidence (SCV records). A composite score was computed as the unweighted mean of the three axes. All judge evaluations used temperature 0 for reproducibility. Confidence intervals represent 95% percentiles.
