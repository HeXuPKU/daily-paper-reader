# EVEE: Interpretable variant effect prediction from genomic foundation model embeddings

Abstract 

Predicting the clinical significance of genetic variants re-mains a central challenge in genomic medicine, with most observed variants classified as variants of uncertain sig-nificance. Here we show that representations from Evo 2, a 7-billion-parameter genomic foundation model, support accurate and interpretable pathogenicity prediction across variant types from a single framework. An embedding-based classifier, or “probe”, trained on Evo 2 embed-dings achieves state-of-the-art performance across sin-gle nucleotide variant consequence types (0.997 overall AUROC on 833k ClinVar variants) and generalizes zero-shot to indels (0.991 AUROC), outperforming bioinformatic meta-predictors, protein models, and existing foundation model approaches. Performance is robust across con-servation levels and transfers to deep mutational scan-ning datasets for BRCA1, BRCA2, TP53, and LDLR. To make these predictions interpretable, we train supervised annotation probes to quantify predicted disruptions caused by each variant, then synthesize these disruption profiles into natural language explanations using a frontier rea-soning model. We provide pre-computed predictions and on-demand explanations for all 4.2 million ClinVar variants through the Evo Variant Effect Explorer (EVEE), an interac-tive web resource for the community. This work establishes that representations from genomic foundation models can serve as a unified substrate for both accurate variant effect prediction and mechanistic interpretation, reframing inter-pretability in computational genomics from a trade-off into a complementary product of learned biological structure.

Computational variant effect predictors have proliferated, spanning protein language models such as AlphaMissense [3] and EVE [4], supervised sequence-to-activity models such as AlphaGenome [5] and Nucleotide Transformer v3 [6, 7], evolutionary DNA models such as Evo 2 [8] and GPN-MSA [9], compressed protein lan-guage models [10], and classical meta-predictors such as CADD [11]. However, these tools face complementary limitations: protein-based methods are restricted to mis-sense variants; AlphaGenome focuses on regulatory non-coding effects; and CADD, while genome-wide, integrates 100+ annotation features with crossed terms and rank transformations that obscure individual feature contribu-tions [5, 11, 12]. Critically, none provide human-readable explanations of why a variant is predicted pathogenic— a key requirement for clinical variant classification under ACMG/AMP guidelines [2], which demands categorized evidence rather than opaque scores. Genomic foundation models, large neural networks pre-trained on DNA sequences, learn representations that capture functional constraints across coding and non-coding regions [7, 8, 13]. Evo 2 in particular has demon-strated zero-shot variant effect predictions and internal representations that correspond to biological features such as exon–intron boundaries and protein structural ele-ments [8]. However, systematic evaluation of embedding-based classifiers, or “probes”, and frameworks for generat-ing mechanistic interpretation from these representations have been lacking. Here we address both gaps: we introduce a covariance probe that achieves state-of-the-art pathogenicity predic-tion across SNV and indel variant types and present a supervised annotation-disruption framework coupled with LLM-based synthesis that produces contextualized, human-readable explanations for each variant’s predicted pathogenicity. We evaluated pathogenicity classification on 833,970 ClinVar SNVs from genes shorter than 100 kb. Because pathogenic frac-tions vary dramatically across consequence types (non-sense: 98.3%; synonymous: 0.1%), aggregate met-rics can be inflated by models that learn consequence-type priors rather than variant-level discrimination. We addressed this distributional confound by stratifying on each consequence type [13, 14] (Figure 1b). An Evo 2 covariance probe achieved 0.997 overall AUROC, with strong performance across all consequence types: 0.971 for missense ( n=125,092), 0.961 for synonymous (n=292,369), 0.900 for nonsense ( n=36,050), 0.924 for splice ( n=21,683), 0.929 for UTR ( n=31,911), 0.984 for in-tronic ( n=254,737), and 0.969 for other non-coding vari-ants ( n=3,498). This covariance probe outperformed a lin-ear probe on Evo 2 mean-pooled embeddings, Evo 2 loss-based scoring, CADD, AlphaMissense, GPN-MSA, NTv3, and AlphaGenome composite scores across all variant consequence types. Results were confirmed on a decon-founded benchmark that balances pathogenic and benign labels within each consequence type (Supplementary Fig-ures S4,S5), where the covariance probe (0.971 overall AUROC) was matched only by AlphaMissense for mis-sense variants (0.972 AUROC) while exceeding all other methods. Instead of mean-pooling over the embedding matrix X ∈

RL×d, the covariance probe is trained on the Gram ma-trix X⊤X ∈ Rd×d. This matrix captures the second-order structure of the embeddings, such as the correlations be-tween model dimensions or co-occurrence of sparse fea-tures along the sequence. Since the dimensionality of the matrix is large, in practice we compute a compressed version using linear down-projections (Figure 1a; Meth-ods) [15]. This allows for lightweight probe training that improves over logistic regression on mean-pooled embed-dings. Since Evo 2 is an autoregressive model, we con-catenate the embeddings for the sense and antisense di-rections along the gene to obtain bidirectional embeddings that are used for probe training throughout. The result-ing probe produces well-calibrated pathogenicity estimates (Supplementary Figure S6). Strikingly, the Evo 2 covariance probe generalized zero-shot to indels despite being trained exclusively on SNVs. On 73,961 ClinVar indels, it achieved 0.986 overall AU-ROC, with strong performance across size categories: 0.989 for 1-bp, 0.991 for 2–5 bp, 0.984 for 6–20 bp, and 0.916 for >20 bp indels, as well as 0.991 for insertions and 0.988 for deletions (Figure 1c). This zero-shot transfer outperformed CADD v1.7 InDel (0.980 overall) and sub-stantially exceeded NTv3 (0.828), demonstrating that rep-resentations learned from SNV training capture general principles of sequence disruption that extend to insertion– deletion variation. Performance was lower for frameshifts (0.770) due to severe class imbalance (99% pathogenic), though still substantially above NTv3 (0.602). The covariance probe also showed robust performance across conservation levels, maintaining high AUROC from fast-evolving to highly conserved sites, whereas CADD and GPN-MSA performance degraded at the extremes of the conservation spectrum (Figure 1d). This suggests that Evo 2 representations encode functional constraint information that is complementary to, rather than redun-dant with, phylogenetic conservation. UMAP visualization of reference-subtracted Evo 2 embeddings further con-firmed that the learned representations capture meaning-ful biological structure, revealing clear organization by both pathogenicity and variant consequence type (Figure 1e,f). To validate that Evo 2 representations capture biology be-yond ClinVar-specific patterns, we evaluated transfer to deep mutational scanning (DMS) datasets for four well-characterized genes that were held out from probe train-ing: BRCA1 [16], BRCA2 [17], TP53 [18], and LDLR [19] (Figure 1g). We assessed correlation between model pre-dictions and continuous DMS functional scores (Spear-man |ρ|), comparing Evo 2 mean-pooled and covariance-pooled probes trained on ClinVar labels against CADD, AlphaMissense, and Evo 2 loss-based scoring. We also evaluated probes trained directly on DMS data from the other three genes with both pooling strategies. Across all four genes, ClinVar-trained Evo 2 embedding probes outperformed loss-based scoring, confirming that learned representations encode functional information be-yond next-token likelihood. The covariance probe was competitive with AlphaMissense and CADD across genes and exceeded both on TP53 ( |ρ| ≈ 0.70 ), while CADD showed a particular advantage on BRCA2 ( |ρ| ≈ 0.46 vs. 0.30–0.37 for other methods). Covariance probes consis-tently outperformed their mean-pooled counterparts, mir-roring the gains observed on ClinVar and confirming that second-order embedding structure captures functional in-formation lost under mean pooling. Together, these results demonstrate that Evo 2 embeddings generalize from clin-ical pathogenicity labels to direct experimental measure-ments of variant effects. Having established that Evo 2 embeddings support accu-rate and generalizable pathogenicity prediction, we next asked whether we could make these predictions inter-pretable. The annotation panel spans 251 properties organized across multiple categories: protein structural features, structural context, regulatory marks, protein domains, post-translational modifications, genomic region identity, and variant-level effects. Probes achieved strong per-formance: median AUC of 0.919 across a larger set of 357 annotation types on held-out reference positions (Fig-ure 2b), confirming that Evo 2 embeddings encode rich annotation information that can be reliably decoded. For each variant, we compute a disruption profile using annotation probes trained on Evo 2’s internal representa-tions. Each probe predicts a biological annotation (pro-tein domains, post-translational modifications, splice sites, secondary structure, amino acid identity, and biophysical properties) at each genomic position, and we measure the change in each prediction between the variant and refer-ence sequences as a raw delta. The probes operate at per-position resolution: in addition to disruptions at the variant site itself, we identify significant changes at up to five flanking positions, capturing long-range effects such as splice site disruption propagating across exon–intron boundaries or domain loss extending tens of base pairs from the mutation. The resulting disruption profile pro-vides a structured fingerprint of predicted molecular con-sequences, derived entirely from learned sequence repre-sentations rather than curated databases, and transparent at the individual-variant level. To synthesize disruption profiles into human-readable in-terpretations, we feed the top 10 disruptions (ranked by magnitude) along with variant metadata (gene name, con-sequence type, and HGVS protein/coding notation) into an LLM, which generates a contextualized natural-language explanation of the variant’s predicted pathogenicity (Fig-ure 2d). For a BRCA1 intronic variant (c.4987 −3C >G), the system detects complete loss of splice acceptor recogni-tion at the variant site, with intron-to-exon boundary predic-tion dropping from 1.00 to 0.00 ( ∆ = −1.00 ) and branch-point region prediction collapsing from 0.99 to 0.07 ( ∆ = 

−0.92 ). The polypyrimidine tract, which guides spliceo-some assembly upstream of the acceptor site, similarly drops from 0.98 to 0.12 ( ∆ = −0.86 ). Together, these three disruptions indicate that the model recognizes coor-dinated destruction of the splice acceptor machinery at the exon 15 boundary. RNA studies confirm that this variant produces aberrant, frameshifting transcripts due to weak-ened splice acceptor recognition [20]. The framework cap-tures diverse mechanisms beyond splicing. For an LDLR nonsense variant (p.Ser306Ter), the system identifies a premature termination codon that truncates the protein be-fore the EGF precursor homology domain, detecting coor-dinated loss of EGF-like domain, disulfide bond, and down-stream structural features consistent with elimination of the receptor recycling machinery required for LDL uptake. For a RET missense variant (p.Gly798Ser), the system de-tects near-complete loss of kinase domain identity and cat-alytic domain signatures, correctly attributing pathogenic-ity to disruption of the backbone flexibility at a conserved glycine position within the kinase fold (Supplementary Fig-ures S7,S8). We evaluated interpretation quality using an LLM-as-judge approach against expert panel evidence from Clin-Var. We selected 154 expert-reviewed ClinVar variants (≥3 stars; 98 benign, 56 pathogenic) and used the ex-pert panel’s submission text as ground truth. Interpre-tations were scored on three axes: mechanism cover-age (using a 5-level rubric assessing the specificity of correctly identified molecular mechanisms), biological ac-curacy (consistency of biological claims with expert ev-idence), and specificity (whether claims are specific to the variant rather than generic). We systematically var-ied the information provided to the interpreting LLM to quantify the contribution of each context component (Fig-ure 2c,e). Starting from genomic coordinates alone (com-posite score 1.31), adding gene name (+0.81), gene con-text such as constraint scores and gene function (+0.22), and HGVS protein notation (+0.43) each improved quality, but the largest gain came from adding Evo 2 probe predic-tions (+1.03), yielding a composite score of 3.80/5 (mech-anism=3.24, biological accuracy=4.05, specificity=4.13) with Claude Sonnet 4.6 as the interpreter (Supplementary Figures S9,S10). We provide all predictions, disruption profiles, and vari-ant interpretations through the Evo Variant Effect Explorer (EVEE), an interactive web application that allows users to browse pre-computed results for 4.2 million ClinVar vari-ants, inspect disruption profiles across annotation cate-gories, and examine LLM-synthesized interpretations. Our results establish Evo 2 embeddings as a unified foun-dation for variant effect prediction that addresses three persistent gaps in the current landscape. First, our ap-proach resolves the variant-type coverage gap: a single covariance probe achieves state-of-the-art performance across all SNV consequence types and indels, in con-trast to the numerous protein-specific tools restricted to missense or coding variants. Second, our probe is robust across the conservation spectrum, maintaining performance where annotation-dependent methods de-grade. Third, the annotation-disruption framework coupled with LLM-based synthesis produces structured, human-readable explanations that move toward the categorized evidence ACMG/AMP guidelines require [2]. Rather than an opaque score, researchers and clinicians can inspect which specific biological properties are disrupted and how they collectively contribute to a variant’s predicted effect.

Several limitations warrant discussion. Evo 2 is trained on a broad evolutionary prior that captures functional con-straints across all domains of life, making it well suited for detecting the strong deleterious effects characteristic of Mendelian pathogenic variants. However, this same prior may be less calibrated for subtler polygenic effects, where variants of small individual effects act in aggregate and may not leave clear evolutionary signatures detectable from local sequence alone. The annotation probes are constrained to predicting known annotations; truly novel molecular mechanisms of pathogenicity would not be cap-tured by this supervised approach. Unsupervised methods such as sparse autoencoders applied to genomic founda-tion model representations [8, 21, 22] offer a promising complementary direction for discovering novel functional features that may not correspond to existing annotations,

# Methods 

Genomic foundation model 

Embeddings. We used the 7B-parameter Evo 2 model [8] to compute (i) per-position log-likelihood scores for variant effect scoring and (ii) intermediate-layer embeddings for representation analysis. Embeddings were extracted from layer 27, selected based on a layer sweep across all 32 blocks (Supplementary Figure S1). 

Harvesting. We extract a fixed-size 65k bp window around each mutation site by evaluating 49k bp upstream context and capturing the 16k bp downstream effect. Our experiments show that accuracy increases with window size, indicating there is a long tail of low-range effects captured by Evo 2 (Supplementary Figure S2). The complete downstream is mean-pooled while only the top 256 diverging position’s embeddings are kept, measured by cosine similarity between reference and variant. Our experiments show this storage reduction strategy has no impact over keeping a trailing contiguous window after the mutation site for covariance probes (Supplementary Figure S3). 

Dataset. Both the reference and variant sequences are harvested in the sense and anti-sense direction. To handle indels, reference and variant sequences are aligned and any unmatched positions are discarded. The extraction used ∼20,000 H100-hours and the resulting dataset has shape 4,252 ,870 × 2 × 512 × 4,096 (variants, var/ref, positions, dimensions) stored as bf16, amounting to ∼34 TB. 

Baseline methods 

AlphaMissense. Pre-computed pathogenicity scores from the AlphaMissense hg38 genome-wide database available for missense variants on ‘MANE Select‘ canonical transcripts only [3]. 

CADD. v1.7 PHRED-scaled scores from pre-computed whole-genome SNV files. Only indels <50 bp were scored [11]. 

GPN-MSA. Pre-computed scores were downloaded from HuggingFace (songlab/gpn-msa-hg38-scores) [9].

L2-regularized logistic regression trained on the element-wise difference of alternative and reference embeddings (alt − ref) using 5-fold gene-holdout cross-validation [6, 7].

Reference and alternative gene-body sequences were passed through the model to predict functional genomic tracks across 9 modalities. Scores were aggregated using CenterMask (log 2 fold-change within variant-centered windows) for chromatin and transcription initiation tracks, and GeneMask (max |ALT − REF | or log-fold change over the gene body) for expression and splicing tracks. The final score was the maximum across all modality-specific scores.

Covariance probe 

For each sequence (reference or alternate), Evo 2 produces an L × d matrix of token embeddings, where L is sequence length of the harvested embeddings and d is embedding dimension. We subtract these position-wise to get the difference 

X, encoding the variant’s effect. Standard mean pooling computes ¯x = (1 /L ) ∑ xi, yielding a d-dimensional summary. The covariance probe instead uses the Gram matrix C = X⊤X ∈ Rd×d, which captures pairwise interactions among embedding dimensions accumulated across all sequence positions. Pathogenicity is predicted using a linear classifier on this matrix. This probing setup can be interpreted as a linear attention mechanism that selectively aggregates positional information, rather than unconditionally summing. 

Dimensionality. Since the full Gram matrix is too large to use directly as a naive linear probe target, we use a low-dimensional projection E = ( XL )⊤(XR ) ∈ Rh×h where h = 64 (yielding an equally large embedding as mean pooling, 

h2 = d = 4096 ). The result is a low-dimensional linear probe using ∼500K parameters (rather than 16M). The embeddings for the forward and backward sequences are concatenated before inputting into the probe. 

Training. The covariance probe is trained with the Muon optimizer using a constant learning rate of 0.005, batch size of 512 × 8 (mini-batch × shards), weight decay of 0.1 for a single epoch. The labels are ClinVar P/LP versus B/LB, excluding 0-star. We use an 80/20 gene-level split; we do not perform model selection based on hyperparameters. The mean-pooled probe is trained with L2-regularized logistic regression using 5-fold gene-holdout cross-validation. The regularization strength C is selected via inner 5-fold stratified cross-validation over 12 values from 10 −4 to 10 1. Features are mean-centered and scaled to unit variance on the training fold. The resulting pathogenicity probe is well-calibrated overall (Supplementary Figure S6).

LLM-based interpretation pipeline 

For each variant, the top-k disruptions (ranked by |∆|) were assembled alongside variant metadata (gene name, conse-quence type, genomic position, LOEUF constraint score) into a structured prompt. Claude Sonnet 4.6 [24] was used to synthesize a natural-language interpretation including: a one-sentence summary of the predicted molecular mechanism, a detailed explanation contextualizing the disruption profile against known biology, calibration information (percentage of ClinVar pathogenic variants at similar pathogenicity scores), and bullet-pointed key evidence. The system prompt (Table S1) describes the disruption signal framework: a probe trained on reference genome activa-tions is run on both reference and variant sequences, producing for each biological feature (helix, conservation, domain membership, etc.) a reference prediction, a variant prediction, and a delta (var − ref). The prompt explains that large negative deltas indicate feature disruption while near-zero deltas indicate preservation, and instructs the LLM to lead with the disruption story, use gene function context, and avoid referencing external clinical prediction tools. The user prompt is assembled programmatically from variant-specific data. It includes: gene name and variant coor-dinates, consequence type, HGVSp/HGVSc notation, protein domain annotations with LOEUF constraint score, gene function summaries, calibrated pathogenicity percentage, a Region Disruptions table (peak probe signal change across 

±32 kb for the top-10 features by |∆| ≥ 0.2, with reference, variant, delta, and distance columns), and a Site Disruptions table (probe signal change at the variant position for the top-10 features by |∆| ≥ 0.1, with reference, variant, and delta columns). An example of the full prompt is shown in Table S2. 

Interpretation evaluation 

To evaluate the effect of contextual information on LLM-generated variant interpretations, we conducted a context strati-fication experiment across 154 expert-reviewed ClinVar variants ( ≥3 stars). Five cumulative context configurations were tested, each adding information to all previous levels: (1) genomic coordinates only, (2) +gene name, (3) +gene context (domain annotations, LOEUF constraint, gene function summaries), (4) +HGVSp/HGVSc notation, and (5) +Evo 2 dis-ruption profile (calibrated pathogenicity, region and site disruption tables). Configurations without Evo 2 features used a baseline system prompt; the Evo 2 configuration used the probe-aware system prompt described above. Example prompts for each configuration are shown in Table S2. Interpretations were generated by three Claude model tiers (Haiku 4.5, Sonnet 4.6, and Opus 4.6). Each interpretation was evaluated by two independent LLM judges (Claude Opus 4.6, Table S3) on three axes using a 1–5 scale: mechanism cov-erage using a five-level specificity rubric; and biological accuracy and specificity by comparison against expert-submitted ClinVar evidence (SCV records). A composite score was computed as the unweighted mean of the three axes. Confi-dence intervals represent 95% percentiles. Supplementary Figure S9 shows the composite score stratified by pathogenic and benign variants, and Supplementary Figure S10 shows the per-axis score distributions across context configurations. 

Example interpretations. Tables S4–S8 show representative LLM interpretations at each mechanism score level (1–5) at the Evo 2 context configuration, illustrating the scoring rubric in practice.

Second, well-characterized variants in ClinVar (e.g., CFTR F508del, BRCA1 c.5266dupC) are likely present in the LLM’s training data, meaning the interpreting model may draw on memorized knowledge rather than reasoning from the disruption profile alone.
