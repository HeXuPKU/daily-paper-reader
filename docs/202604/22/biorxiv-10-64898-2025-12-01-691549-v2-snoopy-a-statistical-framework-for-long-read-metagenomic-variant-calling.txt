## Abstract 

Current long-read single-nucleotide variant callers were designed primarily for genomic data—particularly human genomes. While some have been used on metagenomic data, their underlying assumptions and training procedures fail to account for the inherent complexity of metagenomic samples. To date, no long-read variant caller has been purpose-built for metagenomic applications. To address this gap, we present SNooPy, a SNP-calling tool that implements a new statistical framework tailored to long-read metagenomic data. Unlike previous genomic methods, our approach makes no assumptions about the number of haplotypes present, their evolutionary relationships, or their sequence divergence. We demonstrate that SNooPy outperforms both traditional statistical and deep learning–based SNP callers. Our results suggest that future integration of this framework with deep learning approaches could further enhance variant calling performance. SNooPy is freely available on github.com/rolandfaure/snoopy. 

## Introduction 1

A fundamental problem in the analysis of genomics data is 2

the detection of variants: given a consensus genome from one 3

individual and sequence reads from a related but not identical 4

organism, how to compare the two. Differences can comprise single 5

nucleotide variants (SNVs) or more complex structural variations 6

such as rearrangements, insertions and deletions. Variant calling 7

is the first step in numerous genomics applications, from the 8

construction of genealogies to genome wide association studies 9

(GWAS) [22, 25]. It is also a key step in metagenome analysis, 10 

where reads derive from multiple different organisms, typically 11 

microbes, and encompass not only inter-species diversity but also 12 

intra-species strain variation [23, 18]. Increasingly, metagenome 13 

analysis focuses on this strain-level, requiring variant identification 14 

within species [19, 16]. 15 

Not surprisingly given the importance of this problem, many 16 

programs for genomic variant calling have been developed. At 17 

present, two main families of variant callers exist. Historically, 18 

the first approach, statistical variant callers,employed probabilistic 19 

models to differentiate true genetic variants from sequencing errors 20 

[10, 11, 6, 7, 8]. These models were built upon assumptions 21 

regarding the data, typically that sequencing errors were 22 

independent or that the sequenced samples were diploid. A second 23 

paradigm emerged following the introduction of DeepVariant 24 

in 2016 [17], leveraging deep neural networks [14, 29, 1]. 25 

These replaced the statistical tests with “black-box” machine- 26 

learning, which requires training using ground-truth datasets of 27 

known variants. While these callers have achieved state-of-the-art 28 

performance for human genomes, their accuracy remains heavily 29 

dependent on the training data, sometimes underperforming when 30 

calling variants outside of the set of species on which they were 31 

trained [28]. 32 

In metagenomes, multiple strains of the same species may 33 

exist with very different abundances. The species genomes may 34 

also represent novel, previously unseen diversity, generated by 35 

binning de novo assemblies into metagenome-assembled genomes 36 

(MAGs) [19]. These specificities of metagenomes compared to 37 

single genomes can break assumptions behind some statistical 38 

models (typically the assumption that the sample is diploid or 39 

polyploid). Neural network callers will have been typically trained 40 

on known genomes (e.g. human) and are also implicitly learning 41 

the biases of their training data, which might be specific to the 42 

genome and not apply to metagenome use-case. Metagenomics 43

To fill this methodological gap, we present SNooPy, a novel 49 

statistical SNP caller tailored for long-read metagenomic datasets. 50 

Similar to existing tools such as Longshot [8] and NanoCaller [1], 51 

SNooPy exploits the statistical dependencies among reads that 52 

arise from the inherent population structure of the sequenced 53 

sample. However, unlike Longshot and NanoCaller, SNooPy’s 54 

statistical framework is designed for metagenomic samples and 55 

does not make any assumption about ploidy. To do so, we 56 

implement a new statistical test inspired from previous work 57 

on haplotype assembly [9], which makes no assumptions on the 58 

number of haplotypes, their sequencing depth, or the sequencing 59 

error profiles: its only assumption is that sequencing errors occur 60 

independently across distinct reads. 61 

Because of the lack of existing specialized metagenomic long-62 

read SNP callers, we chose to compare SNooPy (0.4.3) with the 63 

widely used genomic SNP callers bcftools (1.22), Longshot (1.0.0), 64 

Nanocaller (3.6.2), Clair3 (1.0.10), and Deepvariant (1.9.0). These 65 

were therefore run slightly outside of their intended application 66 

area but it is common in metagenomics applications to use genomic 67 

variant callers. As detailed below, this demonstrated that SNooPy 68 

significantly outperformed not only statistical methods but also 69 

deep-learning ones (when applied without retraining, as is usually 70 

the case), and, hence, provides a fast and effective means to detect 71 

variants even in noisy long-read metagenome data. 72 

## MATERIALS AND METHODS 73 

Multi-loci variant calling 74 

SNooPy employs a multi-locus analysis strategy, i.e., it does not 75 

attempt to detect individual variants but rather identify and 76 

validates variant groups. This approach leverages the statistical 77 

principle that sequencing and alignment errors occur (nearly) 78 

independently; therefore, the probability of observing correlated 79 

sequencing errors across multiple reads at numerous loci is 80 

vanishingly small. When correlated patterns are detected, they 81 

more likely indicate reads originating from a distinct strain 82 

carrying true variants, rather than sequencing artifacts. 83 

This work builds upon ideas developed in the field of 84 

metagenome assembly, more specifically upon the software 85 

HairSplitter [9] and is based on the code of Strainminer [24]. 86 

This section focuses on the core variant-calling procedure, which 87 

comprises two primary steps: (1) identification of correlated loci 88 

representing candidate groups of single nucleotide polymorphisms 89 

(SNPs), and (2) statistical validation of these candidate 90 

groups. The complete variant-calling algorithm includes additional 91 

validation steps and recovery mechanisms, detailed in subsection 92 

Implementation details .93 

SNooPy processes BAM files through a sliding window 94 

approach. Within each window, the pileup data is transformed 95 

into a binary matrix M , where rows represent individual reads, 96 

columns correspond to genomic loci, and each entry Mij equals 1 97 

if read i contains the reference allele at locus j, or 0 if it contains 98 

an alternative base. To identify candidate variants, the algorithm 99 

starts by computing pairwise correlations between all columns 100 

using chi-square tests, yielding a p-value for each pair of columns. 101 

This p-value is used to perform complete-linkage hierarchical 102 

clustering with a p-value cutoff of 0.05 to group highly correlated 103 

columns. The choice of complete-linkage clustering ensures that 104 

all pairs of columns correlate well. From these groups, we identify 105 

variant patterns as subsets of loci and reads where all bases show 106 

a non-reference allele. 107 

Building upon the statistical framework established in 108 

HairSplitter [9], we developed a statistical test which evaluates 109 

whether observed variant patterns represent authentic variants 110 

(alternative hypothesis) or result purely from coincidental 111 

sequencing errors (null hypothesis). When a pattern passes the 112 

test, the corresponding variants are outputted in a VCF file. 113 

Let a be the number of reads and b the number of loci in the 114 

pattern, among a matrix totalling n reads and m loci. Let s denote 115 

an upper bound on the per-base sequencing error probability, 116 

estimated from the alignment data as described below. 117

To enhance computational efficiency and statistical power, 125

the actual local error rate, we empirically set s as three time the 133 

measured error rate. 134 

For illustrating the statistical strength of the procedure, 135 

consider a pileup of 100 Oxford Nanopore reads with error rate 136 

0.05 (hence s = 3 × 0.05 = 0 .15) spanning 5,000 base pairs. Let 137 

us imagine that among these 5,000 loci, 500 exhibit an alternative 138 

allele in more than 5% of reads and that we observe 5 reads sharing 139 

alternative bases at 10 loci. The probability that this pattern arises 140 

from sequencing errors alone is ≤ 3 × 10 −17 , providing strong 141 

evidence for genuine variants. A graphical illustration of the test 142 

is shown on Figure 1. 143 

Implementation details 144 

Rescuing SNPs 145 

Our multi-locus variant calling approach has three potential 146 

limitations that could result in missed SNPs. We have developed 147 

specific rescue strategies to address each limitation. 148 

Detection of isolated SNPs 149 

Multi-locus variant calling relies on correlations between SNPs 150 

to achieve statistical power. However, isolated SNPs lacking 151 

correlation with other variants cannot benefit from this approach. 152 

While our statistical test remains valid for single SNPs, its power 153 

is substantially reduced. 154

Recovery of high-noise correlated variants 163 

Some true SNPs may be difficult to detect due to elevated local 164 

error rates. During initial hierarchical clustering, such positions are 165 

excluded from variant groups to preserve the statistical power of 166 

our multi-locus test, as including high-noise positions can weaken 167 

the statistical test by excluding rows containing errors from the 168 

tested submatrix. 169 

After establishing high-confidence variant calls, we perform 170 

a recovery phase where all loci are tested for correlation with 171 

confirmed SNPs using chi-square tests. Positions demonstrating 172 

significant correlation (p-value < 10 −6) with established variants 173 

are rescued and called as SNPs. 174 

Detection of multi-allelic sites 175 

At positions harboring multiple alternative alleles, our primary 176 

algorithm typically identifies only the most frequent variant, 177 

potentially masking additional polymorphisms. To address this 178 

limitation, we perform iterative variant calling. After the initial 179 

round, all called variants are masked (i.e., converted to reference-180 

like status in the binary matrix), and the algorithm is re-executed 181 

on the modified data. This iterative process reveals previously 182 

hidden alternative alleles by removing the dominant variant signal, 183 

allowing detection of secondary polymorphisms at multi-allelic 184 

sites. 185 

Transforming a read alignment into matrices 186 

The algorithm begins by transforming read alignments from BAM 187 

format into binary matrices suitable for variant calling. Only the 188 

best alignment of each read is retained. Computing all-versus-189 

all locus correlations scales quadratically, making it extremely 190 

computationally expensive on long contigs. However, two loci 191 

can only exhibit meaningful correlation if they are close enough 192 

to be spanned by a significant number of reads. Therefore, we 193 

lose minimal information by restricting correlation calculations to 194 

nearby loci. To implement this, we partition the reference genome 195 

into fixed-length windows, setting each window to half the median 196 

read length. 197 

Read mapping patterns provide additional information for 198 

strain identification. Reads frequently map to only partial 199 

segments of the reference genome rather than aligning end-to-end, 200 

especially when they come from strains with structural variations 201 

regarding the reference. To exploit this signal, we cluster reads 202 

within each window based on their mapping coordinates. Groups 203 

of at least 5 reads sharing exactly the same mapping coordinates 204 

are grouped together, as they potentially originate from the 205 

same strain. Conversely, reads mapping to different coordinates 206 

within the window are analyzed separately, as they probably 207 

originate from distinct strains with different genomic architectures 208 

or insertion/deletion patterns. This coordinate-based clustering 209 

serves as a pre-filtering step that segregates reads before variant 210 

calling. By analyzing each read cluster independently, we prevent 211 

the conflation of signals from different strains and enhance the 212 

algorithm’s ability to detect strain-specific variants. This approach 213 

is particularly effective when strains exhibit structural variations 214 

that cause their reads to map to different reference coordinates 215 

within the same genomic window. 216 

## RESULTS 217 

Benchmark description 218 

We compared SNooPy (0.4.3) with the widely used SNP callers 219 

bcftools (1.22), longshot (1.0.0), Nanocaller (3.6.2), Deepvariant 220 

(1.9.0) and Clair3 (1.0.10), all run with recommended or default 221 

options. 222 

We benchmarked SNooPy on three sequencing datasets. The 223 

first one is a commercially available mock community, named 224 

Zymobiomics Gut Microbiome Standard, sequenced using ONT 225 

R10.4.1 (SRR17913199). This community has the particularity 226 

of containing five strains of Escherichia coli which are mostly 227 

collapsed in the metaFlye assembly, in which we expect to observe 228 

variants. The second and third ones are a human stool and a 229 

soil sample sequenced in [4] using the latest ONT R10.4.1 flow 230 

cells. Both of these datasets were chosen because PacBio HiFi 231 

sequencing of the same samples were conducted [3] and were thus 232 

available to evaluate the quality of the calls . 233 

All datasets were assembled using metaFlye [12]. We then 234 

called the variants using the assembly as a reference. For the 235 

Zymobiomics Gut Microbiome Standard, we report recall and 236 

precision only on the E. coli contigs, to measure the ability of 237 

the SNP callers to call variants in a multi-strains context. The 238 

soil dataset presented computational challenges due to its very 239 

large size (6.8G), which exceeded the processing capacity of all 240 

tools within our one-week runtime constraint. To address this 241 

limitation, we randomly selected 921 contigs (with an N50 of 56kb) 242 

and conducted our analysis exclusively on this subset. 243 

We encountered a technical issue with DeepVariant, which 244 

crashes when processing loci containing multiple alternative 245 

alleles. We have reported this bug to the DeepVariant GitHub 246 

repository. As an interim solution, we excluded the problematic 247 

contigs from our analyses. Since this exclusion did not significantly 248 

alter the performance metrics of other variant callers, we report 249 

statistics across the restricted dataset for all tools. 250

Evaluation metrics 260 

We assessed the recall and precision of the SNP callers. 261 

Variant call comparison is challenging because SNP callers 262 

implicitly assume that reads align on the reference end-to-end, 263 

an assumption that fails with highly divergent sequences or 264 

structural variants. We therefore excluded variants longer than 265 

5bp from our analysis (less than 0.2% of the variants), as 266 

different SNP callers may legitimately disagree on these calls. 267 

For transparency and reproducibility, all comparison scripts 268 

used in this analysis are available in our GitHub repository 269 

(github.com/RolandFaure/SNooPy). 270 

For the Zymobiomics dataset, we aligned the reference genomes 271 

against the assembly and used this alignment to build a set of 272 

ground truth SNPs. 273 

For the other two datasets, we employed PacBio HiFi 274 

sequencing reads, which we mapped on the assemblies using 275 

minimap2 [13], and validated or invalidated variants based on 276 

their presence in the alignment of the set of HiFi reads on the 277 

same assemblies. This approach has inherent limitations: namely, 278 

the two sequencing experiments might have only sequenced a 279 

(different) sample of the overall diversity. Nevertheless, the 280 

detection of a variant using both technologies strongly supports 281 

its validity. For these two datasets, we defined recall as the 282 

proportion of HiFi-confirmed SNPs that each software successfully 283 

identified, relative to the total pool of HiFi-confirmed SNPs called 284 

by any software. This definition intentionally excludes variants 285 

observed exclusively in HiFi data. The precision metric requires 286 

careful interpretation in this context. When SNPs called from 287 

ONT data lack confirmation in HiFi data, we classify them as 288 

false positives. However, this classification may be overly stringent: 289 

some of these variants may represent true polymorphisms that 290 

simply were not captured in the HiFi sequencing. Indeed, manual 291 

investigation using Logan-Search [5] against SRA revealed that 292 

several supposed “false positives” had been previously observed 293 

in other datasets, suggesting they are likely genuine variants. The 294 

limited throughput of Logan-Search did not allow us to conduct a 295 

systematic analysis of all these putative false positives. 296 

The scripts to normalize, merge and compare the obtained 297 

VCFs to either the ground truth or the HiFi mapping results are 298 

available at https://github.com/rolandfaure/snoopy .299 

SNooPy excels on complex communities 300 

Figures 2 and 3 present the recall and precision metrics obtained 301 

on real and simulated datasets, respectively (exhaustive results 302 

are provided in the supplementary data). SNooPy consistently 303 

achieved the highest recall across all datasets, demonstrating 304 

superior performance for metagenomic variant calling 305 

Clair3, Nanocaller, Longshot and, to a lesser extent, Bcftools, 306 

showed limited recall in metagenomic contexts, in line with their 307 

documentation that specifically targets diploid applications. Their 308 

performance degraded dramatically when the number of strains 309 

increased (experiment (i)) or when the coverage became uneven 310 

between the strains (experiment (iv)). 311 

DeepVariant matched the performance of SNooPy on the E. 312 

coli strains of the mock community, achieving more than 90% 313 

recall, but called 13% fewer variants than SNooPy on the soil 314 

sample and detected barely a third of all variants detected by 315 

SNooPy on the human gut sample. The simulated experiments 316 

elucidate these differences: DeepVariant matches SNooPy’s 317 

performance up to six equally-abundant strains (experiment 318 

(i)), equivalent to the mock community configuration. However, 319 

DeepVariant shows reduced recall under low-coverage conditions 320 

(experiments (iii) and (iv)), the dominant scenario in the soil 321 

sample (11 × average coverage). SNooPy also exhibit superior 322 

performance in strain-rich environments and with unequal strain 323 

coverage (experiments (i) and (iv))—conditions characteristic of 324 

the human gut sample (82 × average coverage). 325 

We analysed variants detected by DeepVariant but missed 326 

by SNooPy. The vast majority of missed variants exhibited 327 

very low coverage (≤4× for 90% of cases), as shown in 328 

Supplementary Figure 1. Manual inspection of a subset of variants 329 

revealed that DeepVariant can outperform SNooPy in high-noise 330 

alignment regions, for example around homopolymeric stretches. 331 

We attribute this advantage to the deep neural network’s capacity 332 

to learn robust features in regions where sequence alignment is 333 

difficult. Despite this, SNooPy’s overall recall remained superior 334 

to DeepVariant, even when considering only low-coverage variants. 335 

SNooPy processed the Zymobiomics, gut and soil samples in 336 

20 minutes, 2h30 and 1h on 48 threads, respectively. This is 337 

comparable to DeepVariant, which completed in approximately 338 

one hour for all the three datasets. The complete list of running 339 

times and RAM usage is available in supplementary data. 340 

## DISCUSSION 341 

In this study, we present a new approach for long-read 342 

metagenomic variant calling based on a simple, non-parametric 343 

test of correlation among reads. To our knowledge, this represents 344 

the first statistical variant-calling framework for long reads built 345 

on assumptions sufficiently general to hold across virtually all 346 

types of sequencing experiments, including metagenomic data. 347 

We implemented this test in SNooPy, a metagenomic variant 348 

caller that performed best among the methods tested, including 349 

the deep-learning state-of-the-art methods. This benchmark shed 350 

light on the limitations of the majority of existing long-read 351 

variant callers when applied to metagenomics data. This was 352 

unsurprising given the data they were trained on and their 353 

underlying assumptions, but represent an important, under- 354 

appreciated caveat for practitioners developing their own long-read 355 

metagenomics analysis pipelines. For instance, Longshot has been 356 

frequently used in metagenomics contexts [21, 20] but performed 357 

poorly in our tests. DeepVariant stands out as an exception, 358 

providing adequate precision and recall in the metagenomic 359 

context. However, the current release (v1.9.0) suffers from a bug 360 

that hampers its practical application in metagenomic analyses. 361 

The strength of our pileup-based statistical variant calling is 362 

that it is grounded in a solid statistical framework explicitly 363 

designed for metagenomics. By contrast, the strength of deep 364 

neural networks, which take whole alignments as input rather 365 

than considering only pileups at few distinct loci, lies in its ability 366 

to draw information even out of noisy, low-coverage alignments 367
