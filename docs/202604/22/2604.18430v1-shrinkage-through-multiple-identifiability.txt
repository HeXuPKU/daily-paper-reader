# Shrinkage through multiple identifiability 

Carlos Garc¬ī ńĪa Meixide, David R¬ī ńĪos Insua Instituto de Ciencias Matem¬ī aticas, Madrid, Spain 

Abstract 

We propose an empirical Bayes framework for aggregating estima-tors obtained from several identification functionals associated to the same causal parameter. The central object is a posterior mean that pools a collection of asymptotically linear estimators of a scalar causal target. We establish consistency in two non-nested regimes: exact identifiability, in which every functional identifies the same causal ef-fect; and a second regime, in which individual functionals are biased but the identification biases are mean-zero across functionals, and the number of functionals grows with sample size. The dependence induced by evaluating all estimators on the same sample is handled through a working independence device that preserves consistency of the point estimator. Inference is organized around a latent heterogeneity hyper-parameter: when it vanishes, the functionals share a common target and we report frequentist confidence intervals for that target via a sandwich variance or subsampling; when it is strictly positive, each functional targets a genuine draw from a mixing distribution and we construct asymptotically valid Bayesian prediction intervals for the la-tent target of a new functional. The two inferential outputs rest on distinct assumption sets and are, therefore, complementary rather than exclusive. We illustrate the framework in the context of augmenting randomized controlled trials with observational evidence. 

# 1 Introduction 

Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) are widely regarded as the gold stan-dard for internal validity, but they are costly, slow, and operationally con-strained. Limited control-arm recruitment can inflate variance and erode power even when identification is clear. Historical and real-world data are frequently more abundant and may contain information relevant to the same target question, motivating the augmentation of RCTs with external con-trols or broader real-world data (Viele et al., 2014; Schmidli et al., 2014; 1

> arXiv:2604.18430v1 [stat.ME] 20 Apr 2026

Galwey, 2017; Schmidli et al., 2020; Kojima, 2023). Uncritical borrowing, however, can introduce bias when external and trial populations differ in region, measurement, or care pathway, so precision gains must be balanced against robustness to cross-study discrepancies (Burger et al., 2021; Bus-gang et al., 2022). Handling such discrepancies calls for causal-inference methodology that accounts for hidden confounding. A recurring feature of that methodology is that a given causal parame-ter, defined in the counterfactual full-data world, is typically linked to the observable data-generating distribution through more than one functional. Multiple identifying functionals for the same causal parameter arise rou-tinely: through distinct valid adjustment sets, through back-door versus front-door formulas (Henckel et al., 2022; Gorbach et al., 2023; Mohammad-Taheri et al., 2023), through inverse probability weighting versus outcome regression under no unmeasured confounding, through several pre-treatment cutoffs in regression discontinuity designs, through several candidate con-trol groups in difference-in-differences, and through instrumental variables indexing several data environments (Peters et al., 2016; Garc¬ī ńĪa Meixide and R¬ī ńĪos Insua, 2025; Meixide and Insua, 2025; Meixide and van der Laan, 2025). Under regular asymptotic linearity each such functional admits an efficient influence curve with variance equal to the semiparametric efficiency bound (Bickel et al., 1993; van der Vaart, 1998), and different functionals for the same parameter generally induce different bounds, so no single choice is uni-formly optimal across data-generating mechanisms (Robins and Rotnitzky, 1994; Hahn, 1998; Gorbach et al., 2023). Recent empirical Bayes propos-als build on asymptotically linear estimators to borrow information across related populations generating different samples (Law et al., 2023); our de-velopment takes a related stance at the level of identification rather than population. Wu et al. (2026) develops an empirical Bayes treatment of a single experiment combined with many independent observational studies of the same target, where the bias distribution is identified through auxiliary calibration studies; in contrast, our estimators arise from multiple identify-ing functionals evaluated on a single sample, so shrinkage borrows across functionals. 1.1 Low-bias regime and minimax pooling 

The question of when to pool RCT and observational data has been ana-lyzed in a minimax framework by Chen et al. (2021). In their setting a single RCT estimator ňÜŌąrct is combined with a single observational estimator ňÜŌąobs 

of possibly biased mean, and the candidate pooled class consists of convex combinations indexed by őĽ ‚ąą [0 , 1]. Let ‚ąÜ denote the observational bias, with an upper bound ¬Į‚ąÜ assumed known. The oracle procedure is the infeasi-ble rule that, given ‚ąÜ, minimizes the mean squared error. It is dichotomous: when the bias is large relative to RCT noise, ¬Į‚ąÜ ‚Č≥ ŌÉrct /‚ąönrct , the oracle sets 

őĽ = 0 and uses the RCT alone; when the bias is small, it pools the two sources as if ‚ąÜ = 0. Chen et al. (2021) derive the minimax rate for mean squared error and for confidence-interval length, and show that adaptivity to an unknown bias has a fundamental limit: without prior information placing the bias in the low regime, no uniformly valid confidence interval can improve on the RCT-only interval by more than a constant factor. Our framework specializes to the low-bias regime precisely when ŌĄ 2 = 0: all identifying functionals‚ÄĒincluding the RCT and the IV-based function-als indexed by subsets of environments‚ÄĒare asymptotically unbiased for the same Ōą‚ąó. There the empirical Bayes combination ňÜŌąEB collapses to a precision-weighted average of J valid estimators and inherits the minimax recommendation to pool. Beyond this regime, the centered-bias setting of Theorem 4.2 extends the pooling recommendation to cases in which indi-vidual functionals are biased but their biases average out across j, so long as J ‚Üí ‚ąě . When biases are systematic and uncentered, consistency is not preserved and the minimax lower bound cautions against expecting both uniform validity and short intervals without additional prior information.

A second source of DiD multiplicity, distinct from staggered timing, arises when the analyst has several candidate control groups to contrast against a single treated group and no principled rationale to prefer one over the others. This situation is pervasive in comparative-effectiveness and phar-macoepidemiologic studies. Consider, for instance, a new agent approved for a chronic condition whose effect on a clinical outcome is to be estimated from routinely collected records. The treated group is unambiguous‚ÄĒpatients ini-tiating the new agent at a known date‚ÄĒbut the choice of comparator is not: plausible controls include patients continuing on the previous standard of care, patients switched to an alternative second-line therapy, patients in a watchful-waiting arm, and patients meeting the indication but untreated for access reasons. Each comparator rests on a different parallel-trends assump-tion, reflecting a different assumed absence of differential secular trends in outcome levels, and there is typically no design argument singling out one comparator as exactly correct. Similar considerations arise in policy eval-uation, where the counterfactual trajectory of a treated jurisdiction may be proxied by neighboring jurisdictions, by demographically matched but geographically distant ones, or by units that adopt the policy later. The fol-lowing example formalizes this design; the empirical Bayes framework then aggregates across the candidate comparators without forcing the analyst to commit to one.

# 8 Discussion 

We have developed an empirical Bayes aggregation framework for combining estimators that arise from several identifying functionals of the same scalar causal target. The central object is a precision-weighted estimator ňÜŌąEB that is consistent for Ōą‚ąó in two non-nested regimes. Under exact identifiabil-ity, every functional identifies Ōą‚ąó and aggregation serves a pure efficiency purpose: the combined estimator borrows strength across functionals that share the same identifying target but differ in their semiparametric efficiency bounds. Under centered identification bias with a growing number of func-tionals, no individual functional is consistent, yet the precision-weighted average concentrates around Ōą‚ąó through a law of large numbers over the functional index, provided the identification biases are mean-zero and the precision weights do not asymptotically concentrate on a single functional. The point estimator has the same closed form in both regimes; what changes is the inferential guarantee. The role of the heterogeneity hyperparameter ŌĄ 2 in the working model is therefore twofold. At the estimation step, ŌĄ 2 controls the degree of shrinkage and is estimated by the pairwise-difference estimator ňÜ ŌĄ 2. At the inferential step, the value of ŌĄ 2 organizes a phase transition in what can be reported. When ŌĄ 2 = 0, the de Finetti mixing measure underlying the hierarchical prior degenerates to a point mass at Ōą‚ąó, there is no latent random varia-tion across identifying targets, and the appropriate output is a confidence interval for the fixed parameter Ōą‚ąó. Proposition 6.1 establishes global P -orthogonality in this regime: the asymptotic distribution of n1/2( ňÜŌąEB ‚ąí Ōą‚ąó)is invariant to the limit of ňÜ ŌĄ 2, and an observation-level sandwich variance‚ÄĒ together with a subsampling procedure that is robust to boundary behavior of ňÜ ŌĄ 2 at zero‚ÄĒdelivers valid confidence intervals. When ŌĄ 2 > 0, the mixing measure is non-degenerate and the latent target ŌąJ+1 of a new identifying functional is a genuine random quantity; the correlated sampling noise is asymptotically dominated by the i.i.d. latent heterogeneity, and a split con-formal procedure delivers asymptotically valid prediction intervals for ŌąJ+1 

under the growth condition n‚ąí1 log Jcal ‚Üí 0. The centering condition (C2) of Theorem 4.2 is substantive and should 27 be examined in each application. In the design of Example 2.5, in which instrumental variables index several data environments, (C2) is plausible whenever the set of environments is not systematically selected to favor functionals that over- or under-estimate the causal effect; it would fail, for instance, if weak first stages produce a common direction of finite-sample bias shared across all subsets. Condition (C4) is automatically satisfied once 

J = 2 q ‚ąí q ‚ąí 1 grows exponentially in the number of environments q, and the convergence rate of the estimator is governed by (max j Wj )1/2, which approaches J‚ąí1/2 when the vj are comparable in size. The framework connects naturally to the minimax data-fusion litera-ture. Chen et al. (2021) show that in the low-bias regime pooling strictly dominates RCT-only estimation, while Li et al. (2025) handle misalignment through finite-dimensional selection-bias models. Our proposal complements both by absorbing between-functional discrepancy into a single heterogene-ity hyperparameter at the estimator level, so that precision-weighted pooling is recovered in the low-bias regime while the centered-bias regime extends the pooling recommendation beyond strict exchangeability of populations. The price for this generality is that inference in the centered-bias regime relies on resampling rather than on closed-form variance formulas, and uni-form confidence-interval length reductions beyond a constant factor are not available without prior knowledge of the bias. Following the calibration device of Wu et al. (2026), a natural extension of our framework is the introduction of calibration functionals : identify-ing functionals applied to a contrast whose true causal effect is known apriori to be zero, such as a placebo cohort, a pre-treatment period, or a sub-population subject to a known null intervention. Calibration function-als can identify the location őľ = E[őĶj ] of the bias distribution and remove the centering condition (C2) of Theorem 4.2. Concretely, the working model becomes Ōąj = Ōą‚ąó + őľ + őĶj with őĶj ‚ąľ N (0 , ŌĄ 2), the marginal likelihood of the calibration functionals is concave in őľ given ŌĄ 2, and the resulting plug-in ňÜ őľ

is subtracted from the substantive functionals before the precision-weighted aggregation, restoring consistency under uncentered identification bias. The illusion-return phenomenon of Wu et al. (2026)‚ÄĒin which jointly estimat-ing ( őľ, ŌĄ 2) from the substantive estimators alone collapses ňÜŌąEB onto the minimum-variance functional‚ÄĒexplains why a separate calibration channel is essential rather than a stylistic preference. A number of directions are left open. A systematic Monte Carlo study under both the exact-identifiability and centered-bias regimes would quan-tify finite-sample coverage of the sandwich, subsampling, and conformal intervals across ( J, n, ŌĄ 2). The efficiency loss of the working-independence 28 marginal likelihood relative to a generalized least squares objective with the full J √ó J sampling covariance has not been quantified; this would clarify the practical cost of the nuisance approximation. A complementary recov-ery route arises when the identification biases őĶj are not mean-zero but their signed structure is known, e.g., when some functionals deliver upper bounds and others deliver lower bounds on Ōą‚ąó; a two-group combination that av-erages group-specific empirical Bayes estimators restores consistency when the group-level biases cancel, in the spirit of the doubly robust principle (Bickel et al., 1993), and formalizing this construction as a partial identifi-cation problem is a natural extension. Finally, the paper focuses on scalar causal targets; extending the framework to vector-valued and functional esti-mands, such as heterogeneous treatment effects indexed by covariate strata, is of practical interest and is left for future work.
