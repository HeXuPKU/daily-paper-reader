Abstract 21 

Large-scale clinical genome sequencing yields vast numbers of variants of unknown significance 22 

(VUSs). The high frequency of VUSs and the paucity of platforms to characterize their 23 

functional impact pose significant challenges for clinical decision making. Here, we present an 24 

integrated end-to-end platform, REVi-SCOPE (Rapid evaluation of variants in single cells by 25 

optogenetics and prime editing), for characterization of the impact of VUSs on cardiac 26 

physiology. Our strategy consists of (1) introduction of variants directly into wild-type (WT) 27 

human induced pluripotent stem cell-derived cardiomyocytes (hiPSC-CMs) via prime editing; 28 

(2) optogenetic assessment of calcium and membrane voltage dynamics in single hiPSC-CMs 29 

within the pool of edited and unedited cells; and (3) in situ single-cell genotyping of the 30

Introduction 38 

The integration of genetic testing into cardiovascular medicine is transforming the diagnosis, risk 39 

stratification, and management of both rare and common diseases 1. However, the growing use of 40 

clinical sequencing has uncovered numerous genetic variants, many with uncertain functional 41 

impact 2,3 . These variants of unknown significance (VUSs) dominate public databases, accounting 42 

for more than 50% of the interpreted variants in ClinVar 3. VUSs pose diagnostic and patient 43 

management challenges, particularly when they fall within genes linked to potentially life-44 

threatening arrhythmias such as long QT syndrome (LQTS) or catecholaminergic polymorphic 45 

ventricular tachycardia (CPVT) 2. Standard strategies for variant interpretation rely on phenotypic 46 

concordance, familial segregation, and in silico prediction models, but the impact of many 47 

variants cannot be confidently predicted by these methods 4–7 . Experimental interrogation can 48 

provide direct insight into variant function, but established methodologies—such as variant 49 

assessment through manual electrophysiological recordings—are difficult to deploy broadly at 50 

scale, particularly for functional measurements within cardiomyocytes 2,8 . Although higher-51 

throughput alternatives have emerged, many rely on simplified experimental contexts or 52 

sequencing-based assays that incompletely capture the physiological and functional complexity 53 

of human cardiomyocytes 9–11 .54 

Human induced pluripotent stem cell-derived cardiomyocytes (hiPSC-CMs) have emerged as a 55 

powerful platform for modeling inherited arrhythmias and cardiomyopathies in a human cellular 56 

context, enabling functional assessment of disease-associated variants 12–14 . Most existing 57 

approaches rely on reprogramming patient cells into patient-derived iPSCs lines or genome-58 

editing reference iPSC lines, both of which require laborious isolation of clonal lines followed by 59

Here we present REVi-SCOPE (Rapid evaluation of variants in single cells by optogenetics and 67 

prime editing), an integrated experimental and analytical platform for scalable characterization of 68 

the impact of VUSs on hiPSC-CM calcium-handling and membrane voltage. By combining 69 

efficient population-level prime editing 19–21 , single-cell optical electrophysiology 22–24 , and 70 

coupled in situ single-cell genotyping 25,26  with automated computational analysis, REVi-SCOPE 71 

enables rapid and systematic functional evaluation of cardiac gene variants. Together, these 72 

advances establish a foundation for more scalable and physiologically relevant interpretation of 73 

genetic variants, moving toward experimentally informed and clinically actionable variant 74 

classification. 75

An end-to-end automated analysis program for high-speed functional imaging of isolated 122 

cardiomyocytes 123 

High-speed Ca 2+ or membrane voltage imaging remains one of the most commonly employed 124 

methods to ascertain the anti-arrhythmic or pro-arrhythmic effects of small molecules or genetic 125 

modifications in isolated cardiomyocytes, including hiPSC-CMs 37,38 . However, traditional 126 

methods for high-throughput Ca 2+ or membrane voltage imaging with single-cell resolution pose 127 

analysis challenges, including accurate cell segmentation and low-complexity waveform 128 

processing 39–41 . To overcome these challenges, we developed CaT-Scan (Cardiomyocyte 129 

automated Tracking for Segmentation and Calcium Analysis) as an end-to-end pipeline to 130 

rapidly and accurately analyze high-speed Ca 2+ or membrane voltage imaging data of hiPSC-131 

CMs. We used KIC imaging to acquire high-speed (≥90 frames/sec) movies of hiPSC -CMs 132 

expressing either AOE-MyoC or AOE-MyoV. After down-sampling to reduce computational 133 

and storage demands, cell segmentation was performed using an algorithm based on Cellpose 42 

134 

(Fig. 2a). For each cell, the pipeline then analyzed the Ca 2+ or membrane voltage signal. It first 135 

denoised the data, corrected for photo-bleaching, and identified paced cells (Fig. 2b). For each 136 

paced cell, it analyzed the filtered Ca 2+ transient or action potential waveform and extracted 137 

physiological parameters including upstroke and downstroke velocity, amplitude, and duration 138 

(Fig. 2b). These data were then auto-collated for further statistical analysis. 139 

We tested CaT-Scan against a previously published algorithm for automated Ca 2+ imaging 140 

analysis, CalTrack 41 . When compared to gold-standard human manual segmentation, CaT-Scan 141 

successfully identified and segmented more cells, with higher Jaccard Index and F1 scores than 142 

the CalTrack benchmark (Fig. 2c –e). This resulted in a significantly higher number of analyzed 143 

hiPSC-CMs. Overall, these data demonstrate that our automated pipeline accurately captures 144 

single-cell Ca 2+ transient and action potential parameters for phenotypic characterization at the 145 

single-cell level. 146

Efficient prime editing in hiPSC-CMs 147 

A significant barrier to the functional testing of cardiac gene variants in hiPSC-CMs is the 148 

prerequisite of clonal line generation. Additional barriers include the low yield of standard 149 

monolayer differentiation methods, batch-to-batch variability, and relative immaturity of hiPSC-150 

CMs 17,18,43,44 . To overcome these limitations we incorporated recently described methods for 151 

efficient and large-scale hiPSC-CM differentiation by suspension culture in stirred bioreactors 43 ,152 

which produces hiPSC-CMs with high yields (>150 x 10 6 hiPSC-CMs), purity (>90% cTnT 153 

positivity), and more mature functional properties compared to monolayer hiPSC-CMs 43 , even 154 

after cryo-recovery. These properties make these bioreactor hiPSC-CMs well suited as the 155 

starting point for our variant characterization pipeline 43 . We validated the high purity of the 156 

bioreactor-derived hiPSC-CMs used in this study by flow cytometry (Supplementary Fig. 1c –g). 157 

Prime editing enables precise insertions, deletions, and single-base substitutions in human cells 158 

without double-strand breaks or donor DNA templates, providing broad coverage of genetic 159 

variant classes with reduced off-target activity compared to Cas9-based editing 19–21 . To enable 160 

efficient delivery of the prime editing system into hiPSC-CMs, we used combined adenoviral 161 

and modified RNA delivery to introduce the prime editor PE5max 20  and engineered prime 162 

editing guide RNA (epegRNA) 19 , respectively. Because PE5max exceeds the adenoviral 163 

packaging limit, it was divided into two vectors: AdV-PEmax, encoding PEmax, and AdV-164 

hMLH1dn, encoding dominant-negative human MLH1 (hMLH1dn) 20  (Fig. 3a and b). 165 

Fluorescence imaging confirmed that both adenoviruses were robustly expressed in hiPSC-CMs 166 

(Fig. 3c and d). 167 

To optimize adenoviral dosing for maximal editing activity, we incorporated a Prime Editing 168 

Activity Reporter (PEAR) 45  into an adenoviral vector (Fig. 3e and f). PEAR contains a GFP 169 

coding sequence disrupted by an inserted sequence. Successful prime editing precisely removes 170 

the inserted sequence and restores GFP fluorescence. GFP fluorescence from AdV-PEAR was 171 

only observed when hiPSC-CMs were transduced with AdV-PEmax, demonstrating that AdV-172 

PEAR activity was contingent upon successful prime editing (Fig. 3g). Using AdV-PEAR as a 173 

functional readout, we titrated AdV-PEmax and quantified GFP + cells by Fluorescence-174 

Activated Cell Sorting (FACS), identifying the dose that maximized reporter activation (Fig. 3h 175

PEAR enrichment enables direct phenotypic readout of prime-edited variants 186 

Our prime editing system introduced the SCN5A N406K variant with more than 50% editing 187 

efficiency. We tested whether action potential prolongation caused by this variant could be 188 

detected within a population of prime-edited hiPSC-CMs using our optimized all-189 

electrophysiology system. We treated 3D differentiated WT hiPSC-CMs with AdV-PEmax, 190 

AdV-hMLH1dn, and the SCN5A N406K epegRNA. After prime editing, we transduced hiPSC-CMs 191

Single-cell genotype–phenotype mapping using varGOLDFISH 217 

Given that the mixture of edited and non-edited hiPSC-CMs impairs our ability to characterize 218 

the functional effect of cardiac gene variants, we pursued methods that would enable single-cell 219 

genotyping and thereby connect each cell’s electrophysiological properties with its genotype. To 220 

enable in situ single-cell genotyping, we integrated single guide Genome Oligopaint via Local 221 

Denaturation Fluorescence in Situ Hybridization (sgGOLDFISH) 25 . This high-sensitivity 222 

imaging technique enables single-allele resolution in genomic DNA in situ. In sgGOLDFISH, a 223 

guide RNA targeting the variant directs Cas9 to cleave the genomic locus, enabling loading of a 224 

superhelicase, which creates local DNA denaturation that enables probe binding. We 225 

implemented a dual-probe design in which the gene of interest was targeted with two fluorescent 226 

probes (labeled with Cy5 or Cy3B) one directed to the variant site (sgGOLDFISH), and a second 227 

targeting a neighboring reference region (refGOLDFISH) collectively referred to as 228 

varGOLDFISH. To minimize false-positive calls, only colocalized signals from both probes 229 

were scored as true mutant alleles (Fig. 5a). 230 

As varGOLDFISH has not previously been applied in hiPSC-CMs, we first evaluated its 231 

performance in this cell type using gRNAs targeting the reference sequence in unedited cells. 232 

Detection efficiencies ranged from approximately 20% to 60%, indicating reliable signal 233

Predicting the pathogenicity of patient-derived variants 266 

To assess the utility of the platform for variant interpretation in a clinical context, we analyzed a 267 

VUS identified in a 5-year-old patient presenting with intermittent heart block and conduction 268 

abnormalities (Fig. 6a). The variant, TRPM4 p.A320V, lies within a highly conserved but 269 

intrinsically disordered region of the Ca²⁺ -activated, non-selective cation channel TRPM4 270 

(Transient Receptor Potential Melastatin 4) 49  (Fig. 6b), and traditional variant prediction tools 271 

such as combined annotation dependent depletion (CADD) 50  yielded inconclusive results. 272 

Following epegRNA screening in HEK293T cells (Fig. 6c), the top-performing epegRNA was 273 

selected and achieved ~40% prime-editing efficiency in WT hiPSC-CMs (Fig. 6d). To 274 

characterize the functional impact of this variant, we treated WT hiPSC-CMs with a prime editor 275 

to install TRPM4 A320V . The population of edited and unedited cells was then functionally 276 

characterized using the AOE-MyoV and AOE-MyoC platforms. The same cells were then 277 

genotyped in situ by varGOLDFISH using an optimized var-gRNA (Supplementary Fig. 4b). 278 

Calcium (Fig. 6e–j) and membrane voltage (Fig. 6k–m) parameters were compared between cells 279 

transfected with a control epegRNA and those transfected with the target epegRNA for 280 

TRPM4 A320V . TRPM4 A320V cells exhibited pronounced abnormalities in calcium signaling, 281 

characterized by markedly more frequent aCREs (Fig. 6f and g). In addition, calcium transient 282 

amplitude was significantly reduced (Fig. 6h and i), while calcium transient duration was 283 

significantly shorter than controls (Fig. 6h and j). Optical action potential recordings revealed 284 

that TRPM4 A320V cells had significantly prolonged APD 50 , suggesting prolonged repolarization 285 

(Fig. 6l,m and Supplementary Fig. 6b). 286 

Together, these results demonstrate that TRPM4 A320V induces mixed electrophysiologic 287 

dysregulation potentially consistent with its function as Ca²⁺ -activated Na + channel. This 288 

integrated genotype–phenotype analysis (Fig. 7) supports functional reclassification of 289 

TRPM4 A320V as a pathogenic variant and highlights the potential of the platform to inform 290 

clinical interpretation. 291

Discussion 292 

Existing strategies for assessing cardiac variants—such as clonal iPSC lines, surrogate cell 293 

systems, or bulk assays—are often low-throughput and insensitive to subtle, context-dependent 294 

effects 2,11,51,52 . To overcome these limitations, we developed REVi-SCOPE, an end-to-end 295 

integrated platform that enables direct functional interrogation of prime-edited hiPSC-CMs at 296 

single-cell resolution, without requiring clonal isolation or extended expansion. By combining 297 

single-cell optogenetic phenotyping with in situ single-cell genotyping, REVi-SCOPE links 298 

precise genomic edits to electrophysiological behavior within individual cells, providing 299 

quantitative and mechanistically interpretable readouts of variant function (Fig. 7). This strategy 300 

overcomes the low throughput of clone-based workflows and the reduced sensitivity of clone-301 

free workflows that analyze populations containing a mixture of cells with and without the 302 

variant of interest. 303 

We established several tiers of characterization with varying complexity and precision. For large 304 

effect variants with high editing efficiency (e.g., >50%), prime editing followed by population-305 

level phenotyping may be sufficient to classify a variant as pathogenic. For intermediate cases, 306 

e.g. large effect variants with intermediate editing efficiency (e.g., 20–50%), PEAR enrichment 307 

may be sufficient for variant classification. However, both population level approaches may 308 

underestimate the variant’s effect size, due to inclusion of unedited cells. Combined single-cell 309 

phenotyping with single-cell genotyping yields the most rigorous assessment of variant effect, 310 

and may be needed for accurate classification of smaller effect variants or variants with low 311 

editing efficiencies (e.g., < 20%). By including a dual-probe approach with a strong signal-to-312 

noise ratio, only cells containing colocalized sgGOLDFISH and refGOLDFISH signals were 313 

classified as edited. This enables accurate identification of edited hiPSC-CMs even at low editing 314 

efficiencies. 315

problem, especially in cases of aborted cardiac arrest or victims of sudden death 53,54 .317

sudden cardiac death (SCD) has critically important implications both for the patient or the 319 

surviving family members harboring the same genetic variant. If a variant is confirmed or 320

unanticipated disease mechanisms and linking cellular phenotypes to clinical presentation. The 328 

demonstrated rapid analysis of the VUS TRPM4 A320V illustrates this capability. Functional 329 

interrogation of TRPM4 A320V -edited cardiomyocytes revealed a complex electrophysiological 330 

phenotype characterized by increased arrhythmogenic calcium release events, reduced calcium 331 

transient amplitude and duration, and prolonged action potential repolarization. Aberrant TRPM4 332 

activity has previously been associated with conduction slowing and atrioventricular block 333 

through membrane depolarization–mediated reduction of NaV1.5 availability 55,56 . The observed 334 

prolongation of repolarization and calcium-handling abnormalities in TRPM4 A320V -edited 335 

cardiomyocytes are consistent with dysregulated depolarizing current and excitation–contraction 336 

coupling, providing a mechanistic framework linking the cellular phenotype to the patient’s 337 

intermittent heart block and conduction abnormalities. These findings support a pathogenic role 338 

for TRPM4 A320V and illustrate how integrated single-cell genotype–phenotype analysis can 339 

bridge molecular dysfunction to clinical electrophysiology. 340 

While this study establishes REVi-SCOPE as the first end-to-end method for the rapid 341 

characterization of potentially lethal cardiac gene variants, several important considerations 342 

remain beyond the focus of this current work. Although we were able to determine the effects of 343 

gene variants, either known to be disease-causing or possibly disease-causing, the exact 344 

sensitivity and specificity of the platform is yet to be determined. Additionally, testing of both 345 

pathogenic and benign variants with comparison to clinical outcomes would firmly establish 346 

REVi-SCOPE as a robust clinical diagnostic tool. Increasing the number of tested variants may 347 

also reveal emergent single-cell phenotypes beyond those tested in this present work that would 348 

be an excellent application of machine learning techniques for extended phenotypic extraction. 349

Development of an end-to-end pipeline for high-speed calcium imaging data analysis 476 

CaT-Scan is an end-to-end pipeline that takes high-speed functional imaging videos of 477 

fluorescent biosensors as inputs, and outputs single-cell quantifications of calcium transient or 478 

membrane voltage parameters and aCREs or EADs. To do so, the following steps are performed: 479 

1) down-sampling of imaging videos; 2) automating cardiomyocyte segmentation; 3) calcium 480 

transient or action potential quality control and pre-processing; 4) quantification of calcium 481 

transient or actional potential parameters and aCREs/EADs. 482 

Unprocessed high-speed videos are 2048 x 2048 pixels for 30-35 seconds, amounting to >10 Gb 483 

per video. In our hands, we empirically chose to down-sample the videos to a size of 256 x 256 484

pixels (amounting to ~1Gb per video) to balance data integrity, processing time, and data 485 

storage. 486 

Next, active cardiomyocytes were segmented. To do so, a pixel-wise standard deviation 487 

operation was performed during the pacing period of a down-sampled video. Segmentation was 488 

then performed on this image using a Cellpose backbone 42  that was fine-tuned on 30 manually 489 

segmented hiPSC-CM videos. Fine-tuning was performed using a 5:1 train: validation split, and 490 

a learning rate of 1 × 10 -4 for 10 epochs. 491 

Next, the mean fluorescent intensity of each biosensor detected within each segmented 492 

cardiomyocyte was retrieved as a function of time. For each cell, a custom MATLAB script was 493 

implemented (Fig. 2b). Pre-processing included denoising (via a median filter, wavelet signal 494 

denoising, and Savitzky-Golay filtering), removing photobleaching effect (via a moving 495 

minimum function), and data normalization (relative to the moving minimum). Cells without 496 

beat frequencies that match the predetermined pacing frequency were removed. Standard 497 

calcium transient or action potential parameters were then obtained per beat and averaged across 498 

all pacing beats. Finally, aCREs or EADs were identified with a minimum threshold of 5% of the 499 

mean pacing amplitude, and the metrics included number and duration. 500

Molecular cloning of epegRNAs and sgRNAs 516 

Prime-editing guide constructs were assembled using a type IIS–based Golden Gate strategy 21 .517 

Plasmid backbones supporting either epegRNA expression (Addgene plasmid 174038) or the 518 

associated nicking sgRNA (Addgene plasmid 65777) were first linearized by overnight digestion 519 

with the appropriate type IIS enzyme (BsaI-HFv2 or BsmBI-v2; New England BioLabs). Linear 520

column-based gel extraction kit (Takara, 740609). 522 

Guide-specific DNA oligonucleotides incorporating the spacer, scaffold, and any 3′ extension 523 

sequences were synthesized by Integrated DNA Technologies. Complementary strands were 524 

combined at equal molar ratios, heated to 95°C for 3 min, and allowed to cool slowly to ambient 525 

temperature to facilitate duplex formation. Annealed oligos were phosphorylated with T4 526 

polynucleotide kinase at 37°C for 1 h. 527 

Assembly reactions (10 μL) contained the purified linear vector, phosphorylated duplex oligos, 528 

the corresponding type IIS restriction enzyme, and T4 DNA ligase. Reactions were incubated at 529 

room temperature for an initial ligation phase and then subjected to cycling between 16°C and 530 

37°C (5 min at each temperature, eight cycles). A final 37°C ligation step and heat inactivation at 531 

80°C were performed before holding reactions at 12°C. Reaction mixtures were transformed into 532 

chemically competent E. coli . Individual colonies were screened by Sanger sequencing to 533 

confirm correct guide assembly. 534
