Abstract 1

Missense variants can have a range of functional impacts depending on factors such as the 2specific amino acid substitution and location within the gene. To interpret their deleteriousness, 3studies have sought to identify regions within genes that are specifically intolerant of missense 4variation. Here, we leverage the patterns of rare missense variation in 730,947 exome 5sequenced individuals in the Genome Aggregation Database (gnomAD v4.1.1) against a null 6mutational model to identify transcripts with regional differences in missense constraint. 7Missense-depleted regions are enriched for ClinVar pathogenic variants, de novo missense 8variants from individuals with neurodevelopmental disorders, and complex trait heritability. 9Following ClinGen calibration recommendations for the ACMG/AMP variant classification 10 guidelines, we establish that variants within regions with <36% of their expected missense 11 variation achieve moderate support for pathogenicity. We integrate this regional constraint 12 measure into a missense deleteriousness metric (named MPC) that effectively stratifies rare 13 and de novo missense variants in individuals with early-onset developmental conditions from 14 controls. These results provide additional tools to aid in missense variant interpretation. 15

Main text 17 

Over the last decade, exome and genome sequencing have enabled variant discovery across 18 hundreds of thousands of individuals 1–6 . As sequencing becomes more routine in both clinical 19 and research settings, it is crucial to improve our ability to interpret the potential impacts of 20 these variants, particularly in understanding disease risk. With the rapidly expanding catalog of 21 observed genomic variation comes an increasingly large number of variants of uncertain 22 significance (VUS) that cannot be conclusively considered harmful (pathogenic) or neutral 23 (benign) 7. Indeed, well over half of the variants in the ClinVar database 8, which hosts disease-24 variant associations, are considered VUS, with thousands more added every year 9.25 26 Classification and interpretation of rare variants has been greatly aided by the release of large 27 reference databases, which have provided the opportunity to study selective forces acting on 28 the human genome and to identify genomic regions under selective constraint by, for example, 29 identifying regions with fewer variants than expected based on mutational models 2,3,10–13 . In 30 particular, gene-level metrics of predicted loss-of-function (pLoF) variant depletion have proven 31 to be valuable in variant classification and identification of novel gene-disease relationships 14–18 .32 The functional impact and selective pressures relevant to missense variation, by contrast, 33 remain challenging to predict, as the effect of a missense variant is governed by the gene 34 housing the variant, the position of the variant in the gene, and the specific amino acid 35 substitution caused by the variant. In fact, 90% of the missense variants in ClinVar are VUS 9,19 .36 To address this, prior work has sought to identify regions within coding genes that are 37 specifically intolerant of missense variation as a way to improve classification 20–31 .38 39 One major application of constraint metrics is in novel gene identification, particularly when 40 searching for a significant burden of de novo (newly arising) variants. Both missense 41 deleteriousness metrics and region-specific missense depletion information have been used to 42 improve power to identify genes associated with neurodevelopmental disorders 15,16,32 , as both 43 approaches help separate signal from noise by highlighting a subset of missense variants that 44 are more likely to contribute to rare disorders. Beyond enrichment in statistical frameworks, 45 these metrics can also be incorporated into clinical workflows for variant classification. This 46 requires that the metrics are calibrated for use in the published guidelines for evaluating variant 47 pathogenicity from the American College of Medical Genetics and Genomics and the 48 Association for Molecular Pathology (ACMG/AMP) 33 . These ACMG/AMP guidelines provide 49 recommendations for how various lines of evidence (e.g., computational, functional) can be 50

Results 71 

Identification of missense constraint regions within transcripts 72 

We explored the patterns of rare missense variant presence or absence in 730,947 exomes in 73 the Genome Aggregation Database (gnomAD) v4.1.1 to quantify missense depletion at the sub-74 genic level. We searched 17,841 MANE Select or canonical protein-coding transcripts that 75 passed quality control (see Methods, Supplementary Table 1 ) for variability in missense 76 constraint, quantified as the number of rare (allele frequency [AF] < 0.1%) missense variants 77 observed in gnomAD divided by the number expected under neutral evolution as estimated from 78 previously described mutational models 3. For each transcript, we applied a recursive search 79 based on likelihood ratio tests over all potential rare missense sites looking for breaks that 80 divide the transcript coding sequence into sequences with statistically significantly different 81 missense observed/expected (OE) values (see Supplementary Note and Supplementary Fig. 82 

1), which we refer to as missense constraint regions (MCRs; Fig. 1a-b ). Through this approach, 83

Enrichment of disease-associated variation in missense-depleted regions 167 

Next, we sought to determine if the signatures of selection revealed by MCRs more broadly 168 recapitulated biological and disease relevance of coding sequences. Here, we filtered to high-169 quality, high-coverage sequences (95% of MCRs or 98.3% of coding space; see 170 

Supplementary Note and Supplementary Fig. 4 for discussion of lower-coverage sequences). 171 Overall, transcripts that are more intolerant to pLoF variation, as measured by the loss-of-172 function observed/expected upper bound fraction (LOEUF) score 3,50 , also tend to be more 173 intolerant to missense variation. This trend is markedly more prominent when measuring 174 intolerance via the most constrained MCR in a transcript vs. across the whole transcript 175 (Spearman ρ = 0.58 vs. 0.51; Supplementary Note and Supplementary Fig. 5 ). We confirmed 176 that, as expected, autosomal genes without disease associations in OMIM 57 tended to be less 177 missense-constrained than genes with autosomal dominant disease inheritance (Welch’s t p < 178 10 -50 for transcript missense OE and Wilcoxon p < 10 -50 for minimum MCR missense OE). 179 However, we found that these non-OMIM genes were also more missense-constrained than 180 genes with autosomal recessive disease inheritance (Welch’s t p = 1.1x10 -16 for transcript 181 missense OE and Wilcoxon p = 9.0x10 -7 for minimum MCR missense OE; Supplementary Fig. 182 

6). Taken together with our finding that 62% (1,511/2,430) of genes that are both LOEUF-183 constrained and severely missense-depleted (first three deciles of LOEUF and MCR with OE < 184 0.4) do not have disease associations in OMIM, this highlights a potential search space for 185 novel gene-disease relationships ( Supplementary Fig. 7 ). 186 187 We defined a set of 695 strongly pLoF-intolerant genes as those in the first three deciles of 188 LOEUF or either of two s het measures 58,59 and with monoallelic association with a developmental 189 phenotype in Gene2Phenotype (G2P) 60 . Evaluating the clinical utility of missense depletion 262 

We then assessed the ability of MCRs to recapitulate clinical disease information by examining 263 the constraint distribution around rare ClinVar P/LP and benign/likely benign (B/LB) missense 264 variants. For example, in 337 genes 269 with both constrained (missense OE < 0.6) and unconstrained (missense OE > 0.9) MCRs, 270 P/LP variants occur 11-fold more frequently in constrained than unconstrained MCRs, while 271 B/LB variants occur 9-fold more frequently in unconstrained than constrained MCRs (odds ratio 272 [OR] = 93, Fisher’s exact p < 10 -50 ). These patterns are also significant, though more mild, in 273 genes associated with autosomal recessive inheritance ( Supplementary Fig. 11c-d, 274

Together, these results demonstrate that our constraint metric is 275 able to distinguish specific sequences within genes that are enriched for pathogenic or benign 276 variants. 277 278 Having confirmed a correlation between constraint and clinical disease information, we then 279 sought to quantify the utility of our constraint metric as a point of evidence for clinical variant 280 classification. To do so, we leveraged a probabilistic framework established by ClinGen 34 that 281 formally identifies score thresholds for computational metrics that match each variant 282 classification tier (e.g., “strong” or “moderate”) under the ACMG/AMP guidelines 33 . We applied 283 this robust quantitative framework to determine the missense OE thresholds that met different 284 levels of clinical evidence strengths evaluated under the population data-derived pathogenic 285 (PP2) and benign (BP1) criteria codes 33 . We did not apply a restriction on gnomAD coverage for 286 this assessment, as individuals leveraging our constraint metric for clinical interpretation may 287 not make such considerations, though we do provide coverage information and scores are 288 coverage-adjusted (see Supplementary Note ). The resulting thresholds are thus conservative 289 estimates. 17 MTR method of segmenting genes into regions is prone to identifying likely spurious 438 “unconstrained” regions as there are no restrictions placed on the size of unconstrained regions, 439 only constrained regions. This results in approximately 16% of unconstrained regions falling 440 under the 10 amino acid minimum applied to constrained regions. Furthermore, the binary 441 nature of the assignment of amino acids as “constrained” or “unconstrained” for input to 442 segmentation means that there is no distinction between regions of varying levels of constraint 443 below the 15th percentile threshold designated “constrained”. As a result, consecutive regions 444 that fall under the “constrained” threshold but have different levels of constraint will be unlikely 445 to be distinguished from each other by the MTR method unless an “unconstrained region” is 446 identified that separates them. In contrast, our MCR method delineates gene segments based 447 on having differing levels of constraint without this binarization, and the size minimum of 16 448 expected variants is applied to all MCRs for robustness. 449 450 We note that constraint, as quantified here by population depletion of observed rare variants, is 451 essentially a footprint of selection on heterozygous variants. Our methodology is thus most 452 powerful in sequences undergoing appreciable heterozygous selection, e.g., genes where 453 severe and early onset diseases are caused by dominant missense variants. However, as 454 demonstrated in Supplementary Fig. 11 , disease variants in genes currently known to be only 455 associated with conditions with recessive inheritance do also associate with detectable 456 constraint, albeit to a lesser degree than dominant variants. This lends further support to a body 457 of evidence that few genes are purely recessive: many “recessive-only” genes are likely only 458 partially recessive, with heterozygotes having measurable phenotypic differences 74–76 . As such, 459 we show that this constraint method is indeed amenable, to an extent, to characterizing 460 selective effects even in genes considered to be solely associated with recessive inheritance. 461 462 An inherent strength of our regional missense constraint metric is its grounding in population 463 genetics and independence of, and thus lack of bias from, disease genetics databases and 464 domain or structural knowledge. We defined the set of sites with possible missense variants using a synthetic Hail Table (HT) 592 containing all possible single nucleotide variants in the exome. We annotated this HT with the 593 Ensembl VEP table and filtered it to variants with the consequence "missense_variant" in the 594 MANE Select or canonical coding transcripts as defined in Transcripts . We then aggregated the total 597 number of observed variant counts per base pair site by summing the number of observed 598 variants over each possible substitution (reference and alternate allele) at the locus. 599 600 As described in more detail for the gnomAD v4.1.1 release 50 , we applied two models (plateau 601 and coverage) to calculate the proportion of expected missense variation per variant 602 (chromosome/locus plus reference and alternate alleles). Modeling deleteriousness of individual missense variants 678 

We designed our missense variant deleteriousness predictor (Missense deleteriousness 679 Prediction by Constraint or MPC) to explicitly incorporate information on amino acid substitution 680 class and position-specific variant effects informed by missense constraint. Briefly, we trained a 681 machine learning model to differentiate “pathogenic” missense variants likely under strong 682 heterozygous selection from “benign” missense variants likely under neutral to near-neutral 683 selection. The “pathogenic” training set consisted of high-quality ClinVar variants (see ClinVar 684 

variants ) labeled as P or LP in 2,987 predicted-haploinsufficient genes (defined as having 685 probability of haploinsufficiency (pHaplo) ≥ 0.86 64 ) or in 359 genes with DD associations in G2P 686 through non-LoF mechanisms (see Gene2Phenotype data ). The “benign” training set consisted 687 of high-quality gnomAD variants AF > 0.1% or ClinVar B/LB variants in the same genes as the 688 “pathogenic” set (see gnomAD variants and ClinVar variants ). Variants matching criteria for both 689 the “benign” and “pathogenic” sets were removed from the training data. We further removed 690 variants with %AN < 20 in the gnomAD v4.1.1 exomes and variants that were not in the 17,841 691 MANE Select/canonical transcripts. 692 693 We evaluated logistic regression and XGBoost (gradient-boosted tree) architectures, 694 incorporating features of MCR and gene constraint, amino acid substitution class severity, 695 PolyPhen-2 for orthogonal information from protein homology and structure, and phyloP for 696 evolutionary selection information across longer timescales. Only variants with all relevant 697 annotations were retained for training and for the full variant score table. The XGBoost model 698 was chosen over logistic regression based on performance on a hold-out gene set and then 699 retrained on all data (20,931 “pathogenic” variants and 93,638 “benign” variants) for the final 700 model. We applied the XGBoost model across the 70,313,598 possible exome-wide missense 701 variants in the Ensembl VEP table with all features to obtain a probability of pathogenicity for 702 each variant. When mi is 0, i.e. when the 709 fitted score for variant i is more severe than for all “benign” variants, this introduces a log-zero 710 error so we set di to 6 (the maximum real value for di is just over 5 when mi is 1). Larger values 711 of di indicate stronger predicted-deleteriousness. See Supplementary Note and 712
