Abstract 10 

Milk urea concentration (MUC) is an indicator of dietary protein utilization and nitrogen use 11 

efficiency in dairy cows. We performed genome-wide association studies (GWAS) on MUC in 12 

early, mid, and late lactation in the Holstein (HOL) and Brown Swiss (BSW) dairy cattle breeds 13 

using imputed sequence variants. We identified 11 and 17 independent quantitative trait loci 14 

(QTL) for MUC across the three lactation stages in BSW and HOL, respectively. While many of 15 

these QTL have previously been reported for MUC and other dairy traits, our study provides 16 

evidence that some QTL exert lactation-stage specific effects. Our findings suggest that variants 17 

at the DGAT1 locus on BTA14 have pleiotropic effects on MUC and other dairy traits. This QTL 18 

showed an early lactation-specific association with MUC but impacted milk and fat yield across 19 

the entire lactation. We fine-mapped two QTL for MUC in early and mid-lactation in BSW on 20 

BTA9 (lead SNP: 9:21392941, P corrected = 1.1E-17) and BTA28 (lead SNP: 28:6518357; P corrected =21

Introduction 27 

Milk urea concentration (MUC) is a reliable indicator of nitrogen use efficiency and nitrogen 28 

excretion in dairy cattle (Powell et al., 2011; Barros et al., 2019). MUC is proportional to blood 29 

urea concentration, which predominantly derives from systemic protein metabolism (Butler et al., 30 

1996; Ghavi Hossein-Zadeh, 2024). Therefore, MUC reflects the activity of multiple biological 31 

pathways involved in protein synthesis, nitrogen metabolism, and the animal's energy balance 32 

(Oltner and Wiktorsson, 1983; Westwood et al., 1998; Ghavi Hossein-Zadeh, 2024). MUC is 33 

likely to share part of its genetic architecture with other milk composition traits, since pathways 34 

of fat metabolism and protein synthesis jointly regulate nitrogen utilization and milk composition 35 

(Hojman et al., 2004; Rzewuska and Strabel, 2013; Jahnel et al., 2023). 36 

Previous studies have demonstrated that MUC is moderately heritable (Buitenhuis and Poulsen, 37 

2023). Selection for lower MUC may reduce urinary nitrogen excretion and improve 38 

environmental sustainability (O’Callaghan et al., 2019; Marshall et al., 2021; Prahl et al., 2022). 39 

However, too low MUC can reflect inadequate protein status and impaired nitrogen metabolism, 40 

potentially compromising health and reproductive performance (Hojman et al., 2004). Therefore, 41 

maintaining an optimal MUC is essential for balancing productivity and health with excess 42 

nitrogen excretion (Zhao et al., 2025). 43

Several genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have reported quantitative trait loci (QTL) for 44 

MUC in cattle (Pegolo et al., 2018; Ma et al., 2023; Atashi et al., 2024). Multi-breed GWAS (Van 45 

Den Berg et al., 2022; Atashi et al., 2024) identified MUC-associated variants show consistent 46 

effects across breeds, suggesting a shared genetic architecture. These studies for MUC relied on 47 

global phenotypes that were estimated over the entire lactation, which precluded the 48 

investigation of stage-specific genetic effects underlying dynamic changes in MUC across 49 

lactation, including those related to metabolic, endocrine, and immune regulation (Bionaz and 50 

Loor, 2008; Mortazavi et al., 2025). While the lactation cycle has pronounced effects on milk 51 

yield and composition (Lund et al., 2008; Strucken et al., 2012), lactation stage-specific analyses 52 

for MUC remain relatively underexplored (Mortazavi et al., 2025). Using MUC measured at 53 

distinct lactation stages may help resolve the temporal genetic architecture of dairy traits across 54 

the lactation cycle. 55 

Here, we investigated the genetic architecture of MUC in early, mid, and late lactation in two 56 

dairy breeds. We identified previously reported and novel QTL including several with lactation 57 

stage-specific effects. We further identified positional and functional candidate genes for two 58 

QTL on BTA9 and BTA28 and prioritized a missense variant in KCNK1 as a candidate causal 59 

variant for MUC in cattle. 60

Genotypes 112 

We considered 178,845 animals from the BSW, OB, HOL, SIM and SF breeds that had 113 

microarray-derived genotypes. The animals were genotyped using 10 arrays comprising between 114 

20K and 777K SNPs. Quality control was performed for each array type separately as described 115 

in Watson et al (Watson et al., 2025). Subsequently, genotype imputation was performed in a 116 

stepwise procedure with Beagle (v 5.4) (Browning et al., 2021) for two breed groups separately: 117 

BSW, OB, and their crosses, and HOL, SIM, SF, and their crosses. In the first step, Illumina 118 

Bovine HD SNP BeadChip genotypes were imputed into low- and medium-density array-derived 119 

genotypes using reference panels of 1,183 BSW/OB and 1,138 HOL/SIM/SF animals. In the 120 

second step, whole-genome sequence data from 607 in-house sequenced BSW and OB (Lloret-121 

Villas et al., 2023), and 1,464 HOL and SIM animals selected from the 1000 Bull Genomes 122 

Project run 9 (Daetwyler et al., 2014) were used as a reference to impute sequence-level variants. 123 

This approach provided genotypes for 26,265,006 sequence variants for the BSW and OB group, 124 

and 37,153,418 sequence variants for the HOL, SIM, and SF group. For the subsequent analyses, 125 

we retained 60,564 BSW and 89,315 HOL animals. 126

Genome-wide association study 135 

Additive GWAS were conducted between the imputed sequence variant genotypes and the 136 

window-specific traits within the two breeds separately using a mixed linear model-based 137 

approach implemented in the BOLT-LMM (v2.4.1) software (Loh et al., 2015). We retained 138 

SNPs that had MAF ≥ 0.01 in each lactation stage-specific cohort. Linkage disequilibrium (LD) 139 

scores were computed with PLINK (v1.9) (Purcell et al., 2007). The LD-pruned subset 140 

considered to construct the model SNP (Loh et al., 2018) contained 1,460,499 and 1,686,281 141 

SNPs in BSW and HOL, respectively. The top four PCs were included as covariates for 142 

association testing. SNPs that deviated from Hardy-Weinberg proportions (P <E-05) were 143 

removed. Residual inflation detected in the GWAS summary statistics was accounted for using 144 

genomic control (Devlin and Roeder, 1999). 145 

Association analysis conditional on the most significantly associated marker was performed 146 

within breeds using the “-cojo” flag of GCTA (Genetic Investigation of ANthropometric Traits 147 

(GIANT) Consortium et al., 2012). Mendelian randomization (MR) was performed with the 148 

GSMR (Zhu et al., 2018) model of GCTA to investigate a putative causal association between 149 

MKG and MUC or FKG and MUC. For each trait pair, both forward and reverse GSMR analyses 150 

were conducted (--gsmr-direction 2). LD-clumped (r² = 0.5) genome-wide significant SNPs 151 

(P GWAS < 5E-08) were used as instrumental variables (IVs), and the HEIDI outlier test (--heidi-152 

thresh 0.1) was applied to remove variants whose effects were inconsistent with the main MR 153

Functional annotation 158 

Sequence variants were annotated using the Ensembl Variant Effect Predictor (McLaren et al., 159 

2016) based on the bovine reference genome (ARS-UCD1.2) and the corresponding Ensembl 160 

annotation (Dyer et al., 2025). The pairwise LD between the lead SNPs and all other SNPs 161

In BSW, our lactation stage-specific GWAS revealed 4, 6 and 1 independent QTL (P corrected < 5E-188 

08, P cojo < 5E-08) for MUC in early, mid and late lactation, respectively. Correspondingly, 6, 6, 189 

and 5 independent QTL were identified in HOL (Figure 1) (Supplemental Table S2; see Notes). 190 

While several of the MUC QTL overlapped genomic regions associated with milk yield, fat yield, 191 

or protein yield (Supplemental Figure S3; see Notes), the overwhelming majority of the MUC 192 

QTL lead variants were not associated with any of the three dairy traits. Differences in sample 193 

size and MUC h 2 were observed across the three lactation stages. The statistical power to detect 194 

QTL for MUC was similar for the early and mid-lactation in both breeds, but was reduced in late 195 

lactation (Wu et al., 2022), which likely limited the detection of significant QTL during this stage. 196 

Several QTL for MUC overlapped genomic regions that had previously been reported to be 197 

associated with MUC and other dairy traits. However, unlike earlier studies that relied on global 198 

phenotypes averaged across the entire lactation cycle, our GWAS used lactation stage–specific 199 

phenotypes, thereby providing a more nuanced view of how these regions might influence MUC 200 

across the lactation cycle. This included a QTL in BSW (BTA19, ~43.9 Mb) (Cecchinato et al., 201 

2014; Lopdell, 2023), and two QTL in HOL (BTA14, ~0.6 Mb; BTA19, 41.7Mb) (Cecchinato et 202 

al., 2014; Van Den Berg et al., 2022; Lopdell, 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Atashi et al., 2024), which 203 

primarily affected MUC in the early lactation. Additionally, a BSW QTL on BTA8 (103.4 Mb) 204 

(Chen et al., 2023) primary affected MUC in mid lactation. 205 

We also identified QTL that were significant across multiple lactation stages. Two HOL QTL on 206 

BTA1 (146.8 Mb) (Atashi et al., 2022) and BTA6 (~85.6 Mb) (Pegolo et al., 2018; Atashi et al., 207 

2024) were detected across all three stages while a QTL on BTA11 (103.2 Mb) (Atashi et al., 208 

2024) was observed in both BSW and HOL in early and mid-lactation only. For all QTL that 209 

were shared across lactation stages, the lead variant from a specific window was typically also 210

Two QTL for MUC reside on BTA9 and BTA28 282 

The GWAS identified two QTL on BTA9 (~21.39 Mb) and BTA28 (~6.52 Mb) in BSW that, to 283

Conclusions 347 

We mapped temporal QTL dynamics for MUC across lactation windows in two Swiss dairy 348 

cattle breeds, showing that stage-specific MUC signals capture meaningful biology. Using these 349 

dynamics, we refined pleiotropy at BTA14, clarified the complex architecture at BTA6, and 350 

prioritized candidate genes for two QTL on BTA9 and BTA28 linked to immune regulation and 351 

osmotic homeostasis. 352
