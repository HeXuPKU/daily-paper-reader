Abstract 18 

Sex differences are commonly observed in neuroimaging phenotypes and in the risk 19 

of brain diseases, yet the underlying genetic mechanisms remain poorly understood. 20 

We investigated sex differences in the genetic architecture of 805 neuroimaging 21 

phenotypes in 22,950 males and 22,950 females matched for sample size and 22 

covariates, and systematically compared sex-stratified with sex-combined genetic 23 

analyses. We found eight variant-trait associations with significant sex differences, 24 

235 fine-mapped sex-dominant causal associations, 457 sex-dominant colocalizations 25 

with sex hormones, and 96 sex-dominant colocalizations with schizophrenia. 26 

Compared with sex-combined analysis, sex-stratified analysis identified 47 new 27 

genetic associations, 170 new fine-mapped causal associations, 1,019 new 28 

colocalizations with sex hormones, and 191 new colocalizations with schizophrenia. 29 

Additionally, sex-stratified analysis improved global heritability and 30 

genetic-correlation estimates and enhanced polygenic prediction for certain 31 

phenotypes. This work highlights the need to routinely perform sex-stratified genetic 32 

association analyses to elucidate sex-specific and sex-shared genetic control of 33 

neuroimaging phenotypes and related disorders. 34 35

Introduction 36 

Sex differences are commonly observed in human brain anatomy, as measured in vivo 37 

by imaging-derived phenotypes (IDPs), including gray matter volume (GMV), 38 

cortical thickness (CT), surface area (SA), and white matter microstructure 1-5 . They 39 

may arise from differing genetic architectures, environmental pressures, 40 

neurodevelopment, and hormone milieu between men and women 6-8 , and contribute to 41 

sex differences in brain functional activity and connectivity, cognitive performance, 42 

emotion regulation, and the epidemiology of neuropsychiatric disorders 9-12 . Although 43 

brain anatomical IDPs are highly heritable 13,14 , the genetic architecture underlying 44 

their sex differences remains poorly understood, because most previous genome-wide 45 

association studies (GWASs) of brain anatomical IDPs were conducted on 46 

sex-combined samples 15-17 , which may be biased by sex differences in genetic 47 

effects 6,18 . Identifying the genetic architecture underlying sex differences in brain 48 

anatomy is crucial for understanding the biological mechanisms driving sex 49 

disparities in brain-related traits, including disorders, and for developing personalized 50 

treatments. 51 

Two previous studies examined sex differences in the genetic architecture of 52 

brain IDPs via sex-stratified GWASs. One reported sex differences in two single 53 

nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) effects on amygdala subregional volumes 18 . Another 54 

assessed sex differences in SNP-based heritability estimates, genetic correlations, and 55 

SNP effects for 1,106 GMV, CT, and SA phenotypes 6. That study reported an overall 56 

similar genetic architecture of these IDPs between sexes but revealed significant sex 57

sample size and covariates, which may introduce bias. Second, they focused 62

architecture of brain white matter IDPs uncertain. Third, other genetic characteristics 64 

remain unexplored. For instance, statistical fine-mapping can prioritize causal 65 

variant-trait associations for functional experiments, and colocalization analysis can 66 

identify shared genetic architecture between brain IDPs and other brain-related traits. 67 

However, sex differences in causal associations and genetic colocalizations remain 68 

unclear. 69 

Compared with sex-combined GWAS, sex-stratified GWAS can not only identify 70 

sex differences in genetic architecture, but also reveal new genetic insights and 71 

improve the precision of genetic analyses and their clinical applications. For example, 72 

a previous study on non-imaging phenotypes indicates that sex-stratified analyses can 73 

discovery novel trait-associated loci and improve polygenic score (PGS) prediction of 74 

those traits 19 . However, a systematic comparison between sex-stratified and 75 

sex-combined GWASs regarding their performance in heritability estimation, genetic 76 

correlation, association discovery, PGS prediction, statistical fine-mapping, and 77 

genetic colocalization is still lacking. For instance, colocalization analysis using 78 

sex-stratified GWASs may reveal sex-dominant colocalizations with sex hormones 79

Based on 48,781 participants (23,060 males and 25,721 females) recruited by the 82 

UK Biobank (UKB) 20 , we created male and female subgroups matched for sample 83 

size (n = 22,950) and covariates, along with a sex-combined group (n = 45,900) by 84 

including participants from both subgroups. We focused on 805 brain IDPs reflecting 85 

gray matter morphology or white matter integrity, and systematically investigated sex 86 

differences in heritability estimates, genetic correlations, SNP effects, causal 87 

associations, and colocalizations. In the same sample (n = 45,900), we also compared 88 

sex-stratified and sex-combined genetic analyses for their performance in heritability 89 

estimation, genetic correlation, association discovery, PGS prediction, statistical 90 

fine-mapping, and genetic colocalization. The overall workflow is illustrated in 91

Results 94 

Participants 95 

All participants were recruited from the UK Biobank 20 . After quality control of 96 

genetic and neuroimaging data, we included 48,781 participants (23,060 males and 97 

25,721 females). The demographic and covariate data are shown in Supplementary 98 

Table 1. We conducted propensity score matching (PSM) 21 to create male and female 99 

subgroups that were matched in sample size (n = 22,950) and covariates. After 100 

matching, the absolute standardized mean differences (SMDs) in all covariates 101 

between the two sex subgroups were less than 0.1 (Extended Data Fig. 2; 102 

Supplementary Table 2), indicating good balance. The 22,950 males and 22,950 103 

females together comprised the sex-combined sample (n = 45,900). We performed 104 

genetic analyses on 16,013,685 autosomal variants and 805 brain IDPs derived from 105 

structural (sMRI) and diffusion (dMRI) magnetic resonance imaging data 106 

(Supplementary Table 3). The 373 sMRI-IDPs comprised the GMVs of 139 brain 107 

regions from volume-based segmentation, the CTs and SAs of 62 cortical regions 108 

from surface-based segmentation, and the volumes of 110 subcortical subregions from 109 

Freesurfer subsegmentation. The 432 dMRI-IDPs evaluated nine white matter 110 

integrity measures across 48 tracts. 111

Genetic correlation 148 

Genetic correlation ( rg) evaluates the shared genetic architecture between two traits 149 

measured in the same sample or between two subgroups of the same trait. We applied 150 

LDSC v.1.0.0 22 to estimate the between-sex genetic correlation for each IDP, along 151 

with the between-IDP genetic correlation within each sex. We focused on sex 152 

differences in both kinds of genetic correlations. 153 

Between-sex genetic correlation. Among the 805 IDPs, we successfully calculated 154 

the between-sex genetic correlations (ranging from 0.55 to 1.19) for 757 IDPs. We 155 

defined a sex difference as a between-sex rg value for an IDP that is significantly 156 

lower than 1 ( P < 0.05/805 = 6.21 × 10 -5 ). Although no IDPs showed significant sex 157 

differences in between-sex genetic correlations at P < 6.21 × 10 -5 , 35 IDPs showed 158 

significant sex differences at P < 0.05 (Supplementary Table 6). 159

Discussion 560 

In this study, we conducted sex-stratified GWASs on 805 brain IDPs in 22,950 males 561 

and 22,950 females matched for sample size and covariates, along with sex-combined 562 

GWASs in the combined sample. We systematically compared sex differences and 563 

sex-stratified versus sex-combined analyses across six perspectives: SNP-based 564 

heritability, genetic correlation, GWAS associations, PGS prediction, statistical 565 

fine-mapping, and colocalization. The novel findings regarding sex differences 566 

included: (1) significantly higher overall genetic correlations in females than in males; 567 

(2) 85 male-specific and 81 female-specific genome-wide significant associations; (3) 568 

133 male-dominant and 102 female-dominant causal associations; (4) 268 569 

male-dominant and 189 female-dominant colocalizations with sex hormones, along 570 

with 82 male-dominant and 14 female-dominant colocalizations with schizophrenia. 571 

Comparing sex-stratified with sex-combined analyses, the novel findings included: (1) 572 

sex-combined analyses overestimated heritability and genetic correlation in males and 573 

underestimated heritability and genetic correlation in females; (2) sex-stratified 574 

analyses identified 47 new GWAS associations, 170 new causal associations, 1,019 575 

new colocalizations with sex hormones, and 191 new colocalizations with 576 

schizophrenia. Compared with a prior study 7 reporting general concordance in genetic 577 

architectures between sexes, this study provides compelling evidence for the value of 578 

sex-stratified analyses, offering novel insights into sex-specific genetic mechanisms 579 

of brain imaging phenotypes that cannot be identified in sex-combined analyses. 580
