# EA-PheWAS: Integrating Phenotype Embeddings with PheWAS for Enhanced Gene-Phenotype Discovery 

Wangjie Zheng 1, Tianyu Liu 2, Leqi Xu 1, Yuhan Xie 1, Yueqian Jing 1, Haoran Shao 1, Hongyu Zhao 1,2* 

1 Department of Biostatistics, Yale University, New Haven, CT, United States 

2 Interdepartmental Program in Computational Biology and Bioinformatics, Yale University, New Haven, CT, United States * hongyu.zhao@yale.edu 

Abstract 

Phenome-wide association studies (PheWAS) enable systematic exploration of relationships between genetic variants and clinical phenotypes derived from electronic health records (EHRs). Conventional regression-based PheWAS treats phenotypes separately and relies on binary phenotype representations, which limits statistical power for rare variants and rare phenotypes and reduces the ability to detect associations with phenotypes that are distributed across clinical codes. To address this limitation, we first developed EmbedPheScan, a phenotype embedding–based prioritization framework that summarizes the phenotypic profiles of rare loss-of-function variant carriers in a continuous embedding space. We then proposed EA-PheWAS by combining these embedding-derived signals with conventional regression-based PheWAS results using the aggregated Cauchy association test. Using the UK Biobank whole-exome sequencing and EHR data, we show that the proposed methods maintain appropriate false-positive control. We then performed genome-wide phenome scans across all genes and across biologically defined gene classes to evaluate EA-PheWAS relative to conventional PheWAS and EmbedPheScan, consistently finding that EA-PheWAS outperformed the other two methods. We illustrate the utility of EA-PheWAS focusing on four genes representing distinct scenarios, including strong-effect disease genes ( PKD1 , PKD2 ), genes with large numbers of rare LoF carriers ( NF1 ), and genes with extremely sparse carrier counts ( FBN1 ). 

# Introduction 1

Phenome-wide association studies (PheWAS) provide a systematic framework for identifying associations 2

between genetic variants and a broad spectrum of clinical phenotypes derived from electronic health 3

records (EHRs) [1]. Originally developed for common single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), PheWAS 4

evaluates genotype–phenotype relationships by testing each phenotype separately using regression-based 5

models, typically encoding disease phenotypes as binary indicators defined by diagnostic codes or 6

phecodes. This paradigm enables genome-wide screening of associations and has been widely adopted in 7

biobank-scale studies [2]. At the same time, the increasing availability of whole-exome sequencing (WES) 8

data and longitudinal EHR data in biobanks has created new opportunities to investigate gene–phenotype 9

relationships at much greater resolution. 10 

PheWAS has been extended to rare variants through gene-level analyses, in which multiple rare 11 

variants within a gene are aggregated using burden [3] or kernel-based tests [4, 5] and evaluated against 12 

each phenotype [6]. This shift from common variants to rare variants is necessary because individual rare 13

effects [7]. However, even in this setting, the overlap between rare variant carriers and phenotype cases is 17 

frequently too limited to support adequately powered association testing, even in large biobanks [6]. 18 

Despite these extensions, the PheWAS approach remains largely unchanged: phenotypes are treated as 19 

separate outcomes, and associations are assessed using covariate-adjusted regression models, typically 20 

logistic regression for binary traits. This formulation does not make use of the structure naturally 21 

encoded in longitudinal EHR which records capture phenotypes as sequences of ICD-10 diagnostic 22 

codes [8], and these codes exhibit hierarchical organization, semantic similarity, and strong co-occurrence 23 

patterns. However, conventional PheWAS collapses this rich structure into binary phenotype indicators, 24 

discarding information about similarity, hierarchy, and comorbidity. This limitation is particularly 25 

important for rare LoF carriers, whose clinical manifestations are often heterogeneous and pleiotropic, 26 

spanning multiple related phenotypes and organ systems [9]. In many cases, carriers of the same gene 27 

exhibit coherent phenotype patterns, but those signals are fragmented across multiple diagnostic codes, 28 

each of which may be too rare to achieve statistical significance when tested independently. As a result, 29 

regression-based PheWAS may fail to recover the broader and mechanistically coherent phenotype 30 

spectrum, instead prioritizing only the most prevalent or utilization-driven phenotypes [10]. 31 

Embedding models provide a principled framework for capturing latent relationships among clinical 32 

concepts by representing them as vectors, where proximity reflects similar clinical context. Methods such 33 

as Word2Vec [11] have been widely applied to EHR data to learn representations of medical codes, 34 

phenotypes, and patients [12 –14]. These approaches have demonstrated strong performance in clustering 35 

prediction, and representation learning tasks, highlighting their ability to encode clinically meaningful 36 

structure. In our previous work, PERADIGM [14], we showed that embedding-similarity–based methods 37 

may substantially improve the identifications of disease-associated genes for rare diseases compared with 38 

conventional association methods using binary phenotypes such as burden tests [3], SKAT, and 39 

SKAT-O [4, 5]. These results suggest that embedding representations may capture clinically relevant 40 

relationships that are not accessible through conventional discrete phenotype representations. 41 

Motivated by these observations, we extend the embedding-based method from rare-disease gene 42 

prioritization to PheWAS. We propose EA-PheWAS (Embedding-Augmented PheWAS), a unified 43 

framework that integrates signals from regression-based PheWAS and embedding-similarity–based 44 

phenotype prioritization. Rather than relying solely on phenotype-similarity-based ranking, EA-PheWAS 45 

leverages phenotype embeddings to complement conventional regression-based PheWAS, borrowing 46 

information from the embedding space to enhance the detection and prioritization of gene–phenotype 47 

associations. We first demonstrate through simulation studies that our method maintains appropriate 48 

control of the false positive rate. We then perform a genome-wide scan across all genes in the UK 49 

Biobank dataset to evaluate the overall performance of EA-PheWAS relative to conventional approaches. 50 

Next, we examine biologically informed gene subsets to evaluate the performance of EA-PheWAS in more 51 

functionally constrained gene sets rather than across all genes genome-wide. Finally, we report detailed 52 

case studies to characterize the conditions under which each method performs better and to illustrate how 53 

EA-PheWAS integrates complementary signals to improve phenotype prioritization. Collectively, these 54 

results demonstrate that EA-PheWAS provides a robust and effective framework for enhancing 55 

phenome-wide association analyses in large-scale population datasets. 56

# Results 57 

## Overview of EA-PheWAS 58 

As shown in Fig 1, EA-PheWAS integrates embedding-based phenotype similarity with regression-based 59 

PheWAS to enhance the discovery of gene–phenotype associations from EHR data and whole-exome 60 

sequencing data. The framework consists of three main components, which are described in detail in the 61 

Methods section. 62 

First, phenotype embeddings are learned from longitudinal EHR data by training embedding models 63 

on sequences of ICD-10 codes. These embeddings capture latent relationships among phenotypes based 64 

on their co-occurrence patterns across individuals. The ICD-10 embeddings are mapped to phecodes, and 65 

individual-level phenotype embeddings are obtained by aggregating the embeddings of phecodes [1] 66 

observed in each individual’s medical records. 67 

Second, we construct gene-level phenotype profiles by aggregating phenotype embeddings from 68 

individuals carrying rare LoF variants in each gene. This representation summarizes the phenotypic 69 

patterns observed among carriers and enables the quantification of gene–phenotype similarity. Based on 70 

this idea, we developed EmbedPheScan, an embedding-based phenome-wide phenotype prioritization 71 

framework that evaluates gene–phenotype relationships in the continuous phenotype embedding space. 72 

For each gene–phecode pair, a similarity is computed between the gene-level embedding and the 73 

phenotype embedding. An empirical null distribution is generated for each gene by repeatedly sampling 74 

random pseudo-carrier groups from the cohort, with each group matched to the observed number of rare 75 

LoF carriers for that gene. For each null replicate, we calculate the gene-level embedding from the 76 

sampled individuals and then calculate similarity scores between this null gene embedding and all 77 

phecode embeddings. Repeating this procedure 10,000 times produces an empirical null distribution for 78 

each gene–phecode pair, which is used to convert the observed similarity score into a standardized 79 

statistic and corresponding embedding-based p-value. 80 

Third, EA-PheWAS integrates the complementary signals from embedding-based phenotype similarity 81 

and regression-based association testing. Specifically, p-values from the embedding-based phenome scan 82 

and the conventional PheWAS analysis are combined using the Aggregated Cauchy Association Test 83 

(ACAT). This aggregation step allows evidence from both approaches to contribute to the final association 84 

statistic, increasing sensitivity to phenotypes that may be weakly detectable by either method alone. 85 

Compared with conventional regression-based PheWAS, EA-PheWAS leverages phenotype similarity 86 

captured by embeddings to borrow information across related clinical phenotypes. By combining 87 

embedding-derived evidence with conventional association testing, our proposed framework may improve 88 

the prioritization of gene–phenotype associations while retaining the statistical interpretability of 89 

p-value–based inference. 90 

## EA-PheWAS can appropriately control false positive rates 91 

To evaluate false-positive control, we performed simulations to compare EA-PheWAS, EmbedPheScan, 92 

and conventional regression-based PheWAS. We considered carrier counts of 50, 120, and 300 individuals 93 

in our simulations. 94 

As shown in the QQ plots (Fig. 2), the three methods exhibited distinct behaviors when carrier counts 95 

are low. With 50 LoF carriers, conventional PheWAS produced a long tail of p-values equal to 1 because 96 

many phecodes had zero overlap between carriers and phenotype cases. With regression-based testing, 97

## EA-PheWAS can identify more trait-associated genes in the UKBB 113 

To evaluate the genome-wide performance of EA-PheWAS, we applied it to all genes in the UK Biobank 114 

dataset and compared the results with conventional PheWAS and EmbedPheScan. Among the 17,233 115 

genes tested, 4,545 genes have curated gene–phenotype relationships in the Human Phenotype Ontology 116 

(HPO) database and were therefore used as the reference set for evaluation (Fig. 3). These genes provide 117 

established phenotype annotations that enable quantitative comparison of phenotype prioritization 118 

performance across methods. 119 

First, we evaluated hit precision across genes by comparing the average hit precision of the three 120 

methods at the Top-5, Top-10, and Top-15 ranked phenotypes (Fig. 3A and Table S1). Hit precision 121 

measures the proportion of top-k prioritized phenotypes that are also present in the HPO reference 122 

database. Across all three ranking thresholds, EA-PheWAS achieved the highest average hit precision, 123 

whereas EmbedPheScan consistently performed between EA-PheWAS and conventional PheWAS. Thus, 124 

both embedding-based approaches outperformed conventional PheWAS, with the integrated framework 125 

showing the greatest overall precision. These results indicate that EA-PheWAS can effectively combine 126 

complementary information from EmbedPheScan and conventional PheWAS to improve recovery of 127 

clinically relevant phenotypes among the top-ranked results. 128 

Next, we evaluated recall, defined as the proportion of ground truth phenotypes recovered among the 129 

significant findings of each method. As shown in Fig. 3C and and Table S2, both EmbedPheScan and 130 

EA-PheWAS achieved higher recall than conventional PheWAS across multiple FDR thresholds, with 131 

EmbedPheScan showing the largest recall improvement. This is likely due to the fact that 132 

embedding-based similarity methods do not rely solely on direct carrier–phenotype overlap and can 133 

therefore identify related phenotypes even when the number of case carriers is small. By integrating 134 

embedding-based evidence with regression-based association testing, EA-PheWAS expands the set of 135 

detected phenotypes relative to conventional PheWAS while maintaining comparable overall behavior. To 136 

further examine this pattern, we quantified how often recall differed between methods across genes. As 137 

shown in Fig. 3C, across different FDR thresholds, EmbedPheScan and conventional PheWAS showed 138 

distinct recall patterns, indicating that the two methods capture complementary gene–phenotype 139

## EA-PheWAS performed well for haploinsufficient genes and tissue-specific 158 

## genes 159 

To further evaluate the ability of EA-PheWAS to identify gene–phenotype relationships, we next 160 

examined biologically informed gene subsets rather than all available genes. Specifically, we focused on 161 

two classes of genes with distinct functional properties: haploinsufficient (HI) genes and tissue-specific 162 

genes. Both groups are expected to exhibit more clinically coherent phenotype patterns and to be 163 

enriched for disease-relevant genes, providing a useful setting for testing whether EA-PheWAS is 164 

especially effective when gene effects are more functionally constrained and phenotypically concentrated. 165 

We first investigated the HI gene group, which consists of genes for which loss of a single functional 166 

copy is sufficient to cause abnormal phenotypic consequences, and are therefore typically under strong 167 

functional constraint. We obtained a list of 417 HI genes from the ClinGen database [15]. Applying the 168 

same evaluation framework to this subset, we found that EA-PheWAS achieved the highest hit precision 169 

among the three methods, whereas EmbedPheScan remained intermediate between EA-PheWAS and 170 

conventional PheWAS (Fig.4A and Table S3). Notably, compared with the genome-wide evaluation across 171 

all genes, the separation between methods was larger in the HI subset, indicating that the advantage of 172 

EA-PheWAS becomes more pronounced in genes with more coherent and penetrant phenotypic effects. 173 

The recall analysis showed a similar pattern (Fig.4B): both EA-PheWAS and EmbedPheScan 174 

outperformed conventional PheWAS, while EA-PheWAS remained closer to conventional PheWAS overall 175 

but incorporated complementary findings from EmbedPheScan. 176

often associated with more localized biological functions and organ-specific disease processes. Because 179 

their effects are concentrated within specific tissues or systems, tissue-specific genes are expected to 180 

generate more structured phenotype patterns, making them particularly well suited for 181

## Gene case study: PKD1 and PKD2 203

(ADPKD), PKD1 and PKD2 [17 – 19]. ADPKD is characterized by progressive renal cyst formation and 205 

enlargement, ultimately leading to chronic kidney disease and end-stage renal failure. In addition to renal 206 

manifestations, ADPKD patients frequently exhibit hypertension, renal insufficiency, and complications 207 

involving the urinary tract and related organs [20, 21]. 208

related phenotypes even when direct carrier–case overlap is limited. The differences among the Top-15 221 

phenotypes across methods are summarized in Fig. 6B. EA-PheWAS not only uniquely identified 222 

phenotypes that are highly ranked by one method but overlooked by the other, but also identified 223

## Gene case study: NF1 265 

Next, we examined NF1 , which has a relatively large number of rare loss-of-function (LoF) carriers in our 266 

cohort (311 carriers). NF1 encodes neurofibromin, a tumor suppressor that negatively regulates RAS 267 

signaling, and pathogenic variants in NF1 are associated with neurofibromatosis type 1, a multisystem 268 

disorder characterized by cutaneous, neurological, and neoplastic manifestations [23–25]. Because of its 269 

relatively large carrier count and broad clinical spectrum, NF1 provides an informative setting for 270 

evaluating the performance of EA-PheWAS in a gene with substantial overlap-based signal as well as 271 

complex phenotype structure. 272 

As shown in Fig. 9B and Table S8, EA-PheWAS achieved the highest hit precision across all Top-K 273 

settings, while conventional PheWAS performed relatively well at Top-5 and Top-10 and EmbedPheScan 274 

showed stronger performance at Top-10 and Top-15. This pattern suggests that, with sufficient carrier 275 

counts, conventional PheWAS can effectively prioritize phenotypes supported by direct carrier–phenotype 276 

overlap, whereas EmbedPheScan captures additional phenotypes through similarity in the embedding 277 

space. By integrating these two complementary signals, EA-PheWAS achieved the most accurate overall 278 

ranking. The detailed comparison of top-ranked phenotypes (Fig. 10A) further illustrates this integrative 279 

behavior. The highest-ranked phenotypes under EA-PheWAS were largely driven by strong signals from 280 

conventional PheWAS, whereas several additional top phenotypes appeared to be promoted primarily 281 

through EmbedPheScan. For example, “benign neoplasm of brain, cranial nerves, meninges” was not 282 

strongly prioritized by conventional PheWAS because of limited direct overlap with NF1 carriers, but was 283 

highly ranked by EmbedPheScan because of its proximity to other neuro-related phenotypes in the 284 

embedding space. More broadly, multiple phenotypes exhibited substantial rank shifts relative to one or 285 

both baseline methods, indicating that EA-PheWAS effectively integrates overlap-based association 286 

evidence with phenotype-similarity information. Consistent with this pattern, Fig. 10B shows that 287 

EA-PheWAS uniquely identified five phenotypes, three of which are present in the HPO reference set. 288 

These include phenotypes such as “cancer of connective tissue,” “facial nerve disorders,” and 289 

“hydrocephalus,” all of which are biologically plausible in the context of NF1 -related neurological and 290 

tissue manifestations [26–28]. In contrast, although conventional PheWAS and EmbedPheScan also 291 

identified method-specific phenotypes, the proportion of HPO-supported phenotypes among these unique 292 

findings was lower than for EA-PheWAS. The embedding-space visualization further illustrates the 293 

complementary nature of the three methods (Fig. 9A). Compared with the PKD1 and PKD2 case studies, 294 

the prioritized phenotypes for NF1 were more dispersed, consistent with the broader involvement of both 295 

neurological and tissue-related systems. Phenotypes identified by conventional PheWAS were widely 296 

separated in the embedding space, whereas those prioritized by EmbedPheScan were concentrated in two 297 

main clusters. EA-PheWAS captured features of both patterns, generating a phenotype profile that 298 

reflects both embedding-based similarity and overlap-based association. Together, these results show that 299 

for genes with relatively large carrier counts and complex multisystem phenotypes, EA-PheWAS can 300 

effectively combine the strengths of conventional PheWAS and EmbedPheScan to improve phenotype 301 

prioritization 302

abnormalities [29, 30]. 310 

As shown in Fig. 11B and Table S9, EA-PheWAS achieved the highest hit precision across all Top-k 311 

settings. In contrast to genes with larger carrier counts, conventional PheWAS performed relatively 312 

poorly for FBN1 , whereas EmbedPheScan showed stronger phenotype prioritization. This pattern is 313 

consistent with the extremely small number of carriers, under which regression-based association testing 314 

has limited power because of the lack of direct carrier–phenotype overlap. As a result, EA-PheWAS relied 315 

more heavily on the signal contributed by EmbedPheScan. The detailed ranking comparison (Fig. 12A) 316 

supports this interpretation. Overall, the phenotype ranking produced by EA-PheWAS more closely 317 

resembled that of EmbedPheScan than that of conventional PheWAS, with the exception of several very 318 

strong signals such as “Chromosomal anomalies and genetic disorders” and “Diseases of tricuspid valve,” 319 

which were also strongly supported by conventional PheWAS. Several biologically relevant phenotypes 320 

with limited overlap in carriers were not highly prioritized by conventional PheWAS but were promoted 321 

by EA-PheWAS through the embedding-based signal. For example, “Mitral valve disease” had limited 322 

direct overlap with FBN1 LoF carriers and was therefore difficult to detect by conventional PheWAS, but 323 

it shares high similarity with other cardiovascular and valvular phenotypes in the embedding space and 324 

has strong biological support in the context of FBN1 -related disease [31]. These results indicate that, for 325 

FBN1 , EA-PheWAS benefits primarily from the phenotype-similarity information captured by 326 

EmbedPheScan. Importantly, EA-PheWAS did not simply reproduce the results of either baseline method. 327 

As shown in Fig. 12B, the three phenotypes uniquely identified by EA-PheWAS were all present in the 328 

HPO reference set, whereas the proportion of HPO-supported phenotypes among method-specific findings 329 

was lower for both conventional PheWAS and EmbedPheScan. Notably, compared with conventional 330 

PheWAS, EmbedPheScan recovered a higher proportion of true phenotypes among its unique findings, 331 

further supporting the value of embedding-based prioritization when carrier numbers are extremely 332 

limited. The embedding-space visualization further illustrates these differences (Fig. 11A). Phenotypes 333 

identified by EA-PheWAS and EmbedPheScan formed relatively compact and coherent clusters, whereas 334 

those identified by conventional PheWAS were more dispersed in the embedding space. Together, these 335 

results show that when carrier counts are extremely low, EA-PheWAS continues to integrate both sources 336 

of evidence but derives greater benefit from the embedding-based component, enabling recovery of 337 

clinically relevant phenotypes that are difficult to detect through overlap-based association testing alone. 338 

# Discussion 339 

Investigating phenotypes associated with a given gene has long been a central goal of human genetics, and 340 

PheWAS has become a key framework for systematically characterizing gene–phenotype relationships. As 341 

whole-exome and whole-genome sequencing data become increasingly available, an important challenge is 342 

how to make effective use of rare variant information for phenome-wide analyses. Unlike conventional 343 

SNP-based PheWAS, which is typically well powered because common variants are observed in a large 344 

number of individuals, rare-variant PheWAS generally requires gene- or region-level aggregation to 345 

combine multiple variants into a burden signal. However, the underlying statistical framework remains 346 

largely unchanged: phenotypes are still represented as binary traits and tested independently, with little 347 

consideration of the relationships among phenotypes. At the same time, the growing availability of EHR 348

EA-PheWAS data provides rich individual-level phenotype information that captures longitudinal and correlated 349

patterns, we developed EA-PheWAS, an embedding-augmented PheWAS framework that integrates 351 

phenotype similarity information with conventional association testing to improve gene–phenotype 352 

discovery. 353 

In EA-PheWAS, we combine two complementary sources of evidence using ACAT. The first is the 354 

conventional regression-based PheWAS signal, which primarily captures associations through direct 355 

overlap between phenotype cases and rare LoF carriers of a given gene and performs well when carrier 356 

counts and phenotype prevalence are sufficiently large. The second is the embedding-similarity signal from 357 

EmbedPheScan, which prioritizes phenotypes according to similarity in the learned phenotype embedding 358 

space and therefore does not rely solely on direct carrier–phenotype overlap. Instead, it leverages 359 

semantic and clinical relatedness among phenotypes, allowing recovery of phenotypes that may be weakly 360 

represented in sparse carrier groups. By integrating these two signals, EA-PheWAS can improve 361 

phenotype prioritization for both relatively common and rare phenotypes associated with a given gene. 362 

This complementary behavior was evident in both the genome-wide evaluation and the gene-level case 363 

studies. EA-PheWAS consistently improved hit precision and recall relative to both conventional 364 

PheWAS and EmbedPheScan across the full gene set as well as across distinct functional gene subsets. 365 

For PKD1 and PKD2 , which represent strong-effect genes for a specific disease, EA-PheWAS recovered 366 

phenotypes related to glomerulonephritis, hypertension, and disease progression by combining direct 367 

association evidence with clinically related phenotypes in the embedding space. For NF1 , which has a 368 

relatively large number of rare LoF carriers, EA-PheWAS benefited substantially from the strong 369 

overlap-based signal detected by conventional PheWAS while incorporating additional biologically 370 

plausible phenotypes from EmbedPheScan. In contrast, for FBN1 , where the number of carriers was 371 

extremely limited, EA-PheWAS relied more heavily on embedding-based similarity and was able to 372 

recover heart relevant phenotypes that were weakly supported by regression alone. Across these settings, 373 

EA-PheWAS not only integrated phenotypes prioritized by either method individually, but also recovered 374 

phenotypes that were not strongly highlighted by either approach alone, including phenotypes supported 375 

by HPO-based ground truth. 376 

Although EA-PheWAS shows strong performance in rare-variant PheWAS using WES data, several 377 

areas remain for further development. First, our analyses were performed in individuals of European 378 

ancestry to minimize potential confounding from population structure and to provide a well-controlled 379 

initial evaluation. Extending the framework to multi-ancestry settings will be an important next step for 380 

improving generalizability. Second, we used Word2Vec-based phenotype embeddings to represent 381 

individual phenotype profiles. This approach performed well in our analyses and provided an effective 382 

starting point, although future work may explore alternative embedding architectures that capture 383 

additional aspects of longitudinal clinical structure. Third, the current study focuses on the UK Biobank, 384 

which offers a large and well-characterized resource for method development and evaluation. Applying 385 

EA-PheWAS to additional biobank-scale datasets and external cohorts will further clarify its robustness 386 

and portability across populations and healthcare systems. 387 

In summary, EA-PheWAS provides a complementary perspective for phenome-wide association 388 

analysis by integrating regression-based association testing with embedding-based phenotype similarity. 389 

Although the method is not designed to uniformly outperform all alternatives in every setting, its primary 390 

strength lies in adaptively combining complementary signals to improve phenotype prioritization across 391 

heterogeneous rare-variant scenarios. 392

# Materials and methods 393 

## UK Biobank 500K dataset and genotype data quality control 394 

We conducted our analysis using the UK Biobank 500K dataset [32, 33]. To reduce potential confounding 395 

due to population stratification, we restricted the study population to individuals of European ancestry. 396 

We applied multiple filtering criteria to ensure data quality and completeness, excluding individuals 397 

without hospital inpatient records, covariates information or whole-exome sequencing (WES) data. To 398 

mitigate bias arising from sample relatedness, we further removed related individuals using kinship 399 

coefficients provided by UK Biobank. Specifically, participants with first- or second-degree relationships 400 

were identified, and one individual was randomly retained from each related family. After applying these 401 

filters, the final study cohort consisted of 328,737 unrelated individuals of European ancestry. This 402 

curated dataset provides a homogeneous population with comprehensive phenotypic and genetic 403 

information, improving the robustness and interpretability of downstream analyses. 404 

To ensure the quality of rare variant analyses, we performed stringent quality control (QC) on the 405 

WES data using PLINK [34]. Variants with a minor allele frequency (MAF) greater than 0.01 were 406 

removed, retaining only rare variants for subsequent analyses. We further excluded variants that deviated 407 

significantly from Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium (HWE; p < 1 → 10 →6), which may indicate genotyping 408 

errors or residual population structure. Samples with missing sex information were also excluded. 409 

Following QC, we identified rare predicted loss-of-function (pLoF) variants, including stop-gain, stop-loss, 410 

startloss, frameshift insertions, frameshift deletions, frameshift substitution and essential splicing exonic 411 

variants, enabling a focused analysis of variants with a high likelihood of functional impact. 412

Fig 1. Workflow of EA-PheWAS. Longitudinal EHR data from the UK Biobank are used to train phenotype embeddings based on ICD-10 sequences. Individual-level phenotype embeddings are constructed and aggregated across rare loss-of-function (LoF) variant carriers to derive gene-level phenotype representations. In parallel, covariate-adjusted regression-based PheWAS is performed to obtain phenotype association p-values. Embedding-based similarity scores between gene-level embeddings and phenotype embeddings are converted into empirical p-values through null sampling. Finally, p-values from the embedding-based phenome scan and conventional PheWAS are combined using the ACAT method to produce aggregated association statistics, enabling improved prioritization of gene–phenotype associations. 19
