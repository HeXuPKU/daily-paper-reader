ABSTRACT 12 Cultivated Northern Wild Rice (Zizania palustris L.) is an obligately outcrossing, self-13 incompatible cereal grown in aquatic paddies in the United States. Genetic improvement has relied 14 primarily on phenotypic recurrent selection, and genomic approaches remain largely unexplored in this 15 emerging crop. We applied a single-plant genome-wide association study (sp-GWAS) framework to 16 dissect vegetative architecture traits in five open-pollinated cultivated populations evaluated across three 17 years (n = 2,173 plants). Plant height (PH), basal stem width (BSW), primary stem width (PSW), flag leaf 18 length (FLL), and flag leaf width (FLW) were analyzed using a mixed linear model accounting for 19 population structure and kinship. Broad-sense heritability ranged from 0.03 to 0.34, and year effects 20 explained up to 54% of phenotypic variance, indicating strong environmental influence. After filtering 21 73,363 SNPs, genome-wide linkage disequilibrium decayed rapidly (r² = 0.1 at ~2.3 kb). A total of 124 22 significant SNPs (FDR < 0.01) were consolidated into 98 loci, of which 46 were associated with multiple 23 traits and 11 were shared across four traits. Candidate genes near multi-trait loci included conserved 24 regulatory classes implicated in grass architecture, including HLH/bHLH transcription factors. Diplotype 25 analyses at candidate loci revealed both simple biallelic and complex multi-allelic haplotype structures, 26

PLAIN LANGUAGE SUMMARY 33 Cultivated Northern Wild Rice is an important specialty crop grown in flooded paddies in the 34 United States. Unlike many major crops, it is naturally outcrossing and highly variable, which makes 35 traditional breeding challenging and slow. Most improvement efforts have relied on selecting plants based 36 only on how they look in the field, and genomic tools have rarely been used. In this study, we used DNA 37 markers to better understand the genetics behind plant structure traits such as plant height, stem thickness, 38 and leaf width. We evaluated more than 2,000 plants from five cultivated populations over three growing 39 seasons. Because weather and growing conditions strongly influence these traits, we used statistical 40 models to separate environmental effects from genetic effects. We identified 98 regions of the genome 41 associated with variation in plant structure. Many of these regions influenced more than one trait, 42 showing that plant height, stem strength, and leaf size are genetically connected. Several regions 43 contained genes similar to those known to control plant growth and development in other grasses. We 44 also found that, in some cases, combinations of nearby DNA variants (haplotypes) explained trait 45 differences better than single genetic markers. Overall, this work shows that modern genomic tools can 46 successfully identify useful genetic variation in cultivated Northern Wild Rice, even though it is highly 47 outcrossing and genetically diverse. These results provide a foundation for using genomic selection to 48 improve plant structure, lodging resistance, and overall performance in breeding programs. 49 50 51

INTRODUCTION 91 Cultivated Northern Wild Rice (cNWR; Zizania palustris L.) is a high-value, small commodity 92 crop grown in irrigated paddies, primarily in Minnesota and California in the United States. The ongoing 93 domestication of cNWR and development of varieties with economically desirable phenotypes have 94 focused on yield, plant vigor, resistance to seed shattering and diseases, uniformity characteristics and 95 seed size (Grombacher et al., 1997; McGilp et al., 2023; Porter, 2019). Breeders of cNWR utilize 96 phenotypic-based recurrent selection for population improvement, which is time-consuming, limits the 97 selection opportunities compared to other advanced breeding schemes, and does not take advantage of the 98 numerous genomic technologies available to researchers. Therefore, it is essential to move towards more 99 efficient breeding methods which can expedite cultivar development. 100 An understanding of the genetic architecture which underlies agronomic traits, through biparental 101 mapping and Genome-wide Association Studies (GWAS), has facilitated crop improvement in many 102 species (Ain et al., 2015; Alqudah et al., 2014; Atwell et al., 2010; X. et al., 2012). However, only two biparental mapping studies have been undertaken in NWR (Kahler et al., 104 2014; Kennard et al., 2002). This limited progress reflects the challenges associated with developing and 105 maintaining biparental mapping populations. These challenges stem from biological constraints, including 106 unorthodox seed storage requirements that limit viability to under two years in storage (Grombacher et 107 al., 1997; Oelke & Porter, 2016) and self-incompatibility mechanisms that reduce seed set and plant 108 viability over generations of selfing. 109 An alternative to biparental mapping is GWAS, which leverages historical recombination and 110 standing genetic variation to identify loci associated with complex quantitative traits (Huang et al., 2009; 111 Huang & Han, 2014). The high levels of genetic diversity present within cultivated and wild populations 112 of NWR make GWAS a particularly attractive approach for trait discovery (McGilp et al., 2025). Recent 113 advancements in Z. palustris genomic resources have made GWAS studies in the species more feasible. 114 These include an annotated chromosome-scale reference genome (Haas et al., 2021) and genome-wide 115 SNP markers generated through genotyping-by-sequencing (GBS) to characterize population structure 116 and genetic diversity across wild and cultivated NWR in the Great Lakes region (McGilp et al., 2025; 117 Shao et al., 2019). Together, these resources establish the necessary foundation for GWAS in NWR and 118 create new opportunities to link phenotypic variation with underlying genetic mechanisms. 119 Traditional GWAS approaches in self-pollinating species often utilize clonally replicated or 120 highly inbred lines to reduce within-genotype variability and improve statistical power (Atwell et al., 121 2010; Brachi et al., 2010; Huang et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2012). However, GWAS has also been 122 successfully applied in outcrossing species and natural populations where genetically identical replicates 123 are not feasible (Hayward et al., 2016; Karczewski et al., 2024; Klein et al., 2005; Marina et al., 2021; 124 McCarthy et al., 2008; Omeka et al., 2022; Pairo-Castineira et al., 2023). To address this challenge 125 explicitly in crops, a single-plant GWAS (sp-GWAS) framework was recently developed and validated in 126 maize ( Zea mays ) (Gyawali et al., 2019) and luffa ( Luffa spp.) (Y.-D. Li et al., 2024). In sp-GWAS, 127 phenotypes are collected from individual plants without replication, and statistical power is recovered 128 through large sample sizes and mixed models that account for kinship, population structure, and 129

For NWR, sp-GWAS presents a practical and scalable strategy for genetic 130 mapping that aligns with the biological realities of an obligately outcrossing species. 131 In this study, we apply a sp-GWAS framework to investigate the genetic architecture of 132 vegetative morphology in cNWR. We focus on plant height, basal stem width, primary stem width, flag 133 leaf length, and flag leaf width, which are traits that can be readily collected on a large population of 134 individuals, are widely studied in other cereal crops, and are directly relevant to cNWR breeding goals. 135 Collectively, these traits influence plant vigor, canopy structure, lodging resistance, and harvestability in 136 aquatic production systems, where excessive height or insufficient stem strength can reduce yield 137 stability. By investigating the genetic associations underlying vegetative architecture, this work aims to 138 evaluate the utility of sp-GWAS in cNWR and to provide a foundation for genomics-enabled 139 improvement of this emerging crop. 140 141 142

Plant Materials. 144 Five open-pollinated populations of cNWR were utilized in this study including: ‘K2’ (released in 1972), 145 the third released cNWR variety, ‘Barron’ (released in 2014), an early-maturing variety with improved 146 resistance to fungal brown spot (Bipolaris oryzeae); ‘FY-C20’, an elite breeding population selected for 147 long seed length; and two cultivars, ‘Itasca-C12’ and ‘Itasca-C20’ (released in 2007 and 2019, 148 respectively), developed through recurrent selection of the ‘Itasca’ cultivar (released in 2000). Candidate Gene and Diplotype Analysis. 232 Candidate genomic regions underlying significant GWAS loci were further examined using a diplotype-233 based approach. For each locus of interest, SNPs located within ±3 kb of the boundaries of the significant 234 SNPs were extracted from the full variant dataset. Genotype data were phased using Beagle to infer 235 haplotype structure within each region. Phased SNP data were then subset into locus-specific VCF files 236 for downstream analysis. Diplotypes were defined based on the allelic state across all SNPs within each 237 locus-specific window and were retained for analysis if represented by more than 25 individuals. 238 Diplotype classes were manually curated and visualized to assess local haplotype structure. Phenotypic 239 distributions of each diplotype were summarized using boxplots. Statistical differences among diplotypes 240 were evaluated separately for each trait using ANOVA. When significant effects were detected, mean 241 separation was performed using Tukey’s honestly significant difference (HSD) test as implemented in the 242 agrocolae package v1.3-7 (de Mendiburu, 2023). All data processing, visualization, and statistical 243 analyses were conducted in R. Figures were generated using ggplot2 and ggpubr v0.60 (Kassambara, 244 2023), with additional custom scripts developed to visualize diplotype structure and phenotypic 245 distributions. 246 To further characterize genes within each significant locus, predicted protein sequences from 247 annotated gene models were extracted and subjected to BLASTP searches against the NCBI non-248 redundant (nr) protein database. Protein similarity searches were conducted using BLAST+ with default 249 parameters, and top hits were retained based on E-value (<1e−5), sequence identity and alignment 250 coverage. Conserved domains were identified using NCBI’s Conserved Domain Database (CDD) to 251 support functional inference. Putative gene functions were integrated with GWAS and diplotype results to 252 prioritize biologically plausible candidate genes underlying trait variation. 253

Weather and Climate. 258 From 2019 (Year 1) to 2021 (Year 3), temperature and precipitation patterns from May through 259 September showed consistent northern temperate seasonal trends with some interannual variability. May 260 was consistently the coolest month, with average temperatures between 9.44°C in Year 1 and 12.48°C in 261 Year 3, while July was the warmest, with average temperatures ranging from 20.38°C in Year 1 to 262 21.49°C in Year 3 (Supplemental Table S3). Average precipitation levels were generally low across all 263 years (< 0.3 inches), with May being the driest month (Supplemental Table S3). August 2020 (Year 2) 264 had the highest recorded precipitation (0.28 inches), whereas the summer of Year 3 was notably dry, with 265 minimal rainfall in June (0.04 inches) and August (0.06 inches). Overall, Year 3 was the warmest and 266 driest year, while Year 1 showed slightly cooler temperatures and higher early-season precipitation. 267

Phenotypic Variation Among Cultivated Populations. 269 Five open-pollinated populations of cNWR were evaluated for plant height (PH), basal stem width 270 (BSW), primary stem width (PSW), flag leaf length (FLL), and flag leaf width (FLW) across three years, 271 totaling 2,173 phenotyped individuals (~145 plants per population per year) (Supplemental Table S2). 272 Year and population were modeled as random effects. Year explained the largest proportion of variance 273 for all traits (6.8 - 54.1%), while population effects were small (0.36 - 2.95%), indicating strong 274 environmental influence but detectable genetic differentiation (Table 1). When averaged across years, PH 275 ranged from 172.1 cm in Itasca-C12 to 189.6 cm in K2; FLL ranged from 35.4 cm in Barron to 38.5 cm in 276 Itasca-C20; FLW was lowest in Itasca-C12 (2.3 cm) and highest in both Itasca-C20 and K2 (2.5 cm); 277 PSW was lowest in FY-C20 and Itasca-C12 (6.3 mm) and highest in K2 (6.5 mm); and BSW ranged from 278 12.8 mm in FY-C20 to 13.5 mm in Itasca-C20 (Table 1). Substantial phenotypic variation was observed 279 between years, with the lowest trait values consistently occurring in Year 2 and the highest in Year 3. 280 Across genotypes, yearly trait means ranged from 152.5 to 204.0 cm for PH, 10.8 mm - 14.6 mm for 281 BSW, 5.6 - 7.3 mm for PSW, 34.3 - 39.2 cm for FLL, and 2.1 - 2.7 cm for FLW (Supplemental Table 282 S4). 283

Overview of Association Signals. Single-plant GWAS (sp-GWAS) was conducted using a mixed linear 364 model (MLM) with population structure and kinship included to control for relatedness and stratification. 365 Manhattan and Q-Q plots for each trait are presented in Figure 3. No significant associations were 366 detected for flag leaf length (FLL; Figure 3e), and subsequent analyses therefore focused on plant height 367 (PH), primary stem width (PSW), basal stem width (BSW), and flag leaf width (FLW). Across the four 368 traits, 124 significant SNPs (FDR < 0.01) were identified and consolidated into 98 independent loci using 369 chromosome-specific LD decay thresholds (Table 3). Each locus consisted of one or more significant 370 SNPs within the threshold and was treated as a distinct genomic interval associated with phenotypic 371 variation. Of the 98 loci, 52 were trait-specific and 46 were associated with two or more traits. Fifty-six 372 loci contained at least one significant SNP within an annotated gene model, providing biologically 373 plausible candidates. However, given marker density and LD structure, these signals could be tagging 374 broader genomic intervals rather than causal variants. For loci without intragenic SNPs, nearby annotated 375 genes within the LD-defined intervals were considered as putative candidates, including potential 376 regulatory targets. Loci were non-randomly distributed across the genome, with clustering observed on 377 chromosomes 6, 7, 8, 10, 12, and 13 (Figure 4). Minor allele frequencies (MAF) ranged from 0.065 to 378 0.499 (mean = 0.284), indicating that most significant alleles segregate at moderate frequencies and may 379 be suitable for deployment in breeding populations (Supplemental Table S6). 380

DISCUSSION 585 

sp-GWAS in a Non-Replicable, Outcrossing Crop. 586 This study represents the first genome-wide association analysis conducted in cultivated Northern Wild 587 Rice, an obligately outcrossing crop with self-incompatibility and atypical seed storage behavior that 588 precludes the replicated, inbred resources used in conventional GWAS. By leveraging large per-year 589 sample sizes across three field seasons, we identified numerous loci associated with vegetative 590 architecture traits despite low to moderate broad-sense heritabilities. The rapid LD decay observed in this 591 panel enabled relatively fine-scale localization of association signals, with multiple significant SNPs 592 frequently collapsing into compact, locus-level intervals under chromosome-specific LD thresholds 593 (Browning et al., 2021; Myles et al., 2009). While causal resolution remains limited, these results indicate 594 that sp-GWAS can recover biologically meaningful association signals in highly heterozygous, non-595 replicable crop systems when sample sizes are large and mixed models appropriately account for 596 relatedness and structure. In this respect, our findings extend prior demonstrations in maize and luffa 597 (Luffa spp.) to an aquatic, self-incompatible cereal system (Gyawali et al., 2019; Li et al., 2024). 598 599 

Environmental Effects and Recoverable Genetic Signal. 600 Vegetative morphology in cNWR was strongly influenced by year-to-year environmental variation, with 601 year effects explaining a substantial proportion of phenotypic variance across traits (6.8 - 54.1%). Broad-602 sense heritability estimates were correspondingly low to moderate (H² = 0.03 - 0.34), consistent with 603 pronounced phenotypic plasticity in stem elongation, culm thickness, and leaf morphology. This pattern 604 aligns with broader cereal literature, where architecture traits integrate multiple developmental processes 605 and respond dynamically to temperature, resource availability, and water status (Sakamoto & Matsuoka, 606 2008; Weng et al., 2011). In aquatic paddy systems, additional hydrologic heterogeneity (e.g., water depth 607 gradients, current, and within-paddy microclimates) likely amplifies environmental variance relative to 608

Polygenic and Integrated Architecture of Vegetative Traits. 618 The strong and uniformly positive phenotypic correlations among vegetative traits provided a useful 619 framework for interpreting the genetic architecture uncovered here. Across traits, association signals were 620 distributed across many loci of small to moderate effect, consistent with a quantitative genetic 621 architecture. Plant height exhibited the largest number of associated loci and the broadest range of 622 estimated SNP effects, whereas stem width and flag leaf width showed fewer associations. This pattern is 623 consistent with GWAS findings in other cereals, where plant height is among the most genetically 624 tractable architecture traits due to its cumulative integration of cell elongation, internode development, 625 and hormonal regulation (X. Huang et al., 2010; Sakamoto & Matsuoka, 2008; Wallace et al., 2014). In 626 contrast, stem and leaf width traits often display greater environmental sensitivity and lower repeatability, 627 reducing power to detect associations even when genetic control is present (Holland et al., 2002; Poorter 628 et al., 2009). Variation in association strength among traits may therefore reflect differences in trait 629 plasticity and measurement precision rather than differences in biological importance. 630 A central outcome of this study is the prevalence of overlapping association signals, with 46 of 98 631 loci associated with two or more traits and eleven loci associated with all four traits (PH, BSW, PSW, 632 FLW). This genetic overlap closely mirrors the underlying phenotypic correlation structure observed in 633 this panel (Supplemental Table 5). Diplotype-Based Interpretation of Locus Effects in an Obligate Outcrosser. 712 The present study demonstrates that plant architectural variation in cNWR is influenced by genomic 713 regions exhibiting distinct haplotype architectures consistent with expectations for an obligately 714 outcrossing species. Because individuals carry two potentially different haplotypes at each locus, 715 phenotypes reflect the combined effects of both alleles rather than simple Mendelian dominance 716 relationships. In this context, diplotype-based analyses provide a biologically appropriate framework for 717 interpreting GWAS signals in highly heterozygous populations and allow evaluation of how local 718 haplotype combinations contribute to phenotypic variation. 719 The value of this approach was evident in the architectures of loci 8.1 and 4.4. Locus 8.1 720 exhibited high haplotype diversity and complex diplotype structure, with sixteen haplotypes forming 721 twenty-one diplotype classes. Phenotypic stratification for plant height occurred across many haplotype 722 combinations, with several haplotypes (e.g., D and K) consistently associated with taller plants across 723 multiple genetic backgrounds, whereas others (e.g., A, C, and L) were repeatedly associated with shorter 724 phenotypes. These patterns are consistent with a multi-allelic locus in which functional variation is 725 represented by haplotype backgrounds rather than individual SNPs. Similarly, locus 4.4 displayed 14 726 haplotypes forming 17 diplotype classes showing consistent directional patterns across plant height, flag 727 leaf width, and stem widths. Haplotypes such as J and K were repeatedly associated with taller plants and 728 thicker stems, whereas haplotypes such as A and M were consistently associated with reduced plant 729 stature and narrower leaves. The coordinated effects across multiple architectural traits suggest that 730 variation within this genomic interval contributes to integrated regulation of canopy development. 731 Fine-scale mapping of locus 4.4 revealed a highly localized pattern of detected sequence 732 variation. All significant and nearby non-significant SNPs occurred within the annotated gene model 733 

ZPchr0004g39193 , while no SNPs were detected within the adjacent gene models in this interval, 734 including the neighboring bHLH transcription factor gene ( ZPchr0004g39205 ). The absence of SNPs 735 within the neighboring bHLH gene model likely reflects the distribution of detectable polymorphism in 736 the current dataset, not evidence that the region lacks underlying variation. Breeding Implications and Future Perspectives. 747 The genetic architecture revealed here has several direct implications for improvement of cNWR. First, 748 vegetative architecture traits in this panel exhibited low to moderate broad-sense heritability and 749 substantial year effects, with environmental variance explaining up to 54% of phenotypic variation. Under 750 such conditions, response to phenotypic selection is expected to be slow and inconsistent, particularly 751 when selection decisions are made on single-plant observations in heterogeneous paddy environments. 752 Similar limitations have motivated the adoption of genomic prediction in other crops for traits 753 characterized by moderate heritability and strong genotype-by-environment interaction (Crossa et al., 754 2017; Heffner et al., 2009). 755 Second, the prevalence of multi-trait loci indicates that vegetative architecture in cNWR is 756 genetically integrated. Direct selection on plant height alone is therefore likely to induce predictable 757 correlated responses in stem robustness and canopy morphology, given the moderate correlations between 758 PH and stem width traits (r ≈ 0.55-0.59) and the strong correlation between PSW and FLW (r = 0.75). 759 However, the direction of desirable change may not always align across traits; for example, breeding for 760 reduced plant height while maintaining or increasing stem thickness. These trade-offs highlight the 761 importance of accounting for trait correlations in selection strategies. Multi-trait genomic selection 762

CONCLUSIONS 793 Single-plant GWAS identified statistically robust associations for vegetative architecture traits in 794 cultivated Northern Wild Rice despite the biological constraints of an obligately outcrossing, self-795 incompatible crop and the strong environmental influence observed across years. Across 2,173 plants 796 evaluated over three seasons, 98 loci were associated with plant height, stem width, and flag leaf width, 797 indicating that useful genetic signals can be recovered in cNWR when analytical approaches are matched 798 to species biology. 799 The results support a model in which vegetative architecture in cNWR is polygenic and 800 developmentally integrated. Nearly half of detected loci were associated with multiple traits, consistent 801 with the observed phenotypic correlations among plant height, stem width, and flag leaf width. Candidate 802 intervals frequently contained genes belonging to conserved regulatory classes implicated in grass 803 development, including TE1-like meristem regulators, HLH/bHLH transcription factors, SPL-class 804 developmental regulators, and chromatin-associated components. At the same time, diplotype analyses 805 showed that several GWAS signals are more appropriately interpreted as local haplotype effects than as 806 isolated SNP associations, which is consistent with rapid LD decay and the highly heterozygous nature of 807 this crop. 808 Together, these findings establish a foundation for genome-enabled improvement of cNWR and 809 support future evaluation of multi-trait genomic prediction approaches for plant architecture in aquatic 810 production systems. More broadly, this study highlights the usefulness of sp-GWAS for genetic analysis 811 in emerging crop species where replicated inbred mapping populations are not feasible. 812
