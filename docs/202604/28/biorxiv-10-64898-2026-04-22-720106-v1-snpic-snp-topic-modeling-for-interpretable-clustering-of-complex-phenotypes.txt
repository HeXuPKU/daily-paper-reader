Abstract 11 

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have cataloged thousands of disease-associated variants, yet a 12 

central challenge remains: decoding the shared, pleiotropic architecture that links complex phenotypes. 13 

Existing approaches, including dimensionality reduction methods and regression genetic models, either 14 

lack interpretability or rely on external linkage disequilibrium (LD) reference panels, limiting their ability 15 

to recover coherent biological mechanisms. 16 

Here we introduce the SNP topic model (SNPic), a generative probabilistic framework that reframes 17 

GWAS summary statistics as a structured corpus and models genetic architecture using principles from 18 

Natural Language Processing (NLP). By treating phenotypes as documents and genes or the whole cor-19 

pus of traits as words, SNPic applies topic models, e.g. Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), to infer latent 20 

“genetic topics”, representing interpretable, overlapping biological modules that jointly explain complex 21 

traits. This formulation enables simultaneous reconstruction of trait relationships and identification of 22 

their underlying molecular drivers. 23 

SNPic integrates two complementary schemes: Sumstat-as-word for capturing global phenotypic struc-24 

ture and Gene-as-word for resolving mechanistic detail, within a unified modeling framework. To ensure 25 

robustness, we introduce a stability-optimized inference pipeline based on bootstrap resampling, allow-26 

ing data-driven selection of topic number and filtering of stochastic signals. Across extensive simulations, 27 

SNPic consistently outperforms conventional dimensionality reduction methods in recovering latent 28 

structure under both linear and non-linear, highly overlapping genetic architectures. 29 

Applied to integrated FinnGen and UK Biobank datasets, SNPic identifies reproducible genetic top-30 

ics corresponding to distinct biological programs, including HLA-mediated immune processes and 31 

transporter-driven metabolic regulation, with strong tissue-specific support. The framework further 32 

generalizes across species, organizing complex traits in maize, Arabidopsis thaliana , and cattle into 33 

biologically coherent modules. 34 

Together, these results establish SNPic as a scalable and interpretable framework that shifts GWAS 35 

analysis from association cataloging toward the construction of an interpretable knowledge graph repre-36 

senting the latent semantic architecture of the genome. By unifying statistical genetics with NLP, SNPic 37 

reframes GWAS analysis as a probabilistic language modeling task, enabling the systematic decoding of 38 

complex trait architectures and delivering a systemic graph of cross-phenotype relationships. 39 

Keywords: Genome-Wide Association Studies; Probabilistic Topic Modeling; Interpretable Knowledge Graph; 40 

Systems Genetics; Precision Medicine 41

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified thousands of disease-associated variants [1–3], yet 43 

analyzing traits individually limits our understanding of the shared genetic architecture underlying these 44 

phenotypes [4–7]. While integrative methods such as polygenic risk scores [8–10], genetic correlations [11, 12], 45 

cross-trait meta-analysis [13], and Mendelian Randomization [14, 15] have begun to address this gap, they 46 

primarily quantify macroscopic statistical overlaps or aggregate risk. Consequently, they often fall short of 47 

revealing the systemic biological mechanisms, such as specific causal pathways, latent molecular networks, 48 

or distinct pleiotropic etiologies, that underlie these observed correlations, highlighting the critical need for 49 

integrative systems genetics approaches [16, 17]. 50 

While widely used in genetics [18–22], traditional dimensionality reduction techniques face fundamental 51 

limitations in capturing highly complex trait architectures. Linear models like PCA oversimplify non-linear 52 

relationships [23, 24]. Consequently, large-scale linear applications, such as truncated SVD applied to the UK 53 

Biobank [25], often yield feature loadings with limited biological interpretability and restricted generative 54 

flexibility. On the other hand, non-linear manifold learning approaches like UMAP operate as a “black 55 

box” lacking transparent feature mapping [26]. By frequently distorting global geometry [27] and erasing 56 

local density [28], UMAP risks generating algorithmic artifacts rather than true biological structures. The 57 

real-world impact of these distortions is significant, for instance, some UMAP visualizations were recently 58 

criticized for artificially segregating continuous human genetic variation into misleading, discrete clusters 59 

[29]. Imposing rigid, discrete clustering boundaries on such flawed embeddings ultimately fails to capture 60 

the inherently overlapping and mixed etiologies of complex diseases [30, 31]. 61 

Beyond dimensionality reduction, statistical geneticists frequently utilize regression models (e.g., struc-62 

tural equation modeling [32]) to explore shared genetic architectures. While highly powerful for estimating 63 

genetic correlations, these approaches are fundamentally designed for continuous mathematical transfor-64 

mations and require external Linkage Disequilibrium (LD) reference panels. Consequently, their reliance 65 

on exact LD matching can restrict their scalability in large-scale cross-cohort integrations, where perfectly 66 

matched reference panels are often unavailable [33, 34]. Besides, recent studies have attempted to find sys-67 

temic mechanisms from GWAS at the pathway level by linking variants to transcriptomic expressions based 68 

on pre-defined biological databases [35]. Because these database-driven pipelines rely on manually con-69 

structed biological “dictionaries”, they limit the discovery of novel, unannotated pleiotropic architectures 70 

that fall outside existing dictionaries. Furthermore, the statistical genetics and computational biology com-71 

munities increasingly recognize the profound value of constructing comprehensive, interpretable knowledge 72 

graphs to systematically decode complex genetic architectures [36, 37]. 73 

However, the genetic architecture of complex traits closely mirrors the compositional structure of human 74 

language. In natural language, reading words in documents like “stock” infers a “finance” topic, while “Lak-75 

ers” infers a “sport” topic. A single document containing both sets of words is naturally understood as a 76 

mixture of these underlying themes (e.g., the business of sports). Furthermore, higher word frequencies for 77 

certain groups of words increase the probability that a document will be categorized into the corresponding 78 

latent topic. By this exact same logic, specific sets of associated genes (analogous to words) regulate overlap-79 

ping biological pathways (analogous to topics), which collectively drive complex phenotypes (analogous to 80 

documents). Taking gout as an example, observing genes such as “SLC2A9” strongly infers a “urate trans-81 

port” topic [38], while genes such as “HLA-DQA1” infers an “autoimmune” topic [39, 40]. This word-topic 82 

representation effectively interprets both the distinct characteristics of gout and its genetic associations 83 

with autoimmune diseases. Such conceptual similarity reveals a powerful, yet largely unexplored analytical 84 

pattern. By conceptualizing the genome as a biological corpus rather than a matrix of effect sizes, we can 85 

directly leverage Natural Language Processing (NLP) [41, 42]. NLP is a branch of Artificial Intelligence 86 

that enables computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language [43]. NLP models, such as 87

A widely adopted topic model is Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) [45, 46], a Bayesian generative model 91 

that mathematically formalizes this exact biological-linguistic correspondence. While biomedical statisticians 92 

have adapted LDA to model complex compositions in phylogenetic trees, gene expression, and microbiome 93 

profiles [47–50], recent efforts have expanded into individual-level clinical models, including treeLFA [51], 94

tronic Health Records by effectively treating individual patients as documents, successfully uncovering 96 

complex clinical multimorbidities and patient-level phenotypes. 97 

Here, we introduce the SNP Topic Model (SNPic), the first framework that directly applies NLP topic 98 

modeling to GWAS summary statistics. The core innovation of SNPic lies in a novel dual-scheme architecture 99 

that reframes genetic data through two complementary “document-word” analogies. First, to capture broad 100 

phenotypic relationships, the Sumstat-as-word scheme treats a focal phenotype as a document and the corpus 101 

of all other analyzed phenotypes as its words . Second, to decode detailed biological mechanisms, the Gene-102 

as-word scheme maps phenotypes ( documents ) directly to specific genes ( words ). Consequently, the inferred 103 

latent topics operate at two distinct resolutions: resolving macro-level disease clusters and identifying the 104 

specific groups of genes driving the phenotypes with latent mechanisms. Supported by a rigorous statistical 105 

pipeline to ensure reproducibility, SNPic transforms GWAS interpretation from merely cataloging isolated 106 

associations to decoding the genetic architecture of complex phenotypes as a composition of interpretable 107 

biological modules. 108 

We demonstrate SNPic’s distinct advantages through comprehensive simulations and the analysis of 109 

diverse real-world datasets, accurately reconstructing complex, biologically meaningful pleiotropic architec-110 

tures across human diseases, Maize, Arabidopsis thaliana , and Cattle. By translating isolated genetic signals 111 

into a cohesive genetic language, SNPic enables the systematic construction of an interpretable knowledge 112 

graph of trait relationships, providing intuitive, systems-level biological interpretations that were previously 113 

neglected by conventional methodologies. 114 

# 2 Results 115 

## 2.1 Framework Overview 116 

SNPic is a probabilistic topic modeling approach to deconstruct the latent genetic architecture of complex 117 

traits. The workflow processes GWAS summary statistics through a pipeline with noise filtering, dual-scheme 118 

representation, and stability-optimized inference (Figure 1). 119 

To ensure robust signal detection, we first standardized input data by filtering for significant SNPs using 120 

a strict and standard genome-wide significance threshold of p < 5 × 10 −8 [54] (Figure 1 I). To avoid over-121 

representation of large LD blocks, significant SNPs were clumped (e.g., r2 < 0.1 within 250kb) prior to 122 

intersection analysis. These filtered signals were then transformed into mathematical “documents” using 123 

two complementary representation schemes: 124 

Summary-Statistics-as-word (Sumstat-as-word): This scheme treats each trait as a document and 125 

the entire corpus of other traits as words, constructing a Trait-Trait Matrix (Figure 1, II left). Phenotypic 126 

co-occurrence is quantified as the number of shared significant SNPs intersection after LD clumping. This 127 

approach captures macroscopic phenotypic relationships and is particularly generalizable for species lacking 128 

detailed genomic annotations, as it relies solely on statistical overlap of SNPs. 129 

Gene-as-word: This scheme maps significant SNPs to target genes (e.g., using the nearest-gene strategy 130 

directly from FinnGen summary statistics information) to construct a Trait-Gene Matrix (Figure 1, II right). 131

An unbiased stability analysis is embedded in SNPic to replace arbitrary hyper-parameter selection. 135 

As illustrated in Figure 1 III, the model employs a bootstrap-based procedure to plot the Stability Score 136 

against the Number of Topics ( K). This allows for the data-driven determination of the optimal K and the 137 

filtering of stochastic noise. Only associations with a Stability (Confidence) Score > 0.25 or top 50% stable 138 

traits are retained, ensuring that the resulting Trait Similarity Networks and Topic Distributions reflect 139 

persistent biological signals. 140 

Upon establishing a stable model, SNPic outputs a suite of analytical results to decipher the genetic 141 

architecture (Figure 1, IV). The framework systematically generates the overarching topic distribution 142 

across all analyzed traits (Figure 1 IV-A) and identifies the top defining words (e.g., driver genes) that 143 

characterize each latent topic (Figure 1 IV-B). Furthermore, these probabilistic assignments are utilized to 144 

construct a Trait Similarity Network (Figure 1 IV-C), visually mapping the complex pleiotropic connections 145 

between distinct phenotypes. To transform these statistical topics into interpretable biological insights, 146 

SNPic integrates well-established downstream analyses. By focusing on the top 50 words per topic, SNPic 147 

performs targeted pathway enrichment analysis (Figure 1 IV-D1) and tissue-specific expression analysis 148 

(Figure 1 IV-D2) to elucidate the mechanisms of each theme. To assess the performance of SNPic, we 149 

validated its capabilities using controlled simulation data alongside public GWAS cohorts from plants (e.g., 150 

maize and Arabidopsis thaliana ), animals (cattle), and large-scale human biobanks (Figure 1 IV-E). 151 

## 2.2 Simulation Performance 152 

To validate the mathematical accuracy of SNPic against ground truth, we conducted comprehensive simu-153 

lations across three scenarios with progressively increasing structural complexity: (1) Case 1: Linear with 154 

Four Groups (Figure 2 (A-B)), (2) Case 2: Linear with Six Groups (Figure 2 (C-D)), and (3) Case 3: Non-155 

linear with Periodic Assignment (Figure 2 (E-F)). Each simulation compared SNPic against two established 156 

dimensionality reduction methods, PCA and UMAP, under both Normal and Gaussian data generation 157 

schemes. The clustering accuracy was quantified as the proportion of correctly reconstructed group mem-158 

berships relative to the ground-truth simulated structure. Simulation rounds 1-10 progressively increased 159 

group overlap from 0% to 90% in 10% increments, with rounds 11 and 12 representing extreme overlap con-160 

ditions of 95% and 99%, respectively. For concise visualization and evaluation, the graphical comparisons 161 

presented in Figure 2 and the numerical values discussed below represent the mean clustering accuracy 162 

averaged across all 12 of these simulation rounds for each scenario. Detailed performance metrics itemized 163 

for every individual round and overlap condition are available in Supplementary Table. 164 

Across all averaged scenarios, SNPic consistently demonstrated robust performance, achieving superior 165 

or comparable reconstruction accuracy relative to PCA while significantly outperforming UMAP in both 166 

linear and non-linear situations. The number of inferred topics ( Ntopics ) dynamically adapted between 4–10 167 

across simulations, closely reflecting the true number of simulated groups and maintaining interpretability. 168 

In linear architectures (Figure 2 A-D), SNPic showed stable accuracy across increasing overlap conditions. 169 

Under the Gene-as-word scheme, Normal SNPic achieved mean accuracies of 0.900 (4-group) and 0.841 (6-170 

group), substantially outperforming both PCA and UMAP. The performance advantage was equally evident 171 

in Sumstat-as-word schemes, where Normal SNPic achieved the highest mean accuracy (0.860 for 4-group, 172 

0.819 for 6-group) among all methods. 173 

In the most challenging non-linear periodic assignment (Figure 2 E-F), SNPic demonstrated substantial 174 

advantages, particularly under the Sumstat-as-word scheme where Normal SNPic achieved a mean accuracy 175 

of 0.895, substantially surpassing PCA (0.577) and UMAP (0.591). This superior performance highlights 176

2.3.1 Maize (Zea mays) 185 

We applied the Sumstat-as-word scheme to maize transcriptomics data collected from distinct experimental 186 

batches [55]. To validate the model’s biological relevance, we focused on key agronomic phenotypes including 187 

plant height, ear height, and kernel traits (Figure 3A). Utilizing the stability-driven selection strategy, we 188 

identified an optimal number of topics of K = 15 for the maize dataset (Figure 3B). 189 

The detailed topic probability distributions and top-associated words revealed that SNPic effectively 190 

identifies consistent trait relationships across datasets (Figure 4A, B). For example, across different experi-191 

mental batches, Ear Weight (represented interchangeably as EW and Ear weight) exhibits strong associations 192 

with Topics 6, 13, and 14, while Cob Weight (represented as CW and Cob weight) is primarily linked to 193 

Topics 6, 8, and 14. Their convergence on shared genetic components, specifically Topics 6 and 14, across 194 

disparate batches indicates that while batch effects inevitably introduce technical variation into the data, 195 

they do not overshadow the underlying biological architecture, and these inherent inter-group relationships 196 

between these biologically coupled traits are robustly preserved. Building upon this, the trait similarity net-197 

work further demonstrates SNPic’s capability for co-analysis across multiple datasets (Figure 4C). Despite 198 

differences in genetic backgrounds and measurement protocols, the network demonstrates that the clustering 199 

is not completely dictated by batch effects. Instead, it successfully preserves broader biological associa-200 

tions. For instance, nodes representing “ear height” and “plant height” from different batches (indicated by 201 

distinct node shapes) not only formed tightly connected, trait-specific sub-networks (indicated by uniform 202 

node colors), but also maintained a clear inter-group topology linking these distinct traits together. This 203 

confirms SNPic’s capacity to extract robust, interconnected biological signals and retain true phenotypic 204 

relationships, even in the presence of technical batch variation. 205 

2.3.2 Arabidopsis thaliana 206 

To assess biological precision, we applied the Sumstat-as-word approach to a classic GWAS cohort of 207 

Arabidopsis thaliana [56]. This dataset serves as a ground-truth benchmark as phenotypes have defined 208 

functional annotations. The optimal number of topics was identified as K = 5 based on the bootstrap mean 209 

confidence score (Supplementary Figure 1A). The resulting topic model demonstrated a robust capacity 210 

to cluster phenotypes according to their pre-defined functional categories, as visualized in the trait sim-211 

ilarity network (Figure 5). The details of each trait are in Supplementary Table 1 and originally in the 212 

supplementary table of the paper [56]. 213 

A particularly insightful finding was the recovery of a “function-interaction” link: the topic distribution 214 

(Supplementary Figure 1B) clearly reveals that FLC (Flowering Locus C), a central regulator of flowering 215 

time, is strongly associated with the same latent genetic topic (Topic 2) as the defense response phenotypes 216 

AvrRpm1 and avrB. While ostensibly distinct, this connection is biologically supported: polymorphisms 217 

in the FRI (Frigida) gene regulate FLC expression, and the same genomic region produces a multi-genic 218 

association with the AvrRpm1 defense response [56–58]. This reveals SNPic’s ability to highlight pleiotropic 219 

connections that cross traditional functional boundaries. 220

## 2.4 Human Cross-Biobank Integration 235 

To construct a map of human disease architecture, we applied the SNPic framework to a combined dataset of 236 

FinnGen and UK Biobank (UKBB) summary statistics. We employed an unbiased, stability-driven pipeline 237 

to filter noise, validated the integration of cross-biobank data, and dissected the biological meaning of the 238 

inferred latent topics. The details of selected diseases are provided at Supplementary Table 3. 239

# 3 Discussion 356 

GWAS have cataloged thousands of trait-associated significant variants, yet a central challenge remains: 357 

moving beyond isolated signals to understand how discrete genetic factors group into coherent programs that 358 

shape complex phenotypes. Here we introduce SNPic, a generative framework that addresses this challenge 359 

through topic modeling, reframing genetic problems as NLP tasks. By analogizing phenotypes to documents 360 

and genetic features, e.g. genes, to words, SNPic infers latent probabilistic topics of genetics, which are 361 

interpretable modules that collectively explain the observed genetic architecture across traits. 362 

Our simulations suggest that this generative framework offers a more direct and interpretable route to 363 

recovering latent structure in overlapping genetic architectures compared to descriptive correlation-based 364 

methods. While PCA and UMAP capture broad patterns of similarity, their performance is limited under the 365 

non-linear dependencies and high-dimensional overlap characteristic of polygenic architectures. In contrast, 366 

SNPic’s probabilistic model recovered the underlying latent structure in both overlapping and non-linear 367 

scenarios (Figure 2). This property aligns with the biological reality of pleiotropy, where a single variant 368 

often participates in multiple biological processes. 369 

SNPic’s inference pipeline uses a data-driven, bootstrap-based procedure for hyper-parameter selection 370 

to identify a stable genetic architecture and determine the optimal number of topics from integrated FinnGen 371 

and UKBB data. By applying a dynamic thresholding strategy, that retains the top 50% of phenotypes or 372 

ensures a Stability Score > 0.25, our pipeline is designed to filter out noisy signals. Disease stability exhibited 373 

category-specific trends: immunological and cardiovascular disorders generally demonstrated higher stability, 374 

whereas highly polygenic or environmentally sensitive traits ranked lower. While conditions with diffuse 375 

genetic architectures (e.g., cancers) generally ranked lower in stability compared to the major-effect loci 376 

(e.g., the HLA region) driving many autoimmune diseases, all evaluated human traits maintained sufficient 377 

structural coherence to pass this dynamic threshold. 378 

Critically, within this high-confidence set, the convergence of identical phenotypes from distinct biobanks 379 

(FinnGen and UKBB) into the same semantic clusters provides evidence that SNPic captures biological 380 

mechanisms rather than technical confounders from LD panels. Since LD panel structures and genotyp-381 

ing platforms differ between these cohorts, the observed consistency in topic distribution implies that the 382 

model is driven by shared functional architecture rather than localized correlation blocks. This demon-383 

strates SNPic’s utility as a powerful tool for cross-cohort meta-analysis. While established frameworks like 384 

GenomicSEM provide powerful tools for modeling continuous genetic covariance, they are highly sensi-385 

tive to mismatches in LD reference panels. In contrast, SNPic operates under a fundamentally different 386 

mathematical background: it acts as an LD-free, discrete generative model. This structural independence 387 

from external LD panels enables robust cross-biobank integration. For example, when integrating data 388 

from the UK Biobank and FinnGen, which are two populations with different demographic histories and 389 

unique LD architectures, SNPic uncovers shared biological mechanisms without the confounding bias that 390 

LD-dependent models would introduce. 391

## 4.1 The SNPic Framework 508 

SNPic is a computational framework that adapts topic modeling to analyze GWAS summary statistics, 509 

transforming them into interpretable representations that reveal latent genetic structures. The core analogy 510 

treats complex traits as documents composed of genetic “words”. 511 

4.1.1 Data Representation Schemes 512 

Formally, let a corpus of p diseases or phenotypes be represented as a set of documents D = {d1, d 2, . . . , d p}.513 

The words, denoted by V , consists of N distinct genetic features (“words”). SNPic implements two 514 

complementary schemes for defining this corpus: 515 

1. Summary Statistics-as-Word (Sumstat-as-word): The word is the corpus of all genotyped or 516 

imputed SNPs, such that V = S = {s1, s 2, . . . , s m}. A word sj is considered present in document di if 517 

the SNP achieves genome-wide significance in the GWAS for disease di.518 

2. Gene-as-Word: The word is defined as a set of genes, V = G = {g1, g 2, . . . , g n}. Significant SNPs are 519 

mapped to their putative target genes via SN P → G mapping, with the current study employing the 520 

nearest-gene strategy, which can be user-adjustable. Some analysis proves the rationale in nearest-gene 521 

strategy [69]. This scheme enables direct biological interpretation through pathway enrichment and tissue 522 

expression analysis. 523

## 4.4 Stability-Optimized Topic Modeling 563 

4.4.1 Bootstrap Stability Analysis 564 

Standard LDA inference is inherently stochastic, resulting in slight variations in topic distributions across 565 

runs due to the probabilistic nature of Gibbs sampling. To ensure robust topic inference and quantify the 566 

reliability of genetic relationships, we implemented a bootstrap-based stability analysis. 567 

For each candidate topic number K, we generated 50 bootstrap replicates by resampling the genetic 568 

features within each trait. Specifically, for each trait document, a new feature count vector was drawn 569 

from a multinomial distribution parameterized by its original word frequencies, thereby maintaining the 570 

total significant variant count per trait while introducing stochastic perturbations. For each replicate, we 571 

performed LDA inference using a collapsed Gibbs sampler (burn-in=500 and iterations=1000) to estimate 572 

the trait-topic distribution matrix Θ. We then calculated the pairwise trait-trait similarity matrix using 573 

the Pearson correlation coefficient of their topic proportion vectors, scaling the coefficients to a [0 , 1] range. 574 

These replicate-specific similarity matrices were used to quantify the estimation uncertainty across stochastic 575 

perturbations, forming the basis for our subsequent model selection and noise filtering procedures. 576 

4.4.2 Optimal Topic Number Selection 577 

Selecting the optimal number of topics ( K) is critical for balancing model resolution with structural robust-578

assessment across the entire tested spectrum. 584

4.4.3 Stable Trait Filtering 609 

Low-heritability traits or those with ambiguous genetic architectures often fail to cluster consistently. To 610 

mitigate noise, we applied a dynamic thresholding strategy to filter out unstable traits. For the selected 611 

optimal K, we established a dynamic stability threshold defined as the minimum between 0 .25 and the 612 

median Confidence Score across all phenotypes. 613 

Traits were retained for downstream network construction and biological interpretation only if their 614 

Confidence Score met or exceeded this dynamic threshold. This mathematical formulation guarantees that 615 

at least 50% of the analyzed phenotypes are retained for robust network construction, while capping the 616 

threshold’s strictness at 0.25. This quality control step ensures that the reported “genetic topics” and trait 617 

clusters reflect persistent biological signals rather than stochastic artifacts of the modeling process. However, 618 

to support broader exploratory analyses—particularly in datasets with lower statistical power or sparse 619 

annotations (e.g., non-model organisms)—the SNPic framework also provides a flexible option to bypass 620 

this filtering step, preserving the complete phenotypic landscape for downstream network construction. 621 

## 4.5 Simulation Study 622 

To rigorously evaluate the performance of SNPic in recovering latent genetic structures, we conducted a 623 

series of controlled simulations under varying levels of genetic overlap and structural complexity. We utilized 624 

LDAK (Version 6.1) to generate synthetic genotype data for 100,000 individuals across 500,000 SNPs [78]. 625 

Phenotypes were simulated with a high heritability ( h2 = 0 .8) and a prevalence of 0.1 to ensure robust 626 

genetic signals. 627 

4.5.1 Simulation Framework 628 

The simulation pipeline followed a systematic six-step procedure to mirror real-world GWAS analysis: 629 

1. Causal Variant Definition: We defined subsets of causal SNPs with precisely controlled pairwise 630 

overlaps to simulate shared genetic architectures. 631 

2. Phenotype Generation: Phenotypes were generated based on the additive effects of the selected causal 632 

variants. 633 

3. GWAS Analysis: Standard GWAS were performed on the synthetic cohorts to obtain summary 634 

statistics. 635 

4. Signal Extraction: Significant associations were filtered using a threshold of p < 10 −4 to retain 636 

informative genetic signals. 637 

5. Gene Mapping: Significant SNPs were mapped to gene features to construct a Gene-as-word 638 

representation. 639 

6. Matrix Construction: Finally, document-term matrices were constructed for input into the topic 640 

modeling framework. 641

4.6.2 Non-coding Gene Filtering 683 

To establish a clean and biologically interpretable “golden baseline” for the Gene-as-word scheme, we 684 

deliberately restricted our words strictly to protein-coding genes. It is worth noting that while the core SNPic 685 

framework does not strictly mandate the exclusion of non-coding genes, this filter was applied primarily 686 

because the downstream pathway and tissue enrichment modules are currently not equipped to process 687 

non-coding data. This conservative strategy isolates the intrinsic decomposition capability of the generative 688 

model while minimizing potential false-positive noise often introduced by complex variant-to-function (V2F) 689 

mapping algorithms in non-coding regions [69]. 690 

To systematically implement this filter, we constructed a comprehensive dictionary of valid protein-691 

coding genes using the authoritative HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee (HGNC) database [83]. We 692 

retrieved the complete HGNC dataset and filtered specifically for entities classified under the locus group 693 

of “protein-coding gene”. To maximize matching accuracy and account for historical nomenclature updates 694 

across different GWAS cohorts, we compiled an exhaustive vocabulary encompassing official gene symbols, 695 

alongside their documented alias and previous symbols. 696 

For the primary SNP-to-gene projection, we employed a fundamental nearest-gene strategy. The resulting 697 

mapped genes were then rigorously cross-referenced against our HGNC-derived dictionary. Any mapped 698 

genetic features falling into non-coding regions, pseudogenes, or uncharacterized RNA genes were explicitly 699 

pruned from the dataset. This rigorous curation ensured that the final document-term matrix inputted into 700 

the LDA model consisted exclusively of high-confidence, biologically translatable protein-coding elements. 701 

Furthermore, to provide a comparative perspective and evaluate the impact of functional mapping, we 702 

conducted a supplementary analysis substituting the nearest-gene approach with a comprehensive V2F 703 

strategy. For this, we utilized multi-tissue expression quantitative trait loci (eQTLs) derived from the GTEx 704 

v8 database [84] to map variants to their functional target genes. To ensure analytical consistency, these 705 

eQTL-derived target genes were subjected to the identical HGNC protein-coding filtration process before 706 

topic modeling. 707
