## Background 42 

Gene diversity can significantly impact the therapeutic effect of drugs in patients . One such example is the cytochrome P450 (CYP) 43 

superfamily of enzymes that play a key role in drug metabolism and can result in either supratherapeutic or subtherapeutic serum 44 

drug concentrations (McDonnell and Dang, 2013) . Genotyping of genes prior to treatment can inform drug selection, dosing 45 

decision s, and avoidance of adverse side effects (Chang et al. , 2015) . CYP2D6 is one of the most clinically significant genes and 46 

CYP2D6 genotype -guided dosing exists for a number of indications , including anti -fungal treatment in hematopoietic stem cell 47 

transplantation, breast cancer chemoth erap eutics , tricyclic antidepressants, and opioids for pain management (Teusink et al. , 2016; 48 

Kiyotani et a l. , 2008; Hicks et al. , 2017; Somogyi et al. , 2015) . A s such, a method for obtaining a fast and accurate readout of a 49 

patient’s CYP2D6 genotype is of great importance . CYP2D6 is highly polymorphic; currently there are over 17 5 known functional 50 

allel es (ea ch designated as a “star -allele”, with CYP2D6*1 being the reference) , many of which differ from another allele by only 51 

a single base substitution (Whirl -Carrillo et al. , 2021, 2012) , and new alleles are constantly being added as they are discovered. 52 

Traditional methods to interrogate genotypes are generally slow and expensive ; typically relying on custo m sets of PCR 53 

reactions to interrogate specific sites along the length of each gene of interest . More contemporaneous approaches have leveraged 54 

high -throughput sequencing data, either whole genome (Chen et al. , 2021; Deserranno et al. , 2023) , or amplicon -based (Hari et al. ,55 

2023; Liau, Cree, et al. , 2019; Liau, Maggo, et al. , 2019; Qiao et al. , 2016; Ammar et al. , 2015; Charnaud et al. , 2022) . These 56 

methods o ften make use of Pacific Biosciences or Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) long reads, allowing the entire gene to 57 

be sequenced in a single read , and thus , facilitating accurate DNA sequencing and haplotyping with a single assay . Regardless of 58

We sought to develop a method of diplotyping genes that uses a “sequence -first” rather than “variant -first” approach . We 61 

aimed to perform allele sequence reconstruction from long read sequence s by generating high -confidence consensus sequences of 62

Further, we ensured our method was unbiased and generalizable , not being restricted to a specific gene. As such, our only input 64 

requirement s are that the dataset is generated from genomic amplicon sequencing usi ng ONT long reads and the dataset contains 65 

reads which completely cover the genomic region s of interest. No information is need ed a priori regarding the specific genomic 66 

target, nor the number of targets. We demonstrate the utility of our approach using ON T sequencing data from CYP2D6 amplicon s67

Assigning reads to inferred amplicons based on whole genome alignment. 119 

We designed our method to perform an unbiased reconstruction of allele sequence s. We base this on an assumption that every 120 

amplicon present in the sequence library will have a subset of reads supporting that amplicon. As such, rather than supplying the 121 

algorithm with the anticipate d amplicon, we use d the sequence reads to infer the genomic loci that were amplified. To achieve this, 122 

we align ed all reads to the human reference genome (hg38) using minimap2 , allowing for only the single best mapping position of 123 

each read . Supplementary alignments, or split reads, were included in order to detect large deletions or structural variants. The 124 

depth of coverage across the whole genome was assessed, and the regions with high coverage were taken to be representative of 125 

amplic ons present in the source sequencing library (Figure 3A). Importantly, this approach does not assume any predefined number 126 

of amplicons, allowing this approach to work, theoretically, for multiplexed samples of any number of amplicons. All of our 127 

samples y ielded a n inferred amplicon region at the expected CYP2D6 locus , and six samples yielded at least one second , distinct 128 

inferred amplicon region (Supplementa ry Table S1 ). Two of these non -CYP2D6 inferred amplicons (NA02016_ligate, 129 

NA07439_ligate) were from the region between CYP2D6 and CYP2D7 , corresponding to the duplication -detecting amplicon from 130 

Wongsurawat et al. One (NA17203_ligate) was from the CYP2D7 locus , possibly derived from a CYP2D6 -CYP2D7 hybrid allele, 131 

where the majority of the amplicon sequence was derived from CYP2D7 . Three (NA02016, NA10005, NA17052_ligate) were from 132 

elsewhere in the genome. NA02016 and NA10005 produced short ( mean 609 bp ) off -target amplicon regi ons from chromosome s133 

4, 9, 12, and 20. NA17052_ligate had an off -target amplicon from from the SORCS2 locus on chromosome 4. Careful inspection of 134 

the identified SORCS2 locus sequence shows it contains a string of 15 contiguous bases at the start ( GCCCTGGGAGGTAGG ), 135

Allele sequence reconstruction performed through unbiased, recursive consensus sequence generation .171 

At this stage, we had obtained subsets of length -matched reads which were likely to have been derived from the same locus . These 172 

subsets, however, contain ed a mix of reads supporting each of the underlying haplotypes present in the original sample. If all 173 

sequence reads were error -free , this would have been a trivial exercise to partition reads based on their exact sequence. Due to 174 

sequencing errors, reads derived from the same haplotype did not share the exact same sequence. In order to reconstruct the 175 

underlying allele sequence s, we took an empirical approach for determining which allele sequences the reads support ed . This was 176 

achieved by find ing partitions of the reads such that all reads in a partition support ed the same sequence (while allowing for some 177 

noise due to sequence errors ). First, we attempt ed to build a consensus sequence from all of the reads in the set, using similar logic 178 

to our previous work (Brown et al. , 2023) . Briefly, the reads were aligned to the inferred amplicon reference sequence, and the 179 

pileup of base support at each position of the alignment was assessed, sequentially , to call a conse nsus base at that position. If a 180 

position wa s reached that did not have sufficient evidence of a homozygous base (homozygosity defined as the top two bases 181 

having a rat io of at least 2:1 ; see Materials and Methods) (Figure 3C), the reads were partitioned into subsets defined by the 182 

observed base s at this heterozygous position (Figure 3D). The consensus sequence generation wa s restarted from the beginning ,183 

recursi vely, on each of these resulting subset s. This process continued until a homozygous consensus sequence wa s successfully 184 

generated from a subset of reads, or the size of a subset fell below the lower limit threshold for reporting (5% of the total starting 185 

read set of length -clustered reads, selected empirically ). 186 

Our approach considered base quality in two ways. In order for a base to contribute to the consensus sequence, the base 187 

must have had a minimum quality of 10 (selected empirically) . If this criterio n wa s not met, a secondary criterion was tested, 188 

requiring a quality score above the 33 rd percentile of observed qualities at that position (selected empirically) . This ensure d that 189 

positions with low er -quality bases across all reads were not completely lo st from the consensus due to lack of coverage, while still 190 

ensuring that the consensus sequence wa s built using the best quality bases available at each position . Strand -specific errors are 191 

also common in ONT data (Krishnakumar et al. , 2018; Branton et al. , 2008) , resulting in a specific sequenc ing error occurring 192

To identify the specific CYP2D6 alleles that each resul ting consensus sequence supported , we first aligned each consensus sequenc e224 

to the CYP2D6*1 reference sequence. All variants (indels and mismatches) were enumerated, and those occurring within coding 225 

regions or splice sites were used for CYP2D6 allele determination. We automated this process by using the CYP2D6 Allele 226 

Definition Table from ClinPGx (Whirl -Carrillo et al. , 2021, 2012) . Briefly, for each known star allel e, all canonical variants were 227 

parsed from the ClinPGx definition table. For alleles with variants that have multiple possible bases allowed , some of which may 228 

include the reference wildtype base , all possible allowed bases are stored. Then, the se t of identified variants for the consensus 229 

sequence was compared to the set of all required or possible variants from all known alleles, and each known star allele was given 230 

a score by summing the number of disagreements in the identified variants from the consensus sequence . Thus, a consensus 231 

sequence that had exactly the same variants as a defined star -allele would receive a score of zero for that allele. Silent mutations 232 

found in the consensus would negatively impact the score across all alleles equally. The allele with the lowest score was returned 233 

as the best candidate allele assignment for the consensus sequence . In the event of an imperfect match, missing and/ or extra variants 234 

in the consensus sequence compared to the candidate star allele reference were also returned, allowing for manual review of the 235 

candidate allele assignment (Table 1 ). In this way, novel variants and alleles can also be readily identified, rather than simply the 236 

best match to known alleles. 237 

Inferring copy number from reconstructed alleles. 238 

Copy number of genes can be an important factor for determining functional effect s. In our results, the r elative copy number of the 239 

reconstructed alleles could be inferred based on the number of reads that contributed to each allele. We pool ed the read counts for 240 

all sequences of the same allele. The presence of multiple distinct consensus sequences for the same allele could be used as evidence 241 

of duplication events ; however , the sequences should be manually inspected to determine if the differences are believe d to be from 242 

true underlying sequence differences or from regions of poor sequence quality such as those near the ends of the amplicon. 243 

Importantly, the lack of multiple distinct sequences does not preclude the presence of a duplication, as each duplicated copy may 244 

indeed have an identical underlying sequence. For these reasons, we propose the read depths as a more reliable estimate of relative 245 

copy number. To interpret the allele read depth ratios, we test ed for ratios > 2:1. In our results, the exact numb er of duplication 246 

events could not be reliably determined , likely due to biases in different allele sequences and sequencing errors, especially in 247 

samples with lower read counts . Therefore, the alleles with a greater than 2:1 ratio were assigned an xN dupl ication. 248

SpecImmune (S. Wang et al. , 2025) , and CoLoRGen (Rubben et al. , 2022) , three “variant -first” approaches . SpecImmune and 307 

CoLoRGen are compatible with ONT amplicon data . Aldy 4 (Hari et al. , 2023) does not officially support ONT amplicon data, 308 

however can be run without phasing or copy number detection to allow genotyping. We did not use any PacBio -specific approaches, 309 

as in our experience, ONT and PacBio data and algorithms a re generally not cross -compatible due to different sequencing error 310 

profiles. For completeness, we also compare d our results to the reported genotypes from Liau et al. and Wongsurawat et al. source 311 

dataset publications ( Table 2 ). 312

## Discussion 368 

We have presen ted our solution to reconstruct allele sequence s from ONT -based amplicon sequencing, demonstrating its 369 

effectiveness on 22 distinct CYP2D6 sequence datasets encompassing different amplicons and flow cells , 11 pooled HL A class I 370 

datasets, 11 existing datasets from other genomic loci, and three datasets from plasmid sequencing . Our approach is unbiased, only 371 

requiring that a subset of reads exist which fully cover the amplicon of interest, but not requiring a priori knowl edge of what the 372 

specific amplicon is. The appeal of this approach in diverse genes such as CYP2D6 is that potential duplications and structural 373 

variants may be reconstructed without pre -existing knowledge of the sequence. For our application of this appro ach to the CYP2D6 374 

locus, we provide post -hoc analysis scripts to automate the variant detection and star -allele assignment, facilitating rapid 375 

interpretation of results. Our approach makes no assumptions regarding the number of gene amplicons (or alleles t hereof) present 376 

in the data, only requiring sufficient coverage of each amplicon in order to be reconstructed and reported. Since the primary output 377 

of our approach is the reconstructed allele sequence, novel variants and alleles are readily detectable. In addition to the reconstructed 378 

allele sequence and assigned star -allele, our pipeline also outputs a file with per -position base frequenc ies and average base 379 

qualit ies , allowing interrogation of specific positions to assess the confidence of the resulting base. This gives the user more 380 

information, allowing manual interpretation of putative novel variants (Figure 4), much like is possible with traditional Sanger 381 

sequencing . Within the context of tamoxifen metabolism by CYP2D6, a recent study demonstrated th at a neural -network model 382 

given full -length gene sequences was better able to predict metabolism rates compared to rates predicted from the assigned star -383 

alleles alone (Van der Lee et al. , 2021) . This demonstrates the increased power of allele sequence r econstruction, and suggests that 384 

such reconstruction s may be even more important in the future as more machine learning models are trained across a wider range 385 

of genes and pathways. 386

calling to build the consensus sequences . This is li mited to two alleles per HLA gene, requires upfront segmentation of reads into 392

Allele sequence reconstruction . Our allele reconstruction method is built as a Python (v3.11) script, and takes the alignm ents of each group of 532 

candidate reads to the ir inferred amplicon reference sequence obtained using minimap2 v2.17 -r941 . These alignments are parsed , along with the 533 

raw fastq files to extract per -base quality scores and unaligned sequence flanking each alignment. The unaligned flanking sequences are treated as 534 

insertions, and are only used if at least 25% of the reads support sequence at that position. To facilitate efficient assessment of the pile -up of base 535 

support at every reference position and possible insertion site s, we use byte matrices to store a representation of the multiple sequence alignment 536 

of all reads. To generate this, we step through every alignment to track the maximum observed insertion at every position. We then create empty 537 

byt e matrices of reads × positions, where positions are offset relative to the reference sequence to allow for all observed insertions . We then step 538 

through all alignments again to load the observed bases a nd base quality scores into the byte matrices. The el ement i,j of the matrix are integer 539 

representations of the base found in th e ith read at position j. Separate matrices are maintained for positive and negative strand alignments, 540 

facilitating strand -specific assessment of coverage . The matri ces are evaluated , position by position, to assess the number of reads supporting each 541 

base at each position. If a position has evidence of heterozygosity (here, defined as the top two most -observed base s having less than a 2:1 ratio in 542 

ab undance, threshold determined empirically) , the reads are partitioned into separate groups based on support of the heterozygous base s (Figure 543 

3C-D), and consensus sequence construction tries again on each of the new read groups independently . This repeats, recursively, until pure (no 544 

evidence of heterozygosity) consensus sequence(s) are derived, or the read groups are too small to continue. Due to the pheno menon of strand -545 

specific errors in ONT data (Krishnakumar et al. , 2018; Branton et al. , 2008) , this test of purity is also performed on the forward and reverse reads 546 

independently . Based on a 5kb referen ce sequence, our algorithm requires 1 CPU core and approximately 3 GB of RAM per 10,000 reads. 547 

, The allele reconstruction script has a number of parameters that can be adjusted to f ine -tune the behavior of the allele reconstruction. 548 

Based on all of the control data, we used the following settings , determined empirically , and explained subsequently : --min_depth_factor 0.25 , --549 

absolute _min_depth_thresh 10 , --min_base_qual_glo bal 10, --min_base_qual_percentile 33, --sig_to_noise_thresh 2. The “ --min_depth_factor” 550 

controls the minimum number of reads needed to support a base at any position, and represents the fraction of the maximum obs erved depth (here, 551

are discarded. The average quality score for each consensus base across all reads used for the consensus is recorded and output in the detailed 562

Automatic genotyping of consensus sequence . Each resu lting reconstructed allele sequence is aligned to the CYP2D6 reference sequence 564 

using Biopython v1.79 pairwise2.align.localxs , and the alignment is parsed to enumerate all variants. All known CYP2D6 variants are catalogued 565 

from the CYP2D6 Allele Definition Table from ClinPGx (Whirl -Carrillo et al. , 2021, 2012) , downloaded from 566 

https://www.pharmgkb.org/page/cyp2d6RefMaterials  on January 21, 2025 (now available at https://www.clinpgx.org/page/cyp2d6RefMaterials ).567 

This file was manually curated to ensure consistency in how each varian t type was reported, allowing for straightforward parsing. For variants that 568 

can have multiple possible bases allowed for a given allele, all possible allowed values are stored. The set of identified variants for each 569 

reconstructed allele sequence is compa red to those from each known allele, and a score is calculated for each known allele based on the number of 570 

disagreements in identified variants within the exon and splice regions of the gene . The allele with the lowest score is returned as the best candid ate 571 

allele assignment for the reconstructed allele sequence , along with any missing or extra variants present in the consensus sequence compared to the 572 

star allele reference should they exist . In this way, manual review of the candidate alleles and identif ied variants allows a user to confirm the allele 573 

assignment, or characterize a novel allele . A helper script is made available on the GitHub code repository to report out the most likely genotype 574 

for each sample based on all identified consensus sequences and abundances. This combines counts for all consensus sequences supporting the 575 

same CYP2D6 allele , and outputs any allele that represents at least 5 % of the reads of that length -cluster . Alleles with a greater than 2:1 ratio to 576 

each other are reported as possibly supporting a duplication or copy number variation event. 577
