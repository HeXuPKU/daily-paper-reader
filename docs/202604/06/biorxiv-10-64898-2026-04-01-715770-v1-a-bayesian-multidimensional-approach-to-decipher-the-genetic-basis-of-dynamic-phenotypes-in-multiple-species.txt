## Abstract 19 

Deciphering the genetic architecture of complex quantitative phenotypes remains challeng ing  in quantitative 20 

genetics. These traits not only depend of multiple genetic factors but are also established over time and 21 

environments. Although quantitative genetics has investigated the genetic determinism of phenotypic plasticity in 22 

contrasted environmental conditions, the time related phenotypic plasticity has received less attention. 23 

Here we proposed a multivariate Bayesian framework, the Bayesian  Varying  Coefficient  Model, designed for 24 

analysing the genetic architecture of the time related phenotypic plasticity by a multilocus approach. We  applied 25 

the BVCM to time series phenotypes measured at various time scales (daily, monthly, yearly) across a diverse set 26 

of biological species. We included in this study: yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae ),  fungi (Fusarium 27 

graminearum ), eucalyptus ( Eucalyptus urophylla × E.  grandis ), and  sweet  cherry tree ( Prunus avium ).  The 28

## 1. Introduction 52 

Understanding the genetic basis of complex quantitative traits remains a challenge due to their oligo/multi -genic 53 

determinism  (Goddard  et al. , 2016) . In agronomy , most  quantitative traits are measured at  the  exploitation age 54 

considered as representative of the genotype performances, or after a fixed number of days following planting or 55 

stress events. However,  phenotypic  traits such as growth, developmental , and phenological processes are 56 

established over time  and  environment s. Analyzing these dynamics require time series data  of each phenotype . In 57 

the context of quantitative genetics, the study of time series offer s the opportunity to  reveal  the  effect  of various 58 

genetic regions in the establishment  of  phenotyp es a nd increase s the accuracy and power of  quantitative trait loci 59 

(QTL ) detection approaches  (Moore  et al. , 2013; Sang  et al. , 2019; Miao  et al. , 2020) . While many efforts have 60

However , the  time -by -time  analysis did not reflect  the dependency of the data measured on  each  genotype for 68 

multiple time points. Secondly, in order to overcome this limitation, the temporal aspect  of phenotype variation 69 

was  incorporated by converting the phenotype into  function parameters  (Ma  et al. , 2002; Wu  et al. , 2004)  or 70

2016; Muraya et al. , 2017; Xu et al. , 2018b,a; Miao et al. , 2020; Li et al. , 2023) . However, the complexity and 72 

informational content of the time series were not fully exploited by these approaches. 73 

Quantitative and association mapping traditionally relied on single -locus approaches,  which  enabled  identifying 74 

major  QTL associated with many mendelian mono /oligo  genic traits such as diseases resistance  (St Clair, 2010) .75 

However, the polygenic genetic architecture of quantitative traits involves numerous loci with a wide range of 76 

effects from few  strong  effects to putative huge number of small effects , as well as potential interactions among 77 

them  (Holland, 2007; Mackay, 2009) . These aspects , which contribute significantly to phenotypic variation,  are 78 

difficult  to statistically identify  (Hill, 2010) . Widely used single -locus methods often lack the power to identify 79 

loci with small effects or their interactions  (epistatic  effects ) (Carlson  et al. , 2004) . Multilocus approaches were 80 

developed  to address these limitations  by  incorporat ing  more than one genomic region within a single  analysis 81 

conducted  for a wide range of statistical models . For instance, composite interval mapping  (CIM  ; Jansen & Stam, 82 

1994; Zeng, 1994)  and the Bayesian -information and Linkage -disequilibrium Iteratively Nested Keyway method 83 

(BLINK method; Huang  et al. , 2018)  represented multilocus strategies with contrasted levels of “multi -84 

dimensions”. CIM involves a series of single -locus scans in which detected QTL s are included as cofactors in 85 

subsequent analyses. This improved detection power and resolution for loci with small effects, although it did not 86 

fully use genome -wide information  (Segura  et al. , 2012) . In contrast, BLINK adopted a more comprehensive 87 

multilocus framework by iteratively selecting an optimal subset of markers as covariates  and  updating them until 88 

the  model converge d (Huang  et al. , 2018) . While multilocus models differed in the  level  of multivariate structure 89 

they exhibited , they consistently outperformed single -locus approaches capturing the complexity of genetic 90 

architectures  (Hoggart et al. , 2008; Segura et al. , 2012; Xu et al. , 2018c; Kaler et al. , 2020; Zhong et al. , 2021; 91 

Adhikari et al. , 2023; Osorio -Guarin et al. , 2024) . In these studies,  the advantages of  multilocus approaches  were 92 

the detection of  more genetic markers associated with already known genetic regions and  the decrease of  the false 93 

positive and false negative discovery rate in association mapping. 94 

Currently, multilocus statistical approaches were based on “stepwise like” variable selection methods. These 95 

methods were broadly contested because of bias in parameter estimations and inconsistencies among model 96 

selection  (Steyerberg  et al. , 2000; Whittingham  et al. , 2006) . Alternatively,  Steyerberg  et al.  (2000)  promoted the 97 

use of penalized likelihood methods  such as the ridge regression  (Hoerl & Kennard, 1970)  in a context of 98 

multicollinearity, or the Lasso regression  (Tibshirani, 1996)  when the number of predictors was large relatively 99

placing shrinkage priors on the regression coefficients, thereby introducing regularization  (Bickel  et al. , 2006) .101 

Among them, the spike -and -slab prior  (Mitchell & Beauchamp, 1988) , the Bayesian Lasso  (Park & Casella, 2008) ,102 

or the horseshoe prior  (Carvalho  et al. , 2009)  may be cited.  In addition, the Bayesian framework also enables the 103 

simultaneous integration of multilocus relationships and  the data structure such as  temporal dependencies. Despite 104 

their potential,  only  few studies explored the application of Bayesian variable selection  approaches  in the analysis 105 

of dynamic phenotypes represented as time series  (Heuclin  et al.  2021  , Qu  et al. , 2022) . Heuclin  et al.  (2021) 106 

introduced the Bayesian  Varying  Coefficient  Model (BVCM), a multilocus model specifically designed for 107 

analy zing dynamic phenotypes. It consisted in a  multivariate linear model that simultaneously allowed for the 108 

selection of genetic markers involved in the variability of phenotypes over time and the estimation of their 109 

dynamic effects. These effects were estimated using either interpolation methods, such as B -splines or P -splines, 110 

or direct approaches like the random walk process. Marker selection is achieved using a spike -and -slab prior, 111 

which yields a posterior probability of selection for each marker, computed simultaneously. 112 

In this study, to demonstrate  the  reliability and efficiency of the BVCM approach, we applied it to time series 113 

phenotypes measured at various time scales (daily, monthly, yearly) across a diverse set of biological species. We 114 

included in this study: i)  Saccharomyces cerevisiae  as a  yeast  model organism with well -characterized dynamic 115 

phenotypes,  ii)  Fusarium graminearum  as a  fungal phytopathogen  model for  studying host plant interaction 116 

dynamics throughout infection cycle , iii) eucalyptus ( Eucalyptus urophylla x E.grandis ) as a  fast -growing tree 117 

species highly studied in quantitative genetics, and iv)  sweet  cherry tree ( Prunus avium ) a s a  fruit tree exhibiting 118 

phenological trajectories influenced by diverse environmental pressures.  Studying  such  a panel of organisms has 119 

two main objectives: first, to demonstrate the robustness and broad applicability of the BVCM model across a 120 

wide range of biological systems; and second, to better understand the genetic architecture of the time related 121 

phenotypic plasticity.  To do so, the results of the BVCM have been compared with those obtained with a validated 122 

method of  marker -trait association . The results of the BVCM have been explored deeper by estimating the gain 123 

in phenotypic variance explained. Then, the contribution of each genetic region identified to phenotype variability 124 

have been evaluated over time. 125

2.3. Statistical  strategies for quantitative trait analysis and QTL detection 176 

For each data set, the missing data at the phenotypic level were imputed using the missMDA package V1.19  (Josse 177 

& Husson, 2016) . The best number of components was optimized with the “Kfold ” method allowing controlling 178 

for overfitting. For molecular data, the missing data were imputed with beagle 5.2  (Browning  et al. , 2018)  using 179 

default parameters. 180 

The genetic regions involved in the determinism of dynamics phenotypes was explored through two statistical 181 

approaches (BLINK and BVCM). Both approaches were based on a multivariate variable selection method, 182 

incorporating all markers in the analysis with BVCM and a partial selection of markers with BLINK. 183 

## 184 

2.3.1 BLINK  approach  (Huang  et al. , 2018) 185 

BLINK method, integrated in GAPIT  (Wang & Zhang, 2021) , performs a stepwise selection using two 186 

multivariate regression models. At each iteration, all markers are tested one at a time in a linear model with a set 187 

of pseudo quantitative trait nucleotides (QTN) as covariates initialized as an empty set. The markers are then 188 

ranked according to their p -value and filtered for their significance (p -value < 0.01) and  only one significant 189 

marker within pairwise correlation groups  (> 0.7)  is retained before evaluation . Then, the best subset of markers 190 

is evaluated through Bayesian information criterion. This set is then considered as the new pseudo QTN set. The 191 

process is repeated until the set of pseudo QTN remain s stable. As a result, the p -value s of the  set of  stable  pseudo 192 

QTN s are  return ed and significant QTL are selected after applying the p -values Bonferroni correction with a 193 

significance threshold set at 0.05/ n with  n being the number of markers used .194 

BLINK model show ed  strong performances in the detection of reliable  loci  in  marker -trait association studies .195 

These  association  studies were carried out for each data set at each time step of the time series. The minor allele 196 

frequency was set on 0.05 and  three  principal components were used to consider the population structure which 197 

was negligible in the context of progenies populations. The marker selection threshold was estimated with a 198

## 205 

2.3.2 BVCM  approach  (Heuclin  et al. , 2021) 206 

The BVCM approach allows  analysing  all measures of the time series in a single step.  In a Bayesian framework, 207 

the approach proposes  a molecular marker selection method based on a Bayesian spike -and -slab prior combined 208 

with a dynamic effect estimation  for  each marker using different  interpolation methods implemented in the  model 209 

: random walk, Legendre, B -spline or P -spline. Here, the B -spline was used for its simplicity (no hyper parameter 210 

settings need to be specified), its capacity to analyze a large number of time steps and because it requires 211 

estimating only a small number of parameters. 212 

The model was inferred with a Monte Carlo Markov chains (MCMC).  Highly  correlated markers were sorted out 213 

as described in  Heuclin  et al.  (2021)  according to a threshold estimated separately for each data set with the aim 214 

to limit the complexity of detecting significantly associated genetic regions by summarizing them into 215 

representative markers. The correlation threshold and the number of markers retained are indicated in the  Table 216 

S1 .217 

The identification of relevant markers using the BVCM has been  conducted  in two steps in order to optimize the 218 

results reliability: 219 

- 1st  step: the MCMC algorithm was run for 10,000 iterations after a burn -in of 20,000 iterations, a thinning 220 

of 5, and for 100 repetitions. The chain convergence was checked by plotting the trace plot of the residual 221 

variance (as a proxy of global convergence). The MCMC samples were compiled and the mean of 222 

posterior marginal probabilities of variable inclusion were computed. The markers with the highest 223 

marginal probabilities were selected to define a subset which was used in the second round. The threshold 224 

of selection and the number of markers are indicated in the  Table S1 .225 

-226 

- 2nd  step: the marker subset was used to run again the MCMC algorithm for 10,000 iterations after a burn -227 

in of 20,000 iterations, a thinning of 5, and for 100 repetitions. As in the first step, the chain convergence 228 

was checked by plotting the trace plot of the residual variance. The MCMC samples were compiled and 229 

the mean of posterior marginal probabilities of variable inclusion were computed. Two thresholds were 230 

used to select the significant markers according to their inclusion marginal probabilities. The first 231 

threshold was 0.8 if a set of markers was available at this threshold. If not, the second threshold of 0.4 232 

was considered to select significant markers  (Table S1) .233

## 4. Discussion 452 

Contrasted datasets were used to assess the performances and  the  advantages of the recently proposed BVCM 453 

model for analy zing the genetic determinism of time -series phenotypes  for  multiple  traits/ species. The BLINK 454 

method, known for reducing false discovery rates in GWAS  (Huang  et al. , 2018) , was applied in a time by time 455 

approach for each time series. BLINK  results were then compared with those obtained with BVCM. Across these 456 

different contexts, BVCM successfully identified major QTLs  already  detected by BLINK while also uncovering 457 

additional QTLs with more punctual or moderate effects over time. These newly identified QTLs allowed to 458 

increase the genetic PVE. The robustness and broad applicability of BVCM make it particularly well -suited for 459 

detecting QTLs associated with dynamic phenotypes , taking advantage of a multilocus approach. 460 

## 461 

4.1. Highly multidimensional analysis  to increase the QTL detection power 462 

The BVCM approach identified larger sets of markers compared to the BLINK stepwise approach. For almost all 463 

species, the markers selected by the stepwise approach were included in these sets and corresponded to markers 464 

with the highest effects. These results confirmed the ability of BCVM to identify additional markers with 465 

potentially biological relevance that can be attributed to two key factors. 466 

First, the analysis of all time points, while accounting for the underlying temporal structure, enhanced the 467 

statistical power by increasing the number of observations relative to the number of parameters. This is especially 468 

useful  when  narrow -sense heritability evolves over time  (Qu  et al. , 2022) . Indeed, as observed in  S. cerevisiae 469 

and in  F. graminearum , the analysis of traits with increasing heritability over time helps to identify/estimate non -470 

null markers with weak effects at the beginning of the follow -up. With the BVCM method, as we are evaluating 471 

a time -series phenotype, we can rely on the trajectory of the phenotype and on periods of high h² to detect weak 472 

signals that would not have been distinguishable from noise during periods of low h²  (Manolio  et al. , 2009) .473 

However, we observed that the differences between the two approaches are less pronounced when the temporal 474 

structure or/and the narrow -sense heritability are weak  as observed for the diameter times series form the 475 

Eucalyptus data set .476 

Second, the simultaneous analysis of all markers using a Bayesian variable selection approach enables the 477 

identification of markers with weaker effects  (Hoggart et al. , 2008; Xu et al. , 2018c; Adhikari et al. , 2023; Osorio -478 

Guarin et al. , 2024) . Based on the “omnigenic model” of complex traits, despite weak effect variants spread across 479 

the genome have no clear function for traits variability, they can impact the regulatory networks of the trait and 480 

explain a significant part of h²  (Boyle  et al. , 2017) . While they can explain a large part of the phenotypic variance, 481 

the detection of QTL with weak effects represent a challenge in  linkage analysis  and association  studies  because 482 

it is hard to statistically distinguish true weak signals from noise  (Yang  et al. , 2010; Shi  et al. , 2016; Boyle  et al. ,483 

2017) . Thus, a special attention has been paid to weak effect variants in GWAS mainly for human diseases because 484 

a high number of them can significantly increase risk factors  (Vsevolozhskaya  et al. , 2019) . For instance,  Wu  et 485 

al.  (2014)  proposed the HC -methods which allowed them to detect signals for very rare and weak effect variants 486

by considering them in clusters (SNP -set) of linked variant s. They were able to distinguish genetic true weak 487 

signals from noise associated with Crohn’s disease in GWAS.  Vsevolozhskaya  et al.  (2019)  proposed a method 488 

of  Augmented  Rank  Truncation (ART) in order to analyze the top ranking SNP and select the best set of markers. 489 

They were able to select variants that would not have passed the significance threshold with p -value evaluation 490 

after GWAS. Other screening methods have also been proposed in order to detect weak effect variants when 491 

GWAS approaches lacked power such as false negative control  (Jeng  et al. , 2024) . Here, because we calculated 492 

the posterior marginal probabilities of association in the Bayesian context, we  could  select the most appropriate 493 

threshold for variants consideration and detect weak effect variants without supplemental statistical evaluation. 494 

Our study also demonstrated that the Bayesian approaches, commonly used in genomic prediction, represent 495 

powerful  alternative s in QTL mapping with a great flexibility in terms of integrating structure from various 496 

sources , such as  known  genetic relationships or temporal phenotype structures  through variance covariance matrix 497 

(Heuclin  et al. , 2021; Qu  et al. , 2022) .498 

## 499 

4. 2. Two -steps to improve  statistical accuracy 500 

In this study, we proposed a two -step approach for identifying relevant markers. The first step, which consisted 501 

in running 100 MCMC algorithms, allowed to handle the multi -collinearity within the marker dataset and 502 

identified genetic regions with high probability of association. The second step further refined the results by 503 

reducing the number of variables.  This two -step approach made it possible to reduce the trade -off between the 504 

number of parameters and the number of observations, and thus to identify a set of promising genetic markers .505 

Two  steps have been used in previous  studies using  Bayesian model s for GWAS.  For instance the BGWAS 506 

(Bayesian GWAS)  model  is based on a first step of variables screening in order to select a set of  promising  genetic 507 

markers  (Williams  et al. , 2023) . A second step select s the more  relevant markers in the set. Th is  approach allowed 508 

to  reduce the false positives detection in  BGWAS.  Yazdani & Dunson (2015)  used a similar procedure with a first 509 

screening of promising SNPs by a Bayesian method, each SNP being evaluated individually. The second step 510 

consisted in a hierarchical model to simultaneously analyze the effect of all the promising SNP  in the  set . This 511 

method led to higher performances  in  genomic prediction and reduced the computational cost in a simulated data 512 

set. In a different way,  Li  et al.  (2011)  started by transforming the  phenotypic values  through supervised PCA in 513 

order to catch the overall genetic structure and reduce noise in data. The second step consisted in a hierarchical 514 

Bayesian model with  a Lasso regression selection method  (Tibshirani, 1996) . The  two steps approach led to an 515 

increase in  results  stability  and brought new insights into the genetic architecture of the human body mass index. 516 

In the BVCM approach, the use of  two  steps  made it easier to identify genetic markers  truly  associated with the 517 

time series variability.  To avoid the need for a preliminary filtering step that removes highly correlated molecular 518 

markers, future research could improve the BVCM approach by incorporating both genomic structuration of 519 

markers and temporal structuration directly into the selection prior .520

4. 3. Genetic dynamics  of phenotype establishment 523 

Compared to the BLINK model, the BVCM combined a multivariate varying coefficient model with Bayesian 524 

variable selection approach, allowing to catch fine temporal genetic dynamics. Across the studied contexts, 525 

heritabilit ies  exhibited temporal variation over time, with a slight increase in  Eucalyptus  (from 0.14 to 0.20) which 526 

was more pronounced in  S. cerevisiae  (from 0.49 to 0.79). The contribution of each genetic region in the 527 

determinism of trait dynamics was also variable over time , such as Sc16_878259  of  S. cerevisiae  being able to 528 

explain from 5.4% to 21.9% of the CO 2 release  PVE.  These observations can be related to different biological 529 

processes  for which  several underlying  genetic determin ants  were involved and occurred  in sequence over time. 530 

For instance in  Arabidopsis thaliana , the development and maintenance of certain phenotypes are regulated by 531 

the CLAVATA -WUSCHEL pathway , which can either  maintain the apical meristem  for plant growth  or transform 532 

the apical meristem into inflorescence meristem for seed production  over time  and environments  (Mayer  et al. ,533 

1998; Schoof  et al. , 2000) . In barley, genes have been detected for being associated with specific stages of grain 534 

filling  (Du  et al. , 2019) . In  sticklebacks, the ontogeny of the body size is mediated by different genetic regions 535 

over stages  (Fraimout  et al. , 2022) . Thus, some ontogenic  stages may represent key shifts in gene expression and 536 

cannot be observed in a one -time analyze.  Therefore , identifying  additional  genetic  regions that influence 537 

phenotype development over time could provide new opportunities for selection in breeding programs . The study 538 

of longitudinal data also allowed us to observe h² dynamics over time, which could reveal critical windows of 539 

high h²  that  should be focused  to enhance  breeding programs. 540 

The BVCM , by integrating the time and genetic structures of data sets,  allowed to increase the number of loci 541 

detected compared to BLINK. According to the Fisher infinitesimal model  (Fisher, 1919) , complex quantitative 542 

traits are supposed to be controlled by many genetic regions with small effects. By identifying  a higher number 543 

of  genetic regions, we were able to explain a larger part of the h² over time. Because a higher number of loci were 544 

considered for estimating the PVE, the PVE calculated for each individual marker decreased when calculated in 545 

the BVCM cluster compared to BLINK cluster. It suggest s that considering only the markers with the highe st 546 

genetic  effect s not only overlook s a part of the genetic variance  (Manolio  et al. , 2009)  but also lead s to 547 

overestimat ing  the PVE of selected markers. In addition to the additive variance significantly  explained b y548 

markers, the genetic architecture of complex traits is  expected to account for variants int eractions such as epistatic 549 

or dominance effects  (Holland, 2007; Mackay, 2009) . Multilocus approaches can detect clusters of markers with 550 

potential epistasis interactions  (Bocianowski, 2014)  which can have a significant impact on the genetic 551 

determinism of complex quantitative traits  (Phillips, 2008; Mackay, 2009) . By considering all markers in a 552

(Scarcelli  et al. , 2007) , for  their role in modulating the heterosis effect in hybrids  (Sang  et al. , 2022) , and  for  their 555 

ability to explain a part of the missing heritability  (Slim  et al. , 2020; Balvert  et al. , 2024)  by accounting for a non -556 

additive part of the genetic variance  (Mäki -Tanila & Hill, 2014) . Our approach account for genetic interactions 557 

but identif ying  the  genetic regions interacting  or  evaluating  the magnitude of  such  effects remains challenging to 558 

measure and clearly identify  (Tyler et al. , 2009; Hill, 2010; Sakai et al. , 2021; Ponte -Fernandez et al. , 2022) .559

## 576 

4. 4. Time shaping of phenotypic plasticity across diverse organisms 577 

Weak effect variants can represent significant  elements of  the time related phenotypic plasticity by their 578 

contribution to the variance and their interactions  (pleiotropy, epistasis, and development)  at the genetic level.  For 579 

instance,  Peiffer  et al.  (2014)  compared several models for the genetic architecture of maize plant height and the 580 

more polygenic (GBLUP) allowed the best prediction for the trait. Still in maize, the flowering time was 581 

genetically controlled by a large number of weak effect loci instead of few major QTL s (Buckler  et al. , 2009) .582 

Small effect variants w ere  also  involved in  the genetic architecture of human traits such as  human face  (Liu  et al. ,583 

2012; Hallgrimsson  et al. , 2014; Adhikari  et al. , 2016) . The consideration of traits dynamics in combination with 584 

multilocus analysis is meaningful in this context because it  increases  the opportunity to detect  weak  effect genetic 585 

variants .586 

Developmental timing is essential for understanding phenotype plasticity.  Conith  et al.  (2021)  showed in the 587 

context of skull bone development in a  fish  progeny of cichlids that the first bones to form impact the formation 588 

of subsequent bones and confuse the relationships between phenotype and genotype.  This work also showed that 589 

the developmental  sequence  can be  constrained by functional timing because the time related phenotypic plasticity 590 

of early forming traits  influences the establishment of subsequent traits.  The study of  branchial bone length 591 

variation  over time in  sticklebacks  revealed  loci  associated with  multiple  development al  stages whereas  the effect 592 

of  other loci were  restricted  to juvenile or adult stage  (Erickson  et al.,  2014) . It allowed them to better understand 593 

the genetic architecture of phenotype establishment and the underlying evolutionary processes.  The analysis of 594 

the genetic architecture of time series phenotypes , which can be related to functional or developmental plasticity, 595 

allow ed  to understand trait relationships, potential trade -off over time and the underlying genetic dynamics. 596
