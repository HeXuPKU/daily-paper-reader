Abstract 14 

Computational tools , including biological language models (LMs) , show substantial promise 15 

in predicting the impact of genetic variants on plant fitness. However, validating variant 16 

effect predictions (VEP) requires experimental populations where genetic variation consists17 

of discrete point mutations rather than segregating recombination blocks. In this study, we 18 

generated a novel population of Brachypodium distachyon mutant lines to evaluate the 19 

accuracy of VEP at single -base resolution. These lines were advanced through single -seed 20 

descent for five generations (M1 to M 5), with whole -genome sequencing performed at M2 and 21 

M5 and phenotypic measurements recorded at M3 and M4. Using state -of -the -art VEP 22 

models, we predicted the functional impact of missense protein -coding variants and gene -23 

proximal non -coding variants . We validated these predictions by estimating the effect of 24 

mutations on whole -plant measurements (burden tests) and their probability of fixation from 25 

M2 to M 5 (purging tests). Among missense variants , the protein LM ESM showed superior 26 

predictive accuracy compared to the bioinformatic standard SIFT and the genomic LM 27 

PlantCAD. Notably , the relationship between VEP scores and allele fixation suggested a log -28 

linear relationship between VEP scores and variant fitness. Among gene -proximal variants, 29 

PlantCAD appeared more accurate than supervised model s of regulatory activity , such as 30 

chromatin accessibility (a2z) and RNA abundance (PhytoExpr) . Collectively , our findings 31 

highlight the utility of state -of -the -art VEP tools as predictors of fitness and demonstrate the 32 

potential of mutant populations to evaluate computational tools for precision breeding 33 

applications. 34

INTRODUCTION 39 

In plant breeding, recent computational advances in variant effect prediction (VEP) provide a40 

unique opportunity for the efficient identification of impactful mutations as targets for 41 

selection or genome editin g (1) . Of particular interest is the prediction of variant effects on 42 

plant fitness, which allows breeders to target alleles with potential benefits for traits such as 43 

crop yield and resilience (2) . Among the most promising developments in this area are 44 

sequence -based deep learning techniques , specifically biological language models (LMs) and 45 

sequence -to -function models. 46 

Biological LMs evaluate the fitness effects of point mutations by estimating their likelihood 47 

within a specific protein or DNA sequence context (3, 4) . ESM (Evolutionary Scale 48 

Modeling ) has emerged as one of the most effective protein LMs for scoring the fitness 49 

consequences of protein -coding variants (3, 5) . Despite being trained on a massive cross -50 

species corpus (UniRef90), ESM has demonstrated high VEP accuracy in studies focused on 51 

human and in vitro assays (6 –8) . Notably, ESM has consistently outperformed SIFT (Sorting 52 

Intolerant from Tolerant), a traditional bioinformatic tool that relies on multiple sequence 53 

alignments (MSAs) to assess evolutionary conservation of amino acid residues (9) .54 

Genomic LMs extend fitness effects predictions beyond protein -coding regions to include 55 

non -coding variants (4) . Unlike protein LMs, which analyze whole protein sequences, 56 

genomic LMs typically predict variant effects within a fixed -length DNA sequence window. 57 

PlantCaduceus ( PlantCAD ) is a prominent example; trained on the genomes of 16 58 

angiosperm species, it has proven effective at VEP in both coding and non -coding regions, as 59 

validated by allele frequency data across diverse accessions (10) . Genomic LMs hold 60 

significant potential for VEP, because they are inherently versatile and , critically, do not 61 

require gene annotations to score variants .62 

A distinct category of sequence -based deep learning involves sequence -to -function models, 63 

which use DNA sequences to predict molecular phenotypes such as chromatin accessibility 64 

(11) or RNA abundance (12) . The regulatory effect s of variants can be predicted by 65 

chromatin -state models like a2z, which was trained on ATAC -seq data from 12 angiosperm s66 

to predict the impact of DNA variants on chromatin accessibility in leaf tissues (11) . While 67 

chromatin accessibility is a secondary proxy for fitness , it has been shown to affect gene 68 

regulation (13) and frequently co -occur with causal variants (14 –16) .69

The regulatory impact of variants can also be assessed via sequence -to -expression models. 70 

Although benchmark studies in humans suggest that chromatin -state models may currently 71 

be more effective for VEP than sequence -to -expression models (17) , it remains unclear 72 

whether the same is true in plants. In plants , the recent PhytoExpr model (12)  has achieved 73 

high accuracy in predicting mRNA abundance from cis -regulatory sequences across 17 74 

species. Similar to previous architectures (18, 19) , PhytoExpr focuses on gene -flanking 75 

sequence s, specifically analyzing sequences spanning 4 kb upstream of the transcription start 76 

site (TSS) to 1 kb downstream, and 1 kb upstream of the transcription termination site (TTS) 77 

to 4 kb downstream. As demonstrated by studies of chromatin accessibility across 78 

angiosperms, these ranges are expected to capture the majority of cis -regulatory features 79 

(15) .80 

Despite the rapid development of VEP techniques , their empirical validation for predicting 81 

fitness effects in plants still lags behind (1) . Most current VEP benchmarks in this field rely 82 

on natural accessions (10, 20, 21) , where association s between variant s and phenotypes are 83 

confounded by extensive linkage disequilibrium (LD) and historical selection (22, 23) .84 

Furthermore, few studies have systematically compared the performance of LMs and 85 

sequence -to -function models within the same experimental context. To address these 86 

limitations, we developed the SIEVE ( Selection of mutations by in silico and experimental 87 

variant effects ) population through untargeted mutagenesis in Brachypodium distachyon .88 

Untargeted mutagenesis provides a unique opportunity to observe the phenotypic impacts of 89 

point mutations with minimal confounding from LD, provided that the mutagenized lines 90 

remain independent (i.e., they have shared no common haplotypes since the mutagenesis 91 

event). While mutant populations have previously been generated in B. distachyon and 92 

related taxa, these have primarily been used for candidate -variant analysis by targeted 93 

genotyping ( e.g., TILLING) (24 –26) . To our knowledge, none have been employed for the 94 

systematic validation of VEP models. In this study, we used sodium azide (NaN 3) to generate 95 

point mutations – predominantly G -to -A and C -to -T transitions – and evaluated their effects 96 

across independent lines derived from a single seed source. This experimental design enabled 97 

us to assess the impact of variants prioritized by VEP techniques at single -base resolution 98 

rather than LD -block resolution in natural populations. 99 

We conducted our study in the model grass B. distachyo n because of decisive ad vantages: its 100 

compact genome (27 2 Mb) , small stature, naturally selfing habit, and rapid generation time 101 

(27) , as well as its close phylo genetic proximity to major cereal crops like wheat, barley, and 102

Material and methods 111 

Plant material 112 

Seeds from Brachypodium distachyon Bd21 -3 were produced by two generations of selfing 113 

from a unique seed (provided by R. Sibout, INRAE). At the M 1 generation, four batches of 114 

seeds were sown. In each batch, 2080 seeds were exposed to 7 mM of NaN 3, while 104 115 

control seeds were exposed to the same solution with no mutagen. Treated seeds were then 116 

sown in trays in the greenhouse (20h/4h light/dark, 21°C/18°C target temperature, at Aarhus 117 

University Flakkebjerg). Successfully grown seedlings were then transferred to pots: 1808 118 

mutants and 96 controls at generation M 1. Pots from each batch were arranged in two 119 

‘blocks’, each of which corresponded to one half of a table (eight blocks and 4 tables used to 120 

grow all plants at M 1). The physical location of blocks changed from one generation to the 121 

next, but plants were assigned to the same blocks across generations. 122 

At each generation from M 1 to M 5, plants were allowed to self -fertilize naturally to produce 123 

the next generation. From generation M 2 to M 5, seeds from each plant line were pre -stored 124 

for one week at 37°C and 20% relative humidity, to improve and synchronize germination 125 

(Barrero et al., 2012) . At generations M 2 and M 5, leaf samples were collected from each plant 126 

line at the juvenile stage (about 5 weeks after sowing). At generations M 3 and M 4, each plant 127 

was phenotyped for the following traits: plant height (distance between the base of the plant 128 

and the tallest spike), germination rate (number of seeds that germinated out of 5), seed 129 

weight (total weight of harvested seeds) , and heading date (number of days until about half of 130 

tillers reach stage 50 “First spikelet of inflorescence just visible” on the Zadoks scale) (29) .131

DISCUSSION 481 

Validating predicted variant effects in mutant populations 482 

In this study, we developed the SIEVE population specifically to evaluate variant effect 483 

predictions at base -pair resolution. To this end, we proposed different statistical analyses to 484 

validate the impact of candidate variants : (i) gene depletion analyses to identify gene classes 485 

under selection; (ii ) burden tests to quantify the association between mutation loads 486 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715642 doi: bioRxiv preprint

(prioritized by VEP) and phenotypes; and (iii ) purging tests to establish the relationship 487 

between VEP scores and the relative fixation probability of mutant alleles. 488 

While previous studies in crops such as maize, potato, and cassava have successfully detected 489 

associations between predicted variant effects and field traits (20, 41 –43) , those 490 

investigations relied on natural accessions. In such diverse populations, extensive physical 491 

linkage – spanning more than 10 kb – often confound s the resolution of variant -trait 492 

associations, making it difficult to demonstrate VEP accuracy at the level of individual 493 

variants. Furthermore, accessions in these studies are typically genotyped by mapping reads 494 

to a single reference genome. This approach introduces significant reference bias, as loci 495 

unique to diverse genomic backgrounds are omitted if they are absent from the reference 496 

assembly (44, 45) . The SIEVE population addresse d both of these limitations (LD -block 497 

resolution and reference bias) by generating genetically independent mutant lines derived 498 

from a single, high -quality reference genotype (Bd21 -3). Unlike existing mutant resources in 499 

species like Brachypodium, barley, wheat, or maize (24, 26, 46, 47) , the genomic and 500 

phenotypic data acquired across generations in SIEVE provided the necessary resources for 501 

validation and calibration of VEP models. Specifically , our data enabled the implementation 502 

of burden and purging tests , which allowed us to compare predicted variant effects with 503 

realized impacts on plant fitness. 504 

Crucially, the deep -learning VEP models evaluated here were trained in multiple species 505 

across the tree of life (ESM) or the angiosperm clade (a2z, PhytoExpr, PlantCAD), rather 506 

than specifically on Brachypodium (5, 10 –12) . Consequently, the successful validation of 507 

these models in the SIEVE population should support their broad application across related 508 

taxa, particularly in crops closely related to Brachypodium, like wheat , barley , and rice .509 

Our gene depletion analyses at the M2 generation proved effective to identify genes involved 510 

in essential functions. However, we did not observe significant deviations in fixation 511 

probabilities across gene classes from M2 to M5. This lack of signal likely stems from 512 

insufficient statistical power to detect small -effect variants within specific gene categories. 513 

Future efforts aiming to resolve this issue will require even larger populations of mutant 514 

lines. Additionally, the observed lack of selection pressure on specific gene classes may be 515 

attributed to our focus on fitness under non -stressful growth conditions  (48) . Applying 516 

specific biotic or abiotic stressors (e.g., drought, pathogen pressure) in future studies could 517 

amplify the selection pressure on genes of interest and facilitate the identification of variants 518 

involved in targeted stress -tolerance traits .519

Predicting fitness effects of variants with biological language models 520 

Consistent with previous benchmarks of variant pathogenicity in humans (6, 49) , biological 521 

LMs proved particularly accurate for predicting variant effects in this study. Among these, 522 

the protein LM ESM demonstrated higher predictive accuracy than the genomic LM 523 

PlantCAD in both burden and purging tests for missense variants. Consequently, according to524 

our validations, protein LMs currently represent the most promising tool for VEP , with 525 

outstanding potential in research and breeding applications. Nevertheless, genomic LMs offer 526 

distinct advantages that make them particularly attractive for VEP. 527 

First, genomic LMs are inherently more versatile, as they can predict the effects of non -528 

coding mutations. In our study, low PlantCAD scores for gene -proximal variants were 529 

significantly associated with negative impacts on both germination rate (Fig. 4b) and overall 530 

fitness (Fig. 5b), highlighting the model's utility for identifying deleterious variants outside of 531 

protein -coding regions. Second, their predictions are independent of transcript annotation 532 

quality. The reliance of protein LMs on accurate annotations can be a significant hurdle when 533 

working with de novo genome assemblies or non -model species where high -quality 534 

transcriptomes are unavailable. As de novo assemblies become increasingly common in plant 535 

genomics (50) , ‘annotation -free ’ VEP techniques like PlantCAD will likely become essential. 536 

Despite key advantages , genomic LMs still face specific challenges, particularly when 537 

applied to functionally heterogeneous variant sets in non -coding regions. Both burden and 538 

purging tests revealed that positive PlantCAD scores of gene -proximal variants did not 539 

correlate with beneficial fitness effects. Future research should investigate whether data 540 

curation practices during pre -training influence d this lack of directionality. For example, 541 

genomic LMs such as GPN and PlantCAD are often pre -trained on datasets where non -542 

coding regions and repetitive sequences are down -sampled or down -weighted (1, 10, 21) .543 

While these practices are intended to mitigate genomic biases, their specific impact on VEP 544 

accuracy in non -coding regions has yet to be established. 545 

Implementing variant effect predictio n in precision breeding 546 

Our aim in this study was to evaluate VEP models for their deployment in crop improvement. 547 

The successful validation of models like ESM should motivate its application for both 548 

genomic prediction and genome editing. 549 

In genomic prediction, modeling the load of deleterious mutations is a promising strategy to 550 

select individuals with enhanced fitness (20, 41, 51) . Future prediction model s could leverage 551

our insight into the relationship between VE P scores and fitness -related traits to estimate the552 

effect of variants on key breeding traits. Our burden tests indicated that missense variants 553 

with an ESM score below the 10 th percentile (threshold: -8.7) exerted a significantly more 554 

deleterious impact on plant height and total seed weight (Fig. 4) . This threshold aligns with 555 

the value of -7 proposed previously (6, 52) , which could serve as a standard criterion for 556 

identifying a subset of putatively deleterious variants to be upweighted in genomic s-assisted 557 

selection . Alternatively, breeders might infer a mathematical function to relate VEP scores to 558 

variant effects on fitness-related traits. Our purging tests suggest that the relationship between 559 

missense VEP scores (e.g., ESM or PlantCAD) and fitness is best modeled on a log -scale, as 560 

evidenced by the linear relationship between these scores and the logarithm of allele fitness. 561 

Our results provide a hypothesis about the relationship between VEP scores and variant562 

effects, which remains to be tested in further studies on realized phenotypic effects under 563 

field conditions .564 

Beyond genomic prediction, VEP scores may also be used to prioritize specific variants for 565 

targeted mutagenesis and ge nome editing . Our findings demonstrate that state -of -the -art VEP 566 

models can detect genetic effects at the resolution of individual mutations, suggesting they 567 

could be applied for target ing beneficial alleles. In such applications, breeders may prioritize 568 

‘back mutations’ , that is, variants revert ing derived, deleterious alleles back to their ancestral 569 

state (53) . Natural candidates for this ‘targeted purging ’ of deleterious variants are DNA 570 

transitions (G:C -to -A:T or A:T -to -G:C) , for two primary reasons: first, G:C -to -A:T mutations 571 

were the predominant class of variants in duced in this study (Fig. 2) ; and second, cytidine (C -572 

to -T) and adenosine (A -to -G) base editors are currently the most advanced base -editing tools 573 

in crops (54, 55) . Notably, recent work has successfully used SIFT scores to target and fix a 574 

deleterious variant in tomato (56) . Proof -of -concept studies leveraging more advanced VEP 575 

models, such as ESM or PlantCAD, will be essential to determine the efficiency of in silico 576 

variant prioritization for targeted purging. In particular, future studies in diverse crop s are 577 

needed to estimate the proportion of variants leading to detectable impacts on traits of 578 

interest, among those prioritized by sequence -based deep learning techniques. 579
