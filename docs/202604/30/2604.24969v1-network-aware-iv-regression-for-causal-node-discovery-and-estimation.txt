# Network-aware IV Regression for Causal Node Discovery and Estimation 

## Samhita Pal Department of Biotatistics, Vanderbilt University Medical Center and Dhrubajyoti Ghosh Department of Biostatistics and Bioinformatics, Duke University April 29, 2026 

Abstract 

Estimating causal effects from high-dimensional, structured exposures is a fun-damental challenge in modern applications ranging from neuroscience and finance to environmental science. While the literature has addressed high-dimensional in-strumental variable (IV) regression, and separately leveraged graph structure in penalized regression, the integration of both, especially for causal support recovery in the presence of latent confounding, remains unexplored. In this work, we propose a novel two-stage regression framework that incorporates instrumental variables and graph-based regularization to uncover sparse causal effects among network-structured exposures. Our method accommodates both valid and partially invalid instruments, and encourages structural similarity among connected predictors through a graph-fused penalty. We establish non-asymptotic guarantees for estimation accuracy and causal variable selection, and demonstrate that our approach yields improved performance over existing methods that ignore network dependencies or invalid IVs. Applied to ADNI brain imaging and genetic data, our method identifies interpretable causal ROIs associated with cognitive outcomes, underscoring the utility of graph-assisted IV regression in neuroscience and beyond. 

Keywords: Instrumental Variables, Graph-constrained LASSO, Causality, High-dimension, ADNI 1

# 1 Introduction 

Network -structured covariates encode prior knowledge that can sharpen high - dimensional analyses. Most existing methods use this structure associatively, borrowing strength across connected features to improve prediction or variable selection without asking whether altering those features would change the outcome. Our focus is causal: we seek to determine which network -linked predictors causally drive the response by combining network regularization with instrumental variable (IV) identification to move from correlation to causation. Li et al. (2019) developed a general framework for exploiting network cohesion, the tendency of linked individuals to behave similarly, in a wide range of prediction problems and showed how to add information from interaction networks to boost both in -sample fit and out -of -sample prediction. They showed finite -sample MSE bounds, consistency under some conditions as well as described when their approach beats OLS or mixed -effects baselines under explicit bias-variance trade-off conditions. Along the same lines, Le & Li (2022) proposed a regression framework that captures network effects non -parametrically while making no assumption of perfect network measurement, and offered provable robustness to perturbations in the relational data. Similarly, Zhou & Müller (2022) introduced the network -based logistic regression model that simultaneously explains (i) each node’s binary label via a standard logistic link and (ii) the probability two nodes are connected via a second logistic link that depends on their labels and a similarity score between their predictor vectors. Motivated by this “network-as-information” principle, we flip the perspective: instead of relationships among samples, we exploit known or data-driven relationships among predictors, for example, biological pathways, functional brain networks, or textual word embeddings in order to guide estimation. By encouraging coefficients of connected features to be learned jointly, our method aims to deliver similar benefits in settings where structure 2resides in the covariates themselves, providing a principled route for harnessing domain knowledge to improve prediction and inference whenever such links are available. Li & Li (2008) tackle the problem of leveraging known network among predictor, specifically biological networks to improve variable selection and prediction in high -dimensional genomic regressions. They propose a network -constrained regularization for linear models that combines an ℓ1 penalty with a smoothness term defined by the graph Laplacian of the predictor network, thereby encouraging sparsity and similar coefficients for linked genes without forcing them to be identical. Their method inherits efficient coordinate -descent solvers and can accommodate high-dimensional covariates. Weber et al. (2023) improves over Li & Li (2008) by introducing the Covariate Coefficient and Connection Sign Estimation (3CoSE) algorithm that removes the restriction that all edges are positive by jointly estimating the regression coefficients and the unknown edge signs in an iterative two -stage loop, repeating until convergence. The Sparse Laplacian Shrinkage estimator Huang et al. (2011) combines the minimax -concave penalty (MCP) with a graph -Laplacian quadratic, where the MCP term enforces sparsity while remaining nearly unbiased, and the Laplacian term fuses coefficients of connected predictors. Several Bayesian methods have also been developed to address the problem of inference and model selection from network-valued covariates. For example, in latent scale Gaussian process regression (ls -GPR) by Ma et al. (2022), the observed adjacency matrix is recast through a logistic latent -space model whose vectors uk generate every edge probability, while in the Bayesian Network -Shrinkage (BNS) prior proposed by Guha & Rodriguez (2021) each edge coefficient is shrunk toward the inner product of analogous latent vectors. Both these methods aim at identifying influential nodes: ls -GPR places Bernoulli-gamma priors on kernel length -scales so that posterior inclusion probabilities reveal which nodes drive the Gaussian -process mean function, whereas BNS turns entire node vectors on or off through Bernoulli indicators that simultaneously zero 3out all incident edge effects. Guha & Rodriguez (2023) have dealt with the classification problem under high-dimensional network covariates using global-local shrinkage priors. While these approaches borrow strength across network-linked covariates, they are purely associational: selected nodes are those that best predict the outcome conditional on other variables, not those whose manipulation would cause a change in the outcome. To our knowledge, no existing method provides formal causal identification or inference for node effects when the covariates themselves form a network. By contrast, the causal -inference literature on social -network data addresses a different axis of dependence, that is, interference and contagion among units. Work on causal influence scores, chain -graph parameterisations, semiparametric peer -effect estimators, and Bayesian exponential -family random -network models all seeks to estimate the impact of treating one individual on the outcomes of others connected through the social graph (Ogburn et al. 2020, Lee et al. 2023, Ogburn et al. 2024, Clark & Handcock 2024). In these settings the network lives in the observation dimension, not among the predictors. Our contribution bridges these two strands: we aim to identify and quantify the causal effects of network -linked predictors themselves, appending their known relational structure to strengthen estimation. Understanding which variables causally influence an outcome is a fundamental objective in many scientific fields, especially in the presence of complex, high-dimensional exposures and latent confounding. In several modern applications, not only are the exposures structured through domain-specific networks, but natural sources of IVs also exist (Chen et al. 2018), motivating the development of methods that integrate IVs with networks to uncover robust causal effects. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we describe our proposed network-assisted instrumental variable framework for high-dimensional causal inference, both in absence and presence of invalid IVs. Section 3 is dedicated to theoretical guarantees for estimation error and support recovery under valid (Section 3.1) and invalid IVs (Section 3.2). 4In Section 4, we again present two simulation setups imitating the theory section, one without (Section 4.1), and another with (Section 4.2) invalid IVs, in order to evaluate the performance of our method. Section 5 demonstrates our approach on a neuroimaging-genetics dataset from ADNI, identifying causal brain regions linked to cognitive decline. Finally, we conclude with a discussion of our contributions and potential extensions in Section 6.

## 2.1 Motivation 

In many scientific and applied domains, the exposures of interest, whether they be brain regions, financial assets, or environmental pollutants, are inherently structured and con-founded, making reliable causal effect estimation a formidable challenge. In neuroscience 5and brain health, researchers aim to uncover which brain regions (regions of interest, or ROIs) are causally linked to cognitive decline, neurodegenerative progression, or behavioral outcomes. These ROIs, measured via imaging biomarkers, form interconnected networks through anatomical or functional pathways, encoding spatial dependencies and shared bio-logical functions. At the same time, latent confounding due to age, lifestyle, or comorbidities undermines naïve associations. Genetic variants, such as single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) or polygenic risk scores, offer natural instruments as they influence brain morphology but are plausibly independent of unmeasured environmental factors, making this setting ideally suited for a network-assisted IV approach. A similar problem emerges in financial systems, where sector-linked assets, stocks or investment portfolios are interrelated through co-movement patterns, shared institutional holdings, or sector classifications. The goal here is to determine which financial exposures causally drive market-level outcomes like returns, volatility, or systemic risk, while accounting for common latent drivers such as macroeconomic trends or investor sentiment. Exogenous policy changes, interest rate shocks, or geopolitical disruptions offer plausible instruments that shift exposures without directly affecting idiosyncratic returns, thereby enabling causal attribution. Environmental science presents yet another parallel: identifying which pollutants causally impact ecological health or biodiversity, despite confounding from unmeasured socio-environmental variables. Pollu-tants often co-occur due to common emission sources such as industrial facilities or vehicular traffic, thereby giving rise to a meaningful graph among exposures. Meanwhile, regulatory interventions or meteorological shifts act as instrumental perturbations. A similar opportu-nity arises in systems chemistry and molecular biology, where the goal is to isolate chemical substructures or metabolites that causally impact biological or phenotypic outcomes such as toxicity or therapeutic response. Here, exposures correspond to high-dimensional molecular features, often structured via reaction pathways or chemical similarity networks. Exogenous perturbations such as randomized compound libraries, CRISPR-based knockouts (Dixit et al. 62016), or catalyst changes can act as valid instruments, while graph-based regularization respects known molecular interactions, enhancing interpretability and robustness in causal discovery. Across all these domains, the exposures form structured networks that encode meaningful relationships, and there exist external perturbations (valid or partially valid IVs) that can help disentangle causal effects. A method that simultaneously leverages graph structure among exposures in presence of confounding and instrumental variation, while remaining robust to latent confounding and invalid instruments, is therefore not only methodologically timely but also indispensable for causal discovery in a wide range of real-world systems. 

## 2.2 Network Penalization under All Valid IVs 

We propose a two-stage IV regression framework to estimate the causal effect of high-dimensional network-valued exposures on an outcome of interest, using IVs. Our setup allows both high-dimensional exposures and high-dimensional IVs, and in addition, the exposures may be linked apriori. An instrumental variable must (i) influence the exposure, (ii) be otherwise unrelated to the outcome except through that exposure, and (iii) remain independent of any unmeasured confounders. The first stage uses penalized variable selection to identify relevant and valid IVs. The second stage performs regression of the predicted network exposure on the outcome, incorporating a graph-constrained LASSO (GL) penalty to respect known connectivity among predictors. ## 2.3 Correction for Invalid IVs 

In presence of high-dimensional IVs, often there is a breach of one or more of the three requirements of a valid instrument. This leads to invalidity of several of the IVs. In MR analysis, a fundamental assumption is that the genetic instruments affect the outcome 8exclusively through the exposure of interest. However, in practice, this exclusion restriction is often violated due to horizontal pleiotropy, where some genetic variants influence the outcome via alternative biological pathways unrelated to the targeted exposures. To address this, we incorporate a correction mechanism that explicitly models and penalizes direct effects of the instruments on the outcome. Our approach builds on the sisVIVE framework proposed by Kang et al. (2016), extending it to multivariate network exposures with structured graph constraints. Specifically, we model the outcome Yi ∈ R for subject i as depending on both the brain region intensities xi ∈ Rp and potentially invalid instruments Zi ∈ Rq through the equation:

When α̸ = 0, some instruments violate the exclusion restriction and are therefore invalid. To ensure identifiability of the causal effects β, we restrict attention to the component of the outcome that is explainable by the instruments. This is accomplished by projecting both sides of the model onto the column space of the instrument matrix Z, using the projection matrix PZ = Z(ZTZ)−ZT. This projection plays a critical role: it filters out variation in the outcome that lies outside the linear span of the instruments, which cannot be explained or mediated through the exposure and therefore cannot contribute to the identification of a causal relationship. While this might appear to discard potentially meaningful variation, it actually improves causal identifiability by ensuring that the estimation focuses solely on the part of the outcome that is instrumentable. This approach is conceptually aligned with the two-stage least squares (2SLS) method, where only the instrumented portion of the exposure is used to infer causality. # 3 Theory 

We develop theory for IVGL and IVGL-S. We first lay out design and curvature conditions ensuring identifiability despite first-stage noise and Laplacian smoothing; under these, Theorem 1 establishes oracle-rate ℓ2 consistency and Theorem 2 proves exact signed support recovery for ˆβIVGL . We then extend the analysis to the sisVIVE-augmented IVGL-S, where a restricted-isometry framework yields parallel error bounds (Theorem 3). Collectively, these results deliver statistically efficient estimation and reliable causal variable selection under endogeneity and network dependence.

## 4.2 Setup 2: Sparse invalid IVs 

Simulation studies were conducted to evaluate the performance of graph-constrained in-strumental variable regression (IVGL) and graph-constrained SISVIVE (IVGL-S) under settings reflecting neuroimaging applications with unmeasured confounding and sparse invalid instruments. The data generataing process is the same as Section 4.1. In addition, to model sparse violations of the exclusion restriction, the first 10 instruments were assigned direct effects on the outcome, with corresponding entries in the vector α0 ∈ Rq set to 5 and all others set to zero, ensuring the presence of sparse invalid instruments in the design. The confounder also directly influenced the outcome with a scalar loading γy, drawn uniformly from [0 .2, 0.7] and assigned a random sign. The outcome was generated according to Y = Xβ 0 + Zα 0 + γyU + ε, where ε contains independent standard normal entries. To incorporate anatomical or functional relationships among the exposures, a graph was constructed by assigning each exposure a random three-dimensional coordinate sampled 19 uniformly from [0 , 100] 3. Euclidean distances were computed between exposure pairs, and undirected edges were placed between pairs with distances less than 30 units, forming the adjacency matrix A. The unnormalized graph Laplacian L = D − A was computed, where 

D is the diagonal degree matrix. The signal strength multiplier si ∈ { 1, 3} controlled the magnitude of the nonzero entries of the exposure - outcome relationship, and the number of active exposures s0 ∈ { 4, 8, 12 } controlled the sparsity level. For each replicate, s0 exposures were selected to form a spatially contiguous cluster on the graph by selecting a seed node and expanding to neighboring nodes until the desired size was reached. The nonzero entries of β0 were drawn uniformly from [0 .5, 1] , scaled by si , and assigned random signs, with the remaining entries set to zero. For each (si , s 0) configuration, 100 replicates were performed. In each replicate, IVGL-S was implemented using an alternating procedure, iteratively estimating direct instrument effects via cross-validated LASSO regression of residualized outcomes on instruments and exposure effects via cross-validated graph-constrained LASSO regression on exposures using the graph Laplacian, iterating up to 30 times or until convergence. The IVGL method was implemented by regressing exposures on instruments using cross-validated LASSO to obtain fitted exposures, followed by graph-constrained LASSO regression of the outcome on fitted exposures using the Laplacian, with cross-validation to select tuning parameters.

# 5 Real Data Analysis 

Causal brain region of interest (ROI) selection for neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and Parkinson’s disease (PD) has garnered attention due to the complex nature of these neurodegenerative diseases. Traditional observational studies pose challenges, such as confounding factors and reverse causation, making causal inference difficult. To elucidate causal relationships, researchers are increasingly employing advanced methodologies like MR, which leverages genetic variants as IVs to investigate these diseases. Understanding which brain regions causally drive neurodegenerative diseases, thus, has the potential to be one of the most pressing and impactful goals in neuroscience. While 21 many studies identify correlations between structural changes in the brain and diseases like Alzheimer’s or Parkinson’s, correlation does not imply causation, and this gap limits both our biological understanding and clinical translation. We propose to use T1-weighted MRI, a widely available and non-invasive imaging modality, to quantify structural brain features across individuals. By leveraging genetic instruments, variants known to influence brain morphology through genome-wide association studies (GWAS), we can apply Mendelian Randomization to pinpoint causal ROIs and estimate whether changes in specific brain regions cause or merely reflect disease progression. A detailed discussion on the existing literature on causal analysis of neurodegenerative diseases has been presented in Supplement A. The only paper that has attempted to uncover causal brain regions for AD is the DeepFEIVR method introduced in Yao et al. (2023). However, being a CNN-based method, their selected causal regions do not immediately have any biological meaning. In this analysis, we leveraged baseline multimodal data from the Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) , a large-scale longitudinal study designed to identify and validate biomarkers for the early detection, progression tracking, and therapeutic evaluation of Alzheimer’s disease (AD) (Mueller et al. 2005, Weiner et al. 2017). ADNI is widely regarded as the reference cohort in neurodegenerative research due to its rich collection of neuroimaging, genetic, cognitive, and clinical data systematically collected under standardized protocols (Jack Jr et al. 2008, Petersen et al. 2010). For our investigation, we focused on the baseline visit to conduct a cross-sectional assessment of the relationship between brain structure and cognitive functioning. Subjects were categorized into three diagnostic groups according to ADNI’s standardized diagnostic criteria: Cognitively Normal (CN) , Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) , and Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) , based on cognitive evaluations, Clinical Dementia Rating (CDR) scales, and clinical adjudication (Petersen et al. 2010, McKhann et al. 2011). Our analytical cohort consisted of 151 CN participants, 22 231 MCI participants, and 102 AD participants, leading to a total sample size of 484 individuals, consistent with prior studies utilizing baseline ADNI data (Knutson et al. 2020, Yang et al. 2023). Unlike previous studies that predominantly model AD diagnosis as a binary or multinomial outcome (Knutson et al. 2020), we selected the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) score as our primary endpoint. MMSE is a validated, continuous, and quantitative measure of global cognitive function that ranges from 0 to 30, with lower scores reflecting more severe cognitive impairment (Tombaugh & McIntyre 1992), and has been shown to capture subtle gradations of cognitive decline more sensitively than categorical clinical labels (Petersen et al. 2010, Yao et al. 2023). Each participant’s brain morphology was characterized using high-resolution T1-weighted MRI scans, the standard imaging modality within ADNI for anatomical assessments (Jack Jr et al. 2010). Complementing the imaging data, we utilized genome-wide single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) genotype data, coded as minor allele dosages (0, 1, 2), obtained from the ADNI genetics core processed through rigorous quality control pipelines (Saykin et al. 2015). To ensure consistency and completeness, we restricted our analysis to participants with complete data on baseline T1-weighted MRI, genome-wide SNP genotyping, and MMSE scores. This resulted in a harmonized multi-modal cohort integrating 62 regional brain features derived from the 

Desikan-Killiany-Tourville (DKT) atlas (Klein & Tourville 2012) and common genetic variation across more than 20,000 SNPs per individual. More details on the MRI image data pre-processing steps may be found in the Supplement B. The QC steps of the genotype data have been detailed in Supplement C. Given the focus on brain structural phenotypes, we started with a comprehensive set of approximately 20,000 SNPs available after QC, reflecting genome-wide common variants. To further refine the instrument set and reduce the dimensionality of the genetic data, we implemented a Sure Independence Screening (SIS) procedure (Fan & Lv 2008), a widely 23 Figure 3: Orthogonal slice views (coronal, sagittal, axial) of baseline T1-weighted MRI scans from representative participants in the Cognitively Normal (CN, N=151), Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI, N=231), and Alzheimer’s Disease (AD, N=102) groups. Regions of interest (ROIs) are overlaid using the Desikan-Killiany-Tourville (DKT) atlas. adopted feature screening approach in high-dimensional statistical analysis. Specifically, for each SNP, we computed its marginal association with the imaging features by calculating the absolute correlation between each SNP and the average voxel intensities across the 62 DKT-defined regions. We then selected the top 300 SNPs exhibiting the highest absolute correlation values. This screening procedure ensures that we retain SNPs that are most relevant to the brain imaging phenotypes, which serve as intermediate exposures in our causal modeling framework, while mitigating the risk of overfitting and improving computational tractability. The final genetic feature matrix thus consisted of 300 SNPs per subject, serving as candidate IVs for the downstream causal analyses linking brain structure to cognitive performance. To disentangle associations from causal effects between regional brain morphology and cognitive performance, we propose a structured three-stage strategy combining network-regularized regression with instrumental variable (IV) methods. First, we use Graph-constrained Lasso to identify brain regions associated with MMSE by enforcing sparsity and anatomical coherence via a graph Laplacian penalty over the DKT-derived brain network (Li & Li 2008, Tibshirani 2011), yielding interpretable associative maps aligned 24 with neuroanatomical topology. However, such associations may be confounded or reversed causally (McKhann et al. 2011, Jack et al. 2013, Pal et al. 2025, Peddi & Ghosh 2025), motivating our second stage, IVGL, which integrates exogenous genetic instruments (the 300 SNPs selected via Sure Independence Screening) into a two-stage regression framework. In the first stage, each ROI is regressed on the selected SNPs using LASSO, and in the second, MMSE is regressed on the predicted imaging features using graph-regularized LASSO to retain anatomical structure. Recognizing the risk of pleiotropy and direct SNP effects on MMSE, we further employ IVGL-S, which jointly estimates both ROI and direct SNP effects via ℓ1 penalties. This final stage yields conservative, robust causal ROI selection even under sparse violations of the exclusion restriction. Together, GL, IVGL, and IVGL-S form a comprehensive causal discovery pipeline tailored to high-dimensional, network-structured neuroimaging data with genetic IVs. The list of all selected ROIs by the three methods are provided in Table 3. The application of the GL yielded a comprehensive set of associative brain regions signifi-cantly related to the MMSE score, capturing widespread cortical and subcortical structures bilaterally. The wide anatomical spread of selected regions is consistent with the known global effects of neurodegeneration on brain structure in Alzheimer’s disease, particularly in relation to MMSE which captures global cognitive function (Petersen et al. 2010, Jack Jr et al. 2010). The entorhinal cortex, selected bilaterally, is recognized as one of the earliest cortical regions to undergo neurofibrillary degeneration, closely correlating with memory decline and early cognitive deficits (Khan et al. 2014). The superior and transverse tem-poral gyri have been associated with language processing deficits and auditory processing abnormalities in AD (Frisoni et al. 2008, Wood et al. 2024). Cingulate subregions, including both caudal and rostral anterior cingulate and isthmus cingulate, are linked with attentional deficits and executive dysfunction in AD (Leech & Sharp 2014, Gu et al. 2025). # 6 Conclusion 

We introduced a novel framework for causal variable selection in high-dimensional regression models that simultaneously incorporates network structure among exposures and leverages instrumental variables to correct for latent confounding. By combining graph-structured penalties with IV-based regression, and further extending to settings with potentially invalid instruments, our method enables robust identification of causal predictors with improved support recovery and interpretability. The empirical results on both simulations and ADNI neuroimaging data highlight the advantage of borrowing strength across neighboring covari-ates through the underlying graph and showcase the method’s ability to yield anatomically coherent and domain-relevant causal discoveries. Theoretically, graph-regularized IV re-gression demonstrates estimation accuracy and also promotes stability in variable selection across correlated or spatially contiguous predictors, a feature particularly important in scientific domains such as neuroscience, environmental studies, and finance, under familiar high-dimensional assumptions. Looking forward, several promising directions emerge from this work. First, our current methodology is focused on cross-sectional data. A natural and impactful extension would 28 be to develop longitudinal graph-regularized IV regression models that account for temporal dynamics in exposures and outcomes, particularly for datasets like ADNI that provide repeated cognitive and imaging measurements over time. Such an approach could enable identification of causal biomarkers that not only affect baseline outcomes but also influence the rate of disease progression. Temporally smoothed graph penalties or dynamic graphs may further enhance this framework. Moreover, although we illustrated our method primarily through the neuroscience application, the core ideas generalize readily to other domains where exposures lie on known or inferred networks and confounding is a major concern. Environmental health (e.g., pollutant networks), econometrics (e.g., asset co-movement networks), and genomics (e.g., gene regulatory networks) are all fertile grounds for such structured causal discovery methods.
