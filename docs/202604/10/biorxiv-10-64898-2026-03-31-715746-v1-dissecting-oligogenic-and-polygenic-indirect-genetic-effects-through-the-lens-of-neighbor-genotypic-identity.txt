## ABSTRACT 11 

Individual phenotypes often depend on the genotypes of other individuals within a group. 12 

These phenomena are termed ‘indirect genetic effects’ (IGEs) and have been distinguished 13 

from direct genetic effects (DGEs) using quantitative genetic models. Recent studies have 14 

utilized high-resolution polymorphism data to enable genomic prediction (GP) and genome-15 

wide association study (GWAS) of IGEs, but unified methods remain limited. Here we 16 

integrate polygenic and oligogenic IGEs using a multi-kernel mixed model incorporating two 17 

random effects with a single covariance parameter. Underlying this implementation, the Ising 18 

model of ferromagnetics enabled us to simplify locus-wise and background IGEs for GWAS 19 

and GP, respectively. Our simulations demonstrated that, while the previous and present 20 

models exhibited similar performance, the present model can infer a trade-off between DGEs 21 

and IGEs. By applying this method to three species of woody plants, we found evidence for 22 

intergenotypic competition in aspen and apple trees, but limited evidence in climbing 23 

grapevines. Based on GWAS, we also detected significant variants associated with the 24 

competitive IGEs on the apple trunk growth. Our study offers a flexible implementation for 25 

GWAS/GP of IGEs, thereby providing an effective tool to dissect the genetic architecture of 26 

group performance. 27

## INTRODUCTION 30 

Individual phenotypes often depend not only on an individual’s own genotype but also on the 31 

genotypes of other individuals within a group. This phenomenon, termed ‘indirect genetic 32 

effects’ (IGEs), is critical for evolutionary trajectories, as it may constrain the response of 33 

direct genetic effects (DGEs) to selection [1–3]. If DGEs and IGEs coevolve, this offers a 34 

valuable perspective for plant and animal breeding to optimize group performance by 35 

mitigating intraspecific competition [4–6]. However, functional traits responsible for IGEs 36 

remain elusive [7]. For example, a set of crop traits designed to reduce competition, termed 37 

an ideotype [8], includes stem height, leaf angle, root length and other dimensions of plant 38 

morphology [9,10]. To address the difficulty in identifying intermediate traits responsible for 39 

IGEs, many researchers have utilized genotype-based IGE models, which allow for trait-blind 40 

analyses of IGEs [1,5,7,11]. 41 

The application of genotype-based IGE models has been enhanced by the increasing 42 

availability of high-resolution and population-wide data of single nucleotide polymorphisms 43 

(SNPs) [3,12]. For instance, recent studies have conducted genome-wide association study 44 

(GWAS) and genomic prediction (GP) to dissect the genetic architecture of IGEs in humans 45 

[13,14], mice [7,15], Arabidopsis thaliana [11], and crops [16,17]. In laboratory mice, IGEs 46 

from cage mates have been analyzed for over 100 phenotypes and transcriptomes [7], where 47 

GWAS identified 24 quantitative trait loci (QTLs) underlying IGEs [15]. Besides mobile 48 

animals, IGEs have been analyzed for the model plant A. thaliana and crops to estimate 49 

mixing abilities among neighboring genotypes [16,18,19]. These pioneering studies provide 50 

the first step to implement GWAS and GP of IGEs. Yet, flexible implementation has not been 51 

available for joint modeling of polygenic and oligogenic IGEs. 52 

In plants, IGEs occur among neighboring individuals as they cannot escape from their 53 

neighbors. These neighboring IGEs include multiple types of ecological interactions such as 54 

competition and facilitation [20–23]. To detect IGEs at a locus level, our previous study 55 

integerated the Ising model of ferromagnetics into a linear mixed model, named ‘Neighbor 56 

GWAS’ [24]. Analogous to physical interactions between two magnets, Neighbor GWAS 57 

formulated frequency-dependent selection at each SNP locus [25]. To reduce false positive 58

Application to real data 208 

We applied the proposed model to the three datasets — trembling aspen ( Populus 209 

tremuloides ), apple ( Malus × domestica ), and grape ( Vitis vinifera ) — to demonstrate its 210 

usability. All three datasets involve woody species and provide individual-level data on 211 

phenotypes, genotypes, and spatial arrangement. Based on the spatial layout of the study 212 

species, we analyzed the influence of nearest neighbors on each trait. 213 

We first analyzed life-history and defensive traits of aspen genotypes grown in a field garden 214 

at Wisconsin of USA, called WisAsp project [29–31]. Among 18 trait records collected over 215 

two years, basal area increment at the later growth stage (BAI2024-2015) alone showed a 216 

significant negative covariance between DGE and IGE ( 𝜌 '( = -0.599 and -0.629, 𝜒 ( = 3.875 217 

and 4.727, 𝑝 = 0.049 and 0.030 with likelihood ratio tests for 2014 and 2015, respectively: 218 

Table S1), suggesting IGEs on growth-related traits out of diverse traits. For the late basal 219 

area increment, we conducted GP based on 10-fold cross-validation. This GP showed the 220 

similar performance between models with and without the covariance in terms of R2 and 221 

MAE (Fig. S5), providing consistent evidence with the simulations (Fig. 2). GWAS of the 18 222

## DISCUSSION 267 

We demonstrated the feasibility of GWAS/GP of IGE based on the simulations and 268 

applications. Especially, we focused on the DGE-IGE covariance 𝜌 '( as a key parameter 269 

underlying the genetic trade-off between individual and group performance. Below, we first 270 

discuss methodological advantages and potential limitations in the proposed model. Then we 271 

discuss biological findings from the real data on woody plant species. 272 

Model performance and limitation 273 

By formulating SNP-wise IGEs based on the Ising model, we integrated oligogenic and 274 

polygenic IGEs into a multi-kernel mixed model. The present model can be exchanged into 275 

previous IGE models using the design matrix 𝐙 instead of 𝐊 ( and 𝛽 %,(. For example, Costa e 276 

Silva et al. [5] defined interacting neighbors by the design matrix, or incidence matrix 𝐙 . This 277 

approach can be implemented using the RAINBOWR package as it allows the flexible use of 278 

𝐙 (Appendix S4). Furthermore, it seems plausible for plants and sessile organisms to assume 279 

weaker IGEs from distant neighbors [5,35]. With 0-1 weights, the design matrix may also 280 

accomodate the spatial distance decay of neighbor genotypic effects [35]. Examples of such 281 

distance decay include the inverse, exponential, or any other decay functions of Euclidean 282 

distance. Therefore, the proposed model can implement preceding IGE models once the 283 

neighbor incidence and its distance decay are made explicit. 284 

While we applied our models to neighboring interactions in land plants, this approach can 285 

also be applied to animal herds or subpopulations. In this case, spatial genetic interactions 286 

based on the Ising model correspond to a population genetic model of frequency-dependent 287 

selection [25] (Appendix S3; Fig. S2). By applying the proposed model to cage-reared 288 

damselflies, previous studies indeed detected negative frequency-dependent selection on 289 

female color polymorphism [25]. Practically, this type of grouping can be achieved by setting 290 

neighbor spatial scales to infinity and subpopulations as a grouping factor in Neighbor 291 

GWAS [see 25 and its vignette]. Given that IGEs have often been analyzed to mitigate 292 

intraspecific competition in livestock [4,6], our method may offer a valuable opportunity to 293 

dissect the genetic architecture of group performance in both animals and plants. 294 

Our simulations demonstrated the performance of the proposed model in GWAS and GP. In 295 

GWAS, models with or without the covariance 𝜌 '( showed a similar performance to detect 296 

causative IGE SNPs. This is likely because total BLUPs are very similar regardless of 𝜌 '( (R2

Genetic architecture of tree competition 308 

To demonstrate the model applicability, we analyzed the three woody species: aspen, apple, 309 

and grape. The aspen and apple exhibited negative genetic covariance between DGEs and 310 

IGEs on their growth. Notably, these negative effects of neighboring genotypes were 311 

amplified as trees matured. These results are consistent with the previous studies on 312 

Eucalyptus trees, which detected significant negative DGE-IGE covariance at the late growth 313 

stage [5]. In contrast, the climbing woody plants, grapevines, did not exhibit large negative 314 

effects of neighboring genotypes on their growth traits. Like other climbing plants, grapes 315 

grow vertically and may therefore experience weak competition with horizontal neighbors. 316 

Furthermore, grapes possess considerable phenotypic plasticity in response to light quality, 317 

such as ultraviolet and far-red wavelengths, to alter their stem length, leaf morphology, and 318 

metabolite content [36,37]. Together with the exceptional but plausible case of grapes, the 319 

parallel evidence from aspen and apples exemplifies inter-genotypic competition within a 320 

plant species. 321 

The present data encompass phenological, chemical, and growth traits, though we did not 322 

observe a significantly negative DGE-IGE covariance for these traits. Regarding 323 

phenological traits, flowering time is often regulated by self-genotypes as shown by high 324 

heritability within a field site [38,39]. We detected substantial phenotypic variance explained 325 

by DGEs, but limited variance explained by IGEs, for flowering time across the three woody 326 

species (e.g., “BS, bud set” in Fig. 3; “Harvesting date” in Fig. S6; and “days.upto.veraison” 327 

in Fig. 5). This suggests that phenological traits are unlikely to be substantially explained by 328 

IGEs. In contrast to phenological traits, some chemical traits exhibited large phenotypic 329

yet to be validated in apples. To this end, our study uncovered a QTL and candidate genes 352

Neighbor GWAS is widely applicable to a broad range of phenotypes and species as far as 354 

spatial information is available alongside phenotype and genotype data. While the present 355 

data include individual trees as the unit of genotype [29,33,34], our method allows for 356 

defining a genotype unit as multiple individuals of a single genotype, such as a single plot of 357 

field crops [48]. It is important to note, however, that spatial metadata may not always be 358 

complete. Even with available spatial information, it is necessary to interpret whether 359 

intergenotypic interactions operate at a given spatial scale. Although our study found little 360 

evidence of grapevine competition, this lack of evidence may have resulted from sufficient 361

Conclusion 364 

The present study provides a flexible model to accommodate polygenic and locus-wise 365 

effects of neighboring genotypes on a complex phenotype. This single model enables both 366 

GP and GWAS of IGEs. The theory of IGEs has frequently involved the evolution of social 367 

interactions [2,49], while empirical applications have predominantly been conducted in 368 

domesticated animals and plants [6,50,51]. Consequently, genomics of IGEs promises to 369 

contribute to both fundamental and applied issues, including multi-level selection and 370 

agricultural applications. Recent research has highlighted the growing need for an 371 

interdisciplinary approach to IGEs [3,12,22]. With the increasing availability of genome-wide 372 

polymorphism data, our methodology may enhance interdisciplinary practice by facilitating 373 

GWAS/GP of IGEs across diverse organisms. 374
