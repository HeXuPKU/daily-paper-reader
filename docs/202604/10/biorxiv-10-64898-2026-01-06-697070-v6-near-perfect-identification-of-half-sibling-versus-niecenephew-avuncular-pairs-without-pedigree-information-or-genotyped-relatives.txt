## Abstract 

Motivation: Large-scale genomic biobanks contain thousands of second-degree relatives without pedi-gree data. Accurately distinguishing half-siblings from avuncular pairs–both sharing approximately 25% of the genome–remains a significant challenge. Current SNP-based methods rely on aggregate Identical-By-Descent (IBD) segment counts and age differences, but substantial overlap in these distributions leads to high misclassification rates. There is a need for a scalable, genotype-only method that can resolve these second-degree ambiguities without requiring observed pedigrees or phenotypic information. 

Results: We present a novel computational framework that achieves near-complete separation of half-siblings and avuncular pairs using haplotype-level sharing features derived from across-chromosome phasing. These features also allow differentiation of nieces/nephews from aunts/uncles within avuncular pairs. By modeling these features with a multivariate Gaussian mixture model (GMM), we demonstrate exceptional classification performance in biobank-scale data. Ground-truth labels for validation were established through a multi-step inference process within a family graph constructed from high-confidence first-degree relationships. All 395 ground-truth avuncular pairs were correctly classified, and 60 of 61 half-sibling pairs were correctly identified. Treating (e.g.) half-sibling status as the positive class, this corresponds to 98.4% sensitivity and 100% specificity. Our results suggest that the framework could perform comparably on other datasets of similar size. This method provides a robust, scalable solution for pedigree reconstruction and the control of cryptic relatedness in large-scale genomic studies.

## 1 Introduction 

Genome-wide datasets routinely contain closely related individuals for whom the precise pedigree relationship is unknown and must be inferred from genotype data alone [5]. As sample sizes in biobank-scale cohorts have grown to hundreds of thousands of individuals, accurately inferring these relationships has become increasingly important for reconstructing pedigree structure, improving haplotype phasing, and supporting down-stream analyses in human genetics, including demographic inference and genealogical applications [6, 7, 9]. A persistent challenge in relatedness inference is distinguishing among relationships beyond the first degree. Parent–offspring and full sibling pairs can be readily distinguished because, although both share approxi-mately 50% of their genome identical-by-descent (IBD), parent–offspring pairs share no genomic segments where both alleles are inherited from the same ancestor (IBD2), whereas full siblings share 25% of their genomes IBD2 as a result of inheriting the same haplotypes from both parents. This clear difference in IBD state patterns makes first-degree relationships straightforward to classify from genomic data. In con-trast, several distinct pedigree configurations produce the same expected sharing for second-degree relatives (25% IBD), including half-siblings, grandparent–grandchild, avuncular (niece/nephew–uncle/aunt), and dou-ble first-cousin pairs, and the genome-wide patterns of IBD sharing are similar across these relationship classes [22, 5]. Among these, half-sibling and avuncular pairs are particularly important to distinguish in biobank data because grandparent–grandchild and double first cousin pairs are rare in cohorts recruited from a single adult generation. Accurately distinguishing half-sibling from avuncular relationships is important for several domains of genomic research. Most directly, the two relationships imply different pedigree structures, so distinguish-ing them improves pedigree reconstruction in large cohorts and other analyses that rely on accurate family structure. It also has methodological implications for genomic analyses: half-siblings are more effective phase anchors than avuncular pairs because they share a parent directly, linking haplotypes across chro-mosomes more effectively than avuncular sharing, which is fragmented by an additional generation of recombination [24]. Finally, distinguishing these relationships can improve the interpretation of family-based anal-yses and clinical genetics. Half-siblings share more similar childhood environments and parental effects than avuncular pairs, and misclassifi-cation can therefore bias estimates of shared variance and genetic risk in pedigrees [20, 21, 25]. Current approaches to distinguish half-sibling from avuncular pairs pri-marily rely on the distribution of IBD segments; half-siblings inherit shared genetic segments directly from their common parent, whereas segments shared by niece/nephew-avuncular pairs have undergone an additional meiosis, typically leading to a higher count of shorter segments [6]. Despite these theoretical differences, empirical distributions of segment length and count exhibit substantial overlap due to the stochastic nature of recombina-tion [19]. Furthermore, while demographic data such as age differences can provide heuristic support, they are often insufficient for high-confidence classification and such data can sometimes be unavailable. In this paper, we propose a novel computational framework that utilizes across-chromosome phasing to resolve the two most common types of second-degree relationships in biobank data. By deriving a 2-dimensional feature vector, we capture the specific inheritance logic of parental haplotypes. We demonstrate that modeling these features with a multivariate Gaussian mixture model (GMM), fitted via the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm [26], provides near-perfect separation between half-sibling and avuncular pairs. Finally, we show that these identified relationships can be used as phase anchors, establishing a feedback loop that improves the perfor-mance of across-chromosome phasing. This approach offers a scalable, genotype-only solution that eliminates the dependency on explicit pedigree or phenotypic data. 

## 2 Methods 

2.1 UK Biobank sample and ancestry definition 

The method was developed and evaluated using data from the UK Biobank [12], focusing on individuals of European descent. This choice was motivated by the fact that individuals of European descent consti-tute the overwhelming majority of the UK Biobank cohort and therefore by the poorer performance of across-chromosome phase estimation in non-European samples [12]. The approach we describe should work equally well in non-European samples of similar size to the UK Biobank. Genetic principal components (PCs) were computed on a linkage-disequilibrium–pruned set of common autosomal SNPs [6]. The smallest 4-dimensional hypercube in PC space containing all individuals with self-described white British ancestry was then identified, and all individuals lying within this hypersphere, regardless of self-described ancestry, were retained as the eligible sample (435,187 individuals). Next, we selected biallelic autosomal SNPs that were present on both the UK BiLEVE and UK Biobank Axiom arrays, had a minor allele fre-quency (MAF) of at least 5%, and had a Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium chi-square test p-value greater than 0.0005. This resulted in a final dataset of 327,744 SNPs for relatedness estimation and downstream analyses [12].

2.4 Across-chromosome phasing 

All UK Biobank individuals were initially phased within chromosomes us-ing Shapeit2 [12]. To extend phase across chromosomes, we implemented a method that assigns each haplotype to a consistent parental origin by leveraging genome-wide patterns of haplotype sharing [1]. The central idea of cross-chromosome phasing is that, although chro-mosomes are initially phased independently, haplotypes inherited from the same parent will exhibit systematically higher genetic similarity to the same set of external individuals (i.e., distant relatives) across the genome. This occurs because a common ancestor of the focal and non-focal individ-uals may transmit IBD segments on multiple chromosomes, and—absent inbreeding—these segments must be inherited through the same parental lineage in the focal individual. As a result, haplotypes from that parent will show elevated SNP similarity to the same non-focal individuals across multiple windows, producing correlated similarity profiles, whereas hap-lotypes from different parents will not. To capture this signal, the genome is partitioned into windows defined by recombination hotspots. Within each window, each haplotype of a focal individual is compared to both haplotypes of all non-focal individuals in a reference sample. For each comparison, we retain the maximum SNP similarity, which reduces sensi-tivity to switch errors in the reference panel. This produces, for each focal haplotype and each genomic window, a vector of similarity scores to the reference individuals. Across windows, haplotypes originating from the same parent will show correlated patterns of similarity to the reference sample, whereas hap-lotypes from different parents will not. The algorithm therefore computes correlations of these similarity profiles across windows and iteratively groups haplotypes into two clusters corresponding to the two parental ho-mologues ( p1 and p2). By aggregating information across the genome in this manner, the method establishes a consistent parental assign-ment across all autosomes without requiring pedigree information or explicit IBD segment detection. Hereafter, we use phased to refer to such across-chromosome phased data.

2.6 Multivariate GMM and Posterior Probability 

P (half-sibling )

To classify pairs, we fit a two-component multivariate normal mixture model to the 2-D feature vectors of the labeled ground-truth set using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm (Implemented in C and R, available at [27]). The joint density for a pair with feature vector x is modeled as: 

f (x|Θ) = πN (x|μ1, Σ1) + (1 − π)N (x|μ2, Σ2) (8) where π represents the mixing proportion, and N (x|μk , Σk ) is the 4-dimensional multivariate normal distribution for component k ∈{half-sibling, avuncular }. For every pair with a complete 2-D feature vec-tor, we compute the posterior probability P (half-sibling ) as the probability of the half-sibling component given the observed vector x:

P (half-sibling |x) = πϕ (x|μhalf-sibling , Σhalf-sibling )

## 3 Results 

3.1 Unsupervised cluster identification and validation 

The classification of second-degree relatives was performed using an un-supervised framework to minimize bias and avoid circularity in validation. The mixture model EM algorithm identified two distinct clusters within the 459 ground-truth (GT) pairs based solely on the 2-D feature vector; critically, the relationship labels (half-sibling or avuncular) are not utilized by the algorithm during the cluster discovery phase [26]. Following the unsupervised estimation of cluster parameters, the GT labels are utilized to assign the biological identity of each component (e.g., identifying which cluster corresponds to the half-sibling class) and to estimate the sensitivity and specificity of the approach for identifying half-sibling pairs (treating the half-sibling class as the positive condition) on this labeled set. This methodology ensures that the performance metrics reflect the model’s ability to recover known biological structures without over-fitting to pre-defined labels. The 2-D multivariate GMM demonstrates near-perfect classification performance when applied to the full UK Biobank candidate set. As shown in Figure 4, the distribution of the posterior probability P (half −sibling )

exhibits sharp bimodal peaks at 0 and 1, reflecting high model certainty in distinguishing between the two pedigree configurations. Using a middle classification threshold of 0.5, the model achieves exceptional separation (Figure 4). All 395 ground-truth avuncular pairs were correctly classified ( T P = 395 ), whereas 60 out of 61 ground-truth half-siblings were correctly identified ( T P = 60 ), corresponding to a sensitivity of 98.4% and a specificity of 100%. Beyond the ground-truth set, the model identified 513 previously un-labeled pairs ( P (half − sibling ) > 0.5), while 5,963 unlabeled pairs were predicted as avuncular (Figure 4). This demonstrates the power of the multivariate approach to recover a substantial number of high-confidence phase anchors from an unlabeled biobank-scale population. 

3.2 Impact of across-chromosome phasing on misclassification 

The high classification accuracy of the multivariate framework suggests that haplotype-level features are robust discriminators; however, the re-maining edge cases highlight the method’s dependence on the quality of across-chromosome phasing. Our analysis indicates that misclassifications are primarily driven by noise in the across-chromosome phasing process rather than a failure of the multivariate model or within-chromosome switch errors.

## 4 Application to Phasing 

Beyond subtyping, the inferred half-sibling and avuncular relationships can be utilized as long-range phase anchors to improve both within- and across-chromosome phasing. Because half-siblings and nieces/nephews inherit shared segments from a single parental lineage, these regions pro-vide critical constraints for parental haplotype assignment. In contrast, while an avuncular relative’s shared regions cannot be cleanly attributed to a specific parent without additional information, they still offer informative links when integrated into the reference panel of a phasing algorithm. We incorporated these relatives into the across-chromosome phasing framework described in Sapin et al. [1] by adjusting the ˆπ(f,i ) exclusion threshold from 0.1875 to 0.33, thereby explicitly including second-degree relatives as phase anchors. For ground-truth nieces/nephews where parental genotypes allowed for direct verification, this relationship-informed constraint measurably enhanced across-chromosome phasing accuracy. These results demonstrate that subtyping second-degree rela-tives provides a robust, genotype-only feedback loop for refining genomic reconstruction in biobank-scale datasets. 

## 5 Conclusion and Future Work 

This study demonstrates that a multivariate Gaussian mixture model (GMM) applied to haplotype-level sharing features provides a robust, scal-able solution for distinguishing half-sibling from niece/nephew–avuncular pairs. By utilizing a two-dimensional feature vector derived from across-chromosome phasing, the method effectively leverages the structural inheritance logic of parental homologues to resolve ambiguities that diploid SNP-based methods cannot. Our results in the UK Biobank show that the model achieves exceptional specificity (100%) and sensitivity (98.4%) in distinguishing these relationships. The high classification accuracy of the multivariate framework sug-gests that haplotype-level features are robust discriminators; however, the remaining edge cases highlight the method’s dependence on the quality of across-chromosome phasing. Our analysis indicates that misclassifications are primarily driven by noise in the across-chromosome phasing process rather than a failure of the multivariate model or within-chromosome switch errors. Improving the stability of across-chromosome haplotype assignment remains a key area for further refinement in biobank-scale pedigree reconstruction.

Beyond relationship subtyping, the inferred half-sibling and avuncular pairs serve as powerful phase anchors for genomic reconstruction. By in-corporating these high-confidence relatives into the reference population, we established a feedback loop that measurably improves the accuracy of across-chromosome phasing. This approach offers a genotype-only so-lution that eliminates the dependency on explicit pedigree or phenotypic data, providing a scalable framework for large cohorts. A critical direction for future work remains the extension of this haplotype-aware framework to resolve third-degree relationships, such as first-degree cousins (FDC) and half-avuncular pairs, who share approx-imately 12.5% of their genome. These configurations are theoretically identical in their expected haplotype sharing because both involve a pair of parents (one for each individual) being related to the first degree—full siblings in the case of first cousins, and a parent-offspring relationship in the case of half-avuncular pairs. Future research will focus on distinguish-ing the four "grand-haplotypes" corresponding to the four grandparents to resolve these ambiguities. By identifying which specific grandparental lineage a shared segment originates from, it may be possible to break the symmetry between these third-degree classes. In summary, this study pro-vides a direct application of across-chromosome phasing with immediate implications for the construction of refined pedigrees and the control of cryptic relatedness in large-scale human populations.
