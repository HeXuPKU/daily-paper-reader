ABSTRACT (250 WORD MAX): 

Genome -wide association studies (GWAS) and polygenic score (PGS) development are 

typically constrained by the data available in biobank repositories in which European cohorts are 

vastly overrepresented. Here, we increase the utility of non -European participant data within the 

UK Biobank (UKB) by characterizing the genetic affinities of UKB parti cipants who self -identify 

as Bangladeshi, Indian, Pakistani, “White and Asian” (WA), and “Any Other Asian” (AOA), 

towards creating a more robust South Asian sample s ize for future genetic analyses. We assess 

the relationships between genetic structure and self -selected ethnic identities and use 

consistent patterns of clustering in the dataset to train a support vector machine (SVM). The 

SVM was utilized to reassign n = 1,853 AOA and WA participants at the subcontinental level, 

and increase the sample size of the UKB South Asian group by 1,381 additional participants. 

We further leverage these samples to assess GWAS performance and PGS development. We 

include environmen tal covariates in the height GWAS by implementing a rigorous covariate 

selection procedure, and compare the outputs of two GWAS models: GWAS null and GWAS env .

We show that PGS performance derived from both GWAS models yield comparable prediction 

to PGS models developed with an order of magnitude larger training , and environmentally -

adjusted PGS models reduce the sex -bias in predictive performance . In summary, we 

demonstrate how GWAS performance can be improved by leveraging ambiguous ethnicity 

codes, ancestr y matched imputation panels, and including environmental covariates. 

KEYWORDS : Height; Genome -wide association study (GWAS); Polygenic score (PGS); UK 

Biobank; South Asia; Environmental covariates

Background: 3

Unequal representation of diverse populations within biobanks has resulted in 4

comparatively few insights into the genetic architecture of complex traits for many non -5

European -descent populations [1]. This Eurocentric emphasis within genetic research is often 6

attributed to the current state of data availability, in which European -descent genomic data is 7

most abundant. Consequently, the prevailing assumption is that smaller datasets will fail to 8

produce informative results. This perspective acts to disincenti vize prospective researchers from 9

attempting to make the best use of the datasets currently available [2]. However, utilizing 10 

smaller samples need not be a futile undertaking. Genotype -phenotype research design can be 11 

optimized to increase the utility of smaller datasets. Here, we highlight how a detailed 12 

consideration of the intersection of genetic ancestry affinities and ethnic identifiers, in 13 

conjunction with incorporating environmental adjustments into GWAS of height can improve the 14 

quality of results output by smaller, often excluded non -European -descent samples. 15 

PGS performance declines when applied to groups outside of the discovery GWAS 16 

training sample [3,4,5], and because European cohorts are vastly overrepresented within the 17 

available data, PGS applicability is much reduced in non -Europeans [6, 7]. Specifically, variation 18 

in frequencies and population -specificity of causal alleles, in conjunction with differences in 19 

linkage disequilibrium (LD) between populations, contribute to the limitations of out -of -sample 20 

PGS portability [8, 9]. Fine -mapping with exome data, improved imputation for GWAS, and 21 

including LD -informed analyses can ameliorate the effects of these factors [4, 10]. Methods that 22 

integrate discovery -sample GWAS summary statistics with LD structure from multi -ethnic 23 

cohorts to improve the predictive utility of European -derived PGS have been a primary focus of 24 

PGS research recently [12,13,14]. However, improving GWAS discovery by increasing the 25 

sample sizes of minority cohorts or including relevant environmental variables have not been 26 

explored as systematically as other approaches. 27 

Using UK Biobank (UKB) South Asian participants as a case study, we aim to test how 28 

the discovery of significant genetic variants and accuracy of PGS prediction of complex traits 29 

may be improved by: 1) considering several levels of participant identity and genetic 30 

characteristics when establishing a sample dataset; and 2) adjusting for environmental 31 

confounders in the GWAS model. We focus on height as a canonical complex trait. The UKB is 32 

a vast resource for public health, epidemiology, and precision medicine research, containing 33 

genetic, phenotypic, and environmental data for over 500,000 adult UK residents [14]. While the 34 

majority of participants self -identify as “white” (defined by Data Field 21000 as including British, 35 

Irish, and “Any other white background”; n > 500,000 as of January 2024), there are over 36 

28,000 individuals who do not identify as such. The Pan -UK Biobank and additional studies 37 

have identified tens of thousands of individuals with primarily non -European genetically inferred 38 

ancestry [3, 16]. The largest geographically -defined grouping of non -“white” -identifying 39 

participants is a conglomerate of South Asian identities, which include Bangladeshi, Indian, and 40 

Pakistani identifying individuals (Data Field 21000). However, ethnic identity does not 41 

necessarily reflect high genetic affinities with other individuals or populations occupying the 42 

same geographical region at the same point in time [17,18,19,20]. 43 

The use of ethnicity as a proxy, or at least as a starting point, for identifying a genetically 44 

homogenous population is further complicated when the ethnic labels participants are able to 45 

choose from are limited, discrete/categorical, follow several naming conventions, and/or are un -46 

defined, as is the case with UKB participants who identify as “White and Asian” (WA) or “Any 47 

other Asian” (AOA). This, in conjunction with the limited UKB Data Fields associated with 48 

genetic affinity available to researchers and historical apprehension to including genetically 49 

“admixed” populations in GWAS research has resulted in reduced inclusion for AOA and WA 50 

participants [5, 21, 22]. To rectify this, we characterize in detail the genetic affinities of UKB 51

Previous investigations into the genetic ancestries of UKB participants have aimed to 63 

broadly categorize participants at the subcontinental level, but in -depth assessments of 64 

population affinities within the identified non -European groupings have been lacking. Privé et al. 65 

(2022) defined nine subcontinental ancestry groups within the UKB cohort and demonstrated 66 

that PGS prediction significantly decreases when PGS models trained on Northwestern 67 

European individuals are applied to the non -European ancestry groups [3]. However, non -68 

European ancestry groups were, in part, restricted to participants whose self -reported ethnicity 69 

(as assessed by a within -UKB PCA) matched their country of origin, resulting in PGS exclusion 70 

for many participants as individuals within diaspora communities were not retained (see Privé et 71 

al. , 2022, Fig. 1 and Table SA1 ).72 

Constantinescu et al. aimed to characterize UKB participants of “non -white British 73 

ancestry” for the explicit purpose of identifying relatively homogenous groups (2022: 2), to 74 

facilitate the inclusion of these participants in future health research [16]. We implement a 75 

similar approach to Constantinescu et al. to characterize a subset of the UKB participants of 76 

“non -white British ancestry” at a subcontinental resolution as well as at the level of 1KG 77 

reference groups [16]. Our subcontinental approach, however, differs at three primary levels 78 

from the approach taken by Constantinescu et al.: 1) we focus our analyses on a UKB South 79 

Asian metapopulation as opposed to their inclusion of all “non -white” individuals, therefore 80 

providing more detail into South Asian population substructure; 2) we utilize a subset of the 81 

1KG -UKB genetic data intersection in our genetic affinity analyses chosen specifically to reduce 82 

the effects of ascertainment bias resulting from the use of the UKB SNP array; and 3) while our 83 

subcontinental classifications are also PCA based, we choose to utilize support vector machines 84 

(SVM) instead of K -means clustering to infer population assignments. 85 

After creating a broader UKB South Asian dataset, our second aim is to assess how 86 

adjusting for environmental confounders in the GWAS model affects GWAS and PGS results. 87 

We implement an environmental variable selection procedure to identify a subset of covariates 88 

to control for confounding in our GWAS model (referred to here as GWAS env ). We then assess 89 

the downstream effects of GWAS env by developing a height PGS model and compare its 90 

performance to published population -matched PGS. 91

Genetic affinities of UKB South Asian participants 95 

The genetic affinities of UKB participants who self -identify as Bangladeshi, Indian, 96 

Pakistani, “White and Asian” (WA), and “Any Other Asian” (AOA) were characterized via three 97 

main methods: principal components analysis (PCA), ADMIXTURE, and support vector 98 

machines (SVM). For each method, the UKB data were projected onto or trained on data from a 99 

globally distributed reference panel (1KG) to characterize genetically how UKB -BIP participants 100 

align with other South Asian groups and to identify underlying genetic patterns of participants 101 

with “ambiguously defined” ethnicities (WA and AOA). For WA and AOA participants, 102 

ascertaining genetic similarities with broader groups (of potentially differing ethnic labels) may 103 

be the only way that these participants are routinely included in future genetics research, since 104 

discretizing populations into ethnic groupings of small sample sizes typically results in their 105 

exclusion from genetic analyses [5, 16, 20]. Therefore, in addition to the PCA and ADMIXTURE 106 

assessments, an SVM was trained to quantitatively integrate information from the previously 107 

calculated PC scores and infer both population -level affinities and subcontinental assignments 108 

for each AOA and WA participant (see Methods section and SI Section 2.2). 109 

Ascertainment bias in the UKB SNP array 110 

To conduct these analyses, the QC -ed UKB SNP array (see Methods) was merged with 111 

the 1KG data ( n = 668,051 shared SNPs). The 1KG data were used to calculate 20 PCs and the 112 

UKB data were projected onto this PC space (FRAPOSA [23]). PC plots were visually assessed 113 

to identify large -scale patterns within and between the UKB groups. 114 

During the initial visual inspections, however, PCA results indicated that Utah residents 115 

with Northern and Western European ancestry (CEU) and British in England and Scotland 116 

(GBR) samples explained most of the genetic variance within the 1KG reference data (prior to 117 

projection of the UKB data) (Fig. 1), prompting further investigation of the data qualities. Several 118 

assessments were employed to identify the root cause(s) driving the inflated European variance 119 

observed in the PC plots, ultimately indicating ascertainment bias of the UKB genotype array as 120 

the primary cause (see Methods, SI Section 1.2.3, and Figures S1.3 – S1.5). Because the array 121 

was designed to highlight European genetic variation, most of the rare alleles on the array are 122 

found at higher frequencies within European groups (Fig. S1.2), artificially inflating the 123 

magnitude of European genetic heterogeneity relative to other globally distributed populations. 124 

When the UKB and 1KG data are intersected for population genetic analyses, globally 125 

representative data (1KG) gets reduced to the variants that, within PCA results, fail to 126 

adequately capture variation among samples with non -European genetic ancestry (i.e., the 127 

formerly globally representative sample becomes primarily representative of genetic variation 128 

within the British Isles). Therefore, for all subsequent analyses (described below), the original 129 

intersection of QC -ed SNPs within both UKB and 1KG data was further reduced to a subset 130 

chosen to reflect broad patterns of expected global genetic diversity ( n = 199,495) following 131 

more stringent MAF thresholds and expulsion of variants that failed F ST assessments (See 132 

Methods section and SI Section 1.2.3 for more details). 133

UKB “Any Other Asian” 203 

Results of PCA, ADMIXTURE, and SVM classifications consistently demonstrate that 204 

UKB participants who self -identify as “Any Other Asian” (AOA) ( n = 1,614) do not comprise a 205 

genetically homogeneous group (as the ethnicity label may imply). PCA results indicate that the 206 

AOA group mostly consists of participants who genetically align with Asian reference 207 

populations. Across PC’s, score distributions highlight substructure within this grouping, 208 

especially along PC’s driven by Asian genetic variance (e.g., PC’s 4, 7, and 8; Fig. 1; SI Section 209 

1.2.1). At least four clusters can be visualized in the PCA plots, consistently aligning with 210 

Japanese sampled in Tokyo (JPT), Chinese Dai sampled in Xishuangbanna (CHB), and the 211 

conglomerate of South Asian clusters, as well as a cluster of participants that tend to fall near or 212 

between the Kinh Vietnamese sampled in Ho Chi Minh City (KHV) and Southern Han Chinese 213 

(CHS) samples. However, not all participants can be characterized solely by Asian substructure. 214 

One participant consistently aligns with 1KG African reference groups, and European ancestry 215 

is also possible for a subset of the participants (Fig. 2). ADMIXTURE analyses further replicate 216 

these results, again indicating substantial genetic substructure within this ethnic label across all 217 

values of k (Fig. 3B) and demonstrating that a small portion of AOA participants are more 218 

genetically similar to European reference populations as opposed to Asian reference 219 

populations (Table S1.11). 220 

SVM classifications reiterate the genetic structure within this ethnic label, inferring 221 

affinities with reference groups across the globe (classifications at ≥ 0, ≥ 0.75, and ≥ 0.9 222 

probabilities are provided in Table S1.8). Using four subcontinental probabilities (with summed 223 

classification probabilities ≥ 0.7), we are able to classify 1,408 AOA participants. Specifically, 224 

2.8% of the total AOA sample classify as European, 20.6% as East Asian, and 64.2% as South 225 

Asian (Table S1.9). These subcontinental classifications are derived from grouping the 226 

population -level assignments, for example the ‘East Asian’ classification is created by summing 227 

CDX, CHB, CHS, KHV, and JPT classification probabilities. The majority of AOA participants 228 

were classified as Bangladeshi or Pakistani, as UKB Indians were not incorporated into the 229 

training data; (see SI Section 1.3.2 for more information), and Indian Telugu (ITU) or Sri Lankan 230 

Tamil (STU) at lower classification probabilities. Of these participants, 159/208 that were 231 

classified as ITU and 309/373 classified as STU (classification probability > 0) report Sri Lanka 232 

as their region of origin. In total, 500 AOA participants report Sri Lanka as their region of origin, 233 

followed by Mauritius ( n = 85), Afghanistan ( n = 72), Kenya ( n = 59), Iran ( n = 33), and Malaysia 234 

(n = 31) (see Fig.S1. 11 for further details). Notably, no participants were classified as Punjabi 235 

from Pakistan (PJL) at any classification probability threshold. Of the 1,613 AOA participants 236 

included in these analyses, 1,036 align at the subcontinental level with South Asian samples. 237

Residual Population Stratification 271 

While comparative analyses collectively suggest that UKB -BIP participants are 272 

genetically most similar to non -UK South Asian groups (i.e., the 1KG South Asian reference 273 

populations), ADMIXTURE results, specifically, indicate slightly higher European affinities within 274 

all UKB groups assessed than the 1KG South Asian samples (Fig. 3) and there are visible (and 275 

quantifiable) discrepancies between UKB WA and AOA ADMIXTURE proportions compared to 276 

the 1KG SVM associated training group (Fig. 3; Fig. S1.12 – S1.14; Tables S1.10 and S1.11). It 277 

is unclear if this is an artifact of the genotype array or a true signal of higher shared genetic 278 

ancestry between UKB participants and other Europeans. 279

Disclaimer regarding genetic affinity analyses : It should be emphasized that human genetic 281 

diversity is not categorical, and the analyses utilized here leverage the incredibly small amount 282 

of genetic diversity that differs among populations [25, 26]. The co -opting of statistical methods 283 

designed to classify and discriminate between discrete data towards analyzing human genetic 284 

variation is employed here only to facilitate the incorporation of typically excluded participants in 285 

genetic studies. The terms used in the Methods and Results sections (e.g., “classification”) 286 

reflect the statistical procedures conducted and are not meant to impose identities or group 287 

affiliations outside of this context. 288

Environmental covariates 292 

Height is a composite phenotype made up of many anatomical regions, structures, and 293 

tissues. The primary contributor to adult stature, however, is the skeletal system. Bone mass 294 

and joint integrity (and consequently, height) peak at around 30 years of age [28, 29] before 295 

gradually breaking down over the next several decades of life. Non -genetic factors that 296 

influence an individual’s peak bone mass include primarily the environment experienced during 297 

ontogeny. To account for these effects, variables encompassing diet, general health, activity 298 

patterns, exposures, and socioeconomic status were requested from the data available in the 299 

UKB. After growth has ceased, systemic health and maintenance become the prominent factors 300 

concerning skeletal mass and morphology. Variables that affect bone mineralization, joint 301 

function, overall health, and the likelihood of incurring fractures were included to account for 302 

post -ontogenetic environmental influences. 303 

Following assessments of the quality, missingness, and correlations among the 304 

requested environmental variables (Methods), nine data fields were selected for inclusion in the 305 

GWAS (Table 2; Figure S2.2). To test if these specific environmental variables are significantly 306 

associated with height, and to identify the variance in height explained by additive 307 

environmental components alone, an ordinary least squares (OLS) regression model was 308 

tested. The full UKB South Asian sample with un -imputed data ( n = 7,331) was used for this 309 

assessment. 310 

The environment -only model explains 6 and 8% of the variance in height in males and 311 

females, respectively (Table 3; Table S2.2). When applied to the combined sex sample, all 312 

variables meet significance (p < 0.01) except for ‘Number of live births’ (Table S2.1), though 313 

visualization of the effect does suggest a weak, negative association with height (Figures S2.4 -314

DISCUSSION 481 

482 

Pre -existing, predominantly European -descent biobank data can be used for genotype -483 

phenotype research, but extra steps must be taken to improve the quality of the genetic data for 484 

non -European participants. For example, as of the submission of the current study , the UK 485 

Biobank only provides genetic ancestry information for ‘White British’ participants (data field 486 

22006), leaving the characterization of genetic affinities among non -‘White’ -identifying 487 

participants to researchers. This can result in discrepancies across studies with regards to 488 

participant inclusion, and therefore, complicate the utility of out -of -sample results and 489 

inferences. By investigating the intersection of sociocultural labels and genotype data, we 490 

increase the UKB South Asian sample size by 20% (originally only comprised of Bangladeshi, 491 

Indian, and Pakistani participants), facilitating the development of larger UKB Asian analytic 492 

groups. Increasing sample sizes, including the diversification of biobank demographics, is only a 493 

partial solution. We demonstrate that including relevant environmental covariates refines the 494 

estimates of GWAS SNP effects and reduces sex -biases in PGS performance . By taking a more 495 

intensive data quality -centered approach to research design, we are able to generate PGS 496 

model s that perform comparably to those trained on datasets incorporating ten times the 497 

number of participants. 498

Genetic affinities of UKB South Asian participants 500 

While the UKB ethnic categories are very limited, we find substantial genetic diversity 501 

among participants within the same label, demonstrating the limited correspondence between 502 

sociocultural labels and genetic affinities. Geopolitically -defined ethnic labels within the UKB 503 

(e.g., BIP) have more consistent patterns of genetic affinities than more ambiguous labels such 504 

as AOA or WA. While broad trends are apparent and distinguish the genetic stratification 505 

between WA and AOA groups at large, there are no definitive indicators demonstrated by the 506 

genetic data that explain participant identities or cultural affiliations at the individual level. 507 

Individual participants who report the same geographic region of origin and are assigned to the 508 

same SVM classification choose to identify as AOA versus WA (of the available options) for 509 

reasons not predictable by genetic analyses. For example, our SVM results show that a higher 510 

proportion of WA than AOA participants classify as European or UKB Pakistani, however within 511 

SVM classifications , the same regions of origin are reflected regardless of participant ethnic 512 

identity (e.g., most of the WA participants classified as UKB Bangladeshi are from India, 513 

Myanmar, The Guianas, Malaysia, and Mauritius, and most of the AOA participants classified as 514 

UKB Bangladeshi are similarly from Mauritius, Myanmar, Nepal, Sri Lanka, and Malaysia 515 

[Figures S1.10 and S1.11]). This highlights, and reiterates, that ethnic identity, genetic 516 

stratification, and shared ancestry are distinct participant descriptors that cannot be used a priori 517 

as proxies for one another. Therefore, ethnic groupings may represent an adequate starting 518 

point for preliminary sample selection but cannot be used, unaltered, as a homogenous or 519 

distinct genetic sample. 520 

Additionally, we replicate previously reported trends regarding South Asian genetic affinities, 521 

particularly with regards to higher genetic similarity of UKB Pakistani participants to Europeans 522 

(Fig.3; Fig.S1.8). Based on previous research [25, 3 7], this is likely a result of shared ancestry 523 

derived from the prehistoric ‘Ancestral North Indian’ (ANI) population [25, 3 7] rather than more 524 

recent gene flow events. More recent population movements are reflected when comparing 525

Environmentally -adjusted GWAS and PGS development 543 

Genetic associations with height are typically assessed via a linear regression model 544 

that is purely additive, ignoring epistasis, as well as gene -environment interactions (GxE). 545 

Currently, model simplification is deemed justifiable due to limitations, such as the low power of 546 

GxE tests, measurement error of the environmental variable of interest, and a lack of knowledge 547 

of the specific biological pathways underlying the interactive effects [3 9]. Once identified, a GxE 548 

feature can be integrated into GWAS modeling simply by entering the interaction term; however, 549 

because of the inherently non -experimental nature of human GxE discovery research , it has 550 

been difficult to disentangle confounding from causality [ 40 ]. Regardless, for complex traits that 551 

have been studied, environmental influences are relevant, significant, and often ignored [21, 552 

41 ]. 553 

There are many environmental exposures that have been repeatedly demonstrated to 554 

affect human stature [4 2,4 3,4 4,4 5,4 6,4 7,4 8,4 9,50 ,51 ,5 2]. Yet most height GWAS only 555 

incorporate sex, age, and a genetic relatedness matrix (GRM) and/or simply the first 10 PCs to 556 

account for population stratification and adjust for confounders (e.g.: Jeon et al., 2024; Sohail et 557 

al., 2023; Wojcik et al., 2019; Yengo et al., 2022; Zoledziewska et al., 2015 [3 4, 5 3, 5 4, 5 5, 5 6]). 558 

In fact, a primary reason for the accessibility of ample height data in medical records is because 559 

of the relationship between height, environmental factors, and health including “cumulative net 560 

nutrition, biological deprivation, and standard of living between and within populations” [ 50 : 1]. 561 

The bulk of population -level differences may be captured by within -sample PCs and GRMs, but 562 

it is unlikely that they capture fine -level environmental effects, and it has been demonstrated 563 

that controlling for population stratification in this manner isn’t always sufficient for highly 564 

polygenic traits [5 7]. 565

Environmental covariates 567 

The addition of environmental covariates provides an opportunity to balance the 568 

introduction of precision variables with the potential to induce colliders due to shared 569 

architecture with a downstream outcome variable being used as a covariate in the model . The 570 

impact of collider bias, collinearity, and confounding on the identification and magnitude of direct 571 

genetic effects often act as justification for excluding heritable environmental covariates from 572 

GWAS and PGS development altogether [5 9], albeit with notable exceptions (e.g., Hoffmann et 573 

al., 2018 [ 60 ]). Here, we sought to test the introduction of environmental covariates that limit the 574 

coheritability t hat could drive potential bias. We focused on traits with limited absolute heritability 575 

to reduce the potential for colliders in our dataset. As we demonstrate, each of the 576

environmental variables alone are associated with effects on the order of centimeters, whereas 577 

the largest singular allelic effect output by the GWAS null or GWAS env is 1.6mm (for p <10 -6). This 578 

then argues for a large non -genetic component to phenotypic variability in contrast to the 579 

marginal genetic contribution per -locus. While the entirety of these effects cannot yet be fully 580 

controlled for, steps can be implemented to mitigate them. When selecting covariates for 581 

inclusion in the GWAS env model, collinearity can be addressed by assessing correlations among 582 

the collection of potential covariates and removing all but one variable in a highly correlated 583 

cluster (see Methods section “Environmental covariates” and SI section 2.2 for details on how 584 

this was implemented here). 585 

To reduce the likelihood of introducing colliders into a GWAS model, both the 586 

associations and direction of hypothesized causal effects among the phenotype of interest and 587 

possible model covariates need to be considered. For collider bias to occur here , height must 588 

have a n associated effect on a covariate used in the GWAS model. It is plausible that height 589 

has an indirect and weak effect on TDI due to sociocultural contexts. Therefore, it is also 590 

possible that an endophenotype of height could be a collider, but we assume that these specific 591 

effects are likely much smaller than the collective impact of overall health on height. Prior work 592 

has demonstrated that the strength of collider bias should be almost perfectly inverse to the 593 

association between collider and exposure variables [61 ]. I f collider bias is introduced, and our 594 

assumptions about the relative magnitude of causal effects of height on TDI or health are true, 595 

then we can expect for these biases to be minimal. Therefore, we argue that h eight is unlikely to 596 

exert more than minimal influence on TDI or health measures . We note that, while previous 597 

estimates of perceived health may contribute significant heritability in the white British 598 

population in UK Biobank, in our own measures we did not observe this same trend in South 599 

Asians, suggesting a minimal effect to no effect in our population of interest . However, due to 600 

the limitations of collider s within causal models , these assumptions cannot be empirically 601 

confirmed within our dataset. Further discussion on the implications of genetic colliders and 602 

confounders introduced by the model covariates can be found in SI Section 2.5 “Extended 603 

Discussion: Heritability of environmental covariates” .604 

Lastly, we do note that the UKB only provides data on adult participants, reflecting the 605 

participants’ recent behavior and what can be recalled from childhood. It is assumed that in 606 

most cases the participant responses regarding current diet and socioeconomic status do not 607 

deviate significantly from childhood patterns, though this cannot be explicitly tested within the 608 

data. There is some research to indicate that this is a reasonable assumption, though it is a 609 

limitation of the study [6 5, 6 6]. 610

GWAS and PGS comparisons 612 

We demonstrate that including select environmental covariates in the model refines 613 

GWAS results; specifically, we i ncreased variant detection, reduce genomic inflation (smaller 614 

values of λ) and reduce effect size (β) standard errors when including environmental covariates 615 

in our GWAS env . Taken together, this suggests that: 1) adjusting for environmental influences 616 

reduces some of the noise that may be mitigating direct genetic effects; 2) the significance of 617 

some variants may be falsely inflated when environmental confounders aren’t adjusted for, 618 

leading to misidentification of causal genetic loci (e.g., the genetic variant is not causal but 619 

covaries with environmental variation); or 3) by incorporating environmental adjustments, SNP β 620 

values are more stable (i.e., have lower uncertainty) and likely reflect more accurate estimates 621 

of “true” effects. Furthermore, SNPs with large deviations in significance between the GWAS 622 

here may represent interactions with one or more of the environmental variables included in 623 

GWAS env . Additional investigation into these variants may generate candidate SNPs for future 624 

GxE research. 625

CONCLUSIONS 644 

645 

Even though ancestry -diverse biobank cohorts remain small, there are steps we can take to 646 

maximize the utility of these samples within the context of genotype -phenotype research. In 647 

addition to commonly applied adjustments, e.g., incorporating LD -informed methods, some 648 

fundamental aspects of research design can be implemented to improve the quality of the 649 

results. We argue that the improving precision in trait measurement and ancestry assignment, 650 

as well as including environmental covariates facilitate the performance of PGS. If the aim of 651 

PGS implementation is not just to predict a trait expression or health outcome, but to 652 

understand why the outcome is predicted, then covariate effects need to be interpretable for 653 

individuals across populations. As such, if biobank -level data is accessible, it should be 654 

standard practice for GWASs of height to incorporate, at a minimum, covariates that may adjust 655 

for within -population variance in height explained by nutritional or socioeconomic variation 656 

amongst individuals. Such exploration remains a challenging, but likely fruitful area for new 657 

research. 658

Environmental covariates 793 

To maximize the retention of participants incorporated in the GWAS, environmental 794 

covariates were imputed using classification and regression trees (CART) within the R statistical 795 

package MICE (Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations), resulting in five completed datasets 796 

[73, 74] (Figure S2.3). Missingness of each covariate prior to imputation is reported in Table 2 797 

(refer to SI Section 2.2 for further details regarding the environmental covariate imputation 798 

process). 799 

After receiving the initial 283 environmental variables requested, the data were placed 800 

into broad categories (Figure S2.2 B). Within each domain, the etiology of each variable, and its 801 

relationships with all other variables and height were considered (Figure S2.2 B). Correlations 802 

among variables within domains were tested, and data conferring the same or highly correlated 803 

information were removed (e.g., “Long -standing illness” and “Overall health rating”). For each 804 

pair of categorical variables, we constructed 2×2 contingency tables and estimated their 805 

association using odds ratios. For all variable pairs, odds ratios were computed on the log scale, 806 

and pairs with |log(OR)| > 2 (i.e., OR < 0.14 or OR > 7.39) were flagged as strongly associated. 807 

Data hypothesized or known to have a strong genetic etiology that may be shared with the 808 

GWAS phenotype were noted (e.g., sitting height and “comparative body size at age 10”) 809 

following the guidance for covariate selection outlined in Aschard et al. [5 8]. Variables of this 810 

nature are not recommended for use as environmental covariates in the GWAS but were 811 

retained to maximize imputation performance (described below). The data were then assessed 812 

for missingness and all variables falling above 20% missingness were discarded prior to any 813 

further analyses. 814 

Variables that passed the missingness threshold were then subjected to a multiple 815 

imputation (MI) procedure, in order to retain the largest sample of individuals with both genotype 816 

and standing height data. MI has been demonstrated to perform as well as or better than the 817 

methods typically employed for dealing with uncertainty in environmental data within genetic 818 

studies [ 75,7 6,7 7]. Additionally, utilizing MI provides an estimate of the uncertainty produced by 819 

using imputed values in downstream analyses, which more transparently reflects the final 820 

results [ 78, 79,80 ]. 821 

Several of these variables (uniformly, within each of the five completed datasets) were 822 

then recoded for simplicity and to better represent the variable of interest, as it pertains to 823 

skeletal health. For example, a dietary restriction variable (UKB data field 6144) originally 824 

allowed for six categorical responses describing which food products the participant “never 825 

eats”, with “dairy products” as an available option. Of all possible dietary data fields with dairy 826 

consumption information, this data field had the least missingness (~2%) within our study 827 

population. Therefore, this variable was recoded as a binary response, such that all responses 828

[82]. At that time, there were 489 South Asian participants within the full reference panel used 855
