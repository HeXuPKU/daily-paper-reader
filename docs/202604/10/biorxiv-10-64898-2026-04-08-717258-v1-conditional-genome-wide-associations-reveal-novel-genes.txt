## Abstract 14 

We introduce two novel approaches for gene discovery based on conditional genome-wide associations. 15 

Experimental validation of gene targets identified by our top-performing approach uncovers three genes 16 

with a previously unknown role in controlling flowering time in Arabidopsis , one of the most well-17 

studied traits in the most well-studied plant genome. This work demonstrates the power of knockoff-18 

based frameworks to uniquely identify novel genes underlying complex traits, a core task across 19 

applications in agriculture and human health. 20

Understanding the genetic basis of complex traits has remained a foremost goal in the biological 23 

sciences over the past few decades. Yet, large portions of gene space remain fully uncharacterized across 24 

the tree of life. Across the human genome, for example, more than a fifth of protein families still have a 25 

completely unknown function 1. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have served as a keystone 26 

methodology for linking genes to variation in complex traits, with a standard GWAS capable of 27 

producing hundreds or thousands of testable hypotheses of gene function. However, many GWAS hits 28 

end up being false discoveries, and overall, tend to explain only a fraction of genetic variance in traits. 29 

This ‘missing heritability’ problem is thought to stem in large part from a multitude of small effect 30 

variants across the genome, which together contribute a major portion of trait variance but are very 31 

difficult to detect using traditional GWAS methods 2,3 .32 

Recent developments in the field of explainable machine learning have shown improvements over 33 

traditional inference methods for gene discovery from genome-wide data. In particular, the knockoff 34 

filter 4, which strictly controls the false discovery rate (FDR) based on comparisons to synthetically 35 

generated variables, is agnostic of the underlying model architecture linking the explanatory variables 36 

with the response and has been useful to address confounding due to genetic linkage and population 37 

structure 5,6 . While knockoffs, which can be based on fundamentally more flexible models than widely 38 

used generalized linear mixed model (GLMM)-based approaches, are now being applied more broadly 39 

beyond humans, most authors to-date validate these approaches based on the ability to recover known 40 

genes discovered with traditional methods. Such a strategy provides little support for the idea that 41 

knockoffs and related methods should be well-suited to reveal novel genome associations. 42 

Here, we present two novel approaches for genomic feature selection and gene discovery. Our 43 

strategy, based on principles for conditional model reliance (CMR) 7, is conceptually similar to knockoffs, 44 

in that the predictive power of a focal variable is assessed by replacing it with a synthetic variable. Unlike 45 

existing knockoff frameworks, the synthetic variable is generated such that it carries only information 46 

about the dependent variable that can be gleaned from other covariates. In other words, by removing a 47

We benchmarked our CMR-based knockoff approaches (GDIP and GDIP-gk) on simulated data 74 

against existing GWA methods based on group knockoffs (SNPknock) 8 and GLMMs (GEMMA) 9 (Fig. 75 

1). For simulated ‘easy’ traits (low polygenicity and medium effect sizes), we found that all knockoff-76 

based methods including our CMR-based approaches dramatically outperformed linear models. Both of 77 

our CMR-based approaches were characterized by significantly higher recall compared to both SNPknock 78 

and GEMMA. On simulated ‘challenging’ traits (high polygenicity with weak effect sizes), performance 79 

of all methods worsened substantially. However, both of our CMR-based approaches maintained their 80 

advantage of higher recall compared to SNPknock and GEMMA, with GDIP-gk demonstrating less 81 

variability but also higher precision than GDIP. Overall, GDIP-gk was the top-performing method 82 

according to the F1 score, with a median improvement from 1.6x - 2.4x over SNPknock depending on the 83 

task (Fig. 1). 84

Together, our experimental validation results support a key role in flowering time for 3 of 11 hits 133 

(27%) identified using our GDIP-gk and knockoff-based approach, but unidentifiable from a GLMM-134 

based GWAS. A great deal of the ‘missing heritability’ in complex traits may be hiding in plain sight, but 135 

only discoverable with improved methods for gene discovery. Our work suggests that much remains to be 136 

learned about the genotype-phenotype map from the existing treasure trove of GWA datasets. 137

All simulations were conducted using SNP data derived from A. thaliana 15 and supplied as part of 152 

the Naturalgwas package 16 . Naturalgwas generates synthetic phenotypes for existing genomic data, 153 

drawing from principal components to model population structure. Each synthetic phenotype is associated 154 

to randomly chosen causal loci, allowing for a fair comparison of power and false discovery between 155 

methods. We simulated one hundred independent phenotypes for the model plant Arabidopsis thaliana 156 

for each of three types of traits: 1) traits with an ‘easier’ genetic architecture for GWAS, 2) traits with a 157 

moderately challenging architecture, and 3) traits with a challenging genetic architecture. For our 158 

phenotype simulations representing an ‘easy’ GWAS task, 10 causal SNPs were drawn from unlinked 159 

loci, as described previously 16 , with effect sizes of each locus set to 100, representing a moderate effect 160 

size relative to the square root of the eigenvalues of the SNP matrix. For our phenotype simulations 161 

representing a ‘challenging’ GWAS task, we considered 20 causal SNPs and an effect size of 10. Finally, 162 

we also included a ‘moderate’ GWAS task with 15 causal SNPs and an effect size of 50. Each 163 

combination of parameters was represented in 100 separate simulations. Parameter values were chosen to 164 

span a full range of effect sizes and polygenicity as described previously 16 .165 

For each trait, we compared results from our GDIP and GDIP-gk algorithms against a GLMM-166 

based model and an existing knockoffs-based approach. For the GLMM, we used GEMMA with a 167 

centered kinship matrix 9, with FDR control using the Benjamini & Hochberg method 17 , implemented with 168 

the p.adjust function in R. For the existing knockoffs-based approach, we used SNPknock 8 with group 169 

knockoffs constructed from unphased genotypes. 170 

## Arabidopsis 1001 Genomes 171 

We benchmarked GDIP-gk on real data using a genomic dataset of 5,775,084 single nucleotide 172 

polymorphisms (SNPs) and flowering time at 10°C from the 1001 Arabidopsis Genomes Project 18 . This 173 

dataset includes 1,003 natural inbred lines of Arabidopsis collected across its native range in Eurasia and 174 

North Africa. 175

## Experimental validation 176 

From the all 69 regions identified by GDIP-gk significant at an FDR threshold of 0.1 177 

(Supplemental Table S1), we excluded 13 also identified as significant in the GLMM-based analysis. We 178 

then manually selected 11 GDIP-gk regions for follow-up experimental validation (Supplemental Table 179 

S3), chosen to be reasonably evenly distributed across the genome. Each of the 11 regions was associated 180 

with at least one and up to four gene models that were within 5 kb either up or downstream of the GDIP-181 

gk hit, totalling 32 genes. We obtained Arabidopsis T-DNA insertion mutant lines for 30 genes that were 182 

represented in the SALK collection and available from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center. After 183 

removing two lines with low germination (SALK_133903C and SALK_040805C), this left 28 total genes 184 

for evaluation. 185 

Seeds were planted in Sungro Promix soil and stratified in the dark at 4C for 7 days. Plants were 186 

transferred to Percival LED-41L2 growth chambers and grown at constant 10°C and 16-hour daylength. 187 

Light intensity was 90 μmol·m⁻²·s⁻¹. After germination, seedlings were thinned to 1 plant per well. Plants 188 

were grown in randomized complete blocks, with 1 replicate per block and 12 replicates per genotype 189 

total. The date of first flowering was recorded for each plant. 190 

Time to flowering was analyzed with a linear mixed model (lme4 v 1.1-37, R version 4.4.2), with 191 

accession as a fixed effect and block as a random effect. Post-hoc comparisons were analyzed with 192 

estimated marginal means (R package emmeans v 2.0.0). A time-to-event analysis was also performed (R 193 

package survival v 3.7-0) with log-rank comparisons of each accession. The results of the two models 194 

were consistent, and the results of the linear mixed model are presented in text. Results of the survival 195 

analysis are presented in Supplementary Table 4. 196
