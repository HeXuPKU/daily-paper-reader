# Abstract 10 

Data contamination—from recording errors to extreme outliers—can compromise 11 

statistical models by biasing predictions, inflating prediction errors, and, in severe cases, 12 

destabilizing performance in high-dimensional settings. Although contamination can 13 

affect responses and covariates, we focus on response contamination and evaluate 14 

Random Forests through simulation. Using a synthetic animal-breeding dataset, we 15 

assess robust Random Forests across several contamination scenarios and validate them 16 

on plant and animal datasets. We thereby clarify the consequences of contamination for 17 

prediction, develop a robust Random Forest framework, and evaluate its performance. 18 

We examine preprocessing or data-transformation strategies, algorithmic modifications, 19 

and hybrid approaches for robustifying Random Forests. Across these approaches, data 20 

transformation emerges as the most effective strategy, delivering the strongest 21 

performance under contamination. This strategy is simple, general, and transferable to 22 

other Machine Learning methods, offering a remedy for robust genomic prediction. In 23 

real breeding data, robust Random Forests are useful when substantial contamination, 24 

phenotypic corruption, misrecording, or train–deployment mismatch is plausible and the 25 

goal is to recover a latent signal for genomic prediction and selection; ranking-based 26 

robust Random Forests are the dependable first option, whereas weighting-based 27 

Random Forests should be used only when their weighting scheme preserves rank 28 

structure and improves prediction. Robustification is not universally necessary, but it 29

# Author summary 35 

Machine learning (ML) methods are widely used for prediction with high-dimensional, 36 

complex data, and supervised approaches such as Random Forests (RF) have proved 37 

effective for genomic prediction (GP) and selection. Yet their performance can be 38 

severely compromised by data contamination if the algorithms rely on classical 39 

data-driven procedures that are sensitive to atypical observations. Robustifying ML 40 

methods is therefore important both for improving predictive performance under 41 

contamination and for guiding their practical use in high-dimensional prediction 42 

problems. To address this need, we develop robust preprocessing, algorithm-level, and 43 

hybrid strategies for improving RF performance with contaminated data. Using 44 

simulated animal data, we show that ranking- and weighting-based robust RF provide 45 

the strongest overall compromise for genomic prediction and selection under 46 

contamination. Validation on several plant and animal breeding datasets further shows 47 

that the benefits of robustification are not universal, but depend on the dataset, trait, 48 

and breeding objective. Although motivated by RF, the framework we propose is 49 

general, practical, and readily transferable to other ML methods. It also offers a basis 50 

for deciding when robustness should complement standard RF rather than replace it 51 

outright. 52

decision-making. In plant, animal, and human genomics, a flagship application is 56 

genomic prediction (GP): forecasting complex quantitative phenotypes—such as yield, 57

Yet flexibility does not guarantee reliability. Like classical methods, ML models can 65

predictive accuracy (PA) and precision [ 15 , 16, 17 , 18 , 19 , 20 , 21 , 22 , 23 ]. Motivated by 68 

the combined effects of many small-effect loci and environmental influences under the 69 

central limit theorem, approximate normality is often invoked for continuous 70 

quantitative traits. In practice, however, phenotypes are often contaminated by 71 

unobserved biological or environmental influences that generate a small fraction of 72 

observations deviating from the bulk distribution, thereby driving marked departures 73 

from normality. These model-relative outliers are not always visually extreme (and may 74 

therefore appear as inliers) and can reflect localized environmental effects or biological 75 

stressors in plant and animal studies (e.g., specific years, field pests, management 76 

conditions), cohort effects or unobserved heterogeneity in human studies, or other latent 77 

sources of variability. Although excluding such observations is often advocated 78 

[24, 25, 26, 27], reliable outlier detection remains inherently challenging, especially 79 

under masking and swamping effects [28]. Importantly, outliers can be scientifically 80 

meaningful, particularly in breeding contexts, making it undesirable to discard them; 81 

what is needed, instead, are modeling approaches that limit their undue influence while 82 

retaining the information they carry [29]. Conceptually distinct—but often 83 

conflated—are data collection or recording errors, which arise from data-acquisition 84 

failures and are typically addressed through data curation. 85 

This vulnerability motivates a central challenge in GP: robustifying high-dimensional 86 

prediction, including ML-based prediction. Even when ML methods do not posit an 87 

explicit distribution for the response, their training and stability can remain sensitive to 88 

the empirical phenotype distribution; departures from normality can destabilize fitting, 89

Here we address this gap by developing robust random forests (RF) for GP. We 95

# Data contamination 214 

In this work, contamination refers broadly to any mechanism that perturbs the 215 

data-generating process, including recording errors, discretization artifacts, placeholder 216 

values, and unobserved biological or environmental influences. To model the subset of 217 

contamination that generates distributional deviations, we adopt Huber’s 218 

ε-contamination framework, in which a small fraction of observations is drawn from a 219 

contaminating distribution G. In our setting, these observations constitute the 220 

model-relative outliers. Although the literature recognizes alternative definitions of 221 

outliers, including distance-based, density-based, and residual-based approaches 222 

[42, 43, 44, 45], we adopt the model-based perspective induced by the contamination 223 

framework. Thus, while contamination spans a wide range of real-world processes, only 224 

the subset represented by the G-component is treated as outlying in our model. 225 

We empirically evaluate the comparative predictive performance of standard and 226 

robustified RF methods on the uncontaminated and contaminated simulated animal 227 

dataset. Contaminated responses are generated under Huber’s contamination mixture 228 

model (1 − ε)F + εG , where F is the original data distribution, G is a contaminant 229 

distribution, and ε is the proportion of contaminating observations [ 46, 47 ]. Because the 230 

baseline responses are approximately N (μ, σ 2) with μ and σ2 estimated from the data, 231 

contamination is implemented by replacing a proportion ε of observations with draws 232 

from G. We consider ε = 2% , 5% & 10% and four contamination types: (i) shift 233 

contamination, with outliers drawn from N (μ + kσ, σ 2); (ii) variance-inflated 234 

contamination, with outliers drawn from N (μ, (sσ )2); (iii) central 235 

variance-deflated (pointmass type) contamination, with outliers drawn from 236

Random Forests for Regression 254 

Random forests [ 48 ] are tree-based ensemble algorithms for classification and regression 255 

and are well suited for high-dimensional problems, including SNP-based predictions. A 256 

schematic representation of the RF algorithm is shown in Figure 2. RF draws N257 

bootstrap samples from the original dataset; each sample is used to grow one tree in the 258 

ensemble. Each tree is constructed by recursively partitioning its bootstrap sample 259 

using a splitting rule until a stopping criterion is satisfied. At every split, only a random 260

RF performance is shaped by hyperparameters that govern ensemble complexity and 276 

tree diversity. The number of trees controls a stability–cost trade-off: more trees 277 

typically stabilize predictions but increase computational time. The number of 278 

candidate features m at each split regulates inter-tree correlation and is therefore 279 

central to tuning: overly large m yields similar trees and limited variance reduction, 280 

whereas overly small m can undersample informative predictors and degrade accuracy. 281 

The minimum number of observations in a terminal (leaf) node controls tree depth and 282 

the bias–variance trade-off: node size one produces fully grown trees, whereas larger 283 

values constrain depth, increasing bias while reducing variance. Together, these tuning 284 

parameters modulate how RF explores high-dimensional predictor spaces and how 285 

reliably it generalizes. 286

Robust Strategies 303 

The lack of robustness of standard RF can be traced to several algorithmic 304 

components—mean-based splitting criteria, aggregation by averaging, and non-robust 305 

resampling and performance assessment—each of which can amplify the influence of 306 

outliers. Two complementary strategy classes mitigate these effects: preprocessing-based 307 

remedies that aim to robustify the data before fitting RF, and algorithm-based remedies 308 

that modify RF itself. Preprocessing strategies leave the RF algorithm unchanged but 309 

reduce contamination influence by transforming the inputs—most commonly the 310 

response—providing a natural, computationally simple pathway to robustness that can 311 

be deployed without redesigning the learning procedure. Algorithmic strategies, in 312 

contrast, introduce robustness directly into RF by altering resampling schemes, impurity 313 

measures or aggregation rules; several such methods exist, although not in the context 314 

of GP studies, and are reviewed in the following sections. In what follows, we examine 315 

six preprocessing-based approaches and four algorithm-based approaches, thereby 316 

isolating where robustness can be gained most simply—at the data interface of RF. 317 

Preprocessing-Based Approaches 318 

When quantitative responses depart from normality or contain outliers, a common 319 

remedy is to transform the response prior to model fitting. Below we detail six 320 

preprocessing strategies. 321

5. Exploratory: Median Winsorization 403 

Beyond classical winsorization, we consider a variant that replaces extreme values 404 

in both tails with the sample median. Unlike standard winsorization—which 405 

truncates to tail quantiles (e.g., 5th and 95th percentiles)—median winsorization 406 

collapses tail extremes toward the center to further dampen outlier influence. This 407 

strategy is not designed to approximate normality and can distort the distribution, 408 

especially under asymmetric contamination: concentrating probability mass near 409 

the center can inflate the peak and reduce variability, potentially exacerbating 410 

departures from normality. 411 

6. Exploratory: Robust Weighting 412

this approach, we first fit a robust location-only model to the response by 414 

M-estimation, yielding observation-level robustness weights ωi ∈ [0 , 1], derived 415 

from the Huber influence function applied to standardized residuals. The 416 

M-estimation procedure uses the Huber loss with tuning constant c = 1 .345, so 417 

large-residual observations are down-weighted while central observations retain 418 

full weight. 419

These six preprocessing strategies—two parametric transformation families (Box–Cox; 438

shared logic is simple but consequential: weaken the leverage of extremes before the 442 

forest splits and averages, and the downstream ensemble becomes steadier, not by 443

Four robust algorithm-based approaches 445 

Robustifying RF at the algorithmic level targets the components through which 446 

contamination propagates—resampling, splitting, and aggregation—thereby reducing 447

MAE impurity, and robust aggregation via (iii) median- and (iv) quantile-based rules. 450

Aggregation level 484 

3. Standard RF aggregates tree predictions by averaging; under contamination, a 485 

small subset of extreme tree predictions can bias the mean. A robust alternative 486 

aggregates by the median (the 0.5 conditional quantile), which dampens the effect 487 

of anomalous predictions and stabilizes the ensemble. 488 

4. More generally, conditional-quantile aggregation, as in Quantile RF, supports 489

quantiles; 67). 491 

Operationally, both median aggregation and quantile regression forests alter only the 492 

pooling step; tree construction remains identical to that of a standard RF. Under 493 

median aggregation, tree-wise point predictions are combined using their median rather 494 

than their mean, thereby reducing sensitivity to extreme tree predictions. By contrast, 495 

quantile regression forests retain the full set of training responses associated with the 496 

terminal nodes reached across trees, preserving distributional information that is then 497 

used at the aggregation stage to estimate the conditional response distribution and to 498 

derive point predictions such as the median, as well as other conditional quantile 499

We adopt a sequential evaluation strategy—screen, retain, and stress-test—to preserve 509 

interpretability and avoid an uninformative combinatorial expansion of methods. 510 

Preprocessing- and algorithm-level remedies are first assessed separately under shift 511 

contamination; only competitive methods advance to variance-inflated 512 

contamination and, if still competitive, to central variance-deflated contamination 513 

and finally to tail variance-deflated contamination. The most competitive 514 

preprocessing and algorithm-level approaches are then combined into hybrid strategies, 515 

which undergo the same sequential screening; only combinations integrating the most 516 

effective components are retained, focusing inference on plausible robustification 517 

pathways rather than exhaustive, low-yield search. Each method was screened and 518 

retained or advanced as follows. Using the original (uncontaminated) data, the standard 519 

RF achieves a PA of approximately 0 .755, which defines the baseline against which 520 

contamination-induced losses are evaluated. To operationalize the sequential screening 521 

across contamination scenarios, we imposed a minimum performance criterion set at 522 

∼ 80% of this baseline, yielding a threshold of PA = 0 .6. Methods attaining PA ≥ 0.6523

Predictive performance assessment 577 

Genomic prediction estimates genetic values—such as genomic estimated breeding 578 

values (GEBVs)—from molecular markers using statistical or machine-learning models. 579 

Because the central objective is accurate numerical prediction, performance is most 580 

directly assessed using error-based criteria, including the prediction-error metrics 581 

defined above. Predictive accuracy is also widely reported because it is scale-free and 582 

easily interpretable, summarizing how well predicted and true values align in rank. Yet 583 

PA can mask systematic bias or rescaling and, when absolute error is the primary 584 

concern, may not faithfully represent prediction quality. 585 

Genomic selection, in contrast, is a downstream decision framework that uses genomic 586 

predictions to rank candidates and choose superior individuals for breeding. Here, 587 

correct ordering—not precise calibration—is the operative goal; thus, PA becomes the 588 

more decision-relevant metric, capturing the fidelity of the ranking that selection 589 

ultimately acts upon. 590 

Accordingly, GP prioritizes accurate values and is best evaluated by error-based 591 

measures, whereas genomic selection prioritizes accurate ranks and is therefore best 592 

summarized by PA—two aims, two evaluation targets, one coherent pipeline from 593 

prediction to selection. 594

# Simulation Results 634 

The relationship between contamination and predictive performance is not invariably 635 

monotonic. In some scenarios, increasing the proportion of contamination does not 636 

immediately reduce PA or increase PEs. This pattern arises because contaminated 637 

observations do not always appear as clearly identifiable outliers. When perturbations 638 

remain within the range of the bulk of the data, they can act as masked points or inliers 639

contamination may therefore fail to cause an immediate deterioration in predictive 641 

performance, a result consistent with the masking effects well documented in the 642 

statistics literature [69, 70, 71]. 643

substantially distorting scale. This partial preservation of rank, coupled with strong 1250

# Discussion and Conclusion 1296 

The substantial variability of RF results across random seeds justifies systematic 1297 

attention to seed dependence when optimizing predictive performance. A practical 1298 

implication is that one should search for a favourable random seed during model 1299 

development and then fix that seed for reproducibility. Even so, exact replication may 1300 

still vary across machines, computing platforms, and software versions. To further 1301 

reduce seed-driven variation, it is advisable to generate predictions from several 1302 

replicated fits and average them. This strategy does not eliminate algorithmic 1303 

randomness, but it does tame it and thus yields more stable inference. 1304 

Simulation setting 1305 

The simulation results reveal most clearly when robustification matters and why. When 1306 

the training data are contaminated and the test data remain clean, robust RF methods 1307 

reduce the influence of atypical observations, recover the underlying signal more 1308 

faithfully, and maintain more stable predictive performance across contamination 1309 

scenarios. Under those conditions, robustification is not a cosmetic adjustment but a 1310 

functional safeguard, because the predictive target is the uncontaminated signal rather 1311 

than the contaminated empirical distribution. The standard RF, by contrast, is more 1312

The uncontaminated simulation results show the complementary case in which 1315 

robustness is not needed and may even exact a small cost. When both the training and 1316 

test data are clean, the standard RF exploits the full structure of the data without 1317 

having to discount any observations and therefore tends to perform best. In that setting, 1318 

robust methods can lose some efficiency because they protect against contamination that 1319 

is absent. The central lesson is therefore conditional rather than absolute: robust RF 1320 

excels when contamination distorts the training data relative to the predictive target, 1321 

whereas the standard RF excels when the data are clean and no protection is required. 1322 

Real data setting 1323 

The real plant and animal datasets present a more difficult and more biologically 1324 

realistic setting because the true data-generating mechanism is unknown. The training 1325 

and test sets are sampled from the same observed data and therefore are likely to share 1326 

non-normality, outliers, heavy tails, and heterogeneous structure. A crucial consequence 1327 

follows: the test set is not the truth, but only a proxy for the unknown target, such as 1328 

the underlying breeding value. Predictive evaluation is therefore conducted against the 1329 

observed empirical distribution rather than against the unobserved signal that 1330 

generated it. 1331 

This distinction helps explain why the standard RF seemingly performs better on 1332 

real data. Because it learns the full empirical distribution, including its irregularities, it 1333 

can align more closely with test data drawn from that same distribution. When unusual 1334 

values or heterogeneous substructures occur in both training and test sets, fitting them 1335 

is not necessarily a defect under empirical prediction; indeed, it may improve apparent 1336 

predictive performance. In such cases, the standard RF benefits from matching the 1337 

evaluation target, even when that target itself contains distortions. 1338 

By contrast, robust RF methods are designed to reduce the influence of atypical 1339 

observations in the training data. That is an advantage when the analytic goal is to 1340 

recover the underlying signal, as in the contaminated-versus-clean simulation setting, 1341 

but it can become a disadvantage when the test data contain the same atypical 1342

The real-data results therefore argue against a simple verdict either for or against 1348 

robustification. Superior performance of the standard RF on observed real datasets does 1349 

not prove that robustness is unnecessary; rather, it shows that empirical prediction and 1350 

signal recovery are not always the same objective. When breeders seek the best 1351 

prediction within the observed population structure, the standard RF may often suffice 1352 

and may indeed be optimal. When, however, there is reason to suspect substantial 1353

responses and the latent target of selection, robust RF becomes much more attractive. 1355 

The choice should thus depend on the inferential aim, the expected contamination 1356 

structure, and the anticipated relation between training and deployment data. 1357 

The ranking- and weighting-based robustification strategies remain especially 1358 

attractive because they are conceptually simple, flexible, and broadly portable across 1359 

machine-learning methods. They operate through transformations of the response or 1360 

through observation-level weighting and therefore do not require altering the core RF 1361 

learning algorithm. That generality is a practical strength. Yet the results also show 1362 

that not all robustification strategies are equally reliable. Across both simulations and 1363 

real data, the ranking-based RF was generally the more stable robust alternative, often 1364 

preserving much of the predictive performance of the standard RF while offering greater 1365 

protection under contamination. Its main strength is that it dampens the influence of 1366 

extreme responses without relying on highly sensitive weighting schemes. 1367 

The weighting-based approach offers greater flexibility, but that flexibility is 1368 

double-edged because its performance depends strongly on how the weights are 1369 

constructed. The analyses of the carbon isotope ratio in the soybean data, and the body 1370

poorly with standard Huber weights but markedly better with Huber weights shrunk 1372

high-dimensional genomic settings with correlated markers. Robustification may 1397 

sharpen the signal, but it does not convert variable importance into a direct measure of 1398
