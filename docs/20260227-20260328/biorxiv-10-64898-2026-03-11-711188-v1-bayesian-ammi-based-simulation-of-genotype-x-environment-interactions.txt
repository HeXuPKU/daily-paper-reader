# Abstract 14 

Genotype-by-environment interaction (GEI) has been studied to identify environment-15 stable/favorable genotypes. The GEI simulation could help refine the inference by incorporating 16 tangible factors such as genomic and environmental information. The Bayesian additive main 17 effect and multiplicative interaction (Bayesian AMMI) model captures the genotype-specific 18 responses across environments, reflecting directional relationships between genotypes and 19 environments. Thus, we propose a Bayesian AMMI-based GEI simulation framework that 20 utilizes high-throughput environmental covariance matrices to generate GEI effects with 21 interpretable directional structure. To demonstrate the proposed approach, two simulated 22 phenotypes were assessed under four levels of GEI variance. In the first simulation (Sim1), GEI 23 effects were sampled from a multivariate normal distribution defined by the GEI matrix. In the 24 second simulation (Sim2), GEI effects were generated by extending Sim1 with the Bayesian 25 AMMI model. In both simulations, increasing GEI variance resulted in lower correlations of 26 phenotypes across environments and stronger genotype-specific sensitivity to environmental 27 variation. Across five cross-validation designs, models accounting for GEI consistently 28 outperformed one that did not, with prediction accuracy generally decreasing as GEI variance 29 increased. Clear distinctions between the two simulated phenotypes were evident from biplot 30 analyses: Sim2 successfully captured environmental relatedness and genotype-specific 31 responses, whereas such structure was absent in Sim1. These results demonstrate that the 32 proposed Bayesian AMMI-based GEI simulation framework enables interpretable visualization 33 of GEI and supports genomic selection strategies under complex environmental conditions. 34

Introduction 36 

The genotype-by-environment interaction (GEI) has been studied in animal and plant breeding 37 aiming to select genotypes that perform consistently (stably) across environments or favorably 38 (locally adapted) in specific environments. Visualization of GEI patterns using biplots is widely 39 applied in plant breeding as an approach to identify genotypes relevant to environments (Fonseca 40 et al. 2022; Jarquín et al. 2016). Recently, Kebede et al. (2024) applied this approach in chickens 41 to explore breed-by-environment interactions, aiming to optimize breeding strategies for 42 smallholder farms. This work highlights the potential for transferring GEI visualization 43 methodologies developed in plant breeding to animal breeding contexts. However, evaluation of 44 genotype performance across diverse and potentially unobserved environments remains 45 challenging. In this sense, simulations  for  evaluation of  genotype performance across 46 

environments , accounting for interpretable visualization information  have  become crucial. 47 

Existing studies utilizing GEI simulations frequently adopt simplified environmental models, 48 which potentially limits their applicability to complex, multi-faceted ecosystems (Liu et al. 2024; 49 Majumdar et al. 2021; Verhulst 2025). However, the actual GEI is likely a genotype’ s response 50 to complex environmental conditions. To account for complex environmental conditions in GEI 51 simulation, we propose the use of high-dimensional environmental covariates within a Bayesian 52 additive main effect and multiplicative interaction (Bayesian AMMI) model. 53 

The AMMI model is a method widely used in plant breeding to characterize GEI patterns 54 between genotypes and environments (Gauch 1988). This combines analysis of variance for 55 additive effects and singular value decomposition (SVD) for non-additive effects (Gauch 1992). 56 This approach enables the visualization of GEI in biplot which provides directional relationships 57 among genotypes and environments. These directional relationships illustrate the extent of 58 genotype stability or their adaptability to specific environments, thereby assisting breeders to 59

To address this constraint, Bayesian treatment of AMMI was introduced to allow inference-62 based estimation of interaction parameters (Crossa et al. 2011; Viele and Srinivasan 2000). 63 Especially as a solution to overparameterization in AMMI, Crossa et al. (2011) proposed using 64 the von Mises-Fisher (VMF) distribution in the Gibbs sampler to simulate the interaction 65 parameters. This approach allows for the derivation of credible intervals for the GEI parameter 66 based on directional relationships between genotypes and environments. In this sense, employing 67 appropriately simulated genotypic and environmental data as prior information, using the VMF 68 distribution, holds the potential to generate new GEI values that incorporate directional 69 information of GEI. 70 

Simulation of environmental variables can be achieved through environmental covariates. 71 Jarquín et al. (2014) introduced covariance structures to account for main and interaction effects 72 of markers and environmental covariates, addressing computational challenges associated with 73 high-dimensional environmental data. In the same way as the genomic relationship matrix 74 (GRM), an environmental covariance matrix can be constructed using environmental covariates, 75 and environmental effects can be represented as random variables drawn from this matrix. 76 Extending this concept, incorporating the environmental covariance matrix into GEI modeling 77 allows for simulation of GEI variables that reflect environmental conditions. 78 

Therefore, the objective of this study was to propose a simulation framework for GEI that 79 employs the Bayesian AMMI model utilizing an environmental covariance matrix. The 80 framework enables GEI simulation for environments characterized by complex, high-throughput 81 environmental covariates. Results & Discussion 248 

Phenotypes and marker effects 249 

The phenotypic correlations across the four simulated environments were compared to evaluate 250 whether the simulated GEI effects reflected the environmental variability. Correlations among 251 phenotypes generally reflected the imposed environmental trends, and their magnitude varied 252 according to the specified GEI variance (Table 3). In both Sim1 and Sim2, phenotypic 253 performance was increasingly influenced by environments as the GEI variance increased. 254 Phenotypic correlations between similar environmental conditions (E1 and E2; E3 and E4) 255 changed little across different GEI variance levels (Figure 2). 256 

Conversely, correlations between contrasting climate types fluctuated, which were close to or 257 below 0 when the GEI variance was set to 1 or 2. As the GEI variance increased, the gap 258 between correlations from similar climate trends (E1 and E2; E3 and E4) and those from 259 contrasting climates (e.g. E1 to E3 or E4) widened. This trend is consistent with the study by 260 Kolmodin and Bijma (2004), which stated that the higher selection response to environments was 261 identified with a higher degree of GEI effects. Both Sim1 and Sim2 exhibited comparable overall 262 phenotypic correlation patterns. 263 

The trends of SNP solutions for correlations across environments were similar to those observed 264 for phenotypes (results not provided). This was consistent with the role of GEI in determining 265 the strength of relationship between genomic trends and environments. Similar with the 266 phenotypic correlation comparisons, the numerical differences between Sim1 and Sim2 were not 267 significant. 268

Regression coefficient 269 

In both phenotypes, the intensity of regression coefficients increased with GEI variance, 270 accompanied by large standard deviations (SD;Table 4). These large SD likely reflected the 271 genotype-specific nature of GEI in both phenotypes. Since the genotypes in this study were 272 randomly selected, the expected response of genotypes to different environments could vary due 273 to difference in the genomic structures of individuals within a population. The trend of 274 variability in this comparison has also been observed in empirical data. In haplotype-by-275 environment studies, the environmental response of genotypes was evaluated at the haplotype 276 levels (Chen et al. 2007; Patiranage et al. 2021). Fine-scale GEI studies in both animal and plant 277 breeding have demonstrated the environmental responsiveness of individual genetic variants as 278 well. For example, Smith et al. (2022) identified sixteen QTL regions associated with growth 279 traits in U.S. Red Angus cattle through a genome-wide association study that accounted for GEI. 280 Similarly, Li et al. (2018) reported significant QTL-by-environment interaction using multi-281 environment and multi-traits QTL analyses for drought tolerance in maize. 282

The genomic prediction accuracy under different levels of GEI variance was evaluated across 284 scenarios. The prediction accuracy from all models decreased as GEI variance increased (Table 285 5). Both M2 and M3 models exhibited better accuracy than M1, which didn’t account for GEI in 286 

the model. The M2 predicted phenotypes more accurately compared to M3 across all scenarios 287 (Figure 3). 288

Utilization of simulation framework 340 

Applying the Bayesian AMMI framework provides two novel insights for GEI simulation: (1) 341 Integration of high-dimensional environmental covariates. The computational cost of building 𝛀 342 is not strongly dependent on the number of environmental covariates. In this sense, the more 343 complex high-dimensional environmental covariates can be considered for GEI simulation study. 344 This allows researchers to account for realized interactions among multiple environmental 345 conditions, enabling the generation of more complex and realistic environmental scenarios. (2) 346 Simulation of directional relationships between genotypes and environments. The Bayesian 347 AMMI approach captures directional relationships accounting for GEI by incorporating pre-348 simulated variables specific to the given generation. This facilitates the investigation of genotype 349 stability and its variation under different breeding strategies. While this simulation framework 350 enables the generation of GEI variables with appropriate directional relationships, future studies 351 should extend the framework to account for multiple chromosomes. 352 

In conclusion, the Bayesian AMMI framework, incorporating high-dimensional environmental 353 covariates, effectively captured directional genotype-environment relationships, producing biplot 354 representations that accurately reflect environmental similarity. This framework enables 355 appropriate simulation of GEI accounting for complex environmental conditions. Based on these 356 findings, we propose using a Bayesian AMMI framework for GEI simulation, with future 357 development needed to extend its application to multiple chromosomes. 358
