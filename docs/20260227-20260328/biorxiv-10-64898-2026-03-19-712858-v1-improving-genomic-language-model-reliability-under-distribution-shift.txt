# Abstract 

Transformer-based Genomic Language Models (GLMs) have achieved strong performance across diverse genomic prediction tasks. However, their tendency toward overconfident predictions—particularly on noisy or unfamiliar data—limits reliability. In genomics, where unknown species and novel variants are common, developing models robust to distribution shift is crucial for dependable predictions. Here, we analyze the impact of several common and novel uncertainty quantification (UQ) methods in the context of GLMs, evaluating their performance across diverse downstream genomic and metagenomic prediction tasks. Comparing model behavior on both in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) data, we show that temperature scaling and epistemic neural networks are capable of improving classification reliability across multiple GLM architectures and domains. # Introduction 1

In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have risen to become one of the central 2

tools in academia and industry due to their flexibility and ability to learn on unlabeled 3

data [1–3]. In biology, the sequential nature of nucleotide and amino acid sequences 4

makes genomics tasks a suitable application for these tools [4]. Here, genomic sequences 5

are treated as structured, information-rich texts, enabling models to understand 6

genomes in a manner analogous to natural language. These genomic language models 7

(GLMs) are trained on a massive corpus of DNA to predict masked sequences [5–7], 8

building powerful sequence representations capable of diverse downstream predictive 9

tasks, including gene expression prediction, variant effect estimation, and the 10 

classification of regulatory elements [8–12]. As a result, GLMs are rapidly becoming a 11 

powerful alternative to traditional bioinformatics pipelines, enabling insights into both 12 

conserved and species-specific mechanisms of genome function [13, 14]. 13 

However, while fine-tuning these models on domain specific tasks has resulted in 14 

significant performance gains over both traditional techniques and models trained from 15 

scratch, they often fail to accurately assess data that is out of distribution (OOD) [15], 16 

resulting in overconfident predictions when applied to new domains [16, 17]. Therefore, 17 

it remains an open question: How can we produce more trustworthy genomic AI? 18

# Methods 49 

Genomic Language Models 50 

Genomic language models (GLMs), such as DNABERT [6, 7] and Nucleotide 51 

Transformer [5], leverage the context-aware capacity of transformer-based networks for 52 

genomic sequence modeling. In this study, GLMs are employed as foundation models : 53 

we adopt publicly available pretrained checkpoints, originally trained on large-scale 54 

genomic sequence corpora, and subsequently fine-tune them for specific downstream 55 

prediction tasks. 56 

We consider several such foundation models, spanning both transformer-based 57 

architectures (Nucleotide Transformer [5], DNABERT-2 [7]) and more novel long-range 58 

models (HyenaDNA [9], CARMANIA [26]). All models are fine-tuned for task-specific 59 

classification using the Hugging Face AutoModelForSequenceClassification 60 

API [27]. 61

epistemic uncertainty by augmenting a predictor with an epistemic index z [42]. The 122 

index is treated as a latent random variable (drawn from a fixed distribution) that 123 

parameterizes a family of predictors fθ (x, z ), where each value of z corresponds to a 124

predictive distributions {p(y | x, z )}. Averaging these predictions provides an estimate 127 

of the marginal predictive distribution, while variability across index samples provides a 128 

principled, ensemble-like summary of epistemic uncertainty without training multiple 129 

independent models. 130

Regulatory sequence benchmarks. Short regulatory sequence tasks (enhancers, 160

experiments, we train models to predict gene classes exclusively, using the three 172 

provided evaluation conditions to function as different levels of distribution shift: test , 173 

a held-out split sampled from the same distribution as the training set (ID); taxa out 174 

holds out specific taxa present in the original dataset while maintaining the same gene 175 

classes (Near-ID); and gene out holds out genes and their associated labels (OOD). 176 

Simulated bacterial and non-bacterial reads. For our taxanomic classification 177 

setting, we use Pbsim [48] to simulate 6kbp reads from a collection reference genomes 178 

sourced from the RefSeq database utilizing Woltka’s pipeline [49, 50], downloaded in 179 

July 24, 2023. Models are then trained on bacterial reads for each taxonomic rank 180 

(family, order, class, and phylum) and evaluated on three test sets: novel genus , 181 

containing reads from previously unseen genera within training families (Near-ID); 182 

novel family , containing reads from bacterial families absent from the training set 183 

(Near-OOD); and nonbacterial , containing reads from non-bacterial taxa (OOD). 184

shifts. On the gene taxa gene-class prediction task, under the majority of the ID 315 

backbone–task pairings, the baseline softmax is already well calibrated and robust to 316

evaluation. In these regimes, additional stochasticity from approximate Bayesian models 319 

(dropout , epinet ) often yields mixed outcomes and can degrade both calibration and 320 

error, consistent with perturbing otherwise stable decision boundaries on matched 321 

distributions. 322 

In contrast, temperature scaling is consistently effective on highly represented 323 

ID settings, where the held-out validation data that is used for correction is similar to 324 

the testing sets. For both datasets ( test and taxa out ), temperature scaling can 325

Under distribution shift, epinet provides the most consistent improvements in 444 

calibration across both domains. In regulatory task mismatches and in metagenomic 445 

novelty regimes (including taxonomic shifts with unseen genera/families), epinet 446 

repeatedly reduces ECE relative to the softmax baseline, and it does so more uniformly 447 

across backbones than MC-dropout. Importantly, these calibration gains often occur 448 

without commensurate reductions in classification error. This separation indicates that 449 

epinet’s primary contribution is to reduce overconfidence and better align predicted 450 

probabilities with empirical correctness, rather than to substantially alter the decision 451 

boundary. For genomics applications, this distinction is practically meaningful: 452 

calibrated probabilities can support thresholding, abstention, and prioritization for 453 

downstream analysis even when raw accuracy remains unchanged [56, 57]. 454 

However, we also find that improved calibration does not imply reliable OOD 455 

detection. Across both regulatory and metagenomic tasks, AUROC-based 456 

discrimination of ID versus OOD examples using uncertainty scores (including 457 

epistemic/aleatoric decompositions [36–38]) is generally inconsistent and often close to 458 

baseline. While there are some notable exceptions–e.g. CARMANIA on metagenomic 459 

taxonomy prediction, where convolutional epinet-derived scores yield meaningful 460 

AUROC improvements on challenging shifts–Results are far from consistent, and OOD 461 

detection performance depends strongly on backbone biases and the specific form of the 462 

uncertainty estimation [18, 34, 39]. While uncertainty-aware deep learning has been 463 

widely studied for out-of-distribution detection, with successful results reported in 464 

image classification, NLP, and a range of other application domains [22, 29, 34, 39], these 465 

same results are not reflected in our genomic sequence tasks. We hypothesize that this 466 

reflects the nature of biologically realistic genomic shifts, which are often near–OOD 467 

rather than far–OOD. Despite novel function or taxonomy, unseen sequences may 468 

remain evolutionarily related or compositionally similar to the training distribution, 469 

reducing the separability of ID and OOD examples by standard uncertainty scores. This 470 

result motivates treating calibration and OOD detection as related but distinct 471 

objectives, as a method can substantially reduce miscalibration under covariate shift 472 

without producing a dependable detector of shifted inputs. 473 

# Conclusion 474 

We benchmarked UQ methods for four GLM backbones across regulatory and 475 

metagenomic tasks under both ID and multiple biologically motivated distribution 476 

shifts. Three conclusions follow. First, on matched ID data, calibration is often already 477 

strong and additional UQ mechanisms provide limited headroom; in this setting or 478 

similar ones, temperature scaling is an effective, low-cost calibration method when the 479 

validation distribution is representative. Second, under distribution shift, calibration 480 

degrades substantially, and epinet provides the most consistent improvements in OOD 481 

calibration across backbones and tasks, reducing overconfidence even when classification 482 

error remains high. Third, uncertainty scores and epistemic/aleatoric decompositions do 483 

not consistently yield strong OOD detection performance as measured by AUROC, 484 

although specific backbone–method combinations can occasionally improve detection in 485 

select settings. Taken together, the most robust near-term benefit of UQ for GLMs is 486 

improved probabilistic calibration, which should be implemented on a case-by-case basis 487 

depending on the degree of expected shift a model may face in practice. 488
