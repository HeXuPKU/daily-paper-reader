Abstract 24 

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) are widely used to identify genetic loci underlying various 25 

agronomic traits. Conventional Manhattan plots provide an effective two-dimensional (2D) summary 26 

of an individual GWAS result. However, recent advances in high-throughput phenotyping have led to 27 

study designs that generate multiple GWAS outputs across time points, traits, or experimental 28 

conditions. In such settings, biological insight increasingly depends on comparative interpretation of 29 

multiple association maps, yet panel-based arrangements of 2D plots fragment related information and 30 

impede recognition of shared or dynamic genetic signals. Here, I present 3D-Manhattan, an interactive 31 

visualization framework that integrates multiple GWAS results within a unified three-dimensional 32 

(3D) coordinate system. By extending the conventional Manhattan plot with an additional axis 33 

representing time, trait, or condition, 3D-Manhattan enables simultaneous, axis-aligned comparison 34 

of association landscapes while preserving genomic coordinates and statistical values. The tool is 35 

implemented as a stand-alone, browser-based application using WebGL-based rendering and supports 36 

smooth interaction without server-side computation. The framework provides flexible visualization 37 

controls, region highlighting, and variant-level correspondence across datasets, facilitating exploratory 38 

analysis of stable and context-dependent genetic associations. Collectively, 3D-Manhattan provides 39 

an alternative approach for visualizing multi-dimensional GWAS results and offers a powerful 40 

platform for visualizing general a series of genome-wide datasets. 41

Introduction 48 

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have become a central analytical framework for dissecting 49 

the genetic basis of quantitative traits across a wide range of biological systems. By testing statistical 50 

associations between genome-wide genetic variation and phenotypic diversity, GWAS have enabled 51 

the identification of loci underlying agronomic performance, physiological processes, and 52 

developmental regulation (Hamblin et al., 2011; Huang and Han, 2014; Lipka et al., 2015; Myles et 53 

al., 2009). The widespread availability of high-density genotyping platforms and reference genomes 54 

has further established GWAS as a routine and scalable approach in both model organisms and crop 55 

species (Huang et al., 2013; Nguyen et al., 2019). Traditionally, the visualization of GWAS results 56 

has relied on Manhattan plots, which provide a compact two-dimensional (2D) representation of 57 

association signals along genomic coordinates (D. Turner, 2018; LiLin-Yin, 2024). This visualization 58 

paradigm has proven highly effective for highlighting genomic regions that exceed statistical 59 

significance thresholds and for facilitating rapid candidate locus identification. As a result, Manhattan 60 

plots have become a de facto standard for reporting GWAS results and are supported by a broad 61 

ecosystem of software tools. 62 

63 

However, the landscape of GWAS applications has changed substantially in recent years. Advances 64 

in high-throughput phenotyping have dramatically expanded both the temporal resolution and 65 

dimensionality of phenotypic data (Yang et al., 2020). Imaging-based phenotyping, continuous sensor 66 

measurements, and automated experimental platforms now routinely generate dense time-series data 67 

and multi-trait measurements across developmental stages or environmental conditions (Bac-68 

Molenaar et al., 2015; Camargo et al., 2018; Neumann et al., 2017, 2015; Pauli et al., 2016; Ren et al., 69 

2018; Wang et al., 2015). These advances have given rise to time-resolved and multi-dimensional 70 

GWAS designs, in which association analyses are performed repeatedly across time points, traits, or 71 

experimental contexts. In such studies, the analytical challenge is no longer limited to interpreting a 72 

single association map. Instead, biological insight increasingly depends on the comparative 73 

interpretation of multiple GWAS outputs: identifying loci whose effects emerge, disappear, or shift 74 

over time; distinguishing stable genetic signals from context-dependent associations; and recognizing 75 

global patterns that span multiple analyses. In practice, these results are often visualized as collections 76 

of separate Manhattan plots arranged across panels or figures. While this approach preserves the 77 

integrity of individual analyses, it fragments related information across disconnected visual spaces and 78 

places a growing cognitive burden on the viewer as the number of plots increases. From a visualization 79 

perspective, this fragmentation represents a fundamental limitation of 2D representations when 80 

applied to inherently multi-dimensional data. When association results are distributed across time, 81 

traits, or experimental conditions, relationships among datasets are not explicitly encoded within a 82 

shared coordinate system. Therefore, subtle but biologically meaningful patterns—such as gradual 83 

shifts in association strength or coordinated changes across loci—may be difficult to recognize through 84 

sequential inspection alone. 85 

86 

Here, I present 3D-Manhattan, an interactive visualization framework designed to address this 87 

limitation by embedding multiple GWAS results within a three-dimensional (3D) space. By extending 88 

the conventional Manhattan plot with an additional axis representing time, trait, or experimental 89 

condition, 3D-Manhattan enables multiple association landscapes to be examined simultaneously 90 

within a unified coordinate system. This representation preserves genomic context while enhancing 91 

the visual continuity across datasets. Coupled with an interactive user interface that supports dynamic 92 

manipulation of viewing parameters, 3D-Manhattan facilitates exploratory analysis of complex 93 

GWAS results and provides an intuitive means to uncover patterns that are difficult to discern using 94

Materials and Methods 97 

Implementation of 3D-Manhattan 98 

3D-Manhattan was developed on an Apple Mac Studio with an Apple M4 Max processor and 36 GB 99 

RAM. The latest version of 3D-Manhattan is available on the GitHub repository 100 

(https://github.com/hashimotoshumpei/3D-Manhattan). The repository includes detailed instructions 101 

on how to use the tool. An interactive AI-assisted coding workflow was partially utilized during 102 

development to facilitate rapid prototyping and iterative UI refinement, with all code reviewed and 103 

finalized by the author. 3D-Manhattan is implemented as a browser-based stand-alone visualization 104 

tool using JavaScript and WebGL (via the Three.js library). 105 

106 

Results 107 

Concept and Implementation Overview 108 

This tool is designed to integrate multiple genome-wide association study (GWAS) results derived 109 

from serial or multi-dimensional datasets, including time-course phenotypes, multiple traits, or model 110 

evaluation outputs (Fig.1). In this workflow, each dataset is independently subjected to GWAS, 111 

generating standard association result files that are subsequently imported into the 3D-Manhattan 112 

interface (Fig.1). These datasets are then processed and rendered within a unified 3D coordinate 113 

system, in which individual Manhattan plots are stacked along an additional axis representing time 114 

points, traits, or experimental conditions. Importantly, genomic coordinates and −log₁₀(p) values are 115 

preserved across all datasets, enabling direct, axis-aligned comparison within a consistent genomic 116 

reference frame. The interactive interface allows users to dynamically control key visualization 117 

parameters, such as chromosome selection, inter-dataset spacing, point size, and axis scaling, 118 

facilitating both global overview and detailed inspection of association patterns. As illustrated in Fig. 119 

1, the framework supports multiple output modes, including genome-wide stacked Manhattan plots, 120 

chromosome-specific visualizations, and variant-level connections across datasets. Thus, these 121 

features enable simultaneous exploration of multiple GWAS results within a single spatial context, 122 

providing a structured and intuitive representation of complex, multi-dimensional association data. 123 

124 

User Interface (UI) and User Experience (UX) 125 

The tool operates locally within modern web browsers and does not require server-side computation 126 

or installation of external dependencies. GWAS results are supplied as tab-separated value (TSV) files 127 

containing chromosome identifiers, base-pair positions, and association statistics (e.g., −log₁₀(p) 128 

values). For each dataset, chromosomes are mapped to continuous genomic coordinates according to 129 

their physical lengths, consistent with conventional Manhattan plot representations, thereby enabling 130 

genome-wide visualization with proportional spacing. A distinctive feature of this tool is that multiple 131 

GWAS datasets are stacked along the z-axis to generate a 3D Manhattan plot. The 3D view supports 132 

interactive manipulation, allowing users to adjust the viewing angle via mouse dragging and to control 133 

zoom levels using the mouse wheel. Visualization parameters, including vertical scaling, point size, 134 

inter-dataset spacing, axis offsets, and significance threshold lines, can be interactively adjusted 135 

through a graphical UI. Chromosome colors can also be customized, with independent color 136 

assignment for odd- and even-numbered chromosomes. The 3D plot, rendered at an arbitrary 137 

viewpoint, can be exported at high resolution in PNG, JPEG, or PDF formats from any selected 138 

viewpoint. Furthermore, conventional 2D Manhattan plots corresponding to individual datasets are 139 

displayed alongside the 3D visualization, enabling detailed inspection while preserving a genome-140 

wide overview (Fig. 2). Together, these features provide a flexible and intuitive UI that accommodates 141

Genome-wide and chromosome-level comparison 144 

A genome-wide view of stacked GWAS results is shown in Fig. 3A, enabling direct comparison of 145 

association signals across multiple datasets. Shared association peaks across datasets are readily 146 

identified as vertically aligned signals, whereas dataset-specific peaks appear as isolated features. The 147 

tool also supports focused visualization of individual chromosomes as well as alternative chromosome 148 

arrangements, facilitating detailed examination of regions of interest (Fig. 3B). Such views are 149 

particularly useful for exploring temporal changes in association strength or trait-specific genetic 150 

effects across datasets. 151 

152 

Visualization of variant correspondence across datasets 153 

3D-Manhattan further provides a highlighting function for specific genomic regions or individual 154 

variants. By selecting a chromosome and specifying genomic coordinates, users can assign an arbitrary 155 

color to the corresponding region (Fig. 3C). To highlight a single polymorphic site, the same genomic 156 

position is specified as both the start and end coordinates. Multiple regions can be highlighted 157 

simultaneously, and highlighted regions are reflected in real time in both the three-dimensional and 158 

two-dimensional plots. A distinctive feature of 3D-Manhattan is its ability to visually link variants 159 

located in similar genomic regions across different datasets (Fig. 3D). By drawing connecting lines 160 

between corresponding regions, the tool emphasizes reproducible signals and facilitates identification 161 

of stable or dynamic genetic associations across conditions. The color of the connecting lines can be 162 

freely customized by the user. This functionality is particularly valuable for visualizing serial GWAS 163 

results, in which the persistence or disappearance of association signals across developmental stages 164 

provides insight into stage-specific genetic regulation. 165

Discussion 167 

The visualization of multiple GWAS results has been commonly achieved by arranging 2D Manhattan 168 

plots across panels or stacking them vertically. While effective for presenting individual analyses, this 169 

approach fragments related datasets into separate visual contexts. Consequently, comparisons across 170 

time points, traits, or experimental conditions rely on sequential inspection. Continuity across datasets 171 

is therefore not explicitly encoded within a unified coordinate system. As the number of GWAS 172 

outputs increases, such panel-based representations impose a growing cognitive burden and limit 173 

efficient comparative exploration. 174 

175 

3D visualization offers a principled solution to this limitation by enabling multiple analytical results 176 

to be represented within a shared spatial framework. In the context of GWAS, introducing a third axis 177 

corresponding to time, trait, or experimental condition allows multiple Manhattan plots to be 178 

embedded within a single coordinate space. This integration preserves genomic position and signal 179 

intensity. Furthermore, it enables users to examine relationships among datasets simultaneously. As a 180 

result, global patterns, gradual transitions, and condition-specific dynamics can be detected more 181 

readily than with isolated two-dimensional views. 182 

183 

Several tools have explored the use of 3D representations for GWAS data. PheGWAS, for example, 184 

visualizes genome-wide by phenome-wide association matrices as an interactive 3D landscape 185 

(George et al., 2020). This design enables users to explore associations across many phenotypes 186 

concurrently. It is particularly effective for identifying pleiotropic loci and cross-trait relationships. 187 

PheGWAS therefore illustrates the value of 3D visualization for summarizing large-scale association 188

structures (George et al., 2020). Another notable tool, BigTop, represents a more immersive 189 

exploration paradigm by implementing GWAS visualization within a virtual reality environment 190 

(Westreich et al., 2020). In BigTop, Association signals are arranged around the user in a cylindrical 191 

space (Westreich et al., 2020). Chromosomal position is mapped circumferentially, −log 10 (p) is 192 

represented vertically, and an additional dimension such as minor allele frequency is encoded radially 193 

(Westreich et al., 2020). Through point-level interaction, users can inspect individual variants and 194 

their annotations. This design demonstrates how immersive depth perception and multidimensional 195 

encoding can enhance intuitive understanding of GWAS signal distributions. 196 

197 

Despite these advances, existing 3D tools are not primarily designed for the systematic comparison of 198 

multiple GWAS results generated under different conditions. In particular, neither phenome-wide 199 

overview landscapes nor immersive virtual environments explicitly preserve the conventional 200 

Manhattan plot structure required for direct, axis-aligned comparison across analyses. To address this 201 

gap, I developed 3D-Manhattan, an interactive visualization framework that integrates multiple 202 

GWAS outputs into a single 3D coordinate space (Fig.1). Importantly, this integration is achieved 203 

while retaining the familiar Manhattan plot representation. By stacking individual Manhattan plots 204 

along a third axis representing time points, traits, or experimental conditions, 3D-Manhattan enables 205 

direct visual tracking of how association signals emerge, shift, or disappear across analyses (Fig.2). 206 

This tracking is performed within a consistent genomic reference frame, facilitating intuitive 207 

comparison across conditions (Fig.3). 208 

209 

The technical foundation of 3D-Manhattan is its implementation using three.js, a JavaScript 210 

framework built on Web Graphics Library (WebGL) for interactive 3D graphics (Cabello, R. and 211 

three. js Contributors, 2010). Originally released in 2010, three.js was developed to simplify WebGL-212 

based rendering and has since become widely adopted for interactive visualization in modern web 213 

browsers, including WebXR-based virtual and augmented reality applications. In contrast, widely used 214 

GWAS visualization tools and plotting libraries, such as qqman (D. Turner, 2018), CMplot (LiLin-215 

Yin, 2024), matplotlib (Hunter, 2007), and ggplot2 (Wickham, 2016), are primarily optimized for 216 

static, CPU-based 2D visualization. Therefore, they scale poorly to interactive 3D exploration of 217 

genome-wide datasets. By leveraging GPU-accelerated rendering through WebGL, three.js enables 218 

efficient real-time interaction directly within web browsers. As a result, 3D-Manhattan maintains 219 

smooth and responsive performance even when handling large-scale GWAS datasets. 220 

221 

By combining an axis-preserving 3D design with a stand-alone, browser-based implementation, 3D-222 

Manhattan provides a complementary visualization paradigm for multi-condition and multi-trait 223 

GWAS results. Notably, although the visualization framework presented here was developed for 3D 224 

Manhattan plots, it naturally suggests broader applicability to the visualization of other forms of serial 225 

biological data. 226
