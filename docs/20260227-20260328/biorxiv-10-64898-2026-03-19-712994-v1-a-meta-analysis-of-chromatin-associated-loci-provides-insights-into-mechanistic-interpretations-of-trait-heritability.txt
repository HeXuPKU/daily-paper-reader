## Abstract 1

The vast majority of trait-associated loci discovered through genome-wide association studies 2

(GWAS) are non-coding, yet most lack statistical alignment with any discovered expression 3

quantitative trait loci (eQTLs). In particular, eQTLs are depleted at gene-distal regions and at 4

“functionally important” genes – those with strong selective constraint and complex regulatory 5

landscapes – likely due to selective depletion of high-effect variants. Here, we investigate the role 6

of variants with weaker effects on expression transmitted through distal regulatory elements, 7

which are detectable as chromatin accessibility QTLs (caQTLs). We aggregated caQTL data from 8

ten studies derived across different tissues, cell-types and lines, representing 104,024 lead 9

caQTLs across 3,457 samples. We found that, across a range of gene properties, caQTLs are 10 

discovered at functionally important genes more often than eQTLs. These observations are 11 

consistent with a model in which many eQTLs and GWAS hits are mediated through genetic 12 

effects on regulatory elements, which may have weak or context-dependent effects on gene 13 

expression. Our results suggest that caQTL discovery is more sensitive than eQTL discovery in 14 

capturing the molecular consequences of GWAS hits, and can provide complimentary information 15 

to eQTLs by implicating functional mechanisms of additional disease-associated loci. 16 

## Introduction 17 

Almost 90% of trait-associated variants revealed by GWAS are harbored within non-coding 18 

genomic regions 1,2 making it challenging to elucidate the specific genetic mechanisms by which 19 

each variant contributes to complex physiologic traits and disease. Many GWAS signals are linked 20 

to gene regulatory elements and mechanistically modulate physiological traits through variation 21 

that also changes gene expression, (i.e., eQTLs), suggesting that they act principally through cis -22 

regulatory mechanisms 3–6 . However, at current sample sizes, most GWAS hits are not 23 

immediately attached to an eQTL-like signal that might provide a mechanistic explanation 7;24

Expression (GTEx) Consortium estimated that only 43% of GWAS hits statistically linked to (i.e., 26 

colocalized with) an eQTL signal 6. Many factors have been hypothesized to contribute to this 27 

“colocalization gap”, including insufficient statistical power 5,8,9 , a lack of eQTL discovery in the 28 

correct context 7 (e.g. a specific developmental stage 10 , cell type 11 , or cell state in response to a 29 

stimulus 12 ), and post-transcriptional effects (e.g. splicing) not mediated through expression levels. 30 

In a previous report, Mostafavi et al. 13 noted that at current sample sizes, GWAS signals (i.e. 31 

GWAS “hits”) are biased towards different classes of genes from eQTLs. Specifically, compared 32 

to eQTLs, GWAS hits are (1) more distal to genes, and (2) more likely to be found near genes 33 

with high selective constraint, complex regulatory landscapes, and high connectedness in co-34 

expression networks. The depletion of eQTLs at these “functionally important” genes suggests 35 

that in addition to uncovering eQTLs in more contexts and larger sample sizes, there is scope for 36 

additional types of molecular assays to further close the colocalization gap. 37 

In particular, the compaction or openness of chromatin is a central determinant of gene 38 

transcription, both at promoters and at more distal cis -regulatory elements (cREs), whose activity 39 

can enhance or repress the expression of nearby genes 14–16 . One popular approach to identify 40 

cREs leverages the Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin using sequencing (ATAC-41 

seq) 17 , which defines peaks of open chromatin and their relative accessibility. Genetic variants 42 

associated with change in chromatin peak accessibility are known as chromatin accessibility 43 

QTLs (i.e., caQTLs) 18 . Consistent with the belief that the genetic association of eQTLs is often 44 

mediated through their effect on cREs, genetic variants explain a greater proportion of heritability 45 

with chromatin accessibility than with gene expression, and are more readily discovered as 46 

caQTLs than eQTLs 19–23 . In addition, caQTLs colocalize with more GWAS hits than eQTLs when 47 

discovered in the same set of samples 23–25 . caQTLs can also capture genetic effects on enhancer 48 

priming across a broad range of dynamic cell states, while their downstream effects on gene 49

relationship between caQTL and eQTL discoverability and their mechanistic linkage to GWAS hits 51

To explore in more detail, we assembled a large collection of caQTLs currently available in 53 

the public domain to examine their potential mechanistic role in physiologic trait association 54 

compared directly against eQTLs. We hypothesize that the limited power in eQTL discovery is 55 

due to weaker or context-dependent perturbations on expression mediated through cREs, which 56 

caQTL discovery would be more sensitive in capturing. Initially, we replicate the previous 57 

comparison and observation between eQTLs and GWAS hits from Mostafavi et al., expanding 58 

this effort to include an intermediate step: genetic variation changes chromatin accessibility which, 59 

in turn, modulates cis-gene expression, then ultimately to the downstream physiologic trait of 60 

study. We collected caQTLs from ten studies 21,22,24,27–33 and found that, compared to discovered 61 

eQTLs, the properties of caQTLs more closely resemble those of GWAS hits. Specifically, caQTLs 62 

are less depleted than eQTLs near “functionally important” genes – those with strong selective 63 

constraint and complex regulatory landscapes. We show that these observations are consistent 64 

with a model in which many eQTLs and GWAS hits are mediated through genetic effects on cREs. 65 

This model implies that for such GWAS hits, caQTLs are better powered than eQTLs to detect 66 

colocalizations due to a more direct genetic effect. In particular, the model expects caQTLs to 67 

colocalize with GWAS hits more in distal regions and at “functionally important” genes compared 68 

to eQTLs, helping to explain what we observe. These results suggest that with a limited sample 69 

size for discovery, epigenetic association signals can provide complimentary information to eQTLs 70 

by implicating functional mechanisms of additional disease-associated loci. 71

## Results 72 

Comparison of caQTLs to eQTLs and GWAS hits 73 

Following replication of the analysis of eQTLs and GWAS hits carried out by Mostafavi et al., 74 

we incorporated a collection of single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) caQTL summary statistics 75 

gathered from ten different studies across multiple cell and tissue types ( Fig. 1A , Supplementary 76 

Table 1 ). We ran linkage-disequilibrium (LD) clumping to identify independent lead caQTLs, then 77 

removed any SNPs in strong LD (r 2>0.8) with protein-altering SNPs. In order to compare caQTLs 78 

to GTEx eQTLs, we also removed SNPs further than 1 Mb from a transcription start site (TSS), in 79 

line with what was not tested by GTEx for eQTLs; this resulted in a list of 104,024 caQTLs. We 80 

then ran this catalog of caQTLs through the computational pipeline utilized by Mostafavi et al., 81 

allowing us to compare eQTLs, caQTLs, and GWAS hits directly ( Methods ). Briefly, we computed 82 

average values for a range of SNP and gene properties in each list of caQTLs, eQTLs, and GWAS 83 

hits. For gene properties, we first assigned every SNP to its nearest gene (creating lists of “GWAS 84 

genes”, “eQTL genes”, and “caQTL genes”, Fig. 1B ), and considered the properties of that gene. 85 

To account for potential confounding effects due to differences between caQTLs, eQTLs, and 86 

GWAS hits, we also ran this analysis on lists of control SNPs for caQTLs, eQTLs, and GWAS hits, 87 

matched for minor allele frequency (MAF), LD score, and gene density ( Fig 1C ). See Methods 88 

for full details. 89

function intolerant) score 38 , with previous studies having shown that SNP-disease heritability is 94 

enriched near high-pLI genes 38–40 . However, Mostafavi et al. and other studies 38,41–43 reported that 95

In addition to the selective constraint of genes, Mostafavi et al. also considered the level of 102 

regulatory complexity of genes near trait and expression-associated SNPs. Their analyses 103 

showed that GWAS hits were found more frequently near genes with more complex regulatory 104 

landscapes than eQTLs. Specifically, compared to genes near eQTLs, genes near GWAS hits 105 

have more TSSs, have longer enhancers, are more connected in co-expression networks, and 106 

are more likely to be transcription factors (TFs). When including caQTLs in these comparisons, 107 

we found that the regulatory complexities of caQTL genes were between those of eQTL genes 108 

and GWAS genes ( Fig. 3 ). For example, when considering the number of different TSSs used by 109 

genes 44 , GWAS genes had more TSSs than genes mapped to control SNPs (6.4 vs 5.6, 110 

P=3.8×10 -7 ), eQTL genes had fewer (4.4 vs 5.0, P=6.2×10 -12 ), while caQTL genes did not show a 111 

significant difference (5.8 vs 5.8, P=0.57) ( Fig. 3A ). Mostafavi et al. calculated the total length of 112 

enhancers for every gene, based on enhancer–gene links inferred from the Roadmap 113 

Epigenomics Consortium dataset 45 . Using logistic regressions to distinguish QTLs/GWAS genes 114 

from genes linked to random SNPs ( Methods ), we found that GWAS genes had longer enhancers 115 

(P=4.0×10 -5 ), eQTL genes had shorter enhancers ( P=2.5×10 -9 ), while for caQTL genes they were 116 

slightly longer ( P=1.1×10 -3 ) ( Fig. 3B ). A longer set of enhancers is associated with weaker 117 

enhancer activity, since each individual peak contributes proportionally less to the expression of 118 

the gene. Thus, this result suggests that caQTLs are found in weaker enhancers compared to 119 

eQTLs. 120

An extended model of functional variant discovery 149 

To address the observed properties of caQTLs compared to those of eQTLs and GWAS hits, 150 

we expanded the model of discovery presented in Mostafavi et al. to include a chromatin 151 

accessibility component. Given that many genes are widely considered to be regulated through 152 

distal cREs, we elected to consider the subset of GWAS hits in which a variant directly perturbs 153 

a cRE, which in turn directly modulates the expression of a trait-relevant gene ( Methods ,154 

Supplementary Note ). For consistency with the Mostafavi model, we labeled the three relevant 155 

effect sizes as 

β1,

β2, and 

γ. ( Fig. 5A ). We then modeled the discoverability of a single SNP as a 156 

function of these (squared) effect sizes, and visualized the range of effect sizes for which that 157 

SNP was expected to be discovered as a caQTL, eQTL, or GWAS hit. A simple model which does 158 

not account for natural selection demonstrated that a more direct genetic effect on chromatin 159 

accessibility compared to gene expression led to a wider range of SNP discovery for caQTLs 160 

compared to eQTLs, even when the caQTL sample size was lower ( Fig. 5B ). Under natural 161 

selection, this discrepancy became greater for SNPs which affect the expression of “high-trait 162 

effect” genes, or those with high 

γ 2 values ( Figs. 5C-D ). This is consistent with our observation 163 

that caQTLs appear more frequently than eQTLs at genes that are enriched for GWAS hits. 164 

In particular, the model showed that for smaller values of 

β22 (the effect of accessibility on 165 

expression), caQTLs were especially more likely to be discovered alongside GWAS hits 166 

compared to eQTLs ( Fig. 5D ). The reason for this was that weaker enhancers (i.e., peaks with 167 

lower effects on expression) were less constrained by selection, and thus more likely to harbor 168 

common variants affecting accessibility. However, these variants in weaker enhancers had a lower 169

## Discussion 178 

Previous analyses have shown that “functionally important” genes – those with high levels of 179 

selective constraint and regulatory complexity – are enriched for GWAS hits, yet depleted of 180 

eQTLs, suggesting that natural selection reduces the allele frequency of SNPs with a high effect 181 

on expression near such genes. This hinders eQTL discovery near these genes at current sample 182 

sizes. caQTLs potentially represent SNPs with weaker effects on expression that are mediated 183 

through cREs, which should be less depleted by selection. Here, we show that caQTLs appear 184 

more frequently at functionally important genes compared to eQTLs, though not as frequently as 185 

GWAS hits. These results suggest that caQTLs have utility in interpreting GWAS hits at 186 

functionally important genes which currently have no colocalizing eQTL. Note that while caQTLs 187 

had a similar distribution of gene properties to control SNPs, they are enriched for GWAS loci and 188 

in known regulatory regions 24,28,31–33 , distinguishing them from randomly chosen background 189 

SNPs. 190 

Our observations are consistent with a model in which the effect of a SNP on gene expression 191 

is mediated through chromatin accessibility of a cRE. Clearly, this model does not apply to all non-192 

coding SNPs – SNPs in promoters, for instance, can modulate expression without affecting a 193

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted March 20, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.19.712994 doi: bioRxiv preprint

distal enhancer at all. Furthermore, this simplified model also ignores pleiotropic effects, by which 194 

a SNP influences both expression and accessibility independently, or instances where gene 195 

transcription causes an opening of chromatin in the region. However, our results comparing 196 

caQTLs to eQTLs suggest that the accessibility to expression causal pathway dominates over 197 

alternative pathways, and indeed the outsized contribution of caQTLs to disease heritability 198 

compared to eQTLs indicates that caQTLs are the primary mediator of genetic effects on gene 199 

expression 23 . While the genetic architecture of trait association can potentially be more accurately 200 

visualized as a dense web of causal relationships between genes, regulatory elements, and other 201 

epigenetic factors (e.g. methylation, 3D interactions), such modeling is beyond the scope of this 202 

study. Instead, we propose that chromatin accessibility lies on the earlier side of this regulatory 203 

cascade and thus is more directly influenced by genetics. As a result, caQTL discovery is more 204 

sensitive to the effect of SNPs compared to eQTL discovery. We also note that this principle could 205 

generalize to other types of QTLs, such as methylation QTLs 50 or looping QTLs 51 .206 

A prominent limitation of this study is the relative non-uniformity of caQTL discovery across 207 

tissues, compared to the discovery of eQTLs across tissues in GTEx, or the discovery of GWAS 208 

hits across traits in the UKBB. While discovery cohorts in GTEx or the UKBB largely overlap 209 

between tissues or traits, the caQTL studies analyzed here used separate cohorts. In addition, 210 

each caQTL discovery may employ slightly different peak calling algorithms, window sizes for 211 

tests, or models and methods used to assess significance. Furthermore, the set of tissues and 212 

cell types analyzed in caQTL studies is far less diverse and comprehensive than the set of GTEx 213 

tissues. Some tissues are represented in multiple studies, whereas many are not represented in 214 

any. However, the results that we show here appear to be largely consistent across tissue types 215 

and studies, even when caQTLs and eQTLs are discovered in the same set of samples, 216 

suggesting that those properties of caQTLs are not sensitive to tissue type or discovery 217 

parameters. 218

It is also important to note that the differences we describe occur for SNPs discovered at 219 

current sample sizes . Indeed, recent studies observed that as eQTL sample size increases, the 220 

depletion of eQTLs at constrained genes decreases 9, and eQTLs at constrained genes are more 221 

likely to colocalize with GWAS hits 23 . Thus the observed bias is not intrinsic to different types of 222 

variants but the result of a limited sample size, and increasing eQTL power should remain an 223 

important aim in closing the colocalization gap. Instead, our results suggest that while sample 224 

size for molecular QTL studies is limited, epigenetic signals such as caQTLs can capture weaker 225 

genetic regulatory effects with less dependence on context compared to eQTLs, and can provide 226 

complimentary information to eQTLs in interpreting GWAS hits, especially near functionally 227 

important genes or TSS-distal loci. 228 

In total, this work demonstrates the utility of caQTL (and other epigenetic QTL) discovery as 229 

a means of uncovering the mechanisms of genetic regulation, alongside eQTL discovery and 230 

direct experimental perturbation. In particular, with the increase in availability of molecular data 231 

from large, population-scale cohorts, we imagine that a uniform discovery of caQTLs across 232 

multiple tissues in the same cohort will be valuable in understanding how consistent the epigenetic 233 

regulatory landscape is across tissue types. The relative context-dependence of caQTL discovery 234 

compared to eQTL discovery is also worth further investigation – that is, the extent to which 235 

context-specific eQTLs are ubiquitously found as caQTLs across developmental stages, circadian 236 

rhythm cycles, or stimuli responses. Ultimately, continuing to assay epigenetic molecular 237 

phenotypes alongside gene expression will aid in closing the colocalization gap for complex-trait 238 

heritability. 239

## Methods 240 

Collection and filtering of caQTL summary statistics 241 

caQTL summary statistics were downloaded from ten published studies across different sample 242 

groups and tissue types, representing a total sample size of 3,457 ( Supplementary Table 1 ). To 243 

maintain parity with GWAS which use a threshold of P=5×10 −8, we filtered for SNP-peak pairs 244 

with a nominal P-value less than 5×10 −8. We observed that the number of significant single-cell 245 

muscle caQTLs from Varshney et al. 29 far exceeded the counts for other studies; to reduce the 246 

bias of our analysis towards muscle caQTLs, we combined the muscle cell “type 1”, “type 2a” and 247 

“type 2x” summary stats into a list containing only SNP-peak pairs significant in all three cell types. 248 

These three cell types showed very strong sharing of caQTLs in Varshney et al. For SNP 249 

selection, we used the list of SNPs passing all filters in Mostafavi et al. (specifically, 250 

snp_annotations/filter_snps.txt, Web Resources ). Briefly, this set started with the list of 13.7 251 

million variants that passed quality control measures for the UKB analyses released by the Neale 252 

lab (see “Data availability” in Mostafavi et al.). They further applied the following filters: biallelic 253 

autosomal SNP; MAF > 0.01 among the unrelated white British individuals in the UKB; 254 

polymorphic in the 1000 Genomes Project phase 3 data. To make the comparisons between 255 

GWAS hits and eQTLs consistent, they removed (1) SNPs in LD (r 2 > 0.8) with predicted protein-256 

truncating or missense mutations, to condition on SNPs putatively acting through gene regulation, 257 

and (2) SNPs >1 Mb away from the TSS of any of 18,332 protein-coding genes, which are not 258 

tested for eQTLs in GTEx. They further removed SNPs in the major histocompatibility complex 259 

region (chr6:28477797-33448354 in hg19). The final filter_snps.txt list contains 6,971,256 SNPs. 260 

For full details of SNP filtering, see “Methods: SNP selection” in Mostafavi et al. 261

LD clumping to identify lead caQTLs 262 

We replicated the procedure in Mostafavi et al. to clump GWAS/eQTL signals on our caQTL 263 

summary stats. For each tissue separately, we used plink’s ( Web Resources ) --clump flag with 264 

parameters: P-value threshold of 5 × 10 −8, LD threshold of r 2 = 0.1 and physical distance threshold 265 

of 1 Mb. While Mostafavi et al. used a UKB resource as the LD reference panel, we did not have 266 

access to individual level UKB data, so we used 1000G Phase 3 EUR plink files as a reference 267 

(Web Resources ) for all caQTL studies except for the DeGorter et al. study, which used LCLs 268 

from individuals across six African populations. To clump the LCL caQTLs discovered in African 269 

populations, we created an African-only reference panel by downloading the “all_hg38” 2022-08-270 

04 reference set from plink resources, then filtering it for the n=667 individuals with a listed 271 

population in [YRI, LWD, GWD, MSL, ESN] ( Web Resources ). After clumping in every tissue, we 272 

concatenated the lists of lead caQTLs across tissues to create the “redundant” list of 111,212 lead 273 

caQTLs. 274 

We refer to this preliminary list of lead caQTLs as “redundant” because several tissues are 275 

represented more than once; for example, bulk liver caQTLs appear in both Wenz et al. 32 and 276 

Currin et al. 22 , neuron caQTLs appear in both Liang et al. 21 and Zeng et al. 28 , and LCL caQTLs 277 

appear in both DeGorter et al. 27 and Jeong and Bulyk 31 . To reduce the bias in our analysis towards 278 

these tissues and cell types, we created a “non-redundant” list of lead caQTLs which included 279 

only the highest powered study for each tissue type (see Supplementary Table 1 for a list of 280 

which studies are included). For every duplicated tissue, the highest powered study (with the 281 

largest sample size) also had the most lead caQTLs. This non-redundant list included caQTLs 282 

from eight of the ten studies, representing a total sample size of 3,204, and consisting of 104,024 283 

lead caQTLs. We used the non-redundant list for all downstream analyses except for tissue-284 

specific analyses, when each tissue in each study in the “redundant” list was analyzed separately. 285

Regression coefficients for gene features 315 

As in Mostafavi et al., to account for correlations between a SNP’s features and the features of 316 

its nearest gene, we ran logistic regressions to calculate effect coefficients (e.g. Fig. 3B ). These 317 

regressions estimate the relative effects of gene properties when classifying QTLs/GWAS hits 318 

from random SNPs, conditioned on other properties. Specifically, for each SNP list, we selected 319 

100,000 random SNPs from the full set of filtered SNPs, and created an indicator variable to be 320 

‘1’ for SNPs in the list, and ‘0’ for random SNPs. We then ran a logistic regression to predict this 321 

indicator variable using normalized gene features of each SNPs nearest gene. As covariates, we 322 

included MAF, LD score, gene density, absolute distance to nearest TSS, total gene length, total 323 

length of gene coding sequence, as well as dummy variables for 20 quantiles of MAF, LD score, 324 

gene density and absolute distance to the nearest TSS. For each SNP type, we first ran this 325 

regression using all measured gene features as predictor variables in a “conditional” model. The 326 

full list of conditional coefficients can be found in Supplementary Fig. 1 and Supplementary 327 

Table 2. We then ran a separate “marginal” model for each gene feature to measure the effect of 328 

that feature conditioned only on the covariates, rather than on the other gene features. The full 329 

list of marginal coefficients can be found in Supplementary Fig. 2 and Supplementary Table 2 .330
