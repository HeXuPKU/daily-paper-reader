Abstract 13 

Genome -wide association studies (GWAS) commonly focus on one quantitative trait at a time, 14 

even when multiple traits are collected. However, joint analysis of multiple traits can increase the 15 

power to detect genetic associations by leveraging trait correlations. Multivariate GWAS is 16 

particularly important for identifying pleiotropic effects and uncovering shared genetic 17 

architecture underlying complex traits. Despite its great potential, multivariate GWAS methods 18 

face substantial computational challenges due to the high dimensionality of polygenic covariance 19 

structures and the cost of scanning genome -wide markers. We present EMmvGWAS, an efficient 20 

computational framework implemented as an R, which dramatically reduces computation time 21 

for multivariate GWAS involving a moderate number of traits. The algorithm estimates the 22 

genetic and environmental covariance matrices from a pure polygenic model using an 23 

Expectation -Maximization (EM) algorithm. To improve computational efficiency during 24 

genome -wide scanning, we use a semi -exact method that fixes the estimated covariance matrices 25 

and treats their ratio, defined as the genetic covariance matrix multiplied by the inverse of the 26 

environmental covariance matrix, as a known constant across all markers. This approach enables 27 

closed -form solutions for marker effects at each locus, dramatically reducing the computation 28 

time without compromising statistical power. We demonstrate the scalability and performance of 29 

our method through both simulation studies and real dataset analyses from rice, mouse and 30 

human populations. The method is implemented in R and is designed to support multivariate 31 

GWAS involving a moderate number of traits, while also accommodating univariate analyses as 32 

a special case. The R package is available at  https://github.com/Jason -Teng/EMmvGWAS .33

1. Background 42 

Genome -wide association studies (GWAS) have significantly advanced our understanding of the 43 

genetic architecture underlying complex traits by identifying genetic variants associated with 44 

quantitative traits (Visscher et al., 2017) . Traditionally, GWAS are often conducted on individual 45 

traits independently, even though multiple correlated phenotypes are always measured in genetic 46 

studies. Analyzing traits individually overlooks valuable trait correlations, thus potentially 47 

reducing the statistical power and increasing the type 1 error rate . Multivariate GWAS leverages 48 

these correlations by using multivariate linear mixed model s, improving power and reducing 49 

false positive associations in identifying pleiotropic genetic variants, which affect multiple traits 50 

simultaneously (Korte et al., 2012; Porter & O’Reilly, 2017; Turley et al., 2018; Zhou & 51 

Stephens, 2014) . Such methods are crucial for uncovering shared genetic bases among related 52 

traits, providing deeper insights into biological pathways and mechanisms underlying complex 53 

diseases and phenotypes (Hackinger & Zeggini, 2017) .54 

55 

Despite their clear advantages, multivariate GWAS methods confront significant computational 56 

hurdles, primarily due to the complexity of modeling high -dimensional covariance structures and 57 

the computational cost associated with scanning genome -wide markers. Estimating genetic and 58 

environmental covariance matrices between traits becomes increasingly demanding as the 59 

number of traits grows, often requiring iterative optimization over large parameter spaces. At the 60 

same time, genome -wide marker scanning involves evaluating potentially millions of SNPs 61 

across all traits, which remains computationally intensive. These two challenges compound in 62 

high -dimensional settings, making many existing multivariate GWAS tools impractical for real -63 

world applications involving many traits. Although R is widely used in statistical genetics, there 64 

is a lack of efficient R packages for multivariate GWAS, especially for analyses involving many 65 

traits. 66 

67 

To address these challenges, we developed EMmvGWAS, a general statistical framework that 68 

supports multivariate GWAS with improved computational efficiency. While suitable for small 69 

trait sets, EMmvGWAS offers substantial speed improvements as the number of traits grows, 70 

making it especially effective for studies involving 6 to 10 traits or more. For variance 71 

component estimation, we applied an approach similar to previous methods that utilize eigen 72 

decomposition of the genetic relationship matrix ( or kinship matrix) and linear transformation to 73 

achieve uncorrelated observations. However, we simplify the model into a weighted linear mixed 74 

model with a random intercept, allowing for more efficient estimation. Variance components are 75 

estimated using an expectation -maximization (EM) algorithm under a pure polygenic model. 76 

77 

For marker scanning, we developed a semi -exact method using an innovative linear 78 

transformation framework. Traditional approximate approaches use variance components 79 

estimated under the null model, offering faster computation but at the cost of reduced statistical 80 

power and lacking a natural extension to multivariate settings (Kang et al., 2010; Zhang et al., 81 

2010) . In contrast, exact methods such as GEMMA maintain statistical rigor but become 82 

computationally burdensome as the number of traits increases (Zhou & Stephens, 2014) . Our 83

EMmvGWAS algorithm addresses these limitations by using a semi -exact method that fix es the 84 

ratio of variance components while explicitly re -estimating the residual co variance matrix at 85 

each locus. This enables a closed -form Wald test for each marker, eliminati ng the need for 86 

iterative optimization during marker testing and substantially reduces time complexity, achieving 87 

a practical balance between computational efficiency and statistical power. 88 

89 

To promote accessibility and reproducibility, we implemented the method in R, a widely used 90 

platform in statistical genetics. Our package supports moderate -dimensional multivariate GWAS 91 

with flexible input for phenotypes, covariates, and kinship matrices, enabling scalable 92 

multivariate analyses and deeper insight into the genetic architecture of complex traits. 93

2. 4 Scanning the entire genome for associations 183

marker effects can be incorporated into the model by re -estimating all parameters for each 185

efficient strategy that fixes the variance matrix ratio estimated from the null model and scans the 187 

genome marker by marker. This strategy allows closed -form estimation of marker effects and 188 

enables re -estimation of the residual covariance matrix for each marker, thereby substantially 189 

improving computational efficiency while maintaining good statistical performance. With the 190 

variance matrix ratio fixed from the null model, genome -wide association testing is carried out 191

2. 5. Dataset 212 

We evaluated the performance and computational efficiency of the new method using three 213 

genetic association datasets from rice, human, and mouse populations. Each dataset includes 214 

genome -wide genotype data and multiple phenotypic traits, enabling a range of multivariate 215 

GWAS applications. The rice dataset consists of 1,495 hybrid rice individuals (Huang et al., 216 

2015) . For the human dataset, w e analyzed a subset of 5,000 unrelated white British individuals 217 

from the UK Biobank (Sudlow et al., 2015) cohort for demonstration purposes. The mouse 218 

dataset was obtained from the CFW outbred population study (Parker et al., 2016) , available 219 

from the Dryad Digital Repository (doi:10.5061/dryad.2rs41). 220 

For the rice and mouse datasets, we applied standard quality control procedures, including 221 

removal of SNPs with minor allele frequency below 0.01 and individuals or variants with 222 

missing data. For the UK Biobank data , g enotype data were filtered using PLINK2 with 223 

thresholds of 0.05 for missing genotype rate and minor allele frequency, along with linkage 224 

disequilibrium pruning (Chang et al., 2015) . Detailed information is provided in Supplementary 225 

Table S1. All phenotypes were standardized prior to analysis. 226

2. 6. Simulation analysis 228 

To evaluate the type I error control and statistical power of our method, we performed null 229 

simulations and alternative simulations using real genotype data from the rice and mouse 230 

datasets. We excluded the human dataset from simulations due to its large sample size and dense 231 

marker coverage, which made simulations computationally impractical. 232 

233 

For null simulations, phenotypes were generated under a pure polygenic model without any 234 

marker effects. Specifically, we simulated 100 sets of phenotypes based on the real genetic 235 

relatedness matrix and the estimated genetic and environmental covariance matrices from the 236 

rice and mouse data. We randomly selected 10,000 SNPs from the real genotype data . We then 237 

computed p -values for all combinations of these SNPs and the simulated phenotypes, resulting in 238 

one million tests per dataset. For alternative simulations , we added SNP effects into the 239 

phenotypes to assess empirical power. We first selected 1,000 evenly spaced SNPs from a set of 240 

SNPs that showed no significant association with either trait (P > 0.1 in all univariate and 241 

multivariate tests ). Each selected SNP was assigned an effect on the first trait to explain a fixed 242 

proportion of phenotypic variance (PVE ). The effect on the second trait was scaled to explain 243 

either 20% or 40% of PVE of the second trait , with effects either in the same or opposite 244 

direction across traits. These synthetic effects were added to the original phenotypes to generate 245

4. Discussion 303 

These results highlight the substantial computational gains offered by EMmvGWAS . While 304 

GEMMA scales poorly with the dimensionality of the trait space, the EMmvGWAS framework 305 

maintains a nearly constant runtime. This efficiency arises from the closed -form re -estimation of 306 

the residual variance, which avoids repeated iterative optimization. The performance advantage 307 

is consistent across organisms with diverse sample sizes and genetic architectures, demonstrating 308 

the scalability and robustness of EMmvGWAS . The human analysis further demonstrates that the 309 

semi -exact framework can efficiently leverage parallel computing, achieving additional 310 

reductions in runtime without compromis e in accuracy. The efficiency of EMmvGWAS opens 311 

the door to comprehensive multivariate GWAS across diverse datasets and trait dimensions. 312 

313 

While exact method s are often expected to produce more significant results, this was not 314 

observed in our comparisons. We suspect that GEMMA’s internal calibration procedure reduces 315 

the apparent significance of its test statistics, leading to closer agreement with the semi -exact 316 

method. QQ plots from null simulations confirmed that EMmvGWAS yields well -calibrated p -317 

values that are comparable to GEMMA, with no evidence of systematic inflation or deflation of 318 

the type 1 error . This indicates that EMmvGWAS does not compromise type I error control while 319 

offering substantial computational efficiency. 320 

321 

For variance component estimation, we employed the expectation -maximization (EM) algorithm 322 

under the pure polygenic (null) model. Despite its simplicity, the EM algorithm provided 323 

sufficiently accurate estimates and was computationally efficient in our analyses. In contrast, 324 

GEMMA uses a more complex procedure that combines parameter -expanded EM algorithm with 325
