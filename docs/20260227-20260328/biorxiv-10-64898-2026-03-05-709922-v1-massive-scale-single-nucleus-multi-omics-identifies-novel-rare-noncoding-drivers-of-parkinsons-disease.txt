Abstract 51 

52 

Most genetic variants contributing to complex diseases reside in the noncoding genome. While 53 common variants uncovered by genome-wide association studies often fail to explain much of 54 the observed heritability of these diseases, rare variants often have higher effect sizes and 55 cumulatively explain a larger portion of heritability. However, rare variants, particularly rare 56 noncoding variants, have remained under-characterized largely due to the difficulties of 57 accurately predicting variant functionality at scale, given that each individual carries an average 58 of ~10,000 rare variants. Here, we generated multi-omic data from >3.3 million nuclei sampled 59 from five brain regions across a cohort of 80 individuals with Parkinson’s disease (PD) and 21 60 neurologically normal control individuals with matched 30x whole-genome sequencing. We use 61 this data to identify cell type-specific features of PD, map cell type-specific chromatin 62 accessibility and expression quantitative trait loci, and train machine learning models to predict 63 the effect of variants on gene regulation. We identify rare noncoding variants statistically 64 associated with sporadic PD and extend our approaches to predict drivers of familial PD of 65 unknown genetic origin. Our results underscore the significance of rare noncoding variants in 66 complex diseases and provide a roadmap for applying similar approaches in other disease 67 systems. 68

Introduction 70 

71 

Parkinson’s disease (PD) is an age-related neurodegenerative disease characterized 72 pathologically by the loss of dopaminergic neurons within the substantia nigra as well as the 73 accumulation of Lewy bodies containing abnormal forms of alpha-synuclein in multiple brain 74 regions, and clinically by a constellation of motor and non-motor symptoms 1. Like many complex 75 diseases, PD is driven by a mix of genetic and environmental factors and is estimated to be 76 ~30-40% heritable 2,3 . Decades of research have aimed to uncover both the molecular and 77 genetic drivers of PD susceptibility to improve risk stratification and nominate novel targets for 78 therapeutic intervention. 79 

The molecular pathophysiology of PD involves disruption across multiple cell types and 80 brain regions. Recently, single-nucleus transcriptomic profiling (snRNA-seq) has been used to 81 study the cell types and molecular processes most affected in PD. For example, previous work 82 has identified populations of vulnerable dopaminergic neurons marked by AGTR1 expression 483 associated with PD-related degeneration and revealed significant glial activation in the midbrain 84 of patients with PD, including microgliosis 5 and upregulation of stress-induced S100B in 85 oligodendrocytes 5–7  in the substantia nigra. The pathology of PD extends beyond the midbrain 86 and is associated with widespread microglial activation and epigenetic dysregulation affecting 87 extranigral regions, including the striatum and cortex 8,9 . The involvement of diverse neuronal 88 and glial cell types in PD-related dysfunction underscores the importance of a multi-regional and 89 cell type-specific approach to dissecting PD pathophysiology. 90 

The genetics of PD and nearly all other complex diseases have been studied with two 91 complementary approaches. First, genome-wide association studies (GWASs) have been used 92 to identify common variants, typically present in more than 1% of the population, that are 93 statistically associated with the risk of sporadic P 10–12 . These common variants often have very 94 small effect sizes, with odds ratios frequently between 0.9 and 1.1, and almost universally 95 reside within the noncoding regions of the genome 10 . Noncoding variants can exert a multitude 96 of effects but are thought to primarily act by perturbing gene regulation through alterations to the 97 recognition sites of sequence-specific transcription factors (TFs) 13–15 . However, as these 98 common variants have low effect sizes, they account for less than 10% of the overall risk for 99 PD, leaving much of the heritability of PD unexplained 2,16,17 .100

A second approach, familial pedigree analysis, has been used to identify ultra-rare, high-101 effect-size variants that drive disease. This approach has identified a small number of highly 102 penetrant driver variants in well-studied genes, such as alpha-synuclein ( SNCA ) and parkin 103 (PRKN )2,18 . While highly effective at identifying driver variants within coding regions, these 104 familial pedigrees are often too small to employ classical segregation analyses within the 105 noncoding genome. As 70–80% of familial PD cases lack a clear coding driver variant, this 106 leaves many high-effect-size noncoding variants to be discovered 19–21 . Together, the limitations 107 of existing genetics discovery approaches highlight a need for more robust prioritization of rare 108 noncoding variants. 109 

Rare noncoding variants remain understudied due to challenges related to scale and 110 interpretability. With respect to scale, over 60 million single-nucleotide variants with a frequency 111 less than 0.5% were identified from the 2,504 individuals in the 1000 Genomes Project 22 . These 112 data indicate that, on average, each person has more than 10,000 rare variants, ~98% of which 113 are noncoding. In fact, ultra-rare variants are estimated to contribute up to 25% of the cis -114 heritability of transcriptional regulation 23 . The abundance of variants and the relatively low 115 number of patients with PD who have whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data available make it 116 challenging to associate rare noncoding variants with disease using existing statistical 117 approaches. Regarding interpretability, the effects of noncoding variants are more challenging 118 to predict than those of coding variants. Gene regulation is highly cell type-specific, and most 119 noncoding variants are not known to exert an impact. We and others 24,25  have turned to 120 machine learning (ML) approaches to address these problems of both scale and interpretability. 121 These ML models 26  learn to predict a molecular measurement, such as chromatin accessibility 122 as measured by the assay for transposase-accessible chromatin (ATAC-seq) 27 , from a given 123 sequence. When paired with cell type-specific chromatin accessibility measurements, this ML-124 based approach enables rapid and robust prediction of the gene regulatory effects of noncoding 125 variants. 126 

Here, we sought to uncover cell type-specific molecular mechanisms of PD and 127 nominate novel genetic drivers of both sporadic and familial disease. We generated a massive-128 scale single-nucleus multi-omic atlas of the human brain spanning five different brain regions 129 across 80 PD patients and 21 healthy controls. With more than 3.3 million nuclei featuring high-130 quality, matched snRNA-seq and snATAC-seq data, as well as 30x WGS data for each 131 individual, this dataset provides an ideal resource for understanding PD-specific molecular 132 alterations and training robust ML models for predicting the effects of noncoding variants. We 133 leverage this data to nominate new drivers of the genetic risk of PD, establishing a novel 134 framework for understanding the molecular and genetic underpinnings of complex disease. 135

To build a molecular atlas that resolves previously underappreciated rare cell types and aid in 142 the interpretation of noncoding variant effects in PD, we performed paired single-nucleus multi-143 omic profiling of chromatin accessibility and gene expression from postmortem brain tissue 144 derived from five brain regions across 101 donors (80 PD patients and 21 controls) ( Fig. 1a, 145 

Supplementary Fig. 1a ). These individuals were volunteers in the Arizona Study of Aging and 146 Neurodegenerative Disorders community aging study and have detailed clinical and 147 neuropathologic annotation ( Supplementary Fig. 1b–c, Supplementary Table 1 ). We selected 148 cases across the neuropathological spectrum of Lewy body formation, which follows a 149 stereotypical progression from brainstem, then limbic, and finally neocortical neurons 28 150 (Supplementary Fig. 1b, Supplementary Table 1 ). To mitigate batch effects across this large 151

After 153 strict quality control, we created three different atlases: (i) 4.2 million nuclei with high-quality 154 snATAC-seq data, (ii) 4.5 million nuclei with high-quality snRNA-seq data, and (iii) the 155 intersection, 3.3 million nuclei with high-quality multi-omic data (snATAC-seq and snRNA-seq) 156 (Fig. 1a, Supplementary Fig. 1d–g ). Dimensionality reduction and clustering revealed seven broad cell types (astrocytes, 162 excitatory neurons, inhibitory neurons, microglia/immune cells, oligodendrocytes, 163 oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs), and vascular/endothelial cells), with many neurons 164 showing brain region specificity ( Supplementary Fig. 2b–c ). These cell types showed minimal 165 detectable bias due to covariates, including biological sex, disease status, donor, and batch 166 (Supplementary Fig. 2d–g ). We further subclustered these broad cell classes based on refined 167 marker genes to identify 30 distinct cell types ( Fig. 1c–f, Supplementary Fig. 3a–d ). To identify 168 which TFs influence cell type-specific regulatory programs, we performed motif enrichment 169 analysis of peaks identified in each cell type ( Fig. 1f ). This analysis identified known cell type-170 specific master regulators, such as SOX9 in oligodendrocytes 29  and SPI family TFs in 171 microglia 30 , as well as novel putative regulators, such as GCM2 in Purkinje cells. Notably, motif 172 enrichment analysis also revealed significant enrichment of CRX binding motifs within 173 accessible chromatin regions of GABAergic neurons in the substantia nigra. While CRX has 174 previously been characterized for its role in retinal photoreceptor differentiation 31 , this suggests 175 potential engagement of transcriptional programs not traditionally associated with this neuronal 176 population. 177 

Focusing on subclusters of neurons, we sought to characterize neuronal subtypes in the 178 substantia nigra and putamen, sites of nigro-striatal neuron soma and their afferent synapses 179 with medium spiny neurons (MSNs) that subserve motor functions heavily impacted in PD. 180 Subclustering of dopaminergic neurons revealed transcriptionally distinct populations ( Fig. 1g, 181 

Supplementary Fig. 4a–b ). In line with prior work 4, we observed an AGTR1 -expressing 182 subcluster (Dopaminergic Subcluster 6) that was reduced in PD compared with controls, 183 providing additional evidence for the selective vulnerability of this dopaminergic neuron 184 population (Fig. 1h–i ). Additionally, we uncovered substantial evidence for the existence of 185 three distinct populations of MSNs within the putamen ( Supplementary Fig. 4c ). As expected, 186 we identified the canonical MSN subtypes marked by expression of dopamine receptor D1 187 (DRD1 ) or D2 ( DRD2 ) but we also identified a third population that expresses both DRD1 and 188 

DRD2 in addition to other previously unappreciated marker genes ( Supplementary Fig. 4d–f ). 189 This population corresponds to “eccentric” MSNs 32,33 , a transcriptionally distinct subtype of 190 MSNs that lies outside the canonical dichotomy of striatal output pathways. Intriguingly, DRD1-191 and DRD2-MSNs separate more cleanly in the RNA subspace than in the ATAC subspace, 192 while the DRD1/2 MSNs remain clearly delineated in both. 193 

We further subclustered the most abundant non-neuronal cell types, uncovering varying 194 degrees of regional specificity ( Supplementary Fig. 5a–b ). Differential expression analysis 195 identified subpopulation-specific markers, such as KLHDC7A + astrocytes in the substantia nigra 196 (Astrocyte Subcluster 1) and IGLC1 + microglia in the substantia nigra (Microglia Subcluster 2), 197 suggesting that non-neuronal cells adopt regionally specialized transcriptional programs tailored 198 to local environments ( Supplementary Fig. 5c ). 199

Cell type-specific transcriptional and cellular alterations reveal coordinated glial and 201 

neuronal responses in PD 202 

203 

Our dataset provides three primary angles from which to understand the molecular 204 pathogenesis of PD: (i) comparison of gene expression and chromatin accessibility differences 205 between cases and controls, (ii) comparison of cellular proportions between cases and controls, 206 and (iii) comparison of gene expression across the distribution of Lewy bodies within the brain. 207 

Using a case-control paradigm, we first performed differential gene expression analysis 208 between cases (N = 80) and controls (N = 21) using pseudobulked expression profiles from 209 each individual within each cell type ( Supplementary Table 2 ). For each test, we corrected for 210 age at death, biological sex, and postmortem interval if those covariates showed a correlation 211 (Pearson’s r p-value < 0.05) with any of the first five principal component values for 212 pseudobulked expression of that cell type ( Supplementary Fig. 6a ). We also performed a 213 permutation-based assessment of the reliability of those differentially expressed genes (DEGs) 214 by shuffling the case/control labels and determining the background number of DEGs that would 215 occur by chance within our dataset, an approach that explicitly accounts for the imbalanced 216 sample sizes of PD and control individuals in our cohort ( Supplementary Fig. 6b ). This 217 analysis highlighted oligodendrocytes, MSNs, excitatory neurons, and astrocytes as the cell 218 types with the greatest changes in transcriptional profile ( Fig. 2a, Supplementary Fig. 6c ). We 219 note that, as expected, the total number of DEGs detected is impacted by the number of cells of 220 the given cell type captured per sample and thus some cell types may remain underpowered 221 even in this large dataset ( Supplementary Fig. 6D ). Although dopaminergic neurons did not 222 pass permutation-based thresholds, pathway analysis of the genes identified converged on 223 dopamine uptake and catecholamine transport. Excitatory neurons similarly showed enrichment 224 for monoamine and catecholamine response pathways, together implicating dysregulated 225 neurotransmitter signaling as a central mechanism in PD pathogenesis 34  (Supplementary Fig. 226 

7a, Supplementary Table 3 ). To further understand regulatory changes associated with 227 transcriptional dysregulation, we performed a targeted analysis of chromatin accessibility at 228 regulatory elements mapping to DEGs, identifying gene-associated peaks with differential 229 accessibility between cases and controls ( Supplementary Table 4 ). Regulatory concordance 230 between chromatin accessibility and gene expression was most pronounced in MSNs, where 231 over half of the DEGs were associated with at least one differentially accessible regulatory 232 element, whereas glial cell types exhibited fewer detectable accessibility changes at DEG-linked 233 regions. 234 

When further stratifying this analysis based on brain region, we found that the most 235 drastic changes in oligodendrocytes and astrocytes occurred within the substantia nigra, the site 236 of the most pronounced neurodegeneration in PD ( Fig. 2b ). Across astrocytes, 237 oligodendrocytes, and excitatory neurons, these DEGs showed greater overlap than expected 238 by chance between brain regions based on permutation testing ( Fig. 2b, Supplementary Fig. 239 

7b ), suggesting that a subset of transcriptional responses in PD is coordinated across regions 240 and reflecting shared injury or response to injury mechanisms. Specifically, among 241 oligodendrocytes, the strongest overlap occurred between the substantia nigra and putamen 242 (Supplementary Fig. 7c–d ). Enrichment analysis further revealed that Oligodendrocyte 243 Subclusters 0 and 1 were most strongly enriched for regional DEGs ( Supplementary Fig. 7e ). 244 The broad regional representation in Oligodendrocyte Subcluster 0 suggests a common 245 oligodendrocyte response to PD, whereas the overrepresentation of substantia nigra 246 oligodendrocytes in Oligodendrocyte Subcluster 1 points to region-specific injury or response to 247 injury in this prominent site of neurodegeneration. Pathway analysis of these overlapping 248 regional DEG sets highlights nitric oxide–cGMP signaling, perisynaptic extracellular matrix 249 organization, and non-integrin membrane-extracellular matrix interactions, pointing to shared 250 alterations in synaptic plasticity and neuron–glia communication across these disease-relevant 251

As expected, the cerebellum, a region largely spared from neurodegenerative 254 features of PD, showed comparatively fewer significant gene expression changes ( Fig. 2b ). 255 Overall, these molecular alterations—particularly those observed in oligodendrocytes—are 256 consistent with previous reports in PD 35–38  and underscore the coordinated molecular responses 257 across cell types and regions within the diseased brain. 258 

In contrast to these differential expression results, we identified a largely distinct set of 259 cell types that showed significant differences in cellular proportions between PD cases and 260 controls. Whereas the majority of our differential expression results were identified in glial cells, 261 all of the cell populations that showed significant differences in cellular proportions were 262 neuronal, including multiple types of inhibitory neurons showing increased proportion in PD ( Fig. 263 

2c, Supplementary Fig. 8a ). This could be explained by compensatory mechanisms in glial 264 cells in response to neurodegeneration or could reflect the loss of some neuron types in 265 response to molecular alterations occurring in glial cells. Within the two brain regions profiled 266 that contain these neuronal types, the cellular proportion differences were more pronounced in 267 the cingulate gyrus than in the middle temporal gyrus ( Supplementary Fig. 8b ). The 268 prominence of changes in the cingulate gyrus is consistent with prior neuropathological and 269 imaging studies showing early Lewy body deposition and neuronal loss in this region, features 270 that have been associated with cognitive decline and neuropsychiatric symptoms in individuals 271 with PD 35,39,40 .272 

To better understand how these changes in transcription and cellular proportions relate 273 to the neuropathological progression of PD, we stratified our cohort based on the Unified 274 Staging System for Lewy Body Disorders 41  that has Stage I (N = 21) for olfactory bulb or 275 amygdala Lewy bodies, which are not neuropathological features of PD progression, and 276 subsequent stages that reflect the stereotypical progression of Lewy body accumulation in 277 individuals with PD: brainstem (Stage IIa; N = 6), then limbic (Stage III; N = 37), and finally 278 neocortical (Stage IV; N = 36) neurons. Stratifying across this progression, we observe that the 279 same cell types showing changes in cell proportion between disease and control in the cingulate 280 gyrus also show progressive changes across disease stages ( Fig. 2d and Supplementary Fig. 281 

8c ). At the transcriptional level, several genes displayed stage-associated expression dynamics. 282 Notably, SERPINH1 (aka HSP47 ), a gene encoding a collagen-specific chaperone implicated in 283 proteostasis and neuroinflammation in PD 7,42 , and PRLR , a gene encoding the prolactin receptor 284 which is linked to dopaminergic signaling 43 , both demonstrated increased expression in 285 astrocytes at late disease stages ( Fig. 2e ). We also identified chromatin accessibility peaks 286 from astrocytes that mapped to SERPINH1 that showed stage-associated changes, suggesting 287 that its transcriptional upregulation may be driven by dynamic regulatory mechanisms 288 (Supplementary Fig. 8d ). To further resolve cell type-specific stage-associated transcriptional 289 patterns, we classified these genes based on their trajectories through the pathological 290 progression of PD ( Supplementary Fig. 8e, Supplementary Table 5–6 ). MSNs were 291 particularly enriched for genes showing monotonic upregulation across Lewy body stages, 292 highlighting progressive, stage-linked transcriptional activation in this neuronal population 293 (Supplementary Fig. 8f ). Overall, these findings reveal progressive alterations in both cellular 294 state and cellular composition, pointing to mechanisms that may underlie the vulnerability of 295 specific cell types in PD. 296

Machine learning models enable rapid prediction of noncoding variant effects 324 

325 

To enable high-throughput prioritization of noncoding variant effects, we trained convolutional 326 neural network models to predict chromatin accessibility from DNA sequence. Specifically, we 327 used ChromBPNet 50  to train cell type-specific models from the 25 cell types with sufficient 328 representation in our dataset. We trained these ML models exclusively on the 21 control 329 individuals to avoid data leakage from genetic signals from patients with PD ( Fig. 4a ). We then 330 used these models to prioritize all rare noncoding variants observed in the 3,611 patients with 331 PD represented in the Global Parkinson’s Genetics Program (GP2; Fig. 4a ), which includes the 332 80 patients with PD sequenced in our study. Here, we define rare noncoding variants as those 333 variants that reside within a peak region in our dataset and have an allele frequency below 0.01 334 in all populations in the Genome Aggregation Database (gnomAD) 51 . We quantified the ML-335 predicted impact of each variant using the Jensen–Shannon Divergence (JSD) between the 336 predicted chromatin accessibility profile of the reference and alternate alleles, allowing us to 337 create a prioritized list of variants predicted to increase or decrease chromatin accessibility ( Fig. 338 

4b–c, Supplementary Fig. 9a ). These 25 ML models each showed robust performance in 339 predicting chromatin accessibility on held-out data (avg. Pearson’s r = 0.73; range 0.68–0.82), 340 with no clear relationship between model performance and number of cells used for training 341 (Supplementary Fig. 9b ). This high performance was observed both in genome-wide peak 342 regions and in cell type-specific peak regions, underscoring a key advantage of using cell type-343 specific models over composite models of chromatin accessibility ( Fig. 4d ). In each cell type, 344 we tested only the variants found within peak regions of the cell type, and on average, 345 prioritized 4% of all tested variants per cell type ( Fig. 4e ). The vast majority of variants had 346 predicted effects only in a single cell type ( Fig. 4f ), consistent with the well-known cell type-347 specificity of gene regulation 52 . Similarly, we observed a significant enrichment of rare variants 348 among the top 1% of effect scores compared with common variants ( Supplementary Data 2 ,349 

Fig. 4g , Fisher’s exact test, p-value < 2.2×10 -16 ; OR = 1.23; 95% CI: 1.22–1.24). Additionally, 350 rare variants exhibited significantly higher effect scores overall compared to common variants 351 (Wilcoxon rank-sum test, p-value < 2.2×10 -16 ), supporting the observation that rarer alleles are 352 more likely to exert strong predicted effects. Mapping of putative enhancer-gene interactions using chromatin conformation capture 358 

and single-nucleus multi-omics 359 

360 

With these prioritized variants in hand, we next sought to map putative regulatory elements to 361 their target genes with the goal of predicting the functional consequences of noncoding variants 362 in our data. To do so, we generated cell type-specific chromatin conformation data (Micro-C) 363 from neurons, oligodendrocytes, microglia, and astrocytes isolated by fluorescence-activated 364 nuclei sorting of middle temporal gyrus tissue ( Supplementary Fig. 10a–c ). To obtain sufficient 365 sorted nuclei from each cell type, we pooled nuclei across many donors prior to sorting (see 366 

Methods ). Given the high depth of the data, we called “loops” 53  at 10-kb resolution, identifying 367 120,512 total loops ( Supplementary Fig. 10d–e ). As expected, the majority of loops spanned 368 distances less than 500 kb, with 57–70% of anchors overlapping with a called ATAC-seq peak 369 in the respective cell type ( Supplementary Fig. 10f–l ). 370 

We then employed a tiered approach to link regulatory elements to their putative target 371 genes ( Supplementary Fig. 11a ). First, we used the Activity-By-Contact (ABC) model 54 , which 372 predicts enhancer-gene connections based on chromatin accessibility and three-dimensional 373 genome architecture. These ABC models were constructed using our cell type-specific snATAC-374 seq and Micro-C data, along with publicly available cell type-averaged Hi-C data and cell type-375 specific H3K27ac ChIP-seq when applicable. For peaks without ABC mapping, we used 376 correlation-based peak-to-gene links 55 , selecting associations with strong correlation scores ( r ≥377 

0.4) and statistical significance (FDR ≤ 0.01). When multiple linked peaks overlapped with a 378 query peak, we prioritized the peak with the greatest physical overlap and selected the gene 379 with the strongest correlation to the query peak. Finally, for the remaining unmapped peaks, we 380 assigned the nearest gene within 25 kb of the transcription start site as a potential target. Any 381 remaining unmapped peaks and the variants they contained were excluded from downstream 382 analyses. This hierarchical strategy yielded comprehensive regulatory element annotations 383 while prioritizing evidence-based functional connections over simple proximity-based 384 assignments ( Supplementary Fig. 11b–d ). 385

Rare noncoding variants impact PD-relevant genes 387 

388 

Application of this prioritization strategy highlighted biologically coherent sets of genes with 389 disproportionate burdens of predicted regulatory variation. Given the central relevance of MSN 390 subtypes highlighted throughout our work, we next focused on genes with high regulatory 391 burden in MSNs, identifying the genes with the highest summed effect variant burden across 392 MSN subtypes, including known neuronal regulators such as RBFOX3 , PDE10A , and PTPRN2 393 (Fig. 4i ). To extend this analysis across all cell types, we ranked genes by their normalized 394 burden of rare noncoding effect variants, accounting for differences in the number of regulatory 395 elements mapped to each gene. This revealed several genes involved in protein trafficking and 396 the ubiquitin–proteasome system, including KLHL12 , TVP23B , and LRRC59 , as well as THOP1 ,397 which contributes to peptide turnover ( Supplementary Fig. 11e ). Nomination of novel noncoding variants driving familial PD 520 

521 

Previous work has demonstrated that ~70% of familial early-onset PD cases and 80% of familial 522 late-onset PD cases do not have a known genetic cause 21 . These studies defined familial PD as 523 a family history of PD in up to a third-degree relative. The ability of ML models to rapidly 524 prioritize noncoding variants makes them well-suited for understanding these familial cases of 525 PD of unknown genetic origin. To this end, we analyzed families with multiple first-, second-, or 526 third-degree relatives with a clinical diagnosis of PD but no coding alteration in a known PD risk 527 gene. We performed genome-wide segregation analysis to identify all variants that tracked with 528 disease status and focused our downstream analyses on two families where no plausible coding 529 driver of PD could be identified. 530 

In the first family, all three brothers developed PD by age 60, although no information 531 was available about their parents ( Fig. 6a ). Due to the high genetic relatedness of the brothers, 532 7,283 variants segregated with the disease ( Fig. 6b ). Applying our ML models, we prioritized 533 102 unique noncoding variants within chromatin accessibility peaks. Of these, the variant with 534 the strongest predicted impact resided within one of the promoters of the PAQR5 gene and was 535 predicted to exert its effects in microglia ( Fig. 6c–d ). Within the brain, PAQR5 is expressed in 536 microglia, perivascular macrophages, and capillary endothelial cells ( Fig. 6e ), and this rare 537 variant is predicted to decrease the binding of an SPI1 transcription factor ( Fig. 6f ). PAQR5 538 encodes a progestin and adiponectin receptor family member, and adiponectin has been shown 539 to have a neuroprotective role in cellular and mouse models of ɑ-synucleinopathies 66 . Although 540 this variant is an intriguing and plausible driver of disease, additional, high-impact noncoding 541 variants were also predicted in this family, necessitating future functional genomics-based 542 studies to validate these and identify the true causal variant ( Supplementary Table 9 ). 543 

We additionally analyzed genetic data from a second family consisting of 4 affected 544 individuals of more distant relatedness ( Fig. 6g ). This pedigree enabled a more robust 545 segregation analysis, resulting in 1,895 segregating variants ( Fig. 6h ). Applying our ML models 546 to segregating variants within chromatin accessibility peaks, we found 15 unique noncoding 547 variants predicted to be functional ( Fig. 6i ). Of those, a variant intronic to the CAMK4 gene 548 arose as the likely causal variant, with a predicted effect in DRD1 MSNs ( Fig. 6j ). CAMK4 is 549 highly expressed in MSNs ( Fig. 6k ) and encodes a calcium/calmodulin-dependent protein 550 kinase implicated in transcriptional regulation in neurons 67 . The variant is predicted to increase 551 FOS-family transcription factor binding, potentially creating a novel gene regulatory element, 552 and likely increase the expression of CAMK4 (Fig. 6l ). Interestingly, gain-of-function coding 553 mutations in CAMK4 have been associated with a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized 554 by hyperkinetic movement 68,69 . Though PD is a hypokinetic movement disorder, the delicate 555 balance of signaling between DRD1 and DRD2 MSNs controls proper movement, and thus 556 perturbation of this balance may lead to either hypokinetic or hyperkinetic movement 70,71 . 563 

Discussion 564 

565 

Our study provides a framework for prioritizing and functionally interpreting rare noncoding 566 variants in complex diseases. By integrating large-scale single-nucleus multi-omic profiling, 567 whole-genome sequencing, and machine learning-based prediction, we bridge a long-standing 568 gap in connecting noncoding genetic variation to cell type-specific regulatory consequences. In 569 PD, where both common variant GWAS signals and rare coding variants have left much of the 570 heritability unexplained, this approach nominates candidate genetic drivers across relevant cell 571 types that have been previously overlooked. These findings underscore the importance of 572 considering noncoding variation, not as background noise, but as a critical component of 573 disease risk architecture. 574 

A major advantage of this study is its comprehensive scale and resolution. We identified 575 disease-associated alterations in both neuronal and non-neuronal cell populations by profiling 576 over 3.3 million individual cell nuclei spanning five distinct brain regions. Our analysis confirms 577 the known selective vulnerability of a specific dopaminergic neuron subset while simultaneously 578 uncovering extensive transcriptional changes in glia and other neuronal subtypes. Further, cell 579 type-specific QTLs and machine learning models developed from this dataset offer a robust 580 platform for statistically assessing and predicting the functional impact of regulatory variants at 581 scale. Notably, our machine learning-enhanced approach for rare variant association identifies 582 genes and biological pathways that align with well-established PD mechanisms, including 583 synaptic plasticity, protein trafficking, and cellular energy metabolism, while also highlighting 584 previously uncharacterized cellular processes and novel candidate genes for further 585 investigation. Importantly, the utility of our framework extends beyond sporadic disease to 586 familial cases of unknown genetic origin. By applying our models to whole-genome sequencing 587 data from familial PD pedigrees, we successfully nominated candidate causal variants in 588 affected individuals, demonstrating the broad applicability of our approach for variant 589 prioritization across different genetic architectures of PD. 590 

Nevertheless, several limitations to our study should be considered. For one, although 591 our schema allows for prioritization of noncoding variants within known chromatin accessibility 592 peaks, it may not capture variants that generate completely novel regulatory elements. Second, 593 although statistical association and allelic imbalance analyses confirm the functional impact of 594 noncoding variants, experimental proof of causality is still required to elucidate the underlying 595 molecular mechanisms. Moreover, while we use large sample sizes from studies such as GP2 596 and the UK Biobank, statistical power for rare variant association is inherently limited. A larger 597 discovery cohort with many more control individuals would undoubtedly increase the replication 598 rate of our findings in rare variant association testing. Similarly, many genetic signals may be 599 masked by aggregation of variants into gene-centric testing units. The increased number of 600 significant sets per cell type identified in our peak-based rare variant association testing 601 suggests that PD genetic signals may be more readily uncovered when testing is performed on 602 smaller, functionally coherent genomic units; however, this approach will require larger sample 603 sizes and improved functional genomics tools to robustly interpret findings at the peak level. 604 Future sequencing studies with larger cohorts, deeper multi-omic profiling, and functional 605 perturbation experiments will be needed to refine variant prioritization and mechanistic 606 understanding. 607

Multiome experimental design and sample pooling approach 929 

Before starting the project, we implemented an experimental design to reduce batch effects and 930 mitigate differences between samples that could be due to technical variability. Instead of 931 running each brain sample individually on one channel of the 10x Genomics chip, we 932 implemented a sample pooling approach and ran the pooled nuclei across multiple channels of 933 the chip. First, we defined a batch as a collective sample in which nuclei were isolated on the 934 same day and in the same manner, counted individually, and subsequently pooled together in 935 equal proportions. This pool subsequently underwent eight individual ATAC-seq transposition 936 reactions and was loaded onto the same Chromium Next GEM Chip J across all eight channels. 937 Most batches consisted of 12 samples. 938 

939 

To randomly assign samples to a particular batch, we used custom code that balanced different 940 cohort characteristics. For the 505 samples, we randomized based on brain region, whether the 941 sample was from an individual with PD or a control, biological sex, and ensured that an 942 individual was only included once within a batch to enable genotype-based demultiplexing. Our 943 experimental design also took into consideration having each of the five brain regions 944 represented within an individual batch, as well as a consistent balance between PD cases and 945 controls across all batches. 946

snATAC-seq and snRNA-seq data preprocessing 1068 

Reads were mapped onto the human reference genome (GRCh38) using CellRanger ARC 1069 (v2.02) software to obtain an aligned BAM, fragment, and raw_feature_bc_matrix files for each 1070 sample (10x Genomics).
