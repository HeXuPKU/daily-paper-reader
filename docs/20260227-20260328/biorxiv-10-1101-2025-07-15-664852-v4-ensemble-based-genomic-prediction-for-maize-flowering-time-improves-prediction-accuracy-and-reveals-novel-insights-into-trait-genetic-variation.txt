While various genomic prediction models have been evaluated for their potential to accelerate genetic gain 16

Article summary 38 

This study targets researchers interested in the performance of genomic prediction model s. To demonstrate 39 

potential advantages of an ensemble of diverse individual genomic prediction models , we investigated the 40 

predict ion of key flowering -time traits (days to anthesis and anthesis to silk ing interval ) in two maize datasets .41 

The ensemble approach consistently improved the prediction performance . The improvement was attributed 42 

to the offset of prediction error s by c ombining multiple different dimensions of trait genetic variation .43 

Ensembles can lead to higher selection accuracy of desirable individuals for applications in crop breeding. 44

Introduction 46 

Crop b reeding programs have targeted the development of more resilient genotypes for harsh biotic and abiotic 47 

stress conditions ( Kholová et al. 2021; Cooper and Messina 2023 ; Werner et al. 2025 ). The pressure s of climate 48 

change ha ve amplified such stress es in agricultural environment s, resulting in increasing demand for better -49 

adapted crop genotypes (Ceccarelli et al. 2010 ; Langridge et al. 2021 ). Consequently, b reeders have 50 

endeavoured to continuously select genotypes with improve d combinations of the target traits to accelerate 51 

genetic gain (Messina et al. 2023 ; Pixley et al. 2023 ).52 

Genomic selection ( Meuwissen et al. 2001 ; Bernardo and Yu 2007; Voss -Fels et al. 2019 ; Khaipho -53 

Burch et al. 2023; Crossa et al. 2025 ; E scamilla et al. 2025 ) is a key approach to accelerate genetic gain . The 54 

prediction of trait phenotypes using gen omic markers can be us ed to reduc e the length of each breeding cycle 55

Numerous genomic prediction models have been investigated across a wide range of applications to 63 

crop breeding programs (Cooper et al. 2014a,b ; Diepenbrock et al. 2022 ; John et al. 2022; Crossa et al. 202 5;64 

Escamilla et al. 2025 ). However , none of the proposed approaches has shown superio r performance for all 65 

traits and datasets . For example, Heslot et al. (2012) showed an absence of a consistent best model across 66 

eight prediction models (parametric and machine learning) applied to wheat, barley and maize datasets. 67 

Similarly, Plavšin et al. (2022) evaluated eight prediction models for quality traits in wheat, revealing that 68 

while ridge regression best linear unbiased prediction (rrBLUP) reached the overall highest performance, the 69 

differences from other prediction models were small and the most accurate genomic prediction model was 70 

dependent on the environment and the trait. Genomic prediction performance was investigated 71 

comprehensively by Washburn et al. (2025) ; the global genotype -by -environment (GxE) prediction 72 

competition evaluated a wide range of genomic prediction models from 128 teams for predicting maize yield 73 

traits. Their re sults showed that no individual genomic prediction model consistently improved prediction 74 

performance. Instead, an ensemble of parametric and machine learning models reached the overall highest 75 

prediction performance. Other empirical studies (Meher et al., 2022; Lourenço et al., 2024; Gibbs et al., 2025) 76 

that also compared the prediction performance of individual genomic prediction models consistently found 77 

that no prediction model was superior to others across different prediction scenarios created by combinations 78 

of environments, crops and datasets. These observed results demonstrate the limitations in the development 79 

of a single genomic prediction model that is stably generalised with high prediction performance in new 80 

environments under cross -validation settings. 81

Th e lack of a best individual genomic prediction model could be a consequence of the No Free Lunch 82 

Theorem (Wolpert and Macready 1997), which stat es that the mean performance of prediction models 83 

becomes equivalent across all scenarios . The lack of a consistently superior model is likely a consequence of 84 

individual prediction models being well -suited for specific scenarios , but not for all . Hence, the continuous 85 

exploration for an all -rounder individual genomic prediction model may not be an optimal approach to 86 

improve overall prediction performance .87 

Alternative ly, as shown in Washburn et al. (2025), multiple genomic prediction models can be 88 

combined as an ensemble to improve prediction performance rather than implementing them independently 89 

(Cooper et al. 2025; Messina et al. 2025 ). For instance, Nascimento et al . (2024) successfully improved 90 

genomic prediction performance for several traits , such as yield and total number of fruits , in an Arabica coffee 91 

breeding program using ensemble s of diverse prediction models . The success of ensembles can be 92 

theoretically explained by the Diversity Prediction Theorem ( Hong and Page 2004; Page 2007; Page 2018) ;93 

the use of diverse prediction algorithms in a Many -Model ensemble lowers the prediction error more than the 94 

average prediction error of individual prediction models . Hence, the prediction error of an ensemble depends 95 

on the evaluated prediction error s of individual models included in the ensemble and the diversity of their 96 

prediction s (Messina et al. 2025 ; Tomura et al. 2025b ). 97 

Using the framework of the Diversity Prediction Theorem, here w e investigate d whether an ensemble 98 

of diverse genomic prediction models improve d prediction performance by predicting maize flowering -time 99 

traits in two well -documented Nested Association Mapping (NAM) examples ( Buckler et al. 2009; Chen et al. 100 

2019) . The parents of th es e two NAM datasets represent a wide range of maize ge netic diversity (Fig. 1) ,101 

extending from domestication to modern inbred lines used for hybrid breeding (Hufford et al. 20 12). This 102 

study was guided by two objectives. Firstly, the prediction performance of the ensemble was compared with 103 

individual genomic prediction models to evaluate the expectations of the Diversity Pre di ction Theorem for 104 

prediction performance improvement. Secondly, the estimates of genomic marker effect s on the target traits 105 

from each genomic prediction model were compared to observe the association between the diversity in 106 

quantified marker effect values and the prediction performance of the ensemble. 107

Materials and Methods 109 

1. Datasets 110 

The TeoNAM dataset (Chen et al. 2019) is a collection of samples from the five recombinant inbred line (RIL) 111 

populations between the maize line W22 and five teosinte types (TIL01, TIL03, TIL11 and TIL14 from Z. 112 

mays ssp. parviglumis and TIL25 from Z.mays ssp. mexicana ). In each RIL population, the F1 was backcrossed 113 

with W22. After the backcross was completed, each population was self -pollinated four times to develop the 114 

RIL populations. A randomised complete block design was applied to test the RILs for each population 115 

contributing to the TeoNAM experiment. Each population was tested twice at the University of Wisconsin 116 

West Madison Agricultural Research Station. For W22TIL01, W22TIL03 and W22TIL11, the RILs were 117 

tested in the summers of 2015 and 2016. For W22TIL14, the RILs were tested in the summers of 2016 and 118 

2017, and for W22TIL25 , the RILs were tested in two different blocks in the summer of 2017. 119 

The MaizeNAM dataset (Buckler et al. 2009) consists of samples from 25 RIL populations derived 120 

from crosses between the maize line B73 and a diverse set of 25 inbred lines from temperate and tropical 121 

regions. The 25 RIL populations were developed through self -pollination to the F5 generation. Each 122 

population was tested in the following locations during the summer in the United States: Aurora in New York, 123 

Clayton in North Carolina, Columbia in Missouri and Urbana in Illinois. Two experiments were conducted at 124 

each location with a randomised design and hence each population was evaluated in the eight environments. 125 

The recorded phenotype scores in each environment were us ed to estimate the best linear unbiased predictions 126 

(BLUPs) with ASReml (v2.0) (Butler et al. 2017) in each population. The best linear unbiased estimates 127 

(BLU Es) were calculated by unshrinking the BLUPs per population using heritability , after removing the mean 128 

phenotype value for each population (Fig. S1). The combination of mean phenotype values and heritability 129 

unique ly characterised each population , showing mild positive correlations as the overall trend ( Fig. S2 ). In 130 

th e main manuscript, BLUEs were employed as the target trait phenotypes of each RIL in each prediction 131 

scenario for the MaizeNAM dataset . The comparable predictio n results and analysis for the BLUPs are 132 

reported in the Supplementary Material (Fig. S3, S10, S11 ). The total number of genomic markers (SNPs) and 133 

RIL samples from each target dataset is summarised in Table 1 for the dataset level and Table S1 for the 134 

population level .135

The selection of the two maize NAM datasets was motivated by the genetic diversity that cover ed a136 

continuum of maize lines from wild relatives to post -domesticat ion lines (Fig. 1 ; Hufford et al. 2012 ). The 137 

TeoNAM dataset contains the high est level of genetic diversity: Crosses of a temperate maize inbred with 138 

representatives of its ancestral species, Teosinte, can uncover components of trait genetic variation fixed 139 

during domestication. In contrast, the MaizeNAM dataset is less diverse, as crosses are limited to within a set 140 

of domesticated maize lines. Using the se two maize NAM datasets , characterised by different levels of genetic 141 

diversity, w e investigated the distinct characteristics of the genomic prediction models for these datasets to 142 

understand the predictive behaviour of the individual models and their ensemble. 143

2. Data preprocessing 145 

We applied the same data preprocessing methods reported by Tomura et al. (2025b). Briefly, for the TeoNAM 146 

dataset, missing SNP markers were imputed by assigning genomic marker values based on flanking markers. 147 

RIL samples with missing genomic markers for an entire chromosome and those without phenotype values 148 

were removed. For the MaizeNAM dataset, missing SNP markers were imputed with flanking markers and 149 

missing phenotypes were removed , as reported by Buckler et al. (2009). Hence, no further imputation was 150 

required. Prior to the ensemble -based analyses, the total number of SNPs was reduced further based on linkage 151 

disequilibrium (LD) using PLINK (v1.9) (Chang et al. 2015) . Any SNP with a squared correlation of 0.8 or 152 

above was removed using a window size of 30 kb and a step size of 5 SNPs in both datasets. 153 

The two flowering -time related traits , days to anthesis (DTA) and anthesis to silking interval (ASI) ,154 

were measured in both datasets. For the TeoNAM dataset, the DTA and ASI traits for each RIL were recorded 155 

across two environments as reported by Chen et al. (2019). These environments were concatenated for the 156 

ensemble genomic prediction analysis following Tomura et al. (2025b) , combining genotype and phenotype 157 

data from two environments into a single dataset with a factor indicating the corresponding environment for 158 

each RIL population dataset .159

Six individual genomic prediction models were implemented and their inferred marker effect s for prediction 186 

were extracted using the computational pipeline tool, EasiGP ( Tomura et al. 2025a ). Following Tomura et al 187 

(2025b), t hree conventional genomic prediction models (ridge regression best linear unbiased prediction 188 

(rrBLUP ; Meuwissen et al. 2001 ), BayesB (Meuwissen et al. 2001) and reproducing kernel Hilbert space 189 

regression (RKHS ; Gianola and Van Kaam 2008 )) and three machine learning models (random forest (RF; 190 

Breiman 2001), support vector regression (SVR; Drucker et al. 1996) and multi -layer perceptron (MLP; 191 

Rosenblatt 1958)) were applied in this study. For rrBLUP, the genomic marker effects are assumed to be 192 

normally distributed for all genomic markers . In contrast, some genomic marker effects can be shrunk to zero 193 

rather than forming a normal distribution in BayesB (Endelman 2011; de los Campos 2014) . BayesB has been 194 

widely applied to genomic prediction for crop breeding and hence used as a benchmark model among the 195 

Bayesian alphabet models in this study. For RKHS, genomic markers are mapped to the Hilbert space using 196 

a kernel that estimates the mean distance between genotypes as a squared Euclidean distance (del los Campos 197 

et al. 2009) . RF consists of numerous decision trees developed from a fraction of the given dataset . Each 198 

decision tree returns prediction values by traversing the tree with input attribute values. The mean predicted 199 

value from all the decision trees was calculated as the final prediction value. SVR predicts values by 200 

optimising the position of a hyperplane that maximises the number of samples included in the space defined 201 

as an epsilon tube. The size of the epsilon tube is de termined by the space between the hyperplane and the 202 

location of support vectors that create the outer boundary of the tube as the decision boundary (Drucker et al. 203 

1996). Hence, the hyperplane is optimised in consideration of adjusting the position of support vectors. MLP 204 

mimics human brain systems by continuous nonlinear aggregation of input data from neighbouring neurons 205 

in a forward direction (Dave and Kamlesh, 2014) . Those six prediction models have been widely used for 206 

genomic prediction in crop breeding and the three prediction models from each category , conventional 207 

(including parametric and semi -parametric) and machine learning , were chosen for balanced comparison . The 208 

ensemble -average model calculate s the mean predicted phenotypes from the predictions returned by the six 209 

individual models with equal weights. The genomic prediction models constructed in this study were trained 210 

using genomic markers in a numerical format to output predicted phenotypes for each prediction scenario. 211 

The details of the prediction scenarios are discussed in “2.6 Model evaluation ”.212

6. Model evaluation 275 

The individual and ensemble genomic prediction models were evaluated by iterative prediction scenarios with 276 

different settings. For the TeoNAM dataset, three different training -test set ratios (0.8 -0.2, 0.65 -0.35 and 0.5 -277 

0.5) were applie d to mitigate the data size effect. In each population -training -test -set scenario, RIL data were 278 

randomly sampled 500 times. Hence, 7,500 prediction scenarios (5 populations * 3 ratios * 500 samples) were 279 

developed per trait. The same three training -test set ratios were used in the MaizeNAM dataset. For each of 280 

the 25 populations in the dataset, the RILs were sampled 50 times for each population -training -test -set scenario, 281 

resulting in 3,750 prediction scenarios (25 populations * 3 ratios * 50 samples) per trait. In each prediction 282 

scenario, the genomic prediction models were trained , and their prediction performance was evaluated using 283 

the Pearson correlation coefficient and mean squared error ( MSE ). After recording the prediction performance 284 

metrics , the developed genomic prediction models were discarded , treating each prediction scenario as an 285 

independent prediction task . Using the record ed predicted phenotypes from the genomic prediction models, 286 

the three components of the Diversity Prediction Theorem (Equation (4)) were calculated for the ensemble .287

Discussion 443 

Breeders have continuously search ed for prediction methods that can consistently and accurately distinguish 444 

individual s with the desired traits in their breeding programs (Cooper et al. 2014a,b; Voss -Fels et al. 2019; 445 

Diepenbrock et al. 2022; Crossa et al. 2025 ; Escamilla et al. 2025 ). The results from previous genomic 446

prediction research indicat e that g enomic prediction models can accurately identify superior individuals under 447 

certain prediction scenarios (Azodi et al. 2019 ; Diepenbrock et al. 2022 ). However, a priori , it is often not 448 

possible to obtain information that determines the optimal combination of scenarios and genomic prediction 449 

models without a significant trial -and -error process , since no trends or patterns in the combinations are often 450 

observed (Jeon et al. 2023; Crossa et al. 202 5). For instance, RKHS reached the highest prediction accuracy 451 

on wheat yield prediction for one wheat dataset (Heslot et al. 2012). However, this trend may not always be 452 

observed. In our study, rrBLUP and BayesB showed relatively high prediction performance across different 453 

prediction scenarios. The prediction performance superiority for RKHS was not consistent, showing 454 

diminished RKHS prediction accuracy at one of the lowest levels for wheat yield traits in another dataset 455 

(Heslot et al. 2012). This trend of inconsistency in the best genomic prediction model was observed in other 456 

studies as well (Endelman 2011; Meher et al. 2022; Lourenço et al. 2024; Montesinos -López et al. 2024) . The 457 

overwhelming empirical evidence strongly supports the conclusion of no consistently outperforming 458 

individual genomic prediction models. 459 

Machine learning models also failed to consistently improve the prediction performance despite their 460 

ability to detect nonlinear prediction patterns , as observed in this study . Heslot et al. (2012) also emphasised 461 

lower prediction performance of machine learning models, including RF, SVR and MLP, compared to 462 

conventional genomic prediction models such as rrBLUP and Bayesian approaches for several yield traits in 463 

wheat and maize. Th eir results aligned with the observed results in our study, showing poor prediction 464 

performance of RF, SVR and MLP when used individual ly . Machine learning approaches did not necessarily 465 

outperform the conventional genomic prediction models despite the expectation of outperformance via 466 

capturing complex nonlinear prediction patterns incorporated in datasets (Howard and Lipka 2025). A smaller 467 

total number of data points (RIL records) in relation to the total number of included genomic markers might 468 

have hindered the detection of key prediction patterns, often described as the curse of dimensionality (Bellman 469 

1957). However, MLP reached higher prediction performance than GBLUP in other prediction datasets and 470 

traits, including yield traits, in wheat, maize and rice (Montesinos -López et al. 2025). The current machine 471 

learning models can outperform the conventional genomic prediction models for some prediction scenarios, 472 

drawing the same conclusion that the most preferable genomic prediction model is highly scenario dependent. 473

In contrast, the ensemble of multiple individual genomic prediction models consistently improved 474 

prediction performance in accor dance with previous investigations ( Kick and Washburn 2023; Washburn et 475 

al. 20 25 ) and the expectations of the D iversity Prediction Theorem , given that the individual genomic 476 

prediction models included in the ensembles were diverse (Hong and Page 2004; Page 2018; Messina et al. 477 

2025) . Hence, t he improvement in prediction performance achieved by the ensemble was marginal in some 478 

cases , depending on the diversity level in the predictive information included in the individual prediction 479 

models . However , the performance of the ensemble approach was always at least comparable to the mean 480 

prediction performance of individual genomic prediction model s. The significance of our study arises from 481 

connect ing the theorem to the empirical investigation, showing that the effect of the theorem is applicable in 482 

actual genomic prediction settings. The ensemble of predicted phenotypes through the mean value calculat ion 483 

consistently reduced prediction error for other genomic prediction studies in wheat (Wallach et al. 2018 ),484 

maize (Kick and Washburn 2023; Tomura et al. 2025b ) and coffee ( Nascimento et al. 2024 ) or reached the 485 

equivalent performance to the best multi -kernel RKHS models in wheat and pig traits (Tusell et al., 2014) ,486 

indicating that the prediction performance of the mean value -based ensemble models in their stud ies reached 487 

the highest or equivalent to th at of the best individual prediction model. Hence , both theory and the body of 488 

empirical evidence indicate that an ensemble of genomic prediction models may be a more efficient approach 489 

than continuous trial -and -error investigation to determine the best individual genomic prediction model for 490 

each trait and dataset .491 

The success of the ensemble approach demonstrated in this study is interpreted in part to be a492 

consequence of the high levels of genetic diversity represented in both datasets (Buckler et al. 2009; Hufford 493 

et al. 2012; Chen et al. 2019 ). The high levels of genetic variation in both NAM experiments contributed to 494 

the high levels of diversity in the predicted phenotypes and the estimated genomic marker effects from the 495 

individual genomic prediction models . While high diversity in predicted values can improve the performance 496 

of the ensemble approach , excessively diverse prediction values may not always lead to further increases in 497 

the prediction performance . The level of diversity needs to be constrained within a certain range in relation to 498 

th e prediction performance of individual prediction models that vary by prediction scenarios (Bian and Chen 499 

2021; Wood et al. 2023 ). The benefit s of such constrain ed levels of diversity align with the implication s of the 500

methodology , there is potential for further performance improvement using advanced ensemble design 543

prediction models with external information to integrate natural biophysical law s with in their prediction 553 

mechanisms (Technow et al. 2015; Messina et al. 2018 , 2025 ; Von Rueden et al. 2021 ; Diepenbrock et al. 554
