Abstract 16 

Accurate selection of favourable crop genotypes has motivated the exploration of diverse prediction 17 

algorithms for crop breeding applications . One genomic prediction method that has not been fully explored is 18 

graph attention network s (GAT) . By directly analys ing graphical data with the attention mechanism , GAT can 19 

incorporate the genotype -to -phenotype (G2P) structure to regulari se predictions . As one potential G2P 20 

structure , a gene network can be inferred from interpretable machine learning mode ls to eff ectively learn key 21 

features of prediction patterns , potentially improving prediction performance . Here , we investigated whether 22 

incorporating such data -driven prior knowledge into GA T improve d prediction performance compared to GAT 23 

models representing a continuum of G2P structures, ranging from infinitesimal to fully connected . Applying 24 

the Diversity Prediction T heorem , we also combined these diverse G2P structures into an ensemble of GAT 25 

genomic prediction models to integrate complementary strengths of multiple models . The results for flowering 26 

time traits in two maize nested association mapping datasets showed a lack of consistent performance 27 

improvement in the data -driven prior knowledge GAT model . However , consistent outperformance was 28 

observed for the ensemble of GAT models. Improved predictions from the ensemble model may be driven by 29 

its ability to capture a more complete representation of the inferred gene network through the integration of 30 

information from diverse G2P structures . The observed results using the GAT methodology provided the 31 

foundation for potential performance improvement using GAT by integrating biological prior knowledge 32 

derived from omics data and empirically verified gene interactions in future research, thereby potentially 33 

enhanc ing the GAT ensemble performance .34

Introduction 36 

Improving the accuracy of genomic prediction models (Meuwissen et al., 2001) has been a primary research 37 

target in crop breeding , motivated by the need for rapid and precise selection of heritable variation to accelerate 38 

genetic gain (Voss -Fels et al. , 2019; Escamilla et al. , 2025 ). Th is research target has promoted the continuous 39 

exploration of novel algorithms that can be applied to the datasets generated by crop breeding programs . From 40 

the early stages of genomic prediction research, parametric approaches, such as genomic best linear unbiased 41 

prediction (GBLUP) and Bayesian regression models, have been the primary target of investigations (Azodi 42 

et al., 2019). While the parametric models are still widely employed in crop breeding today, the emergence of 43 

non -parametric models, such as machine learning and deep learning models , has diversified genomic 44 

prediction models, aiming to capture the complex genotype -by -genotype interactions underlying genomic 45 

markers ( Cooper et al., 2005, 2025; Hammer et al. , 2006; Crossa et al., 2025 ; Messina et al., 2025 ). 46 

Application s of graph neural networks (Scarselli et al., 2008) provide opportunities to emulate the 47 

networks of genes that contribute to the genetic architecture of traits (Cooper et al. , 2005) . The algorithmic 48 

and technical advancement in deep learning models has enabled the analysis of graphically structured datasets 49 

using graph neural networks ( Bhatti et al., 2023; Vatter et al., 2023 ). One considerable advantage of graph 50 

neural networks is the direct incorporation of topology connecting nodes (storing a collection of feature 51 

values ) grounded in underlying relationship s (Scarselli et al., 2008 ). In particular, graph attention network 52 

(GAT; Veličković et al., 2018) models have been a widely utilised graph neural network model due to the 53 

flexibility of the attention mechanism , enhancing performance by allocating higher weights to critical 54 

predictive information (Brody et al., 2021). GAT has improved prediction performance in various tasks and 55 

hence has been utilised as a benchmark model for the evaluation of new prediction model s. Graph neural 56 

networks , including GAT , have already been applied to diverse research areas in biology, including drug 57 

discovery (Abate et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2025a), gene sequence analysis (Wang and Zhang, 2021; Wang et 58 

al., 2023) and disease discovery (Hernández -Lorenzo et al., 2022; Duan et al., 2023). While graph neural 59 

networks have been employed in several cases in crop breeding, such as crop yield prediction by converting 60 

the information from farming plots into nodes (Gupta and Singh, 2023) and suitability evaluation for crops 61

In genomic prediction for crop breeding, g raph neural networks can predict target traits while 64 

consider ing the possible relationship between genomic markers and target phenotypes represent ed as the 65 

genotype -to -phenotype (G2P) structure (Cooper et al. , 2005) . One possible representation of a G2P structure 66 

can be a gene network inferred from a statistical or interpretable machine learning model , considering the 67 

success of data -driven graph neural network approaches in other research areas ( Li et al., 2025; Tang et al., 68 

2025 ; Gouvêa et al., 2026 ). Data -driven prior knowledge can be utilised for genomic prediction when evidence 69 

indicates traits are regulated by complex genomic marker -by -marker interactions (Cooper et al., 2005; Powell 70 

et al., 2022). The direct relationship between genomic markers and traits can represent additive effects, 71 

whereas the interactions between the genomic markers can be used to represent non -additive effects. Such 72 

data -driven prior knowledge can provide prediction models with problem -specific information , expected to 73 

guid e the prediction models to efficiently capture key features that will have the greatest influence on 74 

prediction outcomes (Von Rueden et al., 2021). The benefit of such prior knowledge inclusion is especially 75 

emphasised when the training set size is small (Von Rueden et al., 2021) , which is true for many crop breeding 76 

programs that can only collect a small scale of phenotype data (Ramstein et al. , 2019; Mascher et al., 2024 ).77 

Consequently, the integration of data -driven gene network information as prior knowledge into genomic 78 

prediction models may help capture key additive and non -additive effects, expected to improve prediction 79 

performance ( Cooper et al. , 2005; Zhao et al., 2025). 80 

While graph neural networks with data -driven prior knowledge can be leveraged to improve prediction 81 

performance, other possible G2P structures have already been proposed to represent the relationship between 82 

genomic markers and traits. For instance, infinitesimal structures assume no explicit interactions among 83 

genomic markers , sharing the conceptual similarities with the standard BLUPs and Bayesian prediction 84 

models that primarily estimate additive genomic marker effects (Meuwissen et al. , 2001; Cooper et al., 2021) .85 

In contrast, fully connected graph structures may detect active non -additive interactions by allowing genomic 86 

markers to directly interact with others. Th is structure is inspired by the fully connected mechanism of neural 87 

network s composed of neurons and their edges connecting each other , motivated by mimicking the human 88

to capture a more accurate gene network by remov ing non -additive interactions that hardly influence the traits .92 

This continuum of different G2P structures (Figure 1; the infinitesima l, prior knowledge and fully connected )93 

captures a different facet of standing genetic variation of a trait genetic architecture that is usually influenced 94 

by the mixture of additive and non -additive effects , especially for complex traits . In such cases, the prediction 95 

performance of graph neura l network s based on respective G2P structures can potentially capture distinct 96 

prediction patterns . This has not explicitly been investigated through empirical genomic prediction studies for 97 

crop breeding despite its potential for influenc ing prediction performance .98 

An e nsemble of multiple graph neural networks grounded in different G2P structures could be another 99 

approach to improve prediction performance, as proposed by the Diversity Prediction Theorem (Page, 2018 ;100 

Messina et al. , 2025). The theorem suggests that an ensemble of diverse individual prediction models can be 101 

constructed to lower the prediction error and improve prediction accuracy . Experimental results have 102 

demonstrated that ensembles of multiple genomic prediction models have consistently improved prediction 103 

performance by increasing prediction accuracy and reducing prediction error in comparison to the individual 104 

genomic prediction models (Kick and Washburn, 2023; Meher et al., 2025; Tomura et al., 2025b). Hence, the 105 

ensemble of genomic prediction models, guided by diverse G2P graph structures, can also be evaluated for 106 

potential improvements beyond individual genomic prediction models. 107 

Here, we evaluat ed the prediction performance of graph neural networks with three G2P graph 108 

structures (infinitesimal, fully connected and data -driven prior knowledge ) and an ensemble of these graph 109 

neural networks through application to two maize experimental datasets . This study investigated the following 110 

three objectives. Firstly, the prediction performance of GAT models designed to utilise data -driven prior gene 111 

network knowledge was compared with other GAT models based on G2P graph structures that did not use the 112 

prior gene network knowledge . The prediction performance of the ensemble GAT models was also evaluated 113 

by comparing with each GAT model . Secondly, the performance decay of the GAT models with decreasing 114 

training set size was compared to analyse which GAT model is relatively less responsive to r educ ed training 115

Materials and Methods 120 

1.  Datasets 121 

We used two maize ( Zea Mays ) nested association mapping (NAM) datasets in this study , the TeoNAM (Chen 122 

et al., 2019) and MaizeNAM (Buckler et al., 2009) . The TeoNAM dataset is based on samples of recombinant 123 

inbred lines (RILs) from five populations . The maize inbred line W22 was crossed with five teosinte types ,124 

four from Z. mays ssp. parviglumis and one from Z. mays ssp. mexicana . In each population, RILs were 125 

developed by controlled self -pollination following backcrossing W22 with one of the teosinte types after the 126 

initial F1 cross. All TeoNAM experiments were conducted at the University of Wisconsin West Madison 127 

Agricultural Research Station using a randomised complete block design. The TeoNAM dataset was generated 128 

by evaluating each set of RILs in two environments : the W22TIL01, W22TIL03 and W22TIL11 populations 129 

were evaluated in the summer of 2015 and 2016. The W22TIL14 population was evaluated in the summer of 130 

2016 and 2017. The W22TIL25 population was evaluated in two environments during the 2017 summer. 131 

The MaizeNAM dataset has 25 populations of RILs from crosses between the maize inbred B73 and 132 

25 inbreds sampled from temperate and tropical regions . The 25 RIL populations were developed by 133 

advancing the F1 crosses to the F5 generation by controlled self -pollination. Each RIL population was 134 

evaluated in eight environments with a randomised design in the United States. Two experiments were 135 

conducted at each location in Aurora (New York ), Clayton (North Carolina ), Columbia (Missouri ) and Urbana 136 

(Illinois ). The total number of genomic markers and RILs from each dataset is summarised in Table S1. 137 

The primary reason for selecting these two datasets is the ir contrast in genetic diversity . The TeoNAM 138 

dataset cross ed a domesticated maize line with teosinte, an ancestral species of maize , leading to variation in 139 

allelic frequency for many genomic regions among the crosses . In contrast, the MaizeNAM contains crosses 140 

among post -domesticat ion inbred lines developed by breeding programs , thereby driving higher levels of 141

3.  Genomic prediction models 176 

3.1 Prediction mechani sms 177 

In this study, all GAT (Veličković et al., 2018) models and interpretative analyses were implemented using the 178 

computational to ol , Ensemble AnalySis with Interpretable Genomic Prediction (EasiGP; Tomura et al., 2025 a),179 

developed in Python (v3.11.10). GA T is a graph neural network that can directly analyse graphically structured 180 

data to predict target values . Graph neural networks extract key information for predictions from nodes and 181 

edges in graphs as embeddings, concentrating the information into vectors of numerical format. Hence, the 182 

extraction of embeddings contain ing key predictive information is a critical aspect of graph neural networks. 183 

One significant characteristic of the GAT model is the application of a self -attention mechanism (Brody 184 

et al., 2021) , assigning a weight to each piece of information from graph edges based on the level of their 185 

relevance towards prediction targets. Assigning a larger attention value to an edge passing relevant information 186 

during the embedding process helps the GAT model precisely capture key information from graphs. The 187 

attention mechanism can be written as below (Brody et al., 2021): 188

Three distinctive GAT models (infinitesimal model, fully connected model and prior knowledge 206 

model) were developed based on three form s of possible G2P structures (Figure 1). The infinitesimal model 207 

(Figure 1 a) assigned no edges between the genomic marker nodes to explicitly represent the independent 208 

contribution s of the additive genomic marker effects . Thus, for the infinitesimal GAT model , no non -additive 209 

marker effects were allowed. The fully connected model (Figure 1 b) predicted phenotypes using a graph with 210 

gen om ic marker nodes connected to all the other nodes with undirected edges . In this case, all gen om ic 211 

markers are assumed to interact explicitly with other gen omi c markers to capture the potential for non -additive 212 

marker effects between all combinations of markers . The data -driven prior knowledge model (Figure 1 c) is 213 

intermediate between the infinitesimal and fully connected extremes. The implementation of the prior 214 

knowledge model considered here was supervised by a gene networ k inferred from a random forest (RF ;215

3.2  GAT hyperparameter selection 233 

Hyperparameters of the GAT models were tuned to improve the prediction performance (Table S2 ). The 234 

app lication of the same hyperparameter sets f rom the TeoNAM dataset did not reach high prediction 235 

performance for the MaizeNAM dataset , and hence the hyper parameter sets were tuned at the dataset level for 236 

each GAT model. For the TeoNAM dataset, one hidden layer with 50 neurons was added with a dropout rate 237

0.0004 was used for the optimisation. The number of heads was 1. The number of epochs was set as 200 for 241 

the infinitesimal model and 50 for the remaining models. For the MaizeNAM dataset, the same 242 

hyperparameter settings in the TeoNAM dataset were used for the number of hidden layers, neurons, activation 243

5.  Gene network inference 279 

The extracted genomic marker effect s from the GAT models were projected to their corresponding genomic 280 

marker regions, accompanied by the selected genomic marker -by -marker interaction effect s from RF. These 281 

gene networks were visualised in the form of circos plots (Krzywinski et al., 2009 ) as the final phase in EasiGP 282

Discussion 430 

1. More comprehensive prior knowledge may improve the prediction performance of GAT models 431 

The primary objective of this study was to investigate the possibility of performance improvement in genomic 432 

prediction through the integration of data -driven gene networks as prior knowledge over other G2P structure s,433 

analysed through the implementation of GAT models . A lack of consisten cy in prediction performance 434 

improvement has remained a critical issue despite the introduction of numerous prediction algorithms ,435 

attributed to the effect of the No Free Lunch Theorem (Wolpert and Macready , 1997) . The integration of data -436 

driven prior knowledge was expected to mitigate this problem by customising genomic prediction models into 437 

problem -specific ones. 438 

Contrary to the expected outcome , t here was still a lack of consistency in the overall prediction 439 

performance improvement of the GAT models using data -driven prior knowledge (Figure 2 ; Table S3 ). There 440 

are a number of possible reasons. Th is in consistent performance improvement using data -driven prior 441 

knowledge might have been attributed to the potential imprecision in the captured gen omi c marker -by -marker 442 

interactions. Shapley scores were estimated using SHAP (Lundberg and Lee, 2017) incorporated in EasiGP ,443 

approximating Shapley values as “scores” instead of calculating the actual values to overcome the prohibitive 444 

computational time by leveraging conditional expectations and a perturbation approach (Janzing et al., 2020 ;445 

Molnar et al. , 2020 ; Contreras et al., 2024 ). A series of a pproximation f ormulas , such as kernel -based 446 

approaches , allow s the estimation of Shapley values in a computationally efficient manner ( Lundberg and Lee, 447 

2017 ). However, such an approximation approach may calculate Sha pley scores that may not be aligned with 448 

actual Shapley values , especially when a number of irrelevant attributes were included in the datasets (Huang 449 

and Marques -Silva , 2024) . The approximation formulas can be affected by the irrelevant attributes that can 450 

distort Shapley scores as noise. The leveraged perturbation approach may also affect this imprecision by 451 

considering the attribute value combinations that do not reflect real -world conditions (Contreras et al., 2024). 452 

Hence , Shapley scores may not represent the actual influential size of each attribute (genomic marker) .453 

Inaccurate Shapley scores may have compromise d the effective ness of the supervis ion of the GAT models in 454 

this study .455

To capture more informative prior knowledge from data , genomic marker -by -marker interactions can 456 

be inferred as epistasis using conditional dependencies between the markers (Behrouzi and Wit, 2019). This 457 

approach only extracts genomic marker pairs that are directly conditionally dependent, removing noncausal 458 

relationships created by any genomic marker pair that is conditionally independent but correlated through 459 

another genomic marker that is highly correlated with the two. This alternative approach may help capture 460 

more precise relationships among genomic markers. Exclusion of noncausal connections was not explicitly 461 

implemented in the current Shapley score -based method. Another approach for estimating genomic marker -462 

by -marker interactions is BShap (Sundararajan and Najmi, 2020), which extends Shapley scores using a 463 

baseline. The baseline returns a prediction value excluding the effect of a target attribute (genomic marker). 464 

The contribution of the target attribute is calculated as the difference between the prediction values including 465 

and excluding the target attribute, as employed in Integrated Gradients. These potential approaches may vary 466 

in how they weigh genomic marker -by -marker interaction effects , consequently generating diverse views of 467 

the standing genetic variation . Thus, integration of all these approaches into an ensemble might enhance 468 

prediction performance by providing more comprehensive prior knowledge inferred from data to assist the 469 

GAT models. Alternative approaches for marker -by -marker interaction effect inference are an area for further 470 

investigation as indicated by this study. 471 

The complexity of gen omic marker -by -marker interaction s might also have affected the 472 

informativeness of the data -driven prior knowledge . ASI is a secondary trait (Leng et al., 2022) calculated by 473 

the difference between DTA and DTS and thus can be regulated by the gene network of both quantitative traits 474 

with other crop development traits (Messina et al. , 2019 ). Consequently , ASI might be influenced by t he 475 

combination of two or more distinct gene networks , thereby increa sing the complexity of the trait gene 476 

network . The current inferred gene network likely did not capture such complex interactions between gen omic 477 

markers in ASI due to the lower prediction performance of RF used to calculate the Shapley score s. Hence, 478 

the integrated data -driven prior knowledge may not have improve d the prediction performance of GAT for 479 

ASI regulated by potentially more complex gene networks .480

3. Genomic m arker -by -marker interactions can supplement information loss for small training sets 565 

The prediction performance of the infinitesimal model was more reactive to the size of the training set. The 566 

reduction rate of prediction performance was larger than that of the fully connected or data -driven prior 567 

knowledge models when the size of the training set became smaller , especially for DTA in the TeoNAM dataset 568 

and ASI in the MaizeNAM dataset (Figure 3). Maintaining prediction performance in the non -infinitesimal 569 

models might have been due to the simplicity of the provided graph structures in the GAT infinitesimal model .570 

It might have been relatively easy for the infinitesimal model to learn key prediction patterns with a large 571 

training set due to the absence of explicit complex interactions between gen om ic markers in their graph 572 

structures. Consequently, the infinitesimal model outperformed the other models. However, the simplicity in 573 

the graph structure reduced the effectiveness of the infinitesimal models in learning key predictive patterns 574 

when the allocated training set was small . There is a tendency that a star -shaped (infinitesimal) graphical 575 

structure can cause overfitting as the training set becomes smaller ( Bechler -Speicher et al., 2024 ). In contrast, 576 

the explicit marker -by -marker interactions might have supplemented the information loss derived from the 577 

diminishing size of the training set for the non -infinitesimal models. These explicit marker -by -marker 578 

interactions might have improved the prediction performance of the non -infinitesimal model when the training 579 

set size became smaller. 580 

Among the non -infinitesimal models in this study , the integration of data -driven prior knowledge 581 

showed higher prediction performance as an overall trend. Unnecessary explicit interactions between gen omi c582 

markers can introduce noise , thereby negatively affecting prediction performance. Selecting gen om ic marker -583 

by -marker interactions using RF with Shapley scores might have excluded such unnecessary explicit 584 

interactions. Hence, prior knowledge can provide more accurate marker -by -marker interaction information 585 

into graphs, contributing to performance improvement especially when the training set is small (Schapire et 586 

al., 2002). In crop breeding programs, it can be a challenge to conduct large experiments based on a large 587 

number of individuals with their genotypic and phenotypic information recorded (Ibba et al., 2020; Sneller et 588 

al., 2021 ; Jines et al. , 2025 ). Therefore, using prior knowledge information for the GAT model s can be a 589 

preferable approach compared to the fully connected GAT models. 590

mechanism of the standard ridge regression ( Meuwissen et al. , 2001 ) with a high penalty and Bayesian 592 

regression ( Meuwissen et al., 2001 ) with a strong prior around 0 for many marker effect s. However, a critical 593 

advantage of the prior knowledge model is the direct integration of topological information into the prediction 594 

mechanisms , given that the amount of training data is sufficiently large . GAT prior knowledge models can 595 

utilise the inferred topolog ical information to effectively extract key predictive patterns as embeddings. Such 596 

topological information cannot be utilised by the conventional approaches trained on tabular data .597 

598 

4. Interpretability of genomic prediction models can accelerate genetic gain 599 

The interpretability of the GAT models and the ensemble approach can identify gen omic marker regions that 600 

are highly influential on target traits. Identifying preferred allele combinations in the highlighted gen omi c601 

marker regions using the interpretable GAT models help s breeders select individuals with desirable traits . The 602 

accordance rate of their allele s in those gen omi c marker regions can be used as a selection criterion , in contrast 603 

to considering all alleles across the genome . This potential selection criterion can be used simultaneously with 604 

other criteria , such as the ranking from predicted phenotypes and genetic diversity . The combination of 605 

multiple selection criteria can improve the selection performance of individuals with more desirable traits .606 

Pook et al. (2025) accelerated the genetic gain of simulated maize breeding programs by developing a formula 607 

that combi nes several selection criteria from the view of short -term, long -term genetic gain and the level of 608 

genetic diversity . Hence, t he combination of diverse selection criteria can also positively affect the speed of 609 

genetic gain. 610 

The interpretable genomic prediction models also identified several new genomic marker regions . This 611 

interpretability -oriented genomic prediction approach has been app lied to infer the gene network in other 612 

fields such as detecting genes influencing body heights and schizophrenia in human s ( Askland et al., 2021 ;613 

van Hilten et al., 2021 ; Hequet et al., 2025 ), muscle growth in livestock (Guo et al., 2024) and fungus tole rance 614 

in ash trees (Doonan et al., 2025) . Interpretable machine learning models ha ve started be ing used in crop 615 

breeding with the application of interpretable RF and deep learning models ( Novielli et al., 202 4; Wang et al., 616 

2025 b), but no other studies ha ve collectively utilised interpretable genomic prediction models as an ensemble. 617

Conclusion 625 

The potential for developing GAT genomic prediction methodology for crop breeding applications has been 626
