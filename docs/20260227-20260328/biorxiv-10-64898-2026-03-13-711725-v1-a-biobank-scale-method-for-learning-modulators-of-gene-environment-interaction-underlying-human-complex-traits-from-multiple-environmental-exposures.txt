Abstract 12 

It is increasingly recognized that genetic e !ects on complex traits and diseases are shaped by en-13 

vironmental context. Biobanks that measure diverse environmental exposures alongside genotypes and 14 

phenotypes at scale enable systematic study of gene-environment (G →E) interactions. Existing ap-15 

proaches, however, are limited in their ability to accurately model polygenic G → E involving many expo-16 

sures across genome-wide genetic variants. It is unclear which exposure combinations are relevant for a 17 

given trait while distinguishing true interactions from environment-dependent heteroskedastic noise. To 18 

address these challenges, we develop E”cient multi-eNvironmental Gene-environment Interaction iNfer-19 

ence Estimator (ENGINE), a supervised variance-component framework that learns an embedding that 20 

combines multiple environmental exposures while jointly estimating additive, G →E, and heteroskedastic 21 

noise components. To enable biobank-scale inference, ENGINE makes a single pass over the genotype 22 

matrix to cache genotype-dependent summaries, then assembles normal-equation components and gra-23 

dients at each iteration. In simulations, ENGINE controls type I error rates, achieves high power, and 24 

accurately recovers the environmental embedding while remaining e ”cient at biobank-scale. Applied to 25 

five complex traits paired with lifestyle exposures in N = 291 ,273 unrelated white British individuals 26 

and M = 454 ,207 common SNPs (MAF > 0.01) from the UK Biobank, ENGINE recovered G →E variance 27 

that was on average 1 .4-fold larger than that captured by a single exposure and 5 .5-fold larger than that 28 

captured by the first principal component of the exposures. 29

Gene–environment (G →E) interactions—wherein the magnitude and sometimes the direction of a variant’s 31 

e!ect depends on environmental context—are increasingly recognized as important contributors to the genetic 32 

architecture of complex traits [1–5]. Growing empirical evidence for context-dependent allelic e !ects across 33 

environments [6, 7] suggests that explicitly modeling G →E can increase explained variance, help account for 34 

components of missing heritability, and improve risk stratification in predictive models [8–12]. 35 

In practice, the operative “environment” is rarely a single exposure [13–17]. Large biobanks now mea-36 

sure a wide array of exposures that collectively constitute the exposome [18–22]. Within this exposome, 37 

lifestyle and behavioral factors, medication use, and sociodemographic context covary and can jointly mod-38 

ulate genetic e !ects via G →E. For instance, low-density lipoprotein (LDL) levels may reflect interactions 39 

between lipid-related loci and age, while statin use, diet, and physical activity further modify those genetic 40 

influences [8, 23–25]. Modeling such multi-environment modulation requires methods that can accommodate 41 

many environmental variables, retain interpretability at the level of individual environments, and scale to 42 

biobank-sized cohorts. 43 

Polygenic models quantify the genome-wide contribution of G →E by aggregating variant-level interaction 44 

e!ects, thereby o !ering higher power than single-variant approaches when individual e !ects are weak. In par-45 

ticular, variance component or mixed models are well-suited to model aggregate genetic signal, arising from 46 

additive genetic e !ects and from interactions such as those due to G →E, within a single framework [7, 26–29]. 47 

Early variance component approaches for modeling polygenic G →E (e.g., GCTA-G →E [30], MV-GREML [31], 48 

MRNM [26]) enabled inference for interactions with individual environmental exposures. LEMMA [32] mod-49 

els polygenic G →E by learning an environment score—a data-driven combination of exposures–that allows 50 

multiple environments to jointly modulate genetic e !ects. However, because LEMMA does not explicitly 51 

model environment-dependent residual variance, estimates of G →E variance can be biased upward under noise 52 

heterogeneity, leading to inflated false positive rates [8]. MonsterLM [9] scales to biobank-sized datasets and 53 

yields approximately unbiased estimates in many settings; however, its reliance on pruning and partitioning 54 

the genome into relatively weakly correlated blocks (and its focus on common variation) can limit power and 55 

scope for discovering G →E e !ects. GxEMM [7] and GENIE [8] incorporate context-specific heteroskedastic-56 

ity within a variance-components framework; GENIE is designed to scale to biobank-sized cohorts, whereas 57 

GxEMM becomes computationally infeasible for large samples. Despite these advances, approaches to learn 58 

which combinations of environmental exposures contribute to polygenic G →E on a biobank-scale remain 59 

limited. 60 

Here we present ENGINE, E!cient multi-eNvironmental Gene-environment Interaction iNference Esti-61 

mator , a method that jointly learns the combination of environments that interact with polygenic e !ects 62 

for a given trait, estimates the contribution of each environment to the interaction signal while produc-63 

ing calibrated variance-component estimates for additive e !ects, G →E, and noise by explicitly modeling 64 

environment-dependent heteroskedasticity (Figure 1). ENGINE scales to biobank-sized cohorts by caching 65 

genotype-dependent intermediate quantities so that subsequent model updates avoid repeated full passes 66 

over the genotype matrix. In simulations, ENGINE is well calibrated and accurately estimates the variance 67 

components and the environmental embedding. Applied to five complex traits with lifestyle exposures in 68 

UK Biobank, ENGINE detects substantially larger G →E signal than analyses that consider environments 69 

one at a time or replace them with unsupervised embeddings. Together, ENGINE provides a principled 70 

and tractable path to disentangling how multiple environments jointly modulate genetic e !ects on complex 71 

traits. 72

# 4 Discussion 286 

We present ENGINE, a scalable multi-environment G →E estimator that learns a one-dimensional environ-287 

mental embedding to concentrate interaction signal while explicitly modeling context-dependent noise. The 288 

central computational idea is to precompute genotype-dependent summaries and cache them, so each iter-289 

ation can form gradients and update variance components without repeated passes over the full genotype 290 

matrix. This design makes training practical at biobank scale. Methodologically, cross-fitting and ϖ1 regu-291 

larization reduce leakage from additive e !ects and noise-by-environment components, improving calibration 292 

while retaining strong power under heterogeneous noise. 293 

Interpreting the learned embedding requires care. Because the interaction term is bilinear, optimiza-294 

tion over the environment weights is nonconvex and may converge to di !erent local optima depending on 295 

initialization. Nevertheless, we found that ENGINE yielded generally consistent embeddings and variance-296 

component estimates across random seeds (Figure S4). In practice, stability can be improved by imposing 297 

mild structure—for example, constraining weights to be nonnegative after preprocessing and using multiple 298 

random starts to report a consensus embedding when feasible. We also note that the embedding is trained 299 

in the European-ancestry cohort; transfer to other populations depends on the degree to which causal vari-300

Several directions could broaden applicability and robustness. First, cache size currently scales as O(L2)305

practical remedy is to compress storage using anchored sketches—for example, maintaining a single “anchor” 307 

environment sketch and storing per-exposure projections or scaling factors relative to this anchor—thereby 308 

preserving fast iterations while reducing memory. Second, correlated exposures remain challenging: we 309 

currently standardize and whiten inputs for stability, but learning directly on correlated features would im-310 

prove usability and interpretability. Covariance-aware parameterizations or structured regularization that 311 

explicitly models correlation could replace preprocessing while preserving identifiability. Third, ENGINE 312 

currently learns linear combinations of exposures; incorporating modest nonlinearity (e.g., low-degree fea-313 

ture maps or kernelized expansions with sketching) could capture higher-order environmental e !ects without 314 

compromising scalability. Beyond variance decomposition, the learned embedding can be fixed and reused 315 

in downstream analyses to estimate stabilized SNP-by-embedding interaction e !ects, facilitating locus pri-316 

oritization and biological interpretation. An additional theoretical direction is to establish convergence 317 

guarantees for sketch-based updates and error bounds that quantify approximation accuracy as a function 318 

of the number of random probe vectors. 319 

Overall, ENGINE provides an e ”cient and well-calibrated framework for discovering environmental con-320 

texts that modulate genetic e !ects on complex traits, bridging statistical interpretability with biobank-scale 321 

computation. 322
