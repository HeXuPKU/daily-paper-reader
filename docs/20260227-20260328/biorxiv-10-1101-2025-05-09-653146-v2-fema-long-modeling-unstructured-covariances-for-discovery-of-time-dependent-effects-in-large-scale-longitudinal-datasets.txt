# Abstract 1

While linear mixed-effects (LME) models are common for analyzing longitudinal data, most users rely on 2random intercepts or simple stationary covariance, due to unavailability of computationally tractable 3solutions. Here, we extend the Fast and Efficient Mixed-Effects Algorithm (FEMA) and present FEMA-4Long, a computationally tractable approach to flexibly modeling longitudinal covariance suitable for high-5dimensional data. FEMA-Long can: i) model unstructured covariance, ii) model covariates as smooth 6functions using splines, iii) discover time-dependent effects of covariates with spline interactions, and iv) 7use these flexible longitudinal modeling strategies to perform longitudinal genome-wide association studies 8and discover time-dependent genetic effects, in a computationally scalable manner, suitable for high-9dimensional data. Through extensive simulations, we show that estimates from FEMA-Long are accurate, 10 while being up to several thousand times faster and with minimal carbon footprint. To show the utility of 11 FEMA-Long for discovering novel biological signal, using data from the Norwegian Mother, Father and 12 Child Cohort Study (MoBa), we performed a longitudinal genome-wide association study with non-linear 13 SNP-by-time interaction on length, weight, and BMI of 68,273 infants with up to six measurements in the 14 first year of life. We found dynamic patterns of random effects including time-varying heritability and 15 genetic correlations, as well as several genetic variants showing time-dependent effects, highlighting the 16 applicability of FEMA-Long to enable novel discoveries. FEMA-Long is available at: 17 https://github.com/cmig-research-group/cmig_tools. 18 

Keywords : mixed-effects modeling; GWAS; longitudinal analysis; green algorithm; MoBa; MBRN 19 

# Author summary 20 

Most large-scale datasets have complexities such as repeated measures, related individuals, or other 21 dependencies across samples, preventing the use of standard regression approaches for analysis. In such 22 circumstances, linear mixed-effects modeling is often employed. However, for high-dimensional datasets, 23 fitting these models is quite challenging. Further, most standard uses of linear mixed-effects modeling focus 24 on simpler covariance models, which may not hold. Here, we introduce FEMA-Long, a novel 25 computationally efficient analytical framework for fitting linear mixed-effects models with time-varying 26 random effects, as well as allowing the effect of the covariates to change smoothly over time by using 27 splines. This is particularly relevant when, for example, studying the effect of genetic variants on 28 phenotypes, where the effects could be non-linear over time. The FEMA-Long framework allows time-29 varying heritability as well as discovery of genetic variants that show time-dependent effects. By 30 performing a genome-wide association study on data from the Norwegian Mother, Father and Child Cohort 31 Study (MoBa) using FEMA-Long, we show the discovery of genetic variants with time-dependent effects 32 on infant length, weight, and BMI during the first year of life. # Introduction 1

Large-scale longitudinal datasets are critical for novel discoveries in health and medicine and can transform 2healthcare (1). Longitudinal data allows studying the dynamic processes underlying health and disease 3development/progression by modeling trajectories, examining time-dependent effects of variables, 4investigating association with health indicators and outcomes, and making predictions of outcomes. 5Examples of large-scale densely-sampled datasets include the UK Biobank (https://www.ukbiobank.ac.uk), 6All of Us (https://allofus.nih.gov), the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development℠ Study 7(https://abcdstudy.org), and many others (2). 8To leverage the full potential of these studies, we need computationally efficient modeling strategies, 9optimized for large-scale high-dimensional datasets. These strategies need to account for non-independence 10 of observations (e.g., repeated measurements and relationships between individuals), other sources of 11 variance (e.g., shared environment), covariance in the data (e.g., correlations across time), flexible 12 modeling of longitudinal trajectories, and enabling discovery of time-dependent effects. Linear mixed-13 effects (LME) models are a powerful class of analytical strategies that can account for these issues in 14 longitudinal data such as non-independence of the samples and other known sources of variance modeled 15 as random effects, in addition to including variables with non-linear effects on the outcome and non-linear 16 interactions. Examples of these random effects include the similarity of measurements for the same subject, 17 variance explained by genetics (heritability), variance attributable to shared environment, etc. 18 When using LME models on longitudinal datasets, random effects should be specified as time-varying. For 19 example, heritability can vary significantly over time (3–8). Similarly, correlations of measurements within 20 subjects can change over time (e.g., autocorrelation). However, time-varying random effects are seldom 21 modeled, given the significant computational burden of fitting such models. Thus, researchers use 22 simplified LME models like compound symmetry (constant covariance over time) or making assumptions 23 about the form of covariance (e.g., autoregressive models) which may not fit the data well. In addition, 24 there is a lack of LME modeling software that scales to the needs of high-dimensional data (9) such as when 25 estimating the marginal effects of several million single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in genome-wide 26 association studies (GWAS), or when fitting mass-univariate models to millions of outcome variables, as is 27 common in neuroimaging. This has severely limited our ability to maximize the use of high-dimensional 28 longitudinal datasets. 29 Previously, we developed the Fast and Efficient Mixed-Effects Algorithm (FEMA) (10) for fitting a large 30 number of LME models in a matter of seconds to minutes. FEMA massively accelerates LME fitting by 31 using a covariance-regression approach to estimate random effects parameters and then quantizes these for 32 vectorized fitting of many phenotypes simultaneously. For example, FEMA can fit ~169,000 LME models 33 with ~10,000 observations in ~54 seconds, a speedup of more than 3000 times, compared to standard LME 34

The features of FEMA-Long include i) modeling time-varying random 2effects using unstructured covariance, ii) modeling non-linear effects using splines, iii) examining time-3dependent effects using spline interactions, and iv) performing GWAS, supporting the discovery of time-4dependent effects. Through extensive simulations we show that FEMA-Long accurately models known 5sources of variance in a computationally fast manner while maintaining false positive rates. We demonstrate 6the use of FEMA-Long by performing a longitudinal GWAS on length, weight, and body mass index (BMI) 7of 68,273 infants with up to six repeated measurements (299,447 observations) during the first year of life, 8a highly dynamic period of development, and discovering SNPs showing time-dependent effects. 9

# Description of the method 10 

Nomenclature 11 

We use the term phenotype to refer to a continuous outcome variable (or dependent variable) with the error 12 in the phenotype approximately following a Gaussian distribution; we use the term covariates to refer to 13 independent variables, inclusive of covariates of interest and covariates of no interest; and the term random 14 

effects refers to various known sources of nesting and similarity between observations in the data. For 15 longitudinal data, the term subject or participant refers to a unique individual followed over time while the 16 term observation refers to one measurement for a given subject. Visit or timepoint corresponds to an ordered 17 set of repeated measurements for the same subject; for example, visit 1 means the first measurement (first 18 observation) on the subject, with increasing visit numbers indicating subsequent measurements. For 19 mathematical notation, we use boldface for matrices or vectors, and regular font weight for scalar quantities. 20 

FEMA-Long algorithm overview 21 

Given a set of continuous phenotype(s), covariates, and random effects, the goal is to estimate the fixed 22 effects (beta coefficients for the covariates) while accounting for random effects. In the original FEMA 23 framework (10), we first calculate the ordinary least squares (OLS) residuals of the phenotype; then, we 24 use them to estimate the variance components of the random effects using method of moments (MoM) or 25 a regression estimator with a non-negativity constraint (11). Then, the fixed effects are estimated using a 26 generalized least squares (GLS) solution which incorporates a covariance matrix using the estimated 27 variance components of the random effects. For scalability to large number of variables (such as for voxel-28 wise or vertex-wise data derived from brain imaging data), we use a binning approach – for all outcome 29 variables that have similar variance components for the random effects, the GLS estimation is performed 30 using a common covariance matrix that relies on the average of the variance components of all the outcome 31 variables within that bin. 32

The FEMA-Long framework includes i) time-varying covariances in the random effects using 2unstructured covariances; ii) modeling non-linear effects using splines; iii) examining time-dependent 3effects using spline interactions; and iv) performing GWAS and other similar omics analyses, where we test 4the association of a large number of variables of interest (e.g., genotyping matrix with millions of SNPs) 5with outcome(s), independently (i.e., marginal rather than joint effect), after controlling for covariates and 6random effects. 7In FEMA-Long, we use unstructured covariance to model time-varying covariance of random effects. The 8elements of the unstructured covariance matrix are estimated by using the MoM estimation for every pair 9of visits that are nested within subjects. This results in a matrix of covariance terms for each random effect, 10 which is then used for GLS estimation of the fixed effects. Importantly, this method does not assume a 11 balanced design of all subjects having data for all the visits. 12 FEMA-Long also includes the option to model continuous outcome variables as a smooth function (for 13 example, smooth function of age) using splines, represented by a set of basis functions. We also allow 14 interaction terms between covariates, including those covariates that are modeled via splines. While the 15 estimation of the unstructured covariance matrix uses pre-defined set of timepoints (visits), the spline 16 modeling uses a continuous variable (such as age). This, therefore, brings the framework of generalized 17 additive mixed models to FEMA, allowing flexible modeling of outcome variables as well as performing 18 non-linear interaction analyses. 19 Finally, while the original FEMA was designed for many outcome variables and a common model (mass-20 univariate analyses), FEMA-Long includes a variant of FEMA that is intended for GWAS, where we fit the 21 outcome(s) repeatedly on many models, each differing only in their genetic covariate. For this, we leverage 22 the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem (12,13): first, use stage-1 FEMA to estimate the random effects variance 23 components and fixed effects (omitting genetic variants); then, we GLS residualize genotype vector for the 24 covariates using the stage-1 variance components. Finally, we perform a GLS estimation using the GLS 25 residuals of the phenotype and the GLS residuals of the genotype vector. This framework also allows 26 modeling the interaction of genetic variants with spline basis functions, thereby allowing discovery of 27 genetic variants that may show a nonlinear, time-dependent effect on the phenotype. 28 Further details on each of these steps is described below. 29

Scaling FEMA-Long for GWAS-like analyses 20 

Within GWAS, the goal is to estimate the effect of genetic variants on the phenotype(s), considering one 21 variant at a time. Modeling time-dependent genetic effects using spline interactions 7

This GWAS framework can also include interaction terms – linear interaction or smooth interaction. In case 8of linear interaction, if the main effect of the interacting variable has already been included as an 𝑋 variable 9in stage-1 regression, then, 𝒈 is a matrix with the first column containing the main effect of the genetic 10 variant and the second column containing the elementwise (Hadamard) product of the genetic variant and 11 the interacting variable. In this case, we residualize both columns of 𝒈 for 𝑿 (equation (15) ), followed by 12 estimation using equation (17) , resulting in two coefficients: the coefficient for the main effect and the 13 coefficient for the interaction term. The denominator of the mean squared error (equation (19 )) is adjusted 14 for two parameters, i.e., (𝑛 − 𝑝 − 2) .15 For modeling spline interactions, 𝒈 is a matrix having the elementwise product of the genetic variant and 16 the smooth function 𝑓 (𝑡) . The estimation follows the same procedure, with the estimation of as many 17 coefficients as the columns of 𝒈 . If the basis functions have been appropriately modified and a constant 18 term added (see the section on Modeling spline basis functions), then both the main effect of the genetic 19 variant as well as the interaction of the genetic variant with smooth functions can be estimated. # Application: GWAS on anthropometric features in infants 6

Study sample 7

To demonstrate the features of FEMA-Long (unstructured covariance, non-linear modeling, scalability to 8large datasets, performing GWAS, and discovery of time-dependent genetic effects), we performed a 9GWAS on the length, weight, and BMI of infants in the first year of life using the large-scale Norwegian 10 Mother, Father, and Child Cohort Study (MoBa) (30–32). The MoBa Study is a population-based pregnancy 11 cohort study conducted by the Norwegian Institute of Public Health. Participants were recruited from all 12 over Norway from 1999-2008. The women consented to participation in 41% of the pregnancies. The cohort 13 includes approximately 114,500 children, 95,200 mothers and 75,200 fathers. The current study is based 14 on version 12 of the quality-assured data files released for research in 2019. # Discussion 7

We have adapted and extended our computationally fast linear mixed-effects modeling method FEMA for 8large-scale high-dimensional longitudinal datasets. The features of FEMA-Long include: i) modeling time-9varying random effects using unstructured covariance, ii) incorporating splines for modeling non-linear 10 effects of variables, iii) examining time-dependent effects using spline interactions, and iv) performing 11 GWAS-like analyses by scaling FEMA to estimate marginal effect of millions of variables, including time-12 dependent effects. Our simulation results show that FEMA-Long can accurately model these parameters, 13 while controlling for false positives, and provides exceptional computational performance, tens to 14 thousands of times faster than a standard LME implementation, and at the same time having a much smaller 15 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted March 23, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.05.09.653146 doi: bioRxiv preprint

- 28 -carbon footprint. We have also demonstrated that FEMA-Long is scalable to scenarios with a large number 1of observations and outcome variables such as GWAS or whole-brain neuroimaging analyses. 2As previously mentioned, random effects like heritability can vary significantly over time (3–8). Similarly, 3longitudinal data usually shows some form of time-varying correlation. Our application of FEMA-Long to 4length, weight, and BMI in infants during the first year of life revealed time-varying random effect 5covariances including heritability. These patterns also differed between similar phenotypes. Generally, 6increasing heritability for later timepoints (compared to birth) potentially indicates that the phenotypes at 7earlier timepoints might be more influenced from other sources like the maternal genotype (36,37). 8Importantly, it underscores the need to model these using unstructured covariance as these covariances 9cannot be well-approximated with simpler covariance patterns like compound symmetry or autoregressive 10 models. 11 Additionally, when studying dynamic phenomenon such as development, it is essential to model time-12 dependent effects. Our time-dependent GWAS results revealed several SNPs showing time-dependent 13 genetic effects. In contrast, the discovery of these SNPs was stunted when only examining the longitudinal 14 main effect of SNPs, most notably for weight and BMI. Charting the effect of these SNPs over time revealed 15 several interesting patterns including non-monotonic and curvilinear patterns. These results highlight the 16 two-fold importance of modeling spline interactions for every SNP: first, allowing discovery of SNPs 17 showing non-linear time-dependent effects; and second, ensuring more accurate predictions when using 18 these estimated effect sizes (e.g., polygenic risk scores). Both these aspects cannot be captured by just the 19 main effect of the SNPs and will likely be missed even with linear interaction. 20 The use of unstructured covariance has received relatively little attention in fields with high dimensional 21 data. For example, within neuroimaging, there are no software packages for modeling unstructured 22 covariances, besides AFNI’s 3dLME and 3dLMEr (38) which supports compound symmetry and order 1 23 autoregressive process (9). In genetics, prior work on longitudinal GWAS include: i) computing a rate of 24 change between timepoints (39–41); ii) using standard LME packages (42,43); iii) fitting a growth curve-25 like model to the phenotype and performing GWAS on the model parameters (44,45); and iv) novel tools 26 for longitudinal GWAS like fGWAS (46), GALLOP (47), trajGWAS (48), L-BRAT and RGMMAT (49), 27 RVMMAT (50), and SPA GRM (51). Despite the availability of these tools, they have one or more limitations 28 including inability to: i) handle related individuals, ii) model additional sources of variances, iii) model 29 linear and/or non-linear SNP interactions, iv) estimating effect sizes, and v) scale to a large samples and/or 30 large-number of phenotypes. Importantly, it is rare to see a longitudinal GWAS study specifying a time-31 varying covariance pattern. The closest such recent approach (52) used a range of LMEs with linear or 32 cubic smoothing splines, or cubic slopes for age, with and without a continuous AR(1) covariance pattern. 33 However, this work still relied on collapsing repeated observations into a single measurement (followed by 34 

> .CC-BY-NC 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted March 23, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.05.09.653146 doi: bioRxiv preprint

- 29 -a meta-analysis) as well as removing related observations. In addition, the authors highlighted the 1significant computational burden when scaling to large cohorts. 2FEMA-Long addresses all the above-mentioned limitations, providing a unified framework for performing 3large-scale analyses, modeling unstructured covariances, non-linear effects, and discovery of time-4dependent effects, including time-dependent genetic effects. There are, however, some limitations to our 5work. First, the MoM estimator is not as efficient as maximum likelihood estimator. Second, currently 6FEMA-Long only supports continuous outcome variables, assuming approximately normally distributed 7errors. Third, for GWAS, we assume that the individual contribution of SNPs is small and unlikely to affect 8the covariance estimation of the random effect. Fourth, the computational time, especially for GWAS, could 9be further improved. Fifth, when performing the visit-wise estimation of the covariance pattern, for certain 10 scenarios where the number of shared observations between the visits are too low, the method of moments 11 estimation could be unstable. While FEMA and FEMA-Long are designed for large samples, we envision 12 improving the stability of the covariance estimation by updating our method to perform a joint estimation 13 of all parameters instead of visit-wise estimation; other possibilities include introducing a shrinkage or 14 regularization on the estimation of the covariance parameters and/or enforcing smoothness during the 15 estimation; additionally, we could interpolate the visit-wise estimated covariances, resulting in smoothly 16 changing covariances, and further extending the scope of time-varying random effects. Future work will 17 explore these and incorporate additional features like binning the phenotypes, computation of age-specific 18 risk scores, extension to non-continuous phenotypes such as Bernoulli distribution, allowing statistical 19 inference on the random effects estimates, using longitudinal trajectories for prediction, and the application 20 of FEMA-Long to longitudinal complex trait phenotypes such as mental health. 21 To conclude, FEMA-Long is a powerful novel method that will enable researchers to apply large-scale 22 LME models with unstructured covariances as well as discover time-dependent effects. The unified 23 framework of FEMA-Long not only allows inclusion of subjects with a differing number of timepoints but 24 also handles scenarios like sample relatedness and other sources of variance while providing a25 computationally fast and environmentally green solution for application to genomics, neuroimaging, and 26 other fields. We expect FEMA-Long to facilitate discoveries in the growing number of large longitudinal 27 datasets becoming available. 28
