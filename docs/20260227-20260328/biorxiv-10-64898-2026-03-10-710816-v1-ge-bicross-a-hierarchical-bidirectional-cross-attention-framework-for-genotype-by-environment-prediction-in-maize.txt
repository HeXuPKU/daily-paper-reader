Abstract 8

Genotype-by-environment interactions are central to crop adaptation and yield stability, 9

yet they remain difficult to model for robust prediction across heterogeneous 10 

environments. Although enviromic profiling has improved the characterization of 11 

dynamic field conditions, most existing genomic prediction methods adopt a late-fusion 12 

strategy that encodes genomic and environmental information independently before 13 

global integration, thereby limiting their ability to resolve fine-scale, context-dependent 14 

G x E effects. Here, we developed GE-BiCross, a hierarchical bidirectional cross-15 

attention framework for maize prediction. GE-BiCross incorporates a dual-path feature 16 

extraction module to disentangle independent and cooperative effects, a tokenized 17 

bidirectional cross-attention module to enable reciprocal genotype-environment 18 

interaction learning, and a mixture-of-experts module to adaptively capture 19 

heterogeneous response patterns across environments. Using a large-scale dataset of 20 

approximately 360,000 observations from 4,923 maize hybrids evaluated in 241 21 

environments, GE-BiCross consistently outperformed conventional genomic prediction, 22 

machine learning, and deep learning baselines across six agronomic traits. The greatest 23 

improvements were observed for environmentally responsive and genetically complex 24 

traits. In particular, GE-BiCross achieved an R2 of 0.672 for grain yield and 0.880 for 25 

grain moisture, significantly surpassing all comparison models. Ablation analyses 26 

demonstrated that the three core modules make distinct and complementary 27 

contributions to predictive performance.These results show that deep, bidirectional 28 

integration of genomic and enviromic information can substantially improve modeling 29 

of complex G x E interactions, providing a powerful framework for interpretable 30

Introduction 33 

Global climate change and the increasing frequency of extreme weather events severely 34 

compromise crop yield stability[1]. Consequently, developing climate-resilient 35 

cultivars with phenotypic plasticity and adaptability to dynamic environments has 36 

become a central challenge in modern breeding[2]. Agronomic traits are jointly shaped 37 

by genotype (G), environment (E), and their complex interactions (G×E)[3-5]. 38 

Crucially, G×E interactions not only drive phenotypic variation but also limit both the 39 

transferability of genetic effects across environments and the accuracy of breeding 40 

predictions[6]. 41 

Driven by advances in enviromics, envirotyping has emerged as the third core typing 42 

technology, alongside genotyping and phenotyping[7-9]. It enables the fine-grained 43 

characterization of spatiotemporally continuous environmental factors throughout the 44 

crop growth cycle. While previous studies show that composite environmental variables 45 

(e.g., photothermal time) explain phenotypic variation better than single-factor 46 

variables,[9,10] crop responses to these factors exhibit strong developmental stage-47 

specific heterogeneity. Different growth stages often correspond to distinct 48 

physiological mechanisms. Although integrating environmental data improves 49 

predictive performance[11], most existing genomic prediction models rely on a late-50 

fusion paradigm. In this "separate encoding followed by fusion" framework, genomic 51 

and environmental features are learned independently and interact only globally at the 52 

final stage. This approach is inadequate for capturing locus- and stage-specific G×E 53 

response patterns, and it fails to reflect the biological mechanisms underlying 54 

environmental regulation of gene expression. 55 

To address these limitations, this study proposes GE-BiCross, a hierarchical 56 

multimodal learning framework designed to deeply couple genomic and enviromic data 57 

during representation learning. At its core, GE-BiCross employs a cross-attention 58 

mechanism to establish a heterogeneous query–key–value mapping, enabling selective 59 

retrieval and deep interaction across modalities. While cross-attention has proven 60

effective in modeling condition-dependent interactions in other biomedical applications, 61

experts (MoE) strategy is introduced to adaptively model heterogeneous environmental 66 

response patterns via dynamic routing. 67 

Evaluated on a large-scale dataset comprising 4,923 maize hybrids across 241 68 

environments, GE-BiCross significantly outperformed existing models in predicting 69 

multiple key agronomic traits[14]. Notably, for grain moisture, a trait highly sensitive 70 

to environmental variation, the coefficient of determination (R²) improved by 16.6%. 71 

Ultimately, this study establishes a robust technical foundation for developing 72 

biologically interpretable, climate-smart breeding models. 73 

Material and methods 74 

Dataset Overview 75 

The final dataset used for modeling comprised approximately 360,000 valid 76 

observations, covering 241 trial environments and 4,923 hybrids[14]. Missing value 77 

rates for all six traits were below 5% and were imputed using the mean of the 78 

corresponding trait. The dataset was randomly partitioned by environment into training, 79 

validation, and test sets with a ratio of 8:1:1, ensuring that data from the same 80 

environment did not appear in more than one subset. 81

Comparison Methods and Evaluation Metrics 149 

The effectiveness of GE-BiCross was benchmarked against several genomic prediction 150 

methods, including Genomic Best Linear Unbiased Prediction (GBLUP), and 151 

traditional machine learning models like K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), Random Forest 152 

(RF), and Gradient Boosted Trees (GBT). Additionally, we compared our model against 153 

GEFormer, a recent deep learning framework for G×E prediction. 154 

Model performance was evaluated using two widely adopted metrics: the coefficient 155 

of determination ( 𝑅 ^2), indicating the proportion of phenotypic variance explained by 156 

the model, and the Pearson correlation coefficient (PCC), measuring the linear 157 

correlation between predicted and observed values. All experiments were conducted on 158 

an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 5070ti GPU, and results are reported as the average of five 159 

independent runs to minimize stochastic variability. 160 

Result and Discussion 161 

Architecture of the GE-BiCross framework 162 

We developed GE-BiCross, a hierarchical multimodal learning framework designed to 163 

explicitly model genotype-by-environment (G×E) interactions. As illustrated in Figure 164 

1, the framework comprises three major components. First, a dual-path feature 165 

extraction and effect-disentanglement module captures genotype- and environment-166 

derived representations while decomposing their main and interactive effects. Second, 167 

a tokenized bidirectional cross-attention module facilitates reciprocal information 168 

exchange between genetic and environmental features, enabling the fine-grained 169 

modeling of cross-modal dependencies. Third, a mixture-of-experts (MoE) adaptive 170 

prediction module dynamically integrates these learned representations to generate 171 

trait-specific predictions. The entire framework is trained end-to-end, allowing for the 172 

coordinated optimization of feature extraction, interaction modeling, and final 173 

prediction. 174 

GE-BiCross improves prediction across diverse agronomic traits 175

To evaluate its predictive performance, we compared GE-BiCross with representative 176 

conventional genomic prediction, classical machine learning, and deep learning 177 

methods (GBLUP, KNN, RF, GBT, and GEFormer) across six agronomic traits. GE-178 

BiCross consistently achieved the highest predictive accuracy on the validation set for 179 

all six traits, demonstrating its superiority across traits with distinct genetic 180 

architectures and varying levels of environmental sensitivity. 181 

The most substantial improvement was observed for yield, a highly complex trait 182 

strongly influenced by G×E interactions. GE-BiCross achieved an R² of 0.672, 183 

outperforming the second-best model, RF (R² = 0.615), by 0.057 (a 9.3% relative 184 

improvement) and surpassing GEFormer (R² = 0.515) by 0.157 (a 30.5% relative 185 

improvement). Pairwise t-tests confirmed that these performance gains over all 186 

baseline models were statistically significant (p < 0.01). This highlights the capability 187 

of GE-BiCross to capture the complex interaction effects essential for accurate yield 188 

prediction. 189 

A similarly strong advantage was observed for grain moisture, a trait highly 190 

responsive to environmental variation. GE-BiCross achieved an R² of 0.880, 191 

compared with 0.849 for RF and 0.755 for GEFormer, yielding absolute 192 

improvements of 0.031 and 0.125, respectively. The improvement over RF was 193 

statistically significant (p < 0.05), while the improvement over GEFormer was highly 194 

significant (p < 0.001) (Figure 2). These results underscore the importance of explicit 195 

G×E modeling for environmentally sensitive traits. 196 

GE-BiCross also delivered superior performance for flowering-related phenological 197 

traits. For silking date, the model achieved an R² of 0.950, outperforming GBLUP (R² 198 

= 0.870) by 0.080 (a 9.2% relative improvement). For pollen date, GE-BiCross 199 

reached an R² of 0.940, exceeding RF (R² = 0.892) by 0.048 (a 5.4% relative 200 

improvement). Although the baseline methods already performed well for these traits, 201 

the gains achieved by GE-BiCross remained statistically significant (p < 0.01). This 202 

indicates that the explicit integration of genetic and environmental information 203 

provides additional predictive value, even for traits with strong genetic determinism. 204 

Finally, GE-BiCross demonstrated consistent improvements for morphological traits. 205

Ablation analysis reveals distinct contributions of key GE-BiCross modules 210 

To determine the contribution of each architectural component to predictive 211 

performance, we performed ablation analyses by selectively removing or replacing 212 

core modules of GE-BiCross. Specifically, we evaluated three reduced variants: GE-213 

BiCross w/o MoE, in which the mixture-of-experts layer was replaced by a single 214 

fully connected layer; GE-BiCross w/o effect disentanglement, in which the dynamic 215 

gating mechanism separating independent and cooperative effects was removed and 216 

only cooperative features were retained; and GE-BiCross w/o token fusion, in which 217 

the bidirectional cross-attention module was replaced by direct concatenation of 218 

genotypic and environmental representations. 219 

For grain moisture, the full GE-BiCross model achieved an R² of 0.880, whereas all 220 

ablated variants showed substantial reductions in predictive accuracy. The largest 221 

decline was observed when the MoE module was removed (R² = 0.808), 222 

corresponding to an absolute reduction of 0.072 (8.2% relative decrease). Replacing 223 

token fusion with simple concatenation reduced performance to R² = 0.825 (absolute 224 

decrease of 0.055, 6.3% relative), whereas removing effect disentanglement resulted 225 

in an R² of 0.832 (absolute decrease of 0.048, 5.5% relative). All differences between 226 

the full model and the ablated variants were highly significant (p < 0.001), indicating 227 

that each module contributes substantially to prediction of this environmentally 228 

sensitive trait, with the MoE module having the strongest effect. 229 

A different pattern was observed for yield, for which the full model achieved an R² 230 

of 0.672. In this case, the most pronounced performance loss resulted from removal of 231 

the effect-disentanglement module, which reduced the R² to 0.630 (absolute decrease 232 

of 0.042, 6.3% relative). Removing token fusion reduced performance to R² = 0.641 233 

(absolute decrease of 0.031, 4.6% relative), whereas removal of the MoE module had 234 

a comparatively smaller effect (R² = 0.649; absolute decrease of 0.023, 3.4% relative). 235

The reduction caused by eliminating effect disentanglement was statistically 236 

significant (p < 0.01), suggesting that explicit separation of independent and 237 

cooperative effects is particularly important for prediction of yield, a trait 238 

characterized by complex G×E architecture. 239 

For flowering-related traits, the contributions of individual modules were more 240 

evenly distributed. In prediction of silking date, the full model reached an R² of 0.950, 241 

whereas the variants without MoE, effect disentanglement, or token fusion achieved 242 

R² values of 0.904, 0.898, and 0.901, respectively. These correspond to relative 243 

decreases of 4.8%, 5.5%, and 5.2%, indicating that all three components contribute 244 

comparably to predictive performance. The differences between the full model and all 245 

ablated variants were highly significant (p < 0.001), supporting the conclusion that 246 

robust prediction of flowering phenotypes requires coordinated contributions from 247 

adaptive expert routing, effect disentanglement, and cross-modal feature integration. 248 

Taken together, these ablation results demonstrate that the three major modules of 249 

GE-BiCross make nonredundant and trait-dependent contributions to model 250 

performance. The MoE module appears to be particularly important for 251 

environmentally responsive traits such as grain moisture, whereas effect 252 

disentanglement is especially critical for complex traits such as yield. By contrast, for 253 

flowering traits, predictive gains arise from the complementary action of all modules. 254 

These results provide mechanistic support for the overall design of GE-BiCross and 255 

indicate that its performance advantage is not attributable to a single architectural 256 

component. 257
