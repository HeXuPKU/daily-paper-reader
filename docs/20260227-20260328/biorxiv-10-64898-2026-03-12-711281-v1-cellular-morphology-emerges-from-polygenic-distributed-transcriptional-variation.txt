Abstract 11 

Height and most disease risk are known polygenic traits: characteristics governed by 12 

multiple genes at different loci instead of a select few. Though we are beginning to 13 

understand how genetic variation impacts cell morphology, whether such an analogous 14 

polygenic architecture operates at the cellular level, where morphology integrates 15 

cytoskeletal organization, organelle positioning, and metabolic state, has yet to be 16 

systematically tested. Here, we demonstrate that cellular morphology behaves as a 17 

polygenic trait by integrating multimodal modeling, perturbation profiling, and population-18 

scale genetic variation. A shared latent-space autoencoder trained on four large-scale 19 

perturbation datasets predicts morphology from gene expression and generalizes without 20 

retraining to matched RNA-seq and Cell Painting profiles from 100 genetically diverse 21 

iPSC donors. The model predicted 17 morphological features (R² > 0.6, permutation FDR 22 

q < 0.05), enriched for spatial organelle distribution and cytoskeletal architecture. 23 

Predictive performance does not arise from dominant gene–phenotype relationships: 24 

individual genes contribute modestly, and marginal gene–morphology correlations are 25 

uniformly weak, revealing a distributed regulatory architecture. Despite this polygenicity, 26 

CRISPR perturbation data from the JUMP consortium validates specific model-prioritized 27 

genes, such as the cytoskeletal regulator TIAM1 , membrane trafficking factor RAB31 , and 28 

mitochondrial-associated membrane transporter ABCC5 , as molecular anchors whose 29 

disruption produces feature-specific morphological shifts. Transcriptome-wide 30 

association analyses identify correlational variant–gene–morphology chains linking cis-31 

regulatory variation through mitochondrial metabolism (PDHX ) and iron transport 32 

(SLC11A2 ) to cellular architecture. These results establish cellular morphology as a 33 

polygenic systems phenotype, extending the omnigenic framework to the cellular level 34 

and providing a biological basis for interpreting cross-modal prediction in functional 35 

genomics. 36

Introduction 40 

At the organismal level, complex traits, such as height, body mass index, and most 41 

disease risk, are driven by the distributed influence of thousands of loci modestly 42 

contributing to the overall phenotype rather than a handful of large-effect genes 9. This 43 

allows for variation within traits so that even genes without direct mechanistic relevance 44 

contribute to phenotypic variance through trans-regulatory connections. At the cellular 45 

level, cells translate transcriptional programs are turned into physical architecture via 46 

coordinated regulation of cytoskeletal organization, organelle positioning, and membrane 47 

dynamics. However, whether this translation is a result of strong, direct gene–phenotype 48 

relationships or by the distributed integration of many transcriptional signals remains an 49 

unresolved question in cellular biology. 50 

Cellular phenotypes can be quantified with gene expression and image-based measures 51 

of morphology 1,2 . Transcriptomic profiles can capture regulatory potential and pathway 52 

activity, while morphological phenotypes, commonly measured by multiplexed assays 53 

such as Cell Painting 3, reflect the integrated outcome of molecular, structural, and 54 

biophysical processes across cellular compartments. Understanding how these 55 

modalities relate is central to functional genomics, drug discovery, and precision 56 

medicine; for example, compounds with shared mechanisms of action often cluster by 57 

morphological profile in Cell Painting assays, allowing inference of pharmacological 58 

mechanism from cellular phenotype 4,5 .59 

Recent advances in high-throughput imaging and transcriptomics have yielded large-60 

scale, multimodal datasets which can simultaneously measure gene expression and 61 

cellular morphology across thousands of perturbations 2,6–8 . Computational, and 62 

increasingly machine learning approaches can now predict morphology from gene 63 

expression, and vice versa, with notable accuracy. However, high cross-modal prediction 64 

accuracy raises a question that has received insufficient attention: Does strong prediction 65 

reflect direct, interpretable gene–phenotype mappings, or does it instead reflect a 66 

polygenic architecture, in which many weak transcriptional effects are integrated to 67 

collectively encode cellular architecture? 68 

Existing multimodal integration methods span linear approaches such as principal 69 

component analysis 10 and canonical correlation analysis 11 , as well as deep learning 70 

models capable of capturing nonlinear relationships 12–14 . More recently, generative 71 

frameworks including diffusion models 8 and foundation model–based 15 approaches have 72 

achieved impressive cross-modal prediction. However, these methods prioritize 73 

prediction accuracy over interpretability. The central question remains; does multimodal 74

reflect emergent statistical structure in high-dimensional data without direct mechanistic 76

Here, we directly test the hypothesis that cellular morphology behaves as a polygenic trait 78 

by combining multimodal modeling with perturbational and genetic analyses. While prior 79 

work demonstrated that morphological profiling reveals functional consequences of 80 

genetic variation across human donors 16 and characterized natural expression variation 81 

in similar systems 17 , we extend this framework by training a shared latent-space 82 

autoencoder 13 to four large-scale perturbation datasets with matched gene expression 83 

and Cell Painting profiles 7, and then test whether transcriptional–morphological 84 

relationships learned from experimental perturbations generalize to natural inter-85 

individual variation across genetically diverse human donors with paired morphology and 86 

expression data 16,17 . Beyond prediction accuracy, we evaluate how predictive signal is 87 

distributed across genes, validate model-prioritized genes using independent CRISPR 88 

perturbation data from the JUMP consortium 6, and connect model-derived gene 89 

prioritization to cis-regulated gene expression through transcriptome-wide association 90 

analyses. 91 

Our analyses demonstrate that highly predictable morphological features, enriched for 92 

spatial organelle organization and cytoskeletal architecture, are not driven by strong 93 

single-gene associations but instead emerge from the polygenic, coordinated influence 94 

of many genes with individually modest effects. A subset of these genes, validated by 95 

CRISPR perturbation, act as molecular anchors connecting distributed transcriptional 96 

programs to specific aspects of cellular structure. By integrating these findings with 97 

genetic association analyses, we identify variant–gene–morphology statistically linked 98 

chains linking inherited genetic variation to cellular architecture. Together, these results 99 

establish cellular morphology as a polygenic systems phenotype and provide a biological 100 

framework for interpreting cross-modal prediction. 101 

Results 102 

Transcriptional programs encode large-scale cellular architecture across 103 

perturbations 104 

To investigate how transcriptional variation shapes cellular structure, we analyzed 105 

multimodal perturbation datasets containing matched gene expression and Cell Painting 106 

morphological profiles. We applied a shared latent representation framework 13 to four 107 

large-scale perturbation resources 7: LUAD (lung adenocarcinoma–associated genetic 108 

perturbations), LINCS (Library of Integrated Network-Based Cellular Signatures 109 

pharmacological perturbations), TAORF (transient overexpression), and CDRP-bio 110 

(bioactive chemical perturbations). These datasets collectively span 2,000–10,000 111

morphology features per perturbation. 115 

The modeling framework consists of parallel autoencoders trained independently on 116 

transcriptomic and morphological measurements, whose latent representations are 117 

aligned into a shared 150-dimensional space ( Figure 1 ). Each encoder and decoder is 118 

implemented as a single fully connected linear layer, providing a deliberately simple 119 

architecture that favors biological interpretability over maximal predictive performance, 120 

the focus of much previous work. This shared representation enables direct translation 121 

between modalities and provides a systems-level representation of transcriptional– 122 

morphological coupling. 123 

Across all datasets, reconstruction loss decreased and stabilized during training ( Figure 124 

S1 ), indicating that both transcriptomic and morphological structure were captured within 125 

the learned representations. Final reconstruction errors varied across datasets and 126 

modalities but consistently demonstrated that modality-specific biological variation was 127 

retained within the shared latent space ( Figure 2a ). 128 

We next evaluated whether transcriptional information encoded in this shared 129 

representation could predict morphological features. Using 5-fold cross-validation, the 130 

model achieved robust prediction performance across all datasets, with a substantial 131 

subset of morphological features exhibiting moderate to high predictability ( Figure 2b ). 132 

Prediction accuracy was comparable to or exceeded previously reported multilayer 133 

perceptron models trained on the same data 7, demonstrating that the cross-modal 134 

biological signal can be captured even by relatively simple linear architectures, consistent 135 

with prior observations that linear methods can match or approach deep learning 136 

performance for morphological profiling tasks 1.137 

To identify which aspects of cellular architecture were most tightly coupled to 138 

transcriptional state, we examined morphology features with reproducible predictability 139 

across datasets. We defined "moderately predicted" features as those achieving median 140 

R² > 0.6 across the four perturbation datasets. While this threshold is modest in absolute 141 

terms, it represents the upper tail of the prediction distribution across ~1,000 features, the 142 

vast majority of which show near-zero R², and exceeds permutation-derived null 143 

expectations, identifying a consistent set of features that are reproducibly coupled to 144 

transcriptional variation across independent experimental contexts. We note that the 145 

substantially higher R² threshold used for donor-level prediction (R² > 0.6) reflects a 146 

different analytical context: perturbation-level R² is computed across treatment conditions 147 

with high technical noise, whereas donor-level R² captures stable inter-individual variation 148 

with stronger and more biologically coherent transcriptional–morphological signal. 149

The enrichment of spatial specific organelle-distribution features among highly 157

arrangement of organelles and cytoskeletal networks across the cell body, rather than 160 

localized morphological descriptors such as nuclear shape or texture. This is consistent 161 

with the expectation that coordinated transcriptional changes propagate through 162 

regulatory networks to affect multiple cellular compartments simultaneously, producing 163 

coherent, cell-wide architectural changes rather than isolated feature perturbations 19,20 .164 

Transcriptional–morphological coupling generalizes to natural human genetic 165 

variation 166 

Having established transcriptional prediction of morphology across experimental 167 

perturbations, we next tested whether these relationships extend to naturally occurring 168 

genetic variation. We trained the shared latent model on LUAD perturbation data 7 and 169 

applied it, without retraining or architectural modification, to predict morphology across 170 

100 genetically diverse donors with matched RNA-seq and Cell Painting profiles on 171 

induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) we previously described 16,17 . Donor transcriptomic 172 

profiles were restricted to the same 978 genes in the LUAD data to ensure compatibility 173 

with the perturbation-trained model, and morphology features were restricted to those 174 

shared across datasets. 175 

This cross-context generalization, from pharmacological/genetic perturbation in A549 176 

cells to natural inter-individual variation in iPSCs, represents a stringent test of whether 177 

transcriptional–morphological coupling reflects stable biological relationships or 178 

perturbation-specific statistical associations. 179 

Model performance was evaluated at both population and individual levels. Population-180 

level prediction applied the trained model to all donors simultaneously, yielding a single 181 

R² per morphology feature. Individual-level prediction assessed performance separately 182 

for each donor and averaged feature-level accuracy across individuals. Morphology 183 

features that were highly predictable at the population level were also consistently 184 

predictable across individual donors, with most features clustering near the diagonal 185 

when comparing global and individual prediction accuracy ( Figure 3 ). 186

Predictable morphology arises from distributed transcriptional control 200 

Having identified a set of morphological features that are robustly predicted from 201 

transcriptional data across datasets and donors, we next addressed a central question: 202 

does strong prediction reflect dominant gene–phenotype relationships, or does it emerge 203 

from the distributed integration of many transcriptional signals, suggesting a polygenic 204 

framework? 205 

If cellular morphology were governed by a small number of strong gene–phenotype 206 

relationships, we would expect two properties: (1) the model’s predictive signal would be 207 

concentrated in a few highly weighted genes, and (2) those genes would individually show 208 

strong marginal correlations with morphology. However, should morphology behave as a 209 

polygenic trait, analogous to complex organismal phenotypes 9, prediction should depend 210 

on the collective influence of many genes, each contributing modestly, with weak 211 

individual gene–morphology associations. 212 

Gene contributions to each of the 17 highly predicted morphology features were 213 

quantified using model-derived importance scores (encoder weight magnitudes). 214 

Although prediction relied on sets of genes with elevated weights ( Figure S2 ), the 215 

distribution of contributions was strikingly dispersed: no single gene dominated the 216 

predictive signal for any feature. Marginal relationships between individual gene 217 

expression levels and morphology were uniformly weak ( Figure S3 ), with top-ranked 218 

genes displaying Pearson correlations substantially below what would be expected if 219 

prediction were driven by strong univariate associations. 220

the level of cellular architecture. 233 

Perturbational and genetic analyses reveal molecular anchors of polygenic 234 

morphology 235 

To determine whether model-prioritized genes correspond to biologically meaningful 236 

regulators of cellular architecture, we evaluated gene-level predictions using independent 237 

CRISPR perturbation data from the JUMP Cell Painting consortium 2. This dataset 238 

provides high-content morphology profiles following targeted gene disruption and enables 239 

orthogonal validation of gene–morphology relationships independent of transcription-240 

based prediction. 241 

Across morphology features with strong and reproducible predictability, disruption of 242 

several highly ranked genes produced significant and feature-specific morphology 243 

changes relative to plate-matched non-targeting controls. 244 

For AGP radial distribution features, TIAM1 appears among the top 20 model-ranked 245 

genes contributing to prediction ( Figure 4a ). Although its individual expression shows 246 

weak correlation with morphology ( Figure 4b ), CRISPR-mediated disruption of TIAM1 247 

produced significant shifts in AGP-associated radial distribution ( Figure 4c ). TIAM1 248 

encodes a Rac1-specific guanine nucleotide exchange factor that regulates actin 249 

cytoskeletal remodeling and membrane protrusion 21,22 . The observed perturbation-250 

induced changes in membrane-associated spatial organization are therefore consistent 251 

with its established role in actin dynamics. 252 

For Zernike-based AreaShape features, RAB31 similarly appears among the top 20 253 

predictive genes ( Figure 4d ). Despite weak marginal transcriptional associations ( Figure 254 

4e ), CRISPR disruption of RAB31 produced significant alterations in cell geometry 255 

(Figure 4f ). RAB31 regulates trans-Golgi and endosomal membrane trafficking 23 ,256 

processes that influence plasma membrane composition and cell boundary organization. 257

Collectively, these perturbational results demonstrate that genes prioritized by the shared 277

Importantly, these genes are identified as top-ranked contributors within distributed 279

Discussion 338 

This study addresses a fundamental question about the relationship between gene 339 

expression and cellular structure: does accurate cross-modal prediction imply direct 340 

gene–phenotype mappings, or does it reflect an emergent property of polygenic, 341 

distributed transcriptional regulation? By integrating multimodal modeling with 342 

perturbational and genetic analyses, we demonstrate that cellular morphology behaves 343 

as a polygenic cellular phenotype, governed by the coordinated influence of many genes 344 

with individually modest effects, with a subset of molecular anchors connecting distributed 345 

transcriptional programs to specific aspects of cellular architecture. 346 

The central finding of this work is that strong cross-modal prediction does not imply strong 347 

univariate gene–morphology associations. Even for the 17 morphology features that were 348 

consistently well predicted across four perturbation datasets and 100 genetically diverse 349 

donors, individual gene–morphology correlations were uniformly weak. Instead, 350 

predictive signal was distributed across large gene sets. This pattern directly parallels the 351 

omnigenic model of complex organismal traits, where phenotypic variation arises from 352 

the cumulative influence of many loci, including “peripheral” genes that affect the trait 353 

indirectly through regulatory network connections, rather than from a small number of 354 

large-effect “core” genes 9. Our results extend this framework to the cellular level, 355 

suggesting that the regulatory networks governing morphology propagate transcriptional 356 

variation broadly, such that the full gene expression profile is needed to predict cellular 357 

architecture. Practically, this implies that multimodal models should not be interpreted as 358 

identifying single causal driver genes, but rather as capturing distributed regulatory 359 

programs, and that gene prioritization from such models requires orthogonal 360 

perturbational or genetic validation. 361 

The observation that a model trained exclusively on pharmacological and genetic 362 

perturbations in A549 cells generalizes without retraining to predict morphology across 363 

genetically diverse iPSCs is notable for two reasons. First, it demonstrates that 364 

transcriptional–morphological coupling reflects conserved biological relationships rather 365 

than perturbation-specific or cell-type-specific statistical associations. However, this 366 

generalization should be interpreted with the caveat that the L1000 landmark genes were 367 

specifically selected to capture broadly informative transcriptional variation⁷; some degree 368 

of cross-context transferability may therefore reflect properties of this curated gene panel 369 

as well as underlying biology. Second, the consistency of feature predictability across 370

cellular architecture, consistent with the distributed regulatory model. 386 

The variant–gene–morphology chains identified through TWAS analyses extend this 387

At the same time, most predictive genes lacked detectable genetic association support. 396 

This selective convergence is expected for at least two reasons: first, the donor sample 397 

(N=100) provides limited power for genetic association, particularly for genes with modest 398 

effect sizes; second, much of the transcriptional variation that shapes morphology likely 399 

arises from environmental inputs, stochastic variation, or regulatory interactions that 400 

operate independently of cis-regulatory genetic control. Such partial genetic anchoring is 401 

characteristic of polygenic systems 9,30 .402 

These findings help resolve a conceptual ambiguity that has accompanied the rapid 403 

development of cross-modal prediction methods in biology. High prediction accuracy is 404 

frequently interpreted as evidence for direct mechanistic relationships between data 405 

modalities, an assumption that has influenced how multimodal models are used to 406 

prioritize genes and pathways. Our results demonstrate that this inference is not generally 407 

warranted. Strong predictive performance can emerge from the coordinated integration 408

output of cytoskeletal dynamics, organelle biogenesis, metabolic state, and membrane 419 

regulation, it functions as a “summary statistic” of cellular function that is sensitive to 420 

distributed molecular perturbations. This integrative property likely explains why 421 

morphology-based phenotypic screening has proven effective in drug discovery even 422 

when individual molecular targets are unknown 4,31 : subtle distributed molecular 423 

perturbations can produce detectable, coherent architectural changes precisely because 424 

morphology aggregates signals that are individually below detection thresholds. The 425 

polygenic architecture described here provides a theoretical foundation for this empirical 426 

observation. 427 

Limitations 428 

Several limitations should be considered. First, the shared latent model captures 429 

correlative, not causal, relationships between gene expression and morphology. The 430 

variant–gene–morphology chains provide convergent evidence but do not establish direct 431 

mediation; disentangling causal from correlative contributions will require systematic 432 

perturbation experiments targeting chain components. Second, genetic association 433 

analyses are constrained by sample size (N=100), regulatory context specificity, and the 434 

cis-eQTL resources available in this cohort, likely reducing sensitivity to detect genetically 435 

regulated contributors. Larger cohorts with matched multimodal data will be needed to 436 

achieve comprehensive power. Third, generalization to donor-level data required 437 

restricting RNA-seq measurements to the ~978 genes in the L1000 landmark set, 438 

potentially excluding transcriptional variation outside this panel; genome-wide expression 439 

data may capture additional predictive signal. Fourth, the deliberately simple architecture 440 

(linear encoder/decoder) may underestimate the strength of transcriptional– 441 

morphological coupling relative to more expressive models; however, the consistency of 442 

our biological conclusions across datasets suggests that the distributed architecture we 443 

observe is not an artifact of model capacity, and the comparable performance of linear 444 

and nonlinear methods for this task, supports the interpretability–accuracy trade-off we 445 

have chosen. Fifth, batch correction was performed by the original publishers of each 446 

perturbation dataset⁷ and was not independently re-evaluated here; residual batch effects 447

Future directions 453 

The framework introduced here suggests several avenues for investigation. Extending 454 

shared latent representations to incorporate proteomics, chromatin accessibility, and 455 

metabolic profiling may elucidate how multiple regulatory layers converge on cellular 456 

phenotype. Incorporating causal inference frameworks, such as Mendelian randomization 457 

applied to variant–gene–morphology chains, could help distinguish direct from indirect 458 

contributions to cellular architecture. Scaling multimodal profiling to larger donor cohorts 459 

will improve power for genetic association analyses and enable cell-type-specific 460 

assessments across differentiation states. Finally, conceptualizing morphology as a 461 

polygenic cellular trait raises the possibility of developing morphological polygenic scores, 462 

analogous to polygenic risk scores in human genetics, that aggregate distributed 463 

transcriptional signals into composite predictors of cellular function in disease-relevant 464 

systems. 465

Methods 539 

Data Sources 540 

We utilized two main sources of data: (1) large-scale perturbation datasets from 7 , which 541 

include paired gene expression (L1000) and morphology (Cell Painting) profiles across 542 

four experimental conditions (LUAD, LINCS, TAORF, and CDRP-bio); and (2) individual-543 

level datasets comprising matched RNA-seq and morphology profiles from 100 donors, 544 

with transcriptomic data from 17 and morphological features from 16 .545 

Data Preprocessing 546 

To integrate gene expression and morphology modalities, sample identifiers were 547 

standardized within each dataset and matched across modalities by intersecting shared 548 

well identifiers and enforcing identical row ordering. Duplicate identifiers within each 549 

modality were removed prior to matching (first occurrence retained). 550 

Preprocessing was restricted to numeric features. Non-finite values (±∞) were treated as 551 

missing, and features with ≥20% missingness across samples were excluded. After 552 

feature-level filtering, gene expression and morphology matrices were concatenated and 553 

samples containing any remaining missing values were removed via complete-case 554 

filtering. Across the four perturbation datasets, this quality-control procedure removed 555 

approximately 3–7% of matched samples per dataset, yielding the final analysis cohorts 556 

used for model training. 557 

For datasets derived from raw gene expression counts, values were normalized using 558 

counts per million (CPM) followed by log1p transformation. Genes with mean logCPM ≤ 559 

5.0 were excluded to remove low-expression features. Gene expression features were 560 

then standardized using z-score scaling, and low-variance genes were removed using a 561 

variance threshold of 0.001 applied to the scaled matrix. Morphology features were z-562 

score standardized, and redundant features were removed by excluding morphology 563 

measurements with absolute pairwise correlation > 0.90 prior to model training. All 564 

preprocessing steps were implemented in Python to ensure reproducible handling of 565 

variability across modalities. 566 

Model Architecture 567 

A shared latent space autoencoder framework was used, adapted from the cross-modal 568 

architecture introduced by Yang et al. 13 . The model consists of modality-specific encoders 569 

and decoders for gene expression and morphology, which project inputs into a shared 570 

latent representation. 571

Each encoder and decoder was implemented as a single fully connected linear layer. 572 

Gene expression and morphology inputs were independently mapped to a shared latent 573 

space of dimension 150, followed by reconstruction into their original feature spaces. 574 

Model parameters were initialized using Xavier uniform initialization. The architecture was 575 

implemented in PyTorch. 576 

Model Training 577 

Gene expression and morphology branches were optimized separately using the Adam 578 

optimizer (learning rate = 1 × 10 ⁻⁴). Weight decay was set to 1 × 10 ⁻⁶ for the gene 579 

expression branch and 1 × 10 ⁻⁴ for the morphology branch. Mean squared error (MSE) 580 

was used as the reconstruction loss for both modalities. Models were trained for 200 581 

epochs with a batch size of 16. 582 

Perturbation-Level Training and Evaluation 583 

For each perturbation dataset (LUAD, LINCS, TAORF, and CDRP-bio), a shared 584 

autoencoder was trained on paired gene expression and morphology profiles. 585 

Perturbations were randomly partitioned at the perturbation level into training (70%), 586 

validation (15%), and test (15%) sets using a fixed random seed. All replicate wells 587 

corresponding to the same perturbation were assigned to the same partition to prevent 588 

information leakage. Hyperparameters were selected using validation-set performance 589 

only, and final predictive accuracy was evaluated once on the held-out test set. 590 

Predictive performance was assessed by inferring morphology features from gene 591 

expression profiles in held-out test data using the learned shared latent representation. 592 

Prediction accuracy was quantified using the coefficient of determination (R²), computed 593 

independently for each morphology feature. Performance was compared to previously 594 

reported linear and nonlinear baseline models trained on the same datasets. 595 

Generalization to Individual-Level Data 596 

To evaluate generalization beyond perturbation data, the model trained on the LUAD 597 

dataset was applied without retraining to the donor-level dataset. Because perturbation 598 

datasets were generated using the L1000 assay, donor RNA-seq profiles were restricted 599 

to genes overlapping the L1000 landmark set. Morphology features were likewise 600 

restricted to those shared across datasets. This harmonization enabled direct evaluation 601 

of cross-dataset generalization without architectural modification. 602 

Two complementary evaluation schemes were used. In a global (population-level) 603 

evaluation , morphology predictions were generated jointly across all donors, yielding a 604 

single R² value per feature. In an individual-level evaluation, predictions were generated 605 

separately for each donor, producing one R² value per feature per individual; these values 606 

were then averaged across donors. Feature-level R² values from both schemes were 607 

compared to assess robustness across natural inter-individual variation. 608 

To assess the statistical significance of global (population-level) donor predictions, an 609 

empirical null distribution was constructed separately for each morphology feature. For 610 

each feature, predicted values were permuted relative to true sample labels 5,000 times 611

to form a null distribution. Empirical one-sided p-values were computed as the proportion 614

discovery rate was controlled across morphology features using the Benjamini–Hochberg 616 

procedure. Seventeen morphology features satisfied both the predefined effect-size 617 

threshold (R² > 0.6 at both global and individual levels) and FDR significance (q < 0.05). 618 

Gene Importance and Correlation Analysis 619 

To interpret highly predictable morphology features, relationships between model-derived 620 

gene importance scores and marginal gene–morphology associations were examined. 621 

Gene importance scores. 622 

For each morphology feature, genes were ranked by their contribution weights in the gene 623 

expression encoder. Signed weights were retained to assess concordance between 624 

model-derived importance and marginal associations. 625 

Per-gene correlations. 626 

For each gene–feature pair, Pearson correlation coefficients were computed between 627 

gene expression levels and morphology values across samples. 628 

Gene-Level Expression–Morphology Association Analysis 629 

To assess whether genes prioritized by the shared autoencoder showed independent 630 

association with morphology, a gene-level expression–morphology association scan 631 

(cmTWAS) was performed across donors using linear regression. Analyses were 632 

restricted to genes with cis-eQTL support (eQTL p < 0.05) within the same cohort to 633 

reduce the tested gene set. Prior to testing, both gene expression and morphology traits 634 

were mean-centered. Regression coefficients, standard errors, and two-sided p-values 635 

were computed for each gene–feature pair. Multiple testing across genes was controlled 636 

within each morphology feature using the Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate 637 

procedure (q < 0.05). 638 

SNP → Expression → Morphology Chain Construction 639 

For genes showing significant expression–morphology associations in cmTWAS, putative 640 

SNP → expression → morphology chains were constructed by linking each gene to its 641 

strongest cis-eQTL variant. Cis-eQTL summary statistics (β, standard error, and p- and 642 

q-values) were obtained from precomputed cis-eQTL results (eigenMT-corrected) 643 

generated in the same donor cohort, which account for donor relatedness (kinship) and 644 

latent confounding factors (PEER). For each gene, the top cis-eQTL SNP was selected 645 

and paired with the corresponding cmTWAS association for the morphology feature. 646 

Reported chain tables include eQTL effect size and uncertainty (β, SE) and eigenMT-647 

adjusted q-values, alongside cmTWAS effect sizes, standard errors, and FDR-adjusted 648 

q-values. 649
