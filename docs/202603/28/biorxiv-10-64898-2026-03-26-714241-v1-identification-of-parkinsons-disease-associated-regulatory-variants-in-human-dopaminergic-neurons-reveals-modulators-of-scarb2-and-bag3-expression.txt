# Abstract 32 

A hallmark of Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the degeneration of midbrain dopaminergic neurons 33 

(mDANs). Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified single nucleotide polymorphisms 34 

(SNPs) associated with PD, but causal variants and mechanisms remain unknown. Many PD-associated 35 

SNPs reside in regulatory regions, where they may disrupt transcription factor binding sites (TFBS) and 36 

alter gene expression. To assess how non-coding PD SNPs affect gene regulation in mDANs, we 37 

identify variants predicted to alter TF binding and functionally validate their effects in a cell type-38 

specific context. We integrate time-series transcriptome and chromatin accessibility data from iPSC-39 

derived neurons with chromatin topology and genetic variants. We profile 3D chromatin conformation 40 

in neuronal progenitors (smNPCs) and mDANs using LowC, identifying changes in A/B compartments 41 

and topologically associated domains. PD SNPs are enriched near genes expressed in mDANs, and we 42 

predict 254 regulatory variants that create or disrupt TFBS. Using chromatin conformation data, we link 43 

variants to target genes. At the BAG3 and SCARB2 loci, reporter assays in mDANs show reduced 44 

transcription driven by PD-associated alleles. Knock-down of NR2C2, a putative SCARB2 regulator, 45 

increases SCARB2 expression in differentiating neurons. The PD-associated SCARB2 allele shows 46 

reduced chromatin accessibility in mDANs and is associated with decreased expression in brain eQTL 47 

data. Insertion of PD-associated BAG3 allele by prime editing reduces chromatin accessibility across 48 

cell types, consistent with altered binding of LIM-homeodomain transcription factors. Together, these 49 

results prioritize functional PD SNPs and show that variants at SCARB2 and BAG3 modulate gene 50 

expression in mDANs, providing mechanistic insight into PD. 51

# Introduction 56 

Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the fastest growing neurodegenerative disorder, affecting millions 57 

worldwide with numbers expected to double within the next two decades (1). Its hallmark is the 58 

selective degeneration of midbrain dopaminergic neurons (mDANs), which leads to the well-59 

documented motor symptoms of PD (2). While the etiology of PD is complex, involving both genetic 60 

and environmental factors, genetic predispositions play a crucial role in the disease's development and 61 

progression (3). 62 

Large-scale genomic projects, such as the 1000 Genomes Project, have catalogued millions of genetic 63 

variants across diverse human populations, and together with efforts to map the human epigenome, such 64 

as ENCODE and IHEC, have revealed that the vast majority of these variants are non-coding (4-7). 65 

These non-coding variants are central in regulating gene expression, particularly through transcription 66 

factor binding sites (TFBS) located within enhancers and promoters, and can influence complex traits 67 

and diseases primarily by modulating the activity of these regulatory regions (8,9). 68 

The effects of non-coding variants are largely cell type-specific, depending on the enhancers that are 69 

active in a particular cell type. For instance, a variant located within an enhancer active in mDANs 70 

might have no effect in other cell types, such as liver or muscle cells, where that enhancer is not active. 71 

This specificity arises as each cell type has a unique set of transcription factors that are expressed and 72 

capable of binding to accessible regions within the genome. For example, the non-coding variants 73 

associated with Alzheimer’s disease are typically enriched in enhancers active in microglia (10), often 74 

leading to more severe immune response to the accumulation of tau fibrils during disease progression 75 

(11). 76 

Expression quantitative trait loci (eQTL) analysis has shown that the influence of cis -regulatory variants 77 

on gene expression often manifests as subtle changes, typically altering gene expression only by 10% 78 

to 50%. Despite these modest alterations, the cell type-specific impact can be profound, affecting 79 

disease susceptibility and progression as trans -regulatory variants through a cascade of regulatory 80 

events (12). Furthermore, the regulatory effects of non-coding variants are mediated through networks 81 

of enhancers and transcription factors, each of which may only be active under certain physiological 82 

conditions or stages of cell differentiation (13). This nuanced regulatory landscape underscores the 83 

necessity of studying these variants in cell type-specific models to fully understand their roles in disease 84 

pathogenesis (14,15). For example, expression of the SNCA gene, which encodes alpha-synuclein, a 85 

key protein in PD pathology, has been found to be under control of neuron-specific enhancers carrying 86 

non-coding variants associated with disease risk (16). 87 

In the context of PD, genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified numerous single 88 

nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) associated with the disease, most of which are located in non-coding 89 

regions (17). These variants are expected to play an important role in explaining the missing heritability 90

playing crucial roles in PD pathogenesis – can modify PD susceptibility. However, these interactions 92

Non-coding variants associated with PD are particularly observed in the proximity of genes associated 94 

with cellular functions such as mitochondrial or lysosomal homeostasis (19). Dysfunctions in these 95 

pathways are a notable feature in PD, and PD-associated variants are often located near genes involved 96 

in these pathways, suggesting a potential regulatory impact by these SNPs on lysosomal and other 97 

crucial pathways. 98 

Here, we have utilized a novel integrative approach combining time-series transcriptomic, chromatin 99 

accessibility, and chromosome conformation data from induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC)-derived 100 

mDANs to explore how PD-associated non-coding variants can affect gene regulation. Our analysis 101 

focused on identifying PD SNPs that can alter TF binding in cell type-specific regulatory regions. This 102 

led to the identification of hundreds of candidate variants for detailed functional studies. From these 103 

rs1465922 at the SCARB2 locus and rs144814361 at the BAG3 locus were found to create new TF 104 

binding sites for NR2C2 and LIM homeodomain (HD-LIM) TFs such as LHX1, respectively. These 105 

variants modulate SCARB2 and BAG3 expression and, for SCARB2, were linked to lower expression 106 

in critical brain regions like the substantia nigra. These findings advance our understanding of how non-107 

coding variants contribute to PD pathogenesis and highlight some molecular mechanisms that can drive 108 

disease susceptibility and inform future disease-modifying treatments. 109

version 1.36.0 in R 4.2.3 and Rstudio (v2023.12.1.402). Briefly, heterozygous sites in the ATAC-seq 306 

BAM files of smNPCs, mDAN D15, mDAN D30, mDAN D50 and astrocytes D65 derived from TH-307 

Rep1 cell line, and iPSCs and smNPCs from TH-Rep1-SNP-BAG3 cell line were quantified. The allele 308 

count was then calculated, and a chi-square test or generalized linear mixed model (random effect for 309 

the replicate) using the binomial distribution were computed as indicated to assess statistical 310 

significance between effect and the other allele. 311 

Cell type association with genetic risk of Parkinson’s disease 312 

To evaluate whether common SNPs associated with increased risk of PD in the European population 313 

are enriched in sorted cell type-specific expressed genes, we used Multi-marker Analysis of GenoMic 314 

Annotation (MAGMA) v1.09. MAGMA is a gene set enrichment analysis method that tests the joint 315 

association of all SNPs in a gene with the phenotype while accounting for the LD structure between 316 

SNPs (26). In our study, the SNPs and their corresponding p-values were taken from the summary 317 

statistics of the PD GWAS from Nalls et al . 2019 (17). LD between SNPs was estimated using the 318 

publicly available European subset of 1000 Genomes Phase 3 as a reference panel. The MAGMA 319 

analysis comprises three steps. The first step is the annotation process, where SNPs are mapped to genes 320 

using the NCBI GRCh37 build (annotation release 105). Gene boundaries are defined as the transcribed 321 

region of each gene. A 35 kb window upstream and 10 kb window downstream of each gene were added 322 

to the gene boundaries. Next, the gene analysis step calculates gene-wise p-values based on SNP p-323

Identification of SNPs that affect TF binding 329 

To identify regulatory SNPs that affect TF binding sites and therefore may change target gene 330 

expression, we applied our software SNEEP ((27), GitHub: https://github.com/SchulzLab/SNEEP). The 331 

software requires as input a list of SNPs and a set of TF motifs. For each resulting SNP-TF motif pair, 332 

SNEEP evaluates whether the binding behaviour of the TF is affected by the analysed SNP. 333 

For each timepoint of differentiation (D15 pos, D30 pos, D50 pos, and smNPC), we analysed the SNPs 334 

that overlap with the timepoint-specific accessible regions. For the accessible regions we took the 335 

ATAC peaks from our previous work (22). As TF motifs we used 633 non-redundant human motifs 336 

from the JASPAR database (version 2020) (28), where we excluded for each timepoint of differentiation 337 

the non-expressed TFs (counts <=0.5). 338 

SNEEP incorporates the functionality to consider SNPs only in regions of interest (in our case within 339 

the accessible regions) and excluding TFs not or weakly expressed. To do so, we specified the following 340 

parameter for each timepoint of differentiation: -p 0.05, -c 0.01 -f <ATAC-peaks-timepoint-specific> -341 

t <RNA-seq-timepoint-specific> -e <MappingEnsemblIDToGeneNameForTFs> -d 0.5. Additionally, 342 

the PD GWAS SNPs and the TF motifs are required to run our software. 343 

To find the genes affected by the SNPs identified by SNEEP, the timepoint-specific accessible-regions 344 

had to be linked to their target genes. This was done with STARE (v1.0.4) (29) (GitHub: 345 

https://github.com/SchulzLab/STARE)). For the candidate enhancers the narrowPeaks ATAC-seq files 346 

of each timepoint were used and the signalValue taken as enhancer activity. As gene annotation we 347 

took v38 from GENCODE (30). The LowC data was processed with FAN-C (v.0.9.27) (31) and used 348 

as contact data for STARE at 5 kb resolution. All other parameters were kept at their default values 349

# Results 483 

PD-associated variants are enriched in vicinity of genes expressed in iPSC-derived mDANs 484 

To investigate whether non-coding PD SNPs identified in GWAS can contribute to PD risk by acting 485 

as regulatory SNPs in a cell type-specific manner, we took advantage of our recent multiomic data of 486 

iPSC-derived cultures of astrocytes and dopaminergic neurons (mDANs) (Figure 1A, (22)). The data 487 

consist of paired transcriptomic and chromatin accessibility data from neural progenitor cells 488 

(smNPCs), astrocytes and different time points of differentiation of purified reporter-positive 489 

dopaminergic neurons (mDANs) and reporter negative non-mDANs. The presence of an integrated 490 

mCherry reporter at TH locus enabled the purification of mDANs from mixed neuronal cultures by 491 

FACS (21). Inspection of established markers genes of mDANs, including known transcriptional 492 

regulators and enzymes and transporters involved in dopamine metabolism, confirmed their selective 493 

induction and expression in mCherry positive mDAN population (Supplementary Figure S1). Recent 494 

single cell transcriptomic analysis of all cell types of human substantia nigra have demonstrated a 495 

specific enrichment of PD SNPs in proximity of genes expressed in mDANs in vivo (35,36). Therefore, 496 

using our transcriptomic data, we selected genes with cell type-selective expression for each cell type 497 

and differentiation state and used MAGMA (26) to determine whether the PD SNPs from GWAS meta-498 

analysis (17) are enriched in vicinity of the genes in these sets also in vitro (Figure 1B). Interestingly, 499 

PD SNPs were significantly enriched in proximity of genes showing selective expression in mDANs, 500 

suggesting they might also play an important role in cis -regulatory variation in these cells. 501 

Higher order genome organization in smNPCs and mDANs 502 

Rewiring of the higher-order genome organization is associated with neural development and 503 

differentiation (37-39). Recent genome-wide chromatin conformation capture analyses such as Hi-C 504 

have revealed that the higher-order genome in mammalian cells is spatially segregated into megabase 505 

sized euchromatic or heterochromatin domains known as A or B compartments (40). To annotate 506 

higher-order genome organization in smNPCs and mDANs, and to allow more accurate association of 507 

the identified enhancers to their target genes in smNPCs and purified mDANs, we carried out LowC 508 

experiments to define the long-distance chromatin interactions and topologically associated domains 509 

(TADs) present in these cells. 510 

We calculated PC1 (principle component 1) of the eigenvector of the LowC matrices and called A (PC 511 

> 0) or B (PC1 < 0) compartments with a resolution of 500 kb (Figure 1C). Previous studies have 512 

reported that A or B compartments are enriched in SINE/Alu (short interspersed nuclear elements in 513 

mice, Alu in humans) or LINE retrotransposons, respectively (41). Indeed, our analysis confirmed that 514 

in smNPCs and mDANs, A compartments exhibited higher mean accessibility as measured by ATAC-515 

seq, and were enriched for Alus, while B compartments exhibited enrichment for LINEs (Figure 1D-516 

F). Visual inspection of a representative chromosome (chr 11) further corrobarated these findings by 517

showing that Alu or LINE repeats were enriched in A or B compartments, respectively (Supplementary 518 

Figure S2). 519 

We next tested whether differentiation from smNPCs to mDANs was associated with structural changes 520 

in the higher order genome organization, by plotting PC1 values in 500 kb genomic bins for smNPCs 521 

and mDANs (Figure 1C). This analysis showed that 12.5% of all 500 kb bins showed a decrease in 522 

mDANs (group G1), while 4.5% showed a reciprocal increase (group G3). The rest (82.7%, group G2) 523 

remained unchanged (Figure 1C). A decrease in PC1 values indicates a shift to a more heterochromatin-524 

like state. A considerable fraction of the 500 kb genomic regions, therefore, may exhibit increased 525 

heterochromatin-like properties upon differentiation into mDANs. A recent study showed that 3D 526 

organization of the genome is different between neuronal and non-neuronal cells (39). We mined the 527 

Hi-C data from this report, and calculated A/B compartments in non-neuronal (NeuN negative: 528 

NeuN neg ) or neuronal (NeuN positive: NeuN pos ) post-mortem brain cells (Supplementary Figure S3). 529 

However, we found A/B compartments to remain largely stable between NeuN neg and NeuN pos cells, 530 

with somewhat lower number of changes to those observed in mDAN differentiation (Supplementary 531 

Figure S3A). Taken together, our results indicate that the differentiation of smNPCs to mDANs may be 532 

accompanied by a significant reorganization of the 3D genome, as manifested by the decrease of PC1 533 

values in 12.5% of all 500 kb genomic bins. 534 

We wondered whether the changes in the 3D genome organization were also associated with changes 535 

in transcription during differentiation into mDANs (Figure 1G). We compared the transcription levels 536 

in the 500 kb genomic bins between smNPCs and mDANs. The G1 group of bins that possess higher 537 

PC1 values and are therefore more euchromatic, generally exhibited higher transcription levels. In 538 

contrast, the G3 group of bins that possess lower PC1 values and are therefore more heterochromatic, 539 

also exhibited lower transcription levels. However, direct comparison between smNPCs and mDANs 540 

within each group of regions (G1, G2 or G3) showed no clear changes in transcription levels (Figure 541 

1G). To examine whether there were any changes in chromatin accessibility between smNPCs and 542 

mDANs, we used our ATAC-seq data to compare the groups of regions (Supplementary Figure S2B). 543 

Consistent with the expression levels (Figure 1G), euchromatin regions with higher PC1 values 544 

exhibited more chromatin accessibility, while heterochromatic regions with lower PC1 values exhibited 545 

less chromatin accessibility (Supplementary Figure S2B). Again, there were no significant differences 546 

between smNPCs and mDANs. Based on these observations, we concluded that the changes in higher 547 

order chromatin organization in mDANs, may not necessarily accompany changes in transcription or 548 

chromatin accessibility at genome-wide scale when observed at the resolution of 500 kb bins, consistent 549 

with smNPCs being already committed to neuronal lineage. 550

# Discussion 817 

GWAS studies have demonstrated disease–and trait-associated variants to be mostly located into the 818 

non-coding genome (54,55). The introduction of variants in cis - and trans-regulatory regions can 819 

influence the phenotype of a cell by altering the binding of TFs and their target gene expression in a 820 

cell type-specific manner. Thus, with the majority of disease associated variants located in cell type-821 

specific regulatory regions of the genome, the main challenge is to reliably predict their target genes 822 

and their regulatory impact in a cell type. To overcome this challenge, we have taken an integrative 823 

multiomics approach. Since PD-associated variants were found to be enriched in the vicinity of genes 824 

expressed in mDANs, we combined gene expression, chromatin accessibility, and LowC chromosome 825 

conformation data to map target genes and their interactions with regulatory regions in human mDANs. 826 

By integrating these data with information about PD-associated variants, we computed the expected 827 

changes in binding affinities at TFBS due to the presence of the variants and predicted the affected TFs 828 

and their target genes in smNPCs and mDANs. Through this approach, we reduced 3464 PD-associated 829 

candidate variants with genome-wide significance in PD GWAS to 254 variants with potential to act as 830 

regulatory variants in mDANs and during their differentiation. Previous analysis have already studied 831 

PD-associated variants in regulatory regions in tissues (56) and major cell types of the brain such as 832 

excitatory and inhibitory neurons, oligodendrocytes, and microglia (57,58), including single nuclei 833 

analysis of mDANs (59). Moreover, detailed function of some individual variants have been studied in 834 

neuronal and microglia cultures, or in common cell lines (16,60,61). However, detailed prediction and 835 

validation of the context-specific regulatory impact is often difficult due to limited number of mDANs 836 

in post-mortem samples or lack of relevant experimental model system. The number of putative 837

revealed a role in downregulation of the associated target genes for both variants. 848 

BAG3 has been shown to be involved in the development of the central nervous system in rats. 849 

Specifically, in the prenatal forebrain the expression of BAG3 can be detected in the medial 850 

telencephalic wall of the lateral ventricle and in the Cajal-Tetzius cells of the developing cortex. In the 851 

postnatal brain the expression of BAG3 increased in the neurons of the cortex and hippocampus, 852 

followed by a major decrease, indicating an important role for dynamic BAG3 expression during 853 

neurodevelopment (62). Moreover, as we also show, BAG3 is found in glial cells of the midbrain. 854 

Interestingly, BAG3 shows a higher expression in the hippocampus of one year old mice, suggesting 855 

that BAG3 may be implicated in protein homeostasis and clearance during aging, consistent with a 856 

misregulation of this pathway in age-related diseases such as PD (63). In addition, expression of BAG3 857 

was detected in rat dorsal midline glia cells in the midbrain and in the spinal cord but not in the ventral 858 

midline of the same brain tissues (64). 859 

More relevant for PD, increased expression of BAG3 was reported in transgenic mice overexpressing 860 

mutated human SNCA in dopaminergic neurons (65). Additionally, accumulation of SNCA was 861 

detected in BAG3-depleted cells while overexpression of BAG3 produced the opposite effect through 862 

increased autophagy (65). Finally, regulatory variation at BAG3 locus has been recently associated to 863 

PD as PD-associated SNPs were found to coincide with regulatory regions in microglia as defined by 864 

accessible chromatin and acetylation of histone H3 at lysine 27 (60). In our study, we also found a PD-865 

associated SNP (rs144814361) in open chromatin at the BAG3 promoter in smNPCs and mDANs. 866 

However, the strength of our integrative computational approach was to also use chromatin 867 

conformation data, together with prediction of TF binding change at the accessible site, to allow more 868 

precise prediction. Moreover, our reporter gene assays showed that the presence of rs144814361 results 869 

in altered gene expression in smNPCs and mDANs. Finally, use of genome editing to generate a 870 

isogenic cell line carrying the PD-associated variant confirmed its regulatory potential through strong 871 

modulation of promoter chromatin accessibility. 872

factor expressed in postnatal and adult mouse and rat Purkinje cells (66,67) that are responsible for 874

A common limitation of studies on regulatory variation is the difficulty to identify the exact TF or TFs 876 

whose binding is affected by the variant. In keeping with this, also LHX1 belongs to the HD-LIM family 877

rather than LHX1 alone. Consistent with this, introduction of the PD-associated allele by genome 882

multiple transcription factors may contribute to regulation at this locus. These data, together with 884

SCARB2 locus has been associated with rapid-eye movement (REM) sleep behavior disorder (RBD) 888 

(52) that typically precedes PD as a prodromal stage of dopaminergic neurodegeneration years before 889 

typical motor symptoms become apparent (69-72). SCARB2 is the transporter of the enzyme GCase 890 

encoded by the glycosylcerebrosidase (GBA) gene, mutations in which are the most common genetic 891 

risk factor for PD (73). Consistently, depletion of SCARB2 expression was shown to be detrimental for 892 

lysosomal GCase activity in neurons (48). Several SNPs in the proximity of SCARB2 locus have been 893 

associated to PD through GWAS. For example, the SNPs rs6812193 and rs6825004 have been 894 

associated to PD in populations from European ancestry (74). However, these alleles/variants had no 895 

effect on SCARB2 expression at the transcriptional or protein levels (51), highlighting the difficulty of 896 

associating individual SNPs to their respective target genes identified via GWAS. More recently, a non-897 

coding variant rs11547135, was independently associated with both PD and SCARB2 expression in 898 

blood and brain tissue, in keeping with the eQTL results in Figure 3F (75). In our study, we overcame 899 

some of the challenges by associating prediction from TF binding change with chromatin conformation 900 

and accessibility data, narrowing the analysis to the study of rs1465922, and its ability to introduce a 901 

putative NR2C2 TFBS in the promoter of SCARB2 .902 

NR2C2, also known as testicular receptor 4 (TR4), is an orphan nuclear receptor. In mouse, it has been 903 

shown that NR2C2 knockout leads to difficulties in motor coordination (76). In rats, Young et al . have 904 

shown that NR2C2 is highly expressed in the brain, the spinal cord, the ganglia and neuronal epithelia 905 

(77). Also, at the embryonic stage in rats, NR2C2 is abundant in the central nervous system (CNS), as 906 

well as in the cerebellum and the hippocampus in postnatal rats (78). In our study, the creation of a 907 

NR2C2 TFBS through the presence of rs1465922 led to a decrease in luciferase activity in a SCARB2 908 

promoter reporter assay, indicating a possible repressive action of NR2C2 on SCARB2 expression. In 909 

contrast, knockdown of NR2C2 led to an increase in expression of SCARB2 in human neurons, together 910 

highlighting NR2C2 as a repressor of SCARB2 .911

that differentiation into mDANs is accompanied by a pronounced reorganization of higher-order 913

# Conclusions 926 

We provide a resource of putative candidates of regulatory PD SNPs – and a framework for their 927 

identification and functional validation – and experimentally validate the regulatory potential of two of 928 

the variants. This resource will facilitate future studies that will be needed to test their role as regulatory 929 

variants, and to confirm their contribution to gene regulation and development of PD. With the overall 930 

goal to allow cell type-specific predictions of disease-associated gene network alterations from personal 931 

genomes, and generation of improved risk scores incorporating regulatory impact of disease-associated 932
