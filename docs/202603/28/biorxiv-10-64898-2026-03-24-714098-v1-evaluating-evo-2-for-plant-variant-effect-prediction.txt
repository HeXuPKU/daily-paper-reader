Abstract 15 

The genomic foundation model Evo 2 enables zero-shot variant effect prediction. Here, we 16 

evaluate its performance using Arabidopsis thaliana reproductive barrier genes with 17 

experimentally confirmed gain- and loss-of-function variants, and show that Evo 2 18 

distinguishes functionally impactful variants. Together with a sign-reversal amplitude metric 19 

that recovers a variant missed by standard scoring, these results highlight the potential of Evo 20 

2 for causal variant prioritization in plant GWAS and QTL mapping. 21

The emergence of large-scale genomic foundation models has opened new possibilities for 24 

predicting the functional consequences of genetic variants without task-specific training data. 25 

Evo 2 is a recently published biological foundation model trained on 9.3 trillion nucleotides 26 

from a curated genomic atlas spanning all domains of life, including plant genomes, with a 27 

context window of up to 1 million base pairs at single-nucleotide resolution 1, representing 28 

one of the most comprehensive genomic sequence models available. Evo 2 enables zero-shot 29 

variant effect scoring: given a nucleotide sequence and a candidate variant, the model assigns 30 

a likelihood-based score reflecting the predicted impact on biological function 1. This 31 

capability holds considerable promise for interpreting the functional significance of natural 32 

polymorphisms and for prioritizing candidate causal variants in genetic mapping studies. 33 

Evo 2 has demonstrated strong predictive performance in initial benchmarks, 34 

including the prediction of functional impacts for clinically significant human variants 1. This 35 

raises the prospect of Evo 2 serving as a powerful tool for prioritizing candidate causal 36 

variants within genomic regions identified by genome-wide association studies (GWAS) and 37 

quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping — approaches that are central to both basic plant 38 

research and crop breeding programs 2,3 , yet are often limited by the difficulty of pinpointing 39 

the causal variant among many candidates. Recent plant DNA language models have begun 40 

to suggest that zero-shot scores may aid post-GWAS/QTL causal variant prioritization. 41 

PlantCaduceus, for example, combined zero-shot scoring with GWAS to identify the causal 42 

su1 variant in sweet corn, but was developed as a plant-specific model pretrained on 16 43 

angiosperm genomes 4; here, we ask whether comparable utility extends to Evo 2, a general 44 

genomic foundation model, without plant-specific fine-tuning or additional training. 45 

Extending this characterization to encompass the full spectrum of functional variant classes 46 

(gain-of-function, loss-of-function, and near-neutral) within a single well-characterized 47 

biological system provides a particularly informative test of the model's discriminative 48 

power. The most rigorous such tests require not only experimentally characterized variants of 49 

each functional class but also genes with contrasting selective histories and population-level 50 

phenotypic data reflecting the cumulative effects of multi-variant haplotypes. The plant 51 

interspecific reproductive barrier genes STIGMATIC PRIVACY 1 (SPRI1 ) and SPRI2 in 52 

Arabidopsis thaliana nicely satisfy these criteria. Both genes were originally identified 53 

through GWAS conducted across natural A. thaliana accessions 5,6 . Critically, the functional 54 

consequences of specific variants in both genes have since been established experimentally 5–

SPRI1 encodes a pistil factor required for interspecific pollen rejection in self-57 

incompatible relatives of A. thaliana 5. Because A. thaliana is self-compatible 9, the selective 58 

pressure maintaining SPRI1 function is greatly reduced, allowing a spectrum of functionally 59 

distinct variants, including loss-of-function alleles and a gain-of-function substitution, to 60 

accumulate in natural accessions 5. To evaluate Evo 2 zero-shot variant effect prediction, we 61 

scored all natural variants at the SPRI1 locus (Fig. 1a) catalogued in the 1001 Genomes 62 

Project 10 . For each variant, ∆likelihood was calculated as the change in log-likelihood from 63 

the wild-type (Col-0) to the variant sequence. Strongly negative ∆likelihoods, suggesting 64 

functional impact, were almost exclusively associated with variants in coding regions: gene-65 

disrupting variants such as frameshifts, stop-gained, and splice-site changes accumulated in 66 

the negative tail, whereas variants in 5'UTR, 3'UTR, introns, and intergenic regions remained 67 

clustered near zero (Fig. 1b). On the other hand, variants with positive ∆likelihoods were 68 

considerably fewer, consistent with the expectation that strongly beneficial variants are rare, 69 

whereas most segregating variants are neutral or of small effect 11 . Among these variants, 70 

G155A (a glycine-to-alanine substitution at position 155), an experimentally confirmed gain-71 

of-function variant carried by the SPRI1A haplotype 5,8 , showed a positive ∆likelihood, 72 

consistent with barrier enhancement. However, variants known to impair SPRI1 function, 73 

including E2V, N92H, and stop222C, carried by the SPRI1B haplotype 5,7 , also showed 74 

∆likelihoods near zero or positive in the forward orientation (Fig. 1b), suggesting that 75 

forward-only scoring would fail to flag these variants as functionally significant. 76 

As reverse-complement inconsistency has been noted in DNA language models 12 , we 77 

also calculated ∆likelihoods in the reverse-complement orientation. (Fig. 1c). Among the 78 

gene-disrupting variants (frameshifts, stop-gained, etc.), scores were inconsistent between 79 

orientations: some variants that showed near-zero ∆likelihoods in the forward orientation 80 

yielded strongly negative values in the reverse complement orientation, while others that 81 

scored strongly negative in the forward orientation showed weaker values in the reverse (Fig. 82 

1b, c). This orientation-dependent inconsistency suggests that single-orientation scoring may 83 

not reliably capture the functional severity of gene-disrupting variants. We therefore 84 

calculated an averaged ∆likelihood for each variant, as in the original Evo 2 study 1, as the 85 

mean of the forward and reverse-complement scores (Fig. 1d). The averaged score placed 86 

frameshift and stop-gained variants consistently in the negative tail, except for stop222C. 87 

Although N92H remained near zero, E2V showed one of the most strongly negative averaged 88 

∆likelihoods among missense variants (Fig. 1d). 89

Stop222C is a stop codon elimination caused by a frameshift at the 3′ boundary of the 90 

coding sequence, which extends the protein by 12 amino acids and reduces SPRI1 protein 91 

stability 7. Although the averaged ∆likelihood was near zero due to cancellation of opposite-92 

sign scores, the sign reversal itself may reflect an inherent functional ambiguity of stop codon 93 

loss and the resulting C-terminal extension: such additions can be stabilizing or destabilizing 94 

depending on the protein context, and the model's directional disagreement may capture this 95 

uncertainty. In other proteins or contexts, analogous extensions can be beneficial. The 96 

model's opposite verdicts in the forward and reverse orientations may thus reflect a genuine 97 

biological uncertainty about the functional consequence of this class of variant. 98 

To flag such orientation-discordant variants systematically, we first selected variants 99 

showing sign reversal (i.e., forward and reverse ∆likelihoods with opposite signs) and ranked 100 

them by sign-reversal amplitude [|∆likelihood fwd − ∆likelihood rev | / 2]. The stop222C variant 101 

ranked among the top two by sign-reversal amplitude (Fig. 1f). Notably, this variant would 102 

have been missed by standard averaged ∆likelihood filtering alone, as its average approached 103 

zero; the sign-reversal amplitude metric thus serves as a complementary filter that 104 

specifically recovers this variant class that standard approaches fail to detect. 105 

At the SPRI2 locus (Extended Data Fig. 1a), the forward and reverse ∆likelihoods 106 

showed broadly similar distributions (Extended Data Fig. 1b, c), in contrast to the strand bias 107 

observed at SPRI1 (Fig. 1b, c). The reduced strand bias in SPRI2 may reflect its broader 108 

representation in the Evo 2 training corpus because the SPRI2 protein belongs to the SHI-109 

family of zinc-finger transcription factors, a well-conserved protein family with numerous 110 

homologs across diverse plant lineages, likely providing abundant training signal in both 111 

orientations. The SPRI1 protein, by contrast, is restricted to a limited phylogenetic group 112 

within the Brassicaceae and lacks a well-characterized conserved domain. Therefore, 113 

relatively few homologous sequences were likely available during model training, potentially 114 

making the learned representations more sensitive to orientation. The overall scoring range in 115 

SPRI2 was substantially narrower, and variants with strongly negative averaged ∆likelihoods 116 

were largely absent (Extended Data Fig. 1d). As SPRI2 directly controls cell wall xylan 117 

modification genes and indirectly upregulates SPRI1 expression 6, SPRI2 is subject to broader 118 

functional constraints across tissues and developmental contexts, maintaining purifying 119 

selection in natural populations. Among variants with characterized functional outcomes, two 120 

substitutions in the C-terminal region (S343P and C345S) showed positive averaged 121

To test whether Evo 2 captures functionally relevant variation at the population level, 124 

we calculated variant likelihood scores for natural A. thaliana haplotypes of SPRI1 and 125 

compared them with phenotype scores from heterospecific pollen rejection assays. A 126 

negative correlation was observed between phenotype score and variant likelihood score (Fig. 127 

2), such that haplotypes with greater pollen acceptance (lower SPRI1 function) tended to 128 

carry sequences predicted to be less optimal by Evo 2. Notably, no haplotypes with low 129 

variant log-likelihood scores (less than -7.520) were found in the phenotype score range of 1– 130 

2.5, consistent with the requirement of intact SPRI1 function for the rejection of M. littorea 131 

pollen. 132 

In contrast, some haplotypes, even though they carry the gain-of-function variant 133 

G155A, showed high compatibility scores (more than 3.5), suggesting reduced pollen 134 

rejection activity. Among haplotypes without frameshift or stop-gained variants, these 135 

correspond to haplotypes 11, 25, and 37, which carry G155A together with additional 136 

missense substitutions, and showed lower variant likelihood scores. The additional missense 137 

substitutions carried by these haplotypes — including S50R, P58S, M135T, H27N, and 138 

P218S — each show individual ∆likelihoods near zero or comparable to that of E2V, a 139 

variant that alone has little effect on SPRI1A function (Fig. 1d). Nevertheless, their co-140 

occurrence with G155A partially overrides its gain-of-function effect, reducing pollen 141 

rejection activity — a functionally antagonistic combination that individual variant scoring 142 

alone would not readily predict. 143 

Together, our results suggest that Evo 2 variant effect scoring offers a broadly 144 

applicable zero-shot framework for causal variant prioritization in plant genetics, both for 145 

prioritizing individual candidate variants within GWAS or QTL intervals and for capturing 146 

combinatorial effects through haplotype-level scoring, without the need for plant-specific 147 

fine-tuning or additional training .148

Materials and Methods 150 

Variant datasets 151 

Natural variants at SPRI1 and SPRI2 loci were obtained from the 1001 Genomes Project 10 .152 

For each variant, the corresponding nucleotide substitution was individually introduced into 153 

the Col-0 reference genomic sequence to generate an 8,192 bp input sequence centered on the 154 

locus of interest. As both SPRI1 and SPRI2 are encoded on the reverse strand, input 155 

sequences represent the genomic (sense) strand, with the genes in reverse complement 156 

orientation relative to their coding sequences. 157 

158 

Evo 2 variant effect scoring 159 

Variant effect prediction was performed using the Evo 2 20B parameter model (version 0.5.0; 160 

Brixi et al., 2026). Variant effects were quantified as ∆likelihoods, calculated as the 161 

difference in log-likelihood between the variant and reference sequences: ∆likelihood = log 162 

P(variant) − log P(reference). Scoring was performed independently in both the forward (5' to 163 

3') and reverse complement orientations, yielding two ∆likelihoods per variant. All scoring 164 

was zero-shot, with no task-specific fine-tuning of the model. For each variant, an averaged 165 

∆likelihood was also calculated as ∆likelihood avg = (∆likelihood fwd + ∆likelihood rev ) / 2. For 166 

variants in which the forward and reverse ∆likelihoods carry opposite signs — indicative of 167 

orientation-discordant predictions — a sign-reversal amplitude was additionally calculated as 168 

|∆likelihood fwd − ∆likelihood rev | / 2. All scores were plotted against genomic position (kbp) to 169 

visualize the distribution of variant effects across each locus, with guide lines at ±2σ and ±3σ 170 

overlaid. To account for the characteristically skewed distributions of variant effect scores, 171 

these guide lines were calculated using asymmetric semi-standard deviations: σ⁻ was defined 172 

as the standard deviation of variants scoring below the distribution mean, and σ⁺ as that of 173 

variants scoring above the mean. 174 

175 

Natural haplotype analysis 176 

SPRI1 sequences from natural A. thaliana accessions previously characterized 5 were used for 177 

haplotype-level scoring. For each accession, the full SPRI1 genomic sequence — spanning 178 

from the start codon to the stop codon and including an intron — was extracted and flanked 179 

by the corresponding Col-0 upstream and downstream sequences to generate an 8,192 bp 180 

input sequence. Each sequence was scored with the Evo 2 20B model in both the forward and 181 

reverse complement orientations, and the two log-likelihoods were averaged to yield a per-182
