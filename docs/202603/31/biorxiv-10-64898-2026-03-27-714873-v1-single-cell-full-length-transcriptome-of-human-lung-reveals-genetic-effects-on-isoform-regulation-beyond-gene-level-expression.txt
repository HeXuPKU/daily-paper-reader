Abstract 36 

Genetic regulation of splicing uniquely contributes to trait-associated genome-wide 37 

association studies (GWAS) signals. However, quantitative trait loci (QTL) analysis 38 

using short-read sequencing of bulk tissues fails to capture full-length and cell-type-39 

specific isoforms. Here, we present an isoform-level lung cell atlas from 129 never-40 

smoking Korean women using single-cell long-read RNA-sequencing, identifying 41 

abundant unannotated and cell-type-specific isoforms. Isoform-level signatures of 37 42 

lung cell types display a larger difference and therefore improve cell-type classification 43 

compared to gene-level expression. Notably, isoform-QTLs (isoQTLs) detect 44 

unannotated and/or cell-type-specific isoforms with independent genetic regulation from 45 

expression-QTL (eQTL), supported by enriched splicing functional elements. IsoQTLs 46 

nominate susceptibility isoforms from previously unexplained lung function and cancer 47 

GWAS loci, via eQTL-independent signals. We highlight a potentially functional novel 48 

variant of PPIL6 in multiciliated cells underlying lung cancer risk through alternative 49 

splicing. This isoform-level resource advances our understanding of cell-type-specific 50 

isoform regulation and its contribution to lung traits and diseases. 51

Introduction 52 

> 53

Transcript isoform variation creates functional diversity in protein isoforms or influences 54 

total gene expression levels through the turnover of less stable isoforms 1. Alternative 55 

RNA splicing and isoform composition dynamics could play an essential role in 56 

organogenesis, and misregulation of this process could lead to various human 57 

diseases 2,3 , including cancer and other chronic diseases. However, the full diversity of 58 

isoform structures in different tissues and cell types is not completely understood due to 59 

the dearth of tissue-based and cell-type-specific full-length transcriptome datasets in 60 

diverse populations. 61 

> 62

Consistent with the importance of isoform functionality, it is increasingly evident that 63 

unique genetic contribution to splicing regulation underlies complex diseases. Although 64 

tissue-based studies identified comparable numbers of splicing junction usage 65 

quantitative trait loci (splicing QTL or sQTL) and expression QTLs (eQTLs), they 66 

showed limited overlap of credible set variants. Consistently, sQTLs contributed to 67 

explaining various trait-associated GWAS signals independent from eQTLs 3. For a 68 

typical sQTL mapping using short-read RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) data, either isoform 69 

expression is estimated using probabilistic models 4,5 , or relative splice junction usage is 70 

treated as a proxy phenotype. However, the molecular effects of these junction-based 71 

sQTLs can be elusive; mapping them to a specific isoform is challenging due to shared 72 

junctions and incomplete reference annotation. 73 

> 74

Addressing these limitations, recent full-length transcriptomes of human tissues using 75 

long-read RNA-seq reported hundreds of thousands novel isoforms accounting for 26%-76 

77% of total isoforms 6–8 . For example, an isoform-level transcriptome using 14 tissues 77 

from GTExexhibited tissue-specificity of isoforms and included a substantial proportion 78 

of novel isoforms, with a subset being validated at the peptide level 6. Moreover, 79 

emerging single-cell long-read RNA-seq studies in brain tissues demonstrated isoform 80 

specificity across brain cell types 7,9 , including disease-relevant novel isoforms 7, and 81 

observed an overlap of published sQTLs with cell-type-specific disease gene 10 .82 

Although single-cell eQTL datasets of several human tissue types has highlighted 83 

prevalent cell-type-specific genetic regulation contributing to trait-associated GWAS loci 84 

> 11–13

, single-cell isoform-level QTL datasets are lacking due to high cost and sparsity of 85 

single-cell long-read RNA-seq. Rare cases of single-cell sQTL studies either used 86 

single-cell 5’ short-read RNA-seq 14 or leveraged a full-length transcriptome reference to 87 

interpret junction-based sQTLs 8, where isoform estimation is still vulnerable to errors 88

isoforms are different from gene-level observation and whether isoform QTLs contribute 90 

to GWAS signals of complex traits independent from eQTLs have not been 91 

systematically investigated. 92 

> 93

Lung cancer is a common and complex disease including histological types originating 94 

from distinct epithelial cell types. Although smoking is a well-known risk factor, GWAS 95 

have identified more than 60 risk loci, including those distinct to histological types and 96 

populations 15–17 . Lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD), the most common histological type, 97 

mainly arises from alveolar type 2 (AT2) of lung alveoli 18–20 , with alveolar type 1 (AT1), 98 

club, and alveolar transitional cells/states also being implicated 21,22 . These cell types 99 

have relevance to the regeneration of alveoli that contributes to broader lung function 100 

traits and chronic lung diseases 23 . Indeed, genetic control of lung function traits, 101 

including those defining chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), can contribute 102 

to lung cancer risk 24 . Large-scale lung function GWAS identified >1,000 loci in multi-103 

ancestry studies 25,26 , and genetic correlation as well as overlap of GWAS findings 104 

between lung cancer and lung functions or COPD have been observed 24,27 . Although 105 

previous bulk-tissue based lung eQTLs partially characterized lung cancer and lung 106 

function GWAS loci, the contribution of isoform diversity and sQTLs 28 as well as single-107 

cell QTLs 11,12 have been less explored, especially in non-European populations. 108 

> 109

In this study, we generated a single-cell full-length transcriptome of tumor-distant 110 

normal lung tissues from 129 Korean never-smoking women to investigate the genetic 111 

effects on transcript isoform regulation (Fig. 1). This isoform-level lung cell atlas 112 

revealed the signatures of isoform expression across 37 lung cell types. We mapped 113 

isoform-level QTLs (isoQTLs) across 33 cell types and further deciphered potential 114 

splicing relevant functions of isoQTLs distinct from eQTLs. By integrating isoQTLs with 115 

lung cancer and lung function GWAS, we uncovered the context-specific susceptibility 116 

isoforms and their contribution to GWAS signals independent from eQTLs. 117

Results 118 

Full-length transcriptome uncovers isoform diversity in 129 lung tissues 119 

To profile full-length single-cell transcriptomes of lung tissues from 129 individuals 120 

(Supplementary Table 1 ), we adopted PacBio single-cell multiple amplicon sequencing 121 

(MAS-seq), incorporating cell type balancing and multiplexing ( Fig. 1 ). Of note, we 122 

generated a cell-barcode-matched short-read-based dataset using the same cDNA 123 

libraries (Luong et al. 29 ), which allowed systematic comparisons between two datatypes. 124 

Matching genotype data from blood samples was obtained using DNA genotyping 125 

followed by imputation ( Methods ). We employed cell type balancing to further reduce 126 

sampling bias and enrich epithelial cells, which include cells of lung cancer origin but 127 

are underrepresented in single-cell processing 23,30 . For this, fluorescence-activated cell 128 

sorting (FACS) was used 31 (Methods ), followed by multiplexing of ~6 individuals per 129 

single-cell batch (22 total batches). 130 

> 131

In total, we identified 325,864 full-length isoforms from 129 lung tissues after quality 132 

control (QC)( Methods, Fig. 2A ). These transcripts were defined in six structural 133 

categories (SQANTI3 32 ): full-splice-match or FSM, incomplete-splice-match or ISM, 134 

novel-in-catalog or NIC, novel-not-in catalog or NNC, fusion gene, genomic, and 135 

antisense ( Fig. 2B ). Among these, the known isoforms (FSM) accounted for 82% of 136 

total full-length reads, indicating the total gene levels could be mainly represented by 137 

the known isoforms ( Fig. 2C ). The detected known isoforms accounted for 34.6% of the 138 

annotated transcripts (55,329) from GENCODE v32 ( Supplementary Fig. 1A ). Notably, 139 

83% of the total isoforms were novel transcripts not annotated in GENCODE v32 140 

(representing all the categories except FSM in Fig. 2D ). The high proportion of novel 141 

isoforms is consistent with the findings in recent bulk-tissue long-read sequencing 142 

datasets (26-77%) 33,34 . For 20.0% (4,323/21,616) of genes, only one isoform was 143 

detected, which were mostly annotated isoforms (80% of 4,323 genes) ( Fig. 2E ). For 144 

80% of 21,616 genes, multiple isoforms were detected, where the numbers of novel 145 

isoforms significantly correlated with those of known isoforms (correlation coefficient: 146 

0.44, p-value < 2.2e-16), similar to previous studies 6. Leveraging our sample size, we 147 

surveyed the isoform distribution across 129 individuals (median 55,872 per individual). 148 

We observed known isoforms exhibited bimodal distributions, where a substantial 149 

proportion (7.8%) was widely expressed in all individuals. Novel isoforms were mainly 150 

specific to smaller subsets of individuals compared to the known ones ( Fig. 2F ). 151 

> 152

target of nonsense-mediated decay (NMD) based on the presence of a premature 154 

termination codon located >=50 nucleotides upstream of the last splicing junction. We 155 

found that the proportions of isoforms sensitive to NMD were higher in novel isoform 156 

categories than in known ones (3.4% in known or FSM vs 16.8% in all novel classes), 157

To validate the detected isoform diversity in lung cells, including the prevalence of novel 161 

and individual-specific isoforms, we took multi-layered approaches. 162 

First, we sought replication of our isoforms in the GTEx custom full-length 163 

transcriptome using 14 types of bulk tissues including lung 6. Given a larger sample size 164 

and a higher total isoform number in our data, we replicated 35.8% (31,521) of the 165 

GTEx isoforms. Among the known isoforms, our data detected a higher proportion of 166 

annotated transcripts (34.6% in GENCODE v32 vs. 12% in GENCODE v26 6). For novel 167 

isoforms, 49.8% of the replicated isoforms were annotated as novel in both datasets, 168 

including 729 novel isoforms validated by mass-spectrometry in GTEx. 169 

Second, we further validated novel isoforms at the peptide level by liquid 170 

chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) proteome profiling of bulk 171

Isoform-level lung cell type signatures are more specific than gene-level 209 

> 210

Our barcode-matched dataset enabled us to investigate whether isoform expression 211 

could provide additional cell-type-level information not captured in total expression. To 212 

characterize cell type signatures of isoform expression, we first annotated cell types 213 

using the gene-level expression matrix to align with the established markers of lung cell 214 

types 35 (Methods, Supplementary Fig. 3 ). In total, we identified 37 lung cell types in 215 

four major lung cell categories: epithelial, immune, endothelial, and stromal cells ( Fig. 216 

3A and Supplementary Fig. 4A ). Reflecting our design of epithelial cell enrichment 217 

and cell type balancing, epithelial cells accounted for 45% of total cells ( Fig. 3B ). 218 

Compared to the independent cell type annotation of our short-read dataset 29 , we found 219 

a cell-to-cell annotation match for 84.3% cells, with few rare cell subtypes missing in the 220 

long-read data ( Supplementary Fig. 4B ). 221 

> 222

To detect cell type signatures of isoform expression, differential expression analysis 223 

was performed within each cell type ( Methods ). To address the data sparsity at the 224 

single-cell level ( Supplementary Fig. 5A ) and exclude batch-specific isoforms ( Fig. 225 

2F ), we pseudo-bulked isoform expression into individual levels and retained relevant 226 

abundant isoforms for each cell type ( Methods ). We nominated differentially expressed 227 

isoforms (DEIs, defined as isoforms with log 2(fold change) > 1 and adjusted p-value < 228 

0.05) of each cell type against all the other cell types. Out of 42,824 tested isoforms, we 229 

identified 19,595 DEIs from 10,135 genes across 37 lung cell types ( Fig. 3C ), with 230 

5,719 (29.2%) being novel isoforms. Nearly all of these 10,135 genes (89.9%) were also 231 

differentially expressed in the same cell type at the gene level (adjusted p-value < 0.05, 232 

Methods ). Consistently, 50.8% of these DEIs were the most-abundant isoforms of their 233 

corresponding genes, suggesting that DEIs captured the representative isoforms of cell-234 

type signature genes. Similarly, the DEIs were well-represented in gene-level cell type 235 

markers that we used for cell type annotation, where the most abundant isoforms from 236 

74 (77.9%) markers were detected as DEIs, and 89.2% of them were known isoforms. 237

To assess the cell-type specificity of isoform signatures, we first compared the fold 239 

changes of cell-type-specific expression between isoform- and gene-levels. We found 240 

that 13,163 DEIs (67%) displayed a larger fold change than the corresponding genes in 241 

at least one cell type ( Supplementary Fig. 6A ). These data suggested that a 242 

substantial subset of isoforms are more cell-type-specific than their corresponding 243 

genes, which could be leveraged in cell type annotation as isoform-level markers. For 244 

example, caveolin-1( CAV1 ) is commonly used as an AT1 cell marker 36 but is also 245 

expressed by multiple other cell types within and outside the epithelial cell group at the 246 

gene level ( Supplementary Fig. 6B ). For the most abundant isoform, 201, this pattern 247 

also persisted ( Fig. 3D and Supplementary Fig. 6B ). Notably, we detected a novel 248 

isoform of CAV1 , TALONT003058356 , as a significant DEI, which was expressed by 249 

97.7% of individuals and demonstrated a higher specificity to AT1 ( Fig. 3D and 250 

Supplementary Fig. 6B ). This specificity was maintained at a higher sequencing 251 

coverage, indicating that the isoform specificity could not be simply attributed to the 252 

sparsity or lower expression but are more likely to reflect biological distinction 253 

(Supplementary Fig. 7A-B ). 254 

> 255

To further investigate whether isoform signatures contribute to cell type clustering and 256 

annotation, we compared clustering performance between highly variable isoform and 257 

gene signatures. Benchmarking of clustering performance for cell type annotation 258 

demonstrated that using top-ranking gene-level features outperformed an equal number 259 

of top-ranking isoforms in dataset-wide comparisons. However, top-ranking isoform 260 

features outperformed their matched genes, by gaining additional isoform information 261 

for the same set of genes ( Supplementary Fig. 8A-D ). Furthermore, at the cell-type-262 

level comparisons, 8 of 37 cell types displayed a better performance of isoform-level 263 

features ( Supplementary Fig. 8E ). These data suggested that isoform-level information 264 

for a subset of cell types could contribute to cell clustering and cell type annotation even 265 

when using gene-level ground truth based on conventional marker genes. 266 

> 267

Collectively, our data demonstrated that cell-type-specific isoforms could capture 268 

distinct cell-type differences beyond average gene expression in a subset of lung cell 269 

types. 270

> 273

performed isoQTL mapping on 33 qualifying cell types ( Methods ). To address the data 275 

sparsity, we pseudo-bulked the single-cell counts at the individual level using the same 276 

criteria as differential expression analyses ( Fig. 4A ). 277

isoQTLs reveal genetic regulation of isoforms distinct from average expression 334 

> 335

To investigate whether our isoQTLs represent unique genetic regulation of isoforms not 336 

captured in previous datasets, we first compared our isoGenes with sGenes (genes with 337 

at least one significant sQTLs) of lung tissues. While 41.1% of isoGenes were replicated 338 

at the gene level as sGenes in GTEx (v8) lung tissues (n = 515), 921 isoGenes were 339 

new to our dataset ( Fig. 5A ). Among them, 54.7% were only identified in a single cell 340 

type, suggesting that cell-type specificity not captured in bulk tissues might explain this 341 

subset. Moreover, 29.4% of the 921 isoGenes were for novel transcript isoforms, 342 

suggesting that detection of novel isoforms helped identify additional isoGenes not 343 

captured in short-read-based sQTLs ( Fig. 5B ). Additionally, we asked whether some of 344 

these 921 isoGenes were not detected in GTEx (~85.3% European) due to allele 345 

frequency differences with East Asian populations (EAS). Among 1,319 lead variants of 346 

921 isoGenes,11.9% (158/1,319) showed minor allele frequency (MAF) < 0.01 in EUR 347 

(1000 Genomes Project Phase III), which might not have been tested in the GTEx sQTL 348 

analysis, whereas this proportion was lower (7.7%, 103/1,334) in isoGenes replicated in 349 

the GTEx sGenes ( p = 0.0002987, χ2 test) ( Supplementary Table 4 ). Moreover, for the 350 

isoGenes that are matched to GTEx sGenes, 74.7% (847/1,134) of independent lead 351 

isoQTLs were not found in GTEx sQTLs and 50.5% (428/847) among them were in low 352 

LD (R 2 < 0.1) with the GTEx lead SNP or monoallelic in the EUR population ( Methods ). 353 

These data indicated that our dataset-specific isoGene findings compared to mainly-354 

European bulk sQTLs are driven by cell-type-specific, novel, or East Asian-specific 355 

isoQTLs. 356

Discussion 576 

> 577

In this study, we present an isoform-level lung cell atlas and deploy an open-access 578 

web interface, ISOLUTION, for accessing the data across cell types and individuals. 579 

From this atlas, we identified 270,536 novel transcript isoforms and characterized cell-580 

type signatures of isoform expression in lung tissues from never-smokers. Supported by 581 

the multi-layered validation including proteome profiling, cell-barcode matched short-582 

read data, and single-cell multi-omics data, our dataset provides a reference for 583 

annotating transcript isoforms and exploring isoform functions in lung cell types. The 584 

sample size of our dataset enabled the cell-type-specific isoQTL detection, and 585 

systematic comparisons with cell barcode-matched short-read eQTL dataset uncovered 586 

the independent genetic effects of isoQTLs beyond the total gene expression level. 587 

Integration of isoQTLs with lung cancer subtypes and lung function GWAS identified 588 

new susceptibility isoforms and highlighted contributions of isoQTLs to the GWAS 589 

signals distinct from eQTLs. 590 

> 591

Our study design demonstrated the feasibility of harnessing single-cell long-read RNA-592 

seq at scale to detect isoform diversity. Previous bulk tissue transcriptomes using long-593 

read sequencing reported surprisingly large proportions of novel isoforms that are 594 

tissue-specific albeit in limited sample sizes 6. A recent study of GTEx tissues observed 595 

a strong effect of ancestry on the inter-individual variation of alternative splicing and the 596 

effect of sex on expression variation 61 . Considering added technical variability by 597 

sequencing batches, our design including 129 individuals of similar genetic background, 598 

same sex, and without smoking effect, in 22 cell-type balanced batches helped reduce 599 

the dataset-wide variability for reliable isoform detection. Notably, doubling the 600 

sequencing depth enabled the detection of initially single-batch-specific isoforms in 601 

additional batches. These data, together with the peptide-level validation of novel 602 

isoforms, suggested that a substantial proportion of batch-specific novel isoforms could 603 

be further validated at a higher sequencing coverage. 604 

> 605

The isoform-level lung cell atlas allowed a systematic investigation of the lung cell type 606 

signatures of isoform expression. Single-cell full-length transcriptomes exploring isoform 607 

specificity have been limited to brain tissues 7,9,10 and tumor tissues 62 with tens of 608 

samples from human and mice. Beyond anecdotal examples 9, our systematic analysis 609 

observed isoform-level signatures showing larger cell-type differences than gene level 610 

across 37 lung cell types, including more distinct marker genes at the isoform level. 611 

Moreover, we investigated the contribution of isoform information to cell clustering and 612

Limitations of the study 700 

We recognize that our study has several limitations. First, even though we used twice 701 

the standard read-depth (2 SMRT cells) for each single-cell batch, the isoform-level 702 

expression profiles at the single-cell level are still sparse. Increasing sequencing depth 703 

up to 4 SMRT cells could reduce the sparsity to a degree, but it also proportionally 704 

increases the number of novel isoforms with lower abundance that need to be further 705 

validated. Reassuringly, we found that the cell type specificity of isoform expression did 706 

not result from the sparsity, by increasing the sequencing depth. Second, although our 707 

scale enabled us to perform QTL mapping, the isoQTL findings were limited by the 708 

detection power due to a modest sample size. Allele-specific isoform expression and 709 

alternative splicing would be worthwhile to explore more genetic effects in future 710 

studies. In terms of isoform identification, we performed in-sample proteomics validation 711 

with 11% validated at the peptide level, but the functions of both known and novel 712 

highlighted isoforms and their roles in lung biology and diseases need further 713 

experimental validation. 714
