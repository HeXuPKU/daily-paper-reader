ABSTRACT 16 

Most eQTL and TWAS analyses quantify expression using aggregate, tissue-agnostic transcript 17 

annotations and ignore isoform-level regulation, potentially obscuring or misattributing regulatory 18 

mechanisms. Here, we developed a framework leveraging publicly available long-read RNA-seq 19 

data to perform tissue-informed inference of genetic regulation and prioritize candidate causal 20 

isoforms for breast cancer risk. We quantified gene- and isoform-level expression in breast tumor 21 

(TCGA), non-cancerous mammary tissue, and cultured fibroblasts (GTEx) using three 22 

transcriptome annotations: standard GENCODE, tissue-specific long-read-derived assemblies, 23 

and combined annotations incorporating transcript-isoforms from both. While GENCODE 24 

cataloged over 250,000 pan-tissue isoforms, the tissue-specific long-read assemblies captured 25 

reduced sets of 74,717 isoforms in tumor, 48,057 in fibroblasts, and 22,941 in healthy breast. 26 

We performed eQTL mapping and fine-mapping, followed by colocalization with overall and 27 

subtype-specific breast cancer GWAS and isoform-level TWAS. While most eGenes were 28 

concordant across annotations, approximately 1/3 of lead cis-eQTLs for shared eGenes differed 29 

between long-read assemblies and GENCODE. Further, eIsoform discovery was highly 30 

annotation-specific. In healthy breast tissue, the gold standard tissue for building gene 31 

expression prediction models for TWAS of breast cancer, 46% of eIsoforms identified by the 32 

long-read annotation were unique to that annotation even though 93.7% of them are present in 33 

GENCODE. Despite combined annotations expanding the GENCODE catalog by only 0.6-7.6% 34 

depending on tissue source, 69% of unique significant isoform-trait associations were specific 35 

to a single annotation. Long-read-informed annotations uncovered regulatory associations 36 

entirely missed by GENCODE, including a candidate regulatory isoform at the MARK1 locus 37 

captured only in fibroblasts and a previously unannotated splice variant prioritized as the likely 38 

effector transcript at NUP107 . These findings demonstrate that transcript annotation is not 39 

merely a technical consideration but critically defines the biological hypothesis space for 40

INTRODUCTION 44 

Identifying the likely transcriptomic mechanisms affected by the >200 genetic variants linked 45 

to breast cancer risk remains an open challenge 1–7 . While integrative approaches such as 46 

colocalization and transcriptome-wide association studies (TWAS) help identify causal genes at 47 

breast cancer genome-wide association study (GWAS) loci, these approaches often rely on total 48 

gene expression aggregated across transcript isoforms, potentially obscuring isoform-specific 49 

regulatory effects . Indeed, modeling isoforms rather than genes has been found to increase the 50 

discovery of transcriptome-mediated genetic associations with complex traits by as much as 51 

60% 8–10 . Ignoring isoforms is a notable limitation in breast cancer, where isoform-specific 52 

antagonistic effects are well-documented in genes such as BRCA1 , ERBB2 , KLF6 , AIB1 ,53 

TNC37-42 , and ESR1 11–16 .54 

Both gene- and isoform-level integrative analyses depend upon accurate quantification of 55 

expression. Aggregate transcriptome references like GENCODE, which are often used for short-56 

read RNA-seq quantification, can obscure the tissue-specific regulatory mechanisms underlying 57 

tumorigenesis. They can contain hundreds of thousands of annotated isoforms, many of which 58 

are only weakly or not at all expressed in breast tissue 17,18 . Isoform quantification is further 59 

complicated by the limitations of short-read RNA-seq approaches that can struggle to accurately 60 

resolve transcript isoforms because of read-to-transcript mapping ambiguity 19 . When sequenced 61 

fragments align to exons shared by multiple isoforms, which is a frequent occurrence in genes 62 

with complex splicing patterns, probabilistic assignment of reads generates substantial 63 

uncertainty in isoform-level expression estimates 20 , leading to reduced statistical power in 64 

downstream integrated analyses 19 . However, recent technological advances in long-read RNA-65 

seq have enabled more comprehensive detection of full-length transcript isoforms. While short-66 

read sequencing typically produces reads under 300 bp in length, long-read RNA sequencing 67

In this study, we leveraged long-read RNA-seq-derived transcriptome references for breast-71 

relevant tissues to deliver an improved, tissue-specific view of genetic regulation at the isoform 72 

level across non-cancerous mammary tissue, malignant breast tumors, and cultured fibroblasts. 73 

We compared gene- and isoform-level expression quantitative trait loci (eQTL) mapping, 74 

colocalization, and TWAS for overall and subtype-specific breast cancer across multiple 75 

annotations and tissues. Our analyses yielded several major findings. First, although the long-76 

read transcriptome assemblies are highly specific and contain >100-200K fewer transcripts than 77 

GENCODE, they retained comparable power for mapping GWAS loci with detected eQTLs, 78 

indicating robust coverage of risk-associated loci. Second, the long-read-informed analyses 79 

revealed multiple novel isoform-level regulatory mechanisms that are undetectable with standard 80 

reference transcriptomes. Third, we demonstrated that colocalization and TWAS results are 81 

highly sensitive to even minor changes in the transcriptome assembly used to quantify short-82 

read RNA-seq samples, underscoring the importance of reference selection for downstream 83 

analyses. Finally, we provide an initial catalog of germline genetic effects on isoform-level 84 

expression across tissue states that can serve as a resource for future studies of breast cancer. 85

Long-read transcriptome assemblies greatly refine gene and isoform space 102 

We applied a robust transcriptome assembly and quantification framework 26,27 to jointly 103 

discover and quantify expression of transcript isoforms in three sets of breast cancer-relevant 104 

long-read RNA-seq libraries (Fig. 1a). Compared to the GENCODE reference transcriptome 105 

containing 252,931 transcript isoforms across 63,187 genes 28 , these de novo long-read 106 

assemblies yielded markedly lower numbers of annotated isoforms and genes, both before and 107 

following comprehensive quality control protocols. Across the three tissues, isoform counts from 108 

long-read assemblies decreased over 70% relative to GENCODE, and this corresponded to a 109 

decrease in gene counts by over 63% (Fig. 2a). We resolved the greatest number of high-110 

confidence isoforms (74,717) in our malignant breast tumor samples (N=26; primary biopsies, 111 

patient-derived xenograft tumors, and cell lines) mapping to 18,100 genes. The healthy breast 112 

samples (N=5; primary mammary tissue and cell lines) demonstrated the most pronounced 113 

transcriptome refinement, with only 22,941 high-confidence isoforms assembled across 11,015 114 

genes. 115

Long-read transcriptomes reveal alternative genetic architectures for shared regulatory 168 

features 169 

To identify regulatory genetic variants that may underlie causal breast cancer pathways, 170 

we first performed eQTL mapping in each tissue. We identified 5,827, 2,305, and 1,177 genes 171 

in fibroblasts, breast, and breast tumor, respectively, as harboring one or more significant eQTL 172 

(eGenes) that replicated across all three annotations (Fig. 3a, Online Data). These common 173 

eGenes represented over half (52.9%) of all fibroblast eGenes (identified by any annotation), 174 

31.3% of all breast eGenes, but less than 18% of tumor eGenes identified by at least one 175 

annotation. 176 

As expected, GENCODE and combined annotations showed particularly strong 177 

concordance in eGenes, with over 89% of eGenes overlapping between the two in each tissue. 178 

More notably, long-read assemblies consistently identified a substantial number of unique 179 

features not detected by either reference-inclusive annotation: 1,289 unique eGenes in 180 

fibroblasts, 954 in healthy breast, and 1,320 in tumor, accounting for 16.9%, 28.3% and 50.2% 181 

of all eGenes identified by long-read in each tissue. Fine-mapped eQTLs yielded similar patterns 182 

of cross-tissue and -annotation eGene discovery (Fig. S3). 183

Together, these results suggest that expansive reference annotations do not necessarily 248 

yield expanded insight into trait-relevant regulation. Long-read assembly-derived eQTLs tagged 249 

GWAS loci at rates largely similar to those observed with GENCODE. While most of these loci 250 

were consistent across annotations, this did not imply concordance in the underlying regulatory 251 

features. Instead, the specific eGenes and eIsoforms implicated at shared loci differed 252 

depending on the transcriptome annotation used for expression quantification. Signals relevant 253 

to breast cancer risk thus appear to be captured well by a subset of tissue-relevant isoforms 254 

reliably observed with long-read RNA-seq. These findings suggest that long-read assemblies 255 

provide a more parsimonious and tissue-informed representation of the regulatory landscape 256 

while retaining the capacity to implicate genetic loci relevant to breast cancer risk. 257

DISCUSSION 438 

In this study, we identified 556 unique isoform-level associations with breast cancer risk using 439 

three short-read RNA-seq datasets from malignant and normal breast tissues and cultured 440 

fibroblasts. We found striking differences in the association patterns depending on the transcript 441 

annotations used for expression quantification. These findings suggest that transcriptome 442 

annotations should be treated not merely as default inputs in integrative analyses of gene 443 

regulation, but rather as biologically meaningful choices that shape downstream inference. 444 

Tissue-specific long-read assemblies (1) substantially reduce the isoform space, focusing the 445 

hypothesis space for causal isoforms in the relevant tissue context, (2) avoid spurious 446 

associations with tissue-irrelevant transcripts, and (3) improve the specificity of inferred 447 

regulatory mechanisms at GWAS loci. By prioritizing empirically supported isoforms, long-read-448 

informed annotations can help resolve ambiguity at the isoform level, the most fundamental unit 449 

of transcription that remains unexplored in most integrative analyses of complex traits. 450 

TWAS have come under recent scrutiny for high false positive rates, high false sign rates, 451 

and limited replicability 33,39–41 . While the polygenicity of complex traits has been proposed as 452 

one major driver of these challenges, our current and previous findings suggest that variability 453 

in transcriptome annotations and quantification methods may also contribute substantially 33 .454 

Standard transcriptomic references such as GENCODE and Ensembl are largely tissue-agnostic 455 

and catalog hundreds of thousands of transcripts spanning protein-coding genes, long 456 

noncoding RNAs, pseudogenes, and other biotypes. This expansive isoform space continues to 457 

grow with each release and may increase the likelihood of read misassignment to lowly 458 

expressed or tissue-irrelevant transcripts. In turn, this can dilute true regulatory signals and 459 

ultimately obscure biologically relevant mechanisms. Additional critical strides must be made to 460 

better understand how annotation choice shapes downstream genetic inference. 461

24 One such critical next step is to accurately quantify how uncertainty arising from 462 

overpopulated transcript references impacts statistical power and type I error of eQTL discovery, 463 

TWAS, and colocalization studies. Our results directly motivate the development of improved 464 

simulation frameworks to address this question. Existing tools for QTL simulation rely on 465 

simplified distributional assumptions that do not capture the complexity of isoform-level RNA-466 

seq data. More realistic approaches that generate raw sequencing reads, model multiple modes 467 

of genetic regulation, and incorporate modern library preparation protocols will be needed to 468 

systematically evaluate these annotation-dependent biases in downstream analyses. 469 

While our results suggest that re-quantification using tissue-matched long-read-informed 470 

annotations provides the most precise framework for regulatory inference, this approach may 471 

not always be feasible for large legacy datasets. These constraints motivate the development of 472 

methods that preserve the benefits of annotation-aware inference while reducing the need for 473 

full RNA-seq reprocessing. Moving beyond simple associations toward causal resolution of 474 

driver isoforms will likely require isoform-level fine-mapping frameworks that jointly model the 475 

effects of individual transcripts, total gene expression, and nearby regulatory variants. We 476 

believe incorporating biologically informed priors derived from long-read evidence (e.g., 477 

structural support, splice junction validation) may further improve causal inference while 478 

reducing the need for large-scale resequencing efforts. 479 

Long-read panels remain limited by sample size, high sequencing costs, and scattered 480 

representation across tissue contexts. In this study, healthy breast tissue posed a particular 481 

challenge despite its importance for modeling germline susceptibility to breast cancer and its 482 

status as the gold-standard context in existing breast cancer TWAS. Limited long-read sample 483 

sizes can lead to incomplete transcript capture and reduced sensitivity for low-abundance 484 

isoforms, which in turn, can severely limit discovery. As publicly available long-read resources 485 

expand, combining samples across independent and diverse short-read cohorts for a given 486

METHODS 490 

Long-read transcriptome assembly construction 491 

We obtained raw long-read RNA sequencing FASTQ files via the European Genome 492 

Archive database (accession number EGAS00001004819) from Veiga et al. 18 , who generated 493 

PacBio single-molecule reads from a total of 30 breast tissue samples. These included 4 healthy 494 

breast samples (two primary tissues and two from cell lines HMEC and HS578BST) and 26 495 

breast cancer samples comprising 13 primary breast tumor biopsies (covering hormone 496 

receptor-positive, HER2+, and triple-negative subtypes), 9 patient-derived xenograft tumors, and 497 

4 breast cancer cell lines (HS578T, MCF-7, MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-468). Detailed sample 498 

characteristics (subtype, origin, receptor status, patient demographics) are available in the 499 

supplement of the original publication, where applicable 18 . We also obtained 22 long-read RNA 500 

sequencing samples from cell-cultured fibroblasts and one non-malignant breast mammary 501 

tissue sample from GTEx V9 42 . Fibroblast samples were derived from skin biopsies from the 502 

lower leg, and cell lines were cultured under standard conditions with a subset undergoing 503 

PTBP1 siRNA knockdown, high-quality RNA extraction, poly(A) selection, cDNA library 504 

preparation, and Oxford Nanopore long-read sequencing according to manufacturer protocols 505 

described elsewhere 43 . These raw long-read data served as input for the transcriptome 506 

assembly and quantification procedures described below. 507 

We converted raw FASTQ files to unmapped BAM files with samtools 44 import and 508 

processed as in Veiga et al 18 with the IsoSeq workflow 45 . Briefly, we used lima to remove New 509 

England Biolabs and Clontech adapters and to orient the reads. We used IsoSeq refine to filter 510
