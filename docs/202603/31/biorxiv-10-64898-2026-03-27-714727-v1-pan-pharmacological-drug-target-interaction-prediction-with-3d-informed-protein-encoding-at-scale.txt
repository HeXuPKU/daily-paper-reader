Abstract 16 

Accurate prediction of drug–target binding affinity across multiple 17 

pharmacological endpoints remains challenging, as most deep learning 18 

methodologies focus on a single metric and face a trade–off between 19 

incorporating structural information and computational throughput. Here we 20 

present OmniBind, a multitask framework that resolves both constraints by 21 

encoding protein tertiary structures as discrete token sequences and integrating 22 

them with amino acid sequence features through a gated fusion mechanism. 23 

Trained on over two million compound–protein pairs from BindingDB, OmniBind 24 

simultaneously predicts four pharmacological endpoints in a single forward pass, 25 

providing a pan–pharmacological profile across multiple affinity endpoints for 26 

each compound–target pair. Across adversarial and temporal benchmarks, 27 

OmniBind consistently outperforms state–of–the–art architectures, 28 

demonstrating that structural encoding captures physicochemical interaction 29 

principles rather than ligand–protein co–occurrence patterns. Attention analysis 30 

reveals biologically meaningful binding site recognition, with the model 31 

selectively weighting the ABL1 gatekeeper residue T315 and responding to its 32 

drug–resistance mutation T315I. As a demonstration of practical utility, 33 

proteome–wide screening of 20,421 human proteins recovered 85.7% of known 34 

clinical targets of clozapine within the top 200 predictions, correctly resolving its 35 

pharmacological profile despite structural similarity to clomipramine. OmniBind 36 

provides an accurate, structurally informed, and interpretable platform for multi– 37

Introduction 44 

The identification of compounds that modulate a target protein with the desired 45 

potency and selectivity lies at the heart of drug discovery. Yet fewer than 10% 46 

of candidates successfully navigate the clinical pipeline 1 despite investments 47 

exceeding 2 billion USD per approved therapeutic 2. A fundamental contributor 48 

to this attrition is the combinatorial challenge of navigating a chemical space 49 

estimated to exceed 10 ⁶⁰ molecules 3 against tens of thousands of human 50 

proteins 4. Accurately predicting binding across the full spectrum of clinically 51 

relevant metrics, from fundamental binding affinities ( 𝐾 ! and 𝐾 " ) to functional 52 

activities ( 𝐼 𝐶 #$ and 𝐸 𝐶 #$ ), is therefore essential for prioritizing compounds at the 53 

earliest stages of drug discovery 5.54 

Structure–based molecular docking has long served as a rational 55 

foundation for this task, but is fundamentally constrained by its requirement for 56 

high–resolution experimental structures and its enormous per–pair 57 

computational cost, which renders large–scale screening prohibitive 6. Deep 58 

learning has emerged as a high–throughput alternative 7, yet early architectures 59 

based on convolutional or recurrent neural networks were prone to overfitting on 60 

small, homogeneous benchmark datasets such as Davis 8 and KIBA 9, and failed 61 

to generalize to the noise and heterogeneity inherent in real–world experimental 62 

data 10 . A more fundamental limitation has persisted across both paradigms: the 63 

predominant focus on a single pharmacological metric 11,12,13,14,15 is insufficient to 64

experimental noise, and measurement principles across these metrics 77

datasets such as BindingDB 23 , which contains millions of heterogeneous 79 

interaction records, demands architectures robust to noise and chemical 80 

diversity far beyond that of curated benchmarks. No existing unified framework 81

around three interlocking design principles that together address this trilemma. 84

Results 102 

OmniBind architecture: dual–modality protein encoding and multitask 103 

drug-target interaction prediction 104 

OmniBind integrates chemical features of compounds with structural and 105 

sequential features of proteins through four modules to simultaneously predict 106 

multiple pharmacological metrics ( Figure 1 ). On the compound side, a graph 107 

convolutional neural network extracts atom–level features from molecular 108 

graphs from SMILES representations 26,27 . On the protein side, two parallel 109 

Transformer encoders independently process amino acid sequences and 3Di 110 

sequences generated by ProstT5 28 , capturing sequence–based chemical 111 

features and structure–based biophysical features, respectively. The outputs of 112 

these two encoders are integrated by a gate fusion layer 25 (see Materials and 113 

Methods) that applies learnable sigmoid gates to dynamically weight the 114 

contribution of each modality depending on the binding context. The resulting 115 

fused protein representation is combined with the compound representation and 116 

processed by a five–layer Transformer decoder, which employs cross–attention 117 

to model compound–protein interactions 29 and outputs predicted values for four 118 

pharmacological metrics ( 𝑝 𝐾 ! , 𝑝 𝐾 " , 𝑝𝐼 𝐶 #$ , 𝑝𝐸 𝐶 #$ ) in a parallel manner, which 119 

ranges from fundamental binding affinities ( 𝐾 !, 𝐾 ") to cellular efficacy ( 𝐼 𝐶 #$ , 𝐸 𝐶 #$ ). 120 

To validate the contribution of 3Di integration and the gated fusion 121 

mechanism, we performed an ablation study comparing four protein 122 

representation strategies ( Table 1 ). Single–modality models using only 3Di 123 

structural tokens or amino acid sequences achieved comparable performance, 124

Benchmarking of OmniBind under adversarial and temporal evaluation 138 

protocols 139 

To evaluate the generalization capacity of OmniBind on unseen data, we 140 

benchmarked it against state–of–the–art models 14,15 using two complementary 141 

evaluation protocols: the label reversal test 13 , which probes whether the model 142 

has learned protein–specific interaction features rather than ligand identity, and 143 

temporal validation 14 , which assesses generalization to entirely novel chemical 144 

and protein spaces by evaluating the model on interactions recorded after the 145 

training period, thereby simulating a prospective drug discovery scenario. 146

In the label reversal test, activity labels are deliberately inverted between 147 

training and test sets based on the protein to which each ligand binds ( Figure 148

predict the wrong label 13 . OmniBind achieved a mean RMSE of 1.252 ± 0.032 150 

across the four pharmacological metrics, outperforming DTI–LM 15 (RMSE = 151 

1.400 ± 0.044) and TransformerCPI2.0 14 (RMSE = 1.403 ± 0.019) ( Figure 2B ). 152 

The degraded performance of these large pre–trained models under adversarial 153 

conditions suggests that they rely substantially on statistical correlations 154 

between compound classes and protein families rather than on the underlying 155 

physicochemical principles of molecular recognition 30 .156 

In temporal validation, the model was trained on BindingDB data 23 

157 

through May 2023 and evaluated on 423,647 interaction pairs newly added to 158 

the November 2024 edition, comprising compounds and proteins entirely absent 159 

from the training set ( Figure 2C ). OmniBind achieved a mean RMSE of 1.416, 160 

outperforming all comparison models across RMSE, C–index, AUROC, and 161 

AUPRC ( Figure 2D ), consistent with the results of the label reversal test. 162 

Together, these results demonstrate that OmniBind learns 163 

physicochemically grounded interaction representations rather than memorizing 164 

dataset–specific patterns. This translates to robust predictive performance 165 

under both adversarial and prospective evaluation conditions relevant to real– 166 

world drug discovery. 167

Drug repositioning screening of FDA–approved drugs and structural 169 

validation of predicted interactions 170 

To explore the potential of FDA–approved drugs for novel therapeutic 171 

applications, we screened 1,615 FDA–approved drugs against three enzyme 172 

targets with distinct functional and clinical backgrounds: phosphodiesterase 5 173 

(PDE5) 31 , plasma kallikrein (KLKB1) 32 , and sirtuin 3 (SIRT3) 33 . These targets were 174 

selected to evaluate OmniBind's predictive versatility across diverse protein 175 

classes, spanning a well–characterized phosphodiesterase, a historically 176 

challenging serine protease 32,34 , and a relatively underexplored mitochondrial 177 

deacetylase (SIRT3) 33 . Top–ranked candidates from OmniBind screening were 178 

subsequently validated through a hierarchical pipeline integrating structure 179 

prediction by Boltz–2 18 and docking simulation using MOE 35 , yielding avanafil 36 

180 

as the most promising candidate for PDE5, glecaprevir 37 for KLKB1, and 181 

valrubicin 38 for SIRT3 ( Figure 3 ). 182 

Avanafil is a known PDE5 inhibitor 36 and was confirmed to be absent from 183 

the OmniBind training dataset, making it an external positive control. Its recovery 184 

as the top–ranked candidate for PDE5 demonstrates that OmniBind has learned 185 

physicochemically grounded features of the target rather than memorizing 186 

training data. Structural modeling places avanafil within the PDEase domain in 187 

contact with Val782, a key residue of the hydrophobic pocket, through π–188 

stacking interactions 39 (Figure 3A ). 189

Discussion 267 

OmniBind addresses three interconnected challenges in deep learning–based 268 

drug–target interaction prediction: the generalization to real–world 269 

heterogeneous data, the simultaneous prediction of multiple pharmacological 270 

metrics, and the efficient integration of protein 3D structural information without 271 

sacrificing computational throughput. Incorporating structural information into 272 

proteome–wide screening has been particularly difficult, as explicit 3D 273 

representations such as geometric graph networks require per–protein 274 

computational costs that scale prohibitively when applied to tens of thousands 275 

of targets. OmniBind overcomes this barrier by encoding protein tertiary 276 

structure as discrete 3Di token sequences 24,28 , enabling structural information to 277 

be processed at sequence–level speed and applied to all 20,421 UniProt– 278 

reviewed human proteins 51 in a single screening run. By combining this approach 279 

with large–scale training on BindingDB and multitask prediction of four 280 

pharmacological endpoints through the gate fusion layer, OmniBind consistently 281 

outperformed state–of–the–art models under adversarial and temporal 282 

benchmarks, correctly identified known major targets and known off–targets of 283 

two clinical drugs in proteome–wide screening, and produced attention patterns 284 

that reflect biologically meaningful binding site features. These results establish 285 

OmniBind as a robust and practically deployable framework for pan– 286 

pharmacological drug–target interaction prediction. 287 

A central technical contribution of OmniBind is the combination of 3Di 288 

sequence encoding and adaptive gate fusion for the integration of protein 289

chemical and protein spaces, and scalable to proteome–wide applications. By 354 

making structural information accessible at high–throughput screening scale, 355 

OmniBind provides a practical computational platform for lead optimization, off– 356 

target safety assessment, and drug repositioning. 357

Materials and Methods 358 

Dataset Construction 359 

Two editions of BindingDB 23 were used as the primary data source: May 2023 360 

for model development and November 2024 for temporal validation. BindingDB 361 

was selected because it offers the broadest coverage of experimentally 362 

measured compound–protein binding interactions among publicly available 363 

databases, encompassing diverse assay types, protein families, and chemical 364 

scaffolds, conditions that are essential for training a model intended for real– 365 

world drug discovery applications 23 . To avoid information leakage resulting from 366 

naive random splitting, we constructed two independent test sets with distinct 367 

evaluation objectives: a label reversal test set to assess ligand bias 13 , and a 368 

temporal validation set for time–based generalization assessment (see below for 369 

the details) 14 .370

ambiguity that cannot be adequately represented by sequence–based encoders. 374 

Entries were kept only if both a SMILES representation and an amino acid 375 

sequence were present, with at least one of the four pharmacological metrics 376 

(𝐾 !, 𝐾 ", 𝐼 𝐶 #$ , or 𝐸 𝐶 #$ ) recorded. Inequality signs (e.g., ">" or "<") were removed 377 

to convert activity values into numerical form, and outliers exceeding 10 7 nM 378

OmniBind Model Architecture 444 

OmniBind consists of a compound featurizer, a protein featurizer with a gate 445 

fusion layer, and a Transformer decoder that integrates both representations to 446 

predict multiple pharmacological endpoints simultaneously ( Figure 1 ). 447 

Compound Featurizer 448 

Molecular graphs are encoded using a single–layer Graph Convolutional 449 

Network (GCN) 27 , in which each atom is represented by a 35–dimensional feature 450 

vector calculated via RDKit (Supplementary Table S2 ). A virtual atom 451 

connected to all other atoms in the graph was introduced following the design 452 

of TransformerCPI2.0 14 , enabling the aggregation of global molecular context 453 

alongside local atom–level features. The number of GCN layers was fixed at one, 454 

as preliminary experiments consistent with the findings of TransformerCPI2.0 14 

455 

showed that additional layers cause over–smoothing of atomic features, 456 

degrading predictive performance. 457 

Protein Featurizer 458

independent two–layer Transformer encoders, the Protein Sequence Encoder 460 

and the Protein Structure Encoder, respectively. Each encoder layer uses 8– 461 

head multi–head self–attention with a hidden dimension of 256 and a feed– 462

Hyperparameter Tuning 496 

Hyperparameters were determined through a stepwise grid search 68 . The 497 

numbers of encoder and decoder layers were optimized first, followed by the 498

sequential strategy was adopted to reduce the combinatorial search space 500 

given computational resource constraints. To accelerate each search phase, a 501

Model training was performed using the RAdam optimizer 70 with a learning rate 507 

of 1 × 10 23 and a weight decay of 1 × 10 23. RAdam was selected over 508 

standard Adam because it rectifies the variance of the adaptive learning rate in 509 

early training stages, providing more stable convergence on large, 510 

heterogeneous datasets. Due to the memory demands of processing full 511 

protein sequences through Transformer encoders, a per–device batch size of 2 512 

was used with gradient accumulation over 64 steps, resulting in an effective 513 

batch size of 128. 514 

Because BindingDB entries do not uniformly report all four 515 

pharmacological metrics, a masking strategy was applied to the loss function to 516 

handle missing values. For each batch, the mean squared error (MSE) loss was 517 

calculated independently for each available endpoint and averaged over the 518 

number of metrics present in that batch. For instance, if a batch contains only 519 

𝐾 !, 𝐼 𝐶 #$ , and 𝐸 𝐶 #$ measurements, the loss was defined as the mean of the three 520 

corresponding MSE values. No manual weighting was applied across metrics, 521 

as differential weighting risks over–optimizing toward metrics with larger sample 522 

sizes and may impair the generality of the learned representations. 523 

Distributed training was conducted using the Horovod library 71 across 524 

eight NVIDIA A100 GPUs (40 GB each), completing 100 epochs in approximately 525 

60 hours. Convergence was confirmed by the steady decline in both training and 526 

validation RMSE and a concurrent increase in the validation C–index over the 527
