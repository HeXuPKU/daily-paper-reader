ABSTRACT 8

Rockfishes (genus Sebastes ) show extreme variation in longevity among closely related 9species, but the evolutionary history of this young radiation is highly complex. To unpack 10 these relationships and to associate genotypes with phenotypes, we quantified genealogical 11 discordance among 55 Sebastes species and implemented a phyloGWAS framework that 12 incorporates discordant gene histories into genotype–longevity association tests. We found 13 that genealogical discordance is extremely high: the inferred species tree topology diVered 14 among several ILS-aware methods, with most internal branches having low concordance 15 factors regardless of which method was used. Nevertheless, some phylogenetic structure 16 was shared by all inferred species trees. We used simulations to assess the statistical 17 properties of phyloGWAS applied to complex traits using diVerent genetic relatedness 18 matrices (GRMs) and under varying levels of discordance. Adding an accurate GRM reduced 19 false positives relative to a model without relatedness, but GRMs only modestly increased 20 power to detect true positives. Using multiple approaches on the Sebastes data, phyloGWAS 21 identified several variants associated with longevity. Our results indicate that extreme 22 genealogical discordance is a core feature of Sebastes evolution and that phyloGWAS can 23 help in connecting genotype to phenotype under these conditions. 24

INTRODUCTION 25 

Rockfishes in the genus Sebastes are an exceptional system for studying the evolution 26 of life-history traits. Despite being a relatively recent radiation of approximately 100 species 27 in the northern Pacific Ocean, lineages span more than an order of magnitude in maximum 28 lifespan, from 10 years to 200 years (Love et al. 2002; Mangel et al. 2007). As a result of this 29 remarkable diversity in lifespan, rockfishes have become a model system for this life-history 30 trait and its determinants. In order to understand both the genetic and ecological factors 31 underlying lifespan, a clear understanding of the phylogenetic relationships among the 32 rockfishes is necessary (cf. Felsenstein 1985). 33 The rockfish radiation is estimated to have begun only 8-10 million years ago (Hyde and 34 Vetter 2007; Kolora et al. 2021), though some estimates place the base of the radiation much 35 more recently (Johns and Avise 1998). Regardless of the exact timing, the large number of 36 species and speciation events that followed means that the time between speciation events 37 will be very short. Given the long generation times and moderate eVective population sizes 38 of rockfishes (Gomez-Uchida and Banks 2006), these short internode distances will be 39 exacerbated, resulting in a large amount of incomplete lineage sorting (ILS) and gene tree-40 species tree discordance among loci (Doyle 1992; Maddison 1997; Degnan and Rosenberg 41 2009). Although previous studies of Sebastes have used multiple genes or markers to 42 construct a species tree (Kolora et al. 2021; Wallace et al. 2022; Treaster et al. 2023), none 43 has quantified the amount of genealogical discordance in this clade. 44 Estimating and accommodating gene tree discordance is important in phylogenetics, 45 especially when trying to understand the evolutionary history of genes and traits (like 46 lifespan). If one uses only a single, fixed species tree in comparative analyses, then all genes 47 and traits are forced to follow the species tree (Hahn and Nakhleh 2016). However, when 48 many gene trees do not match the species tree, then standard phylogenetic methods that 49 use only a single tree can be misled about the number of times a trait has evolved, the 50 direction of evolution, the rate of evolution, and the timing of trait transitions (Guerrero and 51 Hahn 2018; Mendes et al. 2018; Hibbins et al. 2020, 2023; Adams et al. 2025; Schraiber et 52 al. 2024; Mo and Hahn 2025). For instance, there are now multiple examples of ancestrally 53 polymorphic traits that are diVerentially fixed among descendant lineages (e.g. Fontaine et 54 al. 2015; Lamichhaney et al. 2016; Li et al. 2016; Pease et al. 2016; Han et al. 2017; Palesch 55 et al. 2018; Wu et al. 2018; Louis et al. 2021; Feng et al. 2022). Using only a single species 56 tree and ignoring gene tree discordance would incorrectly lead to the conclusion that such 57 traits had arisen convergently multiple times. 58 Here, we investigate species relationships and genealogical discordance in Sebastes .59 We construct species trees for Sebastes with three diVerent methods—all of which are 60

High discordance across rockfishes 113 

Given the rapid radiation of a large number of rockfish species in a short time period, 114 we expect to see a large amount of genealogical discordance. Indeed, there was extensive 115 topological conflict in the Sebastes species tree (Figure 2). We first quantified site 116 concordance factors (sCFs) on the CASTER tree (Figure 2a). sCFs measure the fraction of 117 informative nucleotide sites for a single branch that support the species tree, while the two 118 site discordance factors (sDF1 and sDF2) represent the fraction of sites supporting either of 119 the two alternative topologies that are one nearest-neighbor interchange away for the same 120 branch (Minh et al. 2020b; Mo et al. 2023). Across all branches of the Sebastes tree, sCFs 121 are very low (median 36.1%, IQR 16.3%; mean 40.2%; Supplementary Figure 2a). Over one-122 third of internal branches (18 of 53) fall below 33.3%, and only one branch exceeds 75% 123 (Supplementary Figure 2a). For reference, a completely unresolved star tree should have an 124 sCF of 33.3%; values below this indicate that the topological resolution present in the 125 species tree is not the single most common resolution among individually informative sites. 126 If we use any of the other possible species tree topologies (ASTRAL, SVDquartets, or 127 Kolora et al.), we get comparable sCF values (Supplementary Figure 2a). The mean and 128 median sCF across branches are largely the same using these trees as the species tree, 129 though often there are a few more branches with higher sCFs and many more branches with 130 lower sCFs. Genotype –phenotype associations with phyloGWAS 149 

Longevity varies widely within the Sebastes groups, ranging from 12 to 205 years (Figure 150 3). A few attempts have been made to understand the ecological and genetic basis for this 151 variation in longevity using phylogenetic comparative methods, but in each case a single 152 species tree has been assumed to be the correct model for trait evolution (Mangel et al. 2007; 153 Kolora et al. 2021; Treaster et al. 2023). However, the results above suggest that this trait (or 154 the sub-traits that together determine longevity) may not follow a single tree. Indeed, testing 155 for phylogenetic signal for lifespan on the CASTER topology using Pagel’s 𝜆 (Pagel 1999) 156 found no significant signal ( 𝜆 = 0.62 ; likelihood ratio test = 1.25, P = 0.26). This is to be 157 expected for complex traits evolving on trees with high discordance (Mendes et al. 2018). 158 Therefore, in order to find genetic variation associated with longevity, we used a 159 phylogenetic genome-wide association study (phyloGWAS) approach (Pease et al. 2016; Wu 160 et al. 2018). PhyloGWAS attempts to identify variants associated with a trait, and it does so 161 without assuming that a single species tree describes relationships at every site. We 162 assembled biallelic nonsynonymous sites across 52 Sebastes with lifespan records (Figure 163 3). After filtering (see Materials and Methods), 33,716 sites from 957 genes could be tested 164 for associations. 165

The tree-based GRM resolves oV-diagonal structure more finely than the 173 variant matrix-based one, reflecting the larger number of loci contributing to its estimate 174 (Figure 4a,b). 175 Before applying these approaches to data, we carried out simulations to examine the 176 performance of phyloGWAS on quantitative traits; previous studies had only used discrete 177 traits (Pease et al. 2016; Wu et al. 2018). At low discordance, the 181 linear model without a GRM showed inflated FPRs because it does not account for shared 182 history at all. Adding any GRM reduced this inflation by about 41%. At a nominal α = 0.05, power reached up to 0.40, 187 but declined as α decreased. DISCUSSION 204 

Genealogical discordance has become the norm in phylogenetic studies, with 205 evidence for biological discordance due to both ILS and introgression in many diVerent 206 systems at many diVerent times in the past (e.g. Pollard et al. 2006; White et al. 2009; Copetti 207 et al. 2017; Rouard et al. 2018; Hime et al. 2021; Morales-Briones et al. 2021). Here, we found 208 extensive genealogical discordance in Sebastes rockfishes. While it is quite diVicult to 209 compare the amount of discordance among systems with a single measure, as in several 210 other published clades (e.g. Jarvis et al. 2014; Pease et al. 2016; Sun et al. 2021; Larson et 211 al. 2025) there is no gene tree inferred here that matches the inferred species tree (any of 212 them) at every node. 213 Despite extreme discordance, we expected to be able to infer a single, well-supported 214 species tree—these are not mutually exclusive possibilities. However, none of the three 215 methods used here agreed completely, with ~25% of all branches diVering among methods 216 (Figure 1). It is not immediately clear to us whether there is a systematic reason the methods 217 diVer, nor which one represents (or comes closest to representing) the “true” species tree. 218 All three methods are intended to avoid the problems of ILS, and in the limit of infinite data 219 do avoid these problems (Mirarab et al. 2014; Chifman and Kubatko 2014; Zhang et al. 2025); 220 ASTRAL should also not be aVected by the presence of pseudoorthologous genes (Smith and 221 Hahn 2022; Yan et al. 2022). Two of the methods, SVDquartets and CASTER, combine 222 nucleotide sites, while the third method, ASTRAL, combines individual gene trees: this 223 underlying diVerence in the data structure does not seem to lead to a clear distinction in 224 outcomes. While many of the diVerences between methods represent rearrangements 225 around just a single internal branch—and thus could be considered more minor—some 226 rearrangements are much more severe (Supplementary Figure 1). For the minor 227 rearrangements, it may be the internal branches with little support one way or another are 228 simply resolved randomly by each method, as none will output a polytomy. These observed 229 diVerences may then represent these random outcomes. 230 Although many ILS-aware methods have theoretical guarantees if discordance is due 231 only to ILS, there are no such guarantees if introgression is also acting. Indeed, even minimal 232 amounts of introgression can change which topology is most frequent, especially when 233 internal branch lengths are short (Hibbins and Hahn 2024). We have not attempted to detect 234 introgression here due to the uncertainty associated with the species tree topology. While 235 some approaches can take such uncertainty into account (Beckman et al. 2018; Pease 2018), 236 these are best-suited to smaller trees with a smaller number of tips. Regardless, previous 237 studies have reported signatures of admixture among rockfishes (Schwenke et al. 2018; 238

As mentioned in the Introduction, many inferences 243 about the number and direction of evolutionary transitions will be misled in the presence of 244 discordance. These artifacts will aVect both discrete and continuous traits, and can even 245 lead to false signals of accelerated rates of DNA sequence evolution (Mendes and Hahn 246 2016). In order to detect gene- or locus-wise rate changes, especially in a rapid radiation, 247 one should use the appropriate local gene tree topology rather than the species tree 248 topology (e.g. Yan et al. 2023). 249 While discordance causes many problems for comparative methods, it also oVers the 250 opportunity to uncover the genetic basis for traits (Smith et al. 2020). This occurs because 251 discordance decouples the shared species history from each locus history, in eVect 252 randomizing genetic backgrounds. The approach used here, phyloGWAS, takes advantage 253 of discordance to associate genetic variation with phenotypic variation. Although it had been 254 used previously on discrete traits (Pease et al. 2016; Wu et al. 2018), here we apply 255 phyloGWAS to a continuous trait, longevity. We used simulations to explore the power of 256 phyloGWAS applied to continuous traits under diVerent levels of discordance. While this 257 approach does not increase power to detect true associations, it greatly reduced false 258 positives compared with a linear model without relatedness. The reduction in false positives 259 is exactly the advantage discordance provides. For instance, in the case of no discordance 260 at all, the true mutational change(s) would be associated with the branch of the species tree 261 common to all species carrying the phenotype of interest, but so would all other 262 substitutions shared by those species, whether or not they were functional. Discordance 263 can cause disparate species (i.e. not those sharing a single common ancestor in the species 264 tree) to share a trait, thereby helping to identify functional genetic variation. 265 The approach to phyloGWAS used here employs a genetic relatedness matrix (GRM) in 266 a linear mixed model (LMM) to accommodate phylogenetic structure and discordance. 267 While previous studies have used a GRM in some phylogenetic comparative methods 268 (Hibbins et al. 2023), Schraiber et al. (2024) showed that they could be used in a phylogenetic 269 context in a similar way to standard GWAS. These approaches are useful in standard GWAS 270 when there is population stratification, or other kinds of internal population structuring 271 (Zhou and Stephens 2012). Genotype –phenotype association analyses 319 

We used maximum lifespan as the phenotype for 52 Sebastes species . Three species 320 (S. minor, S. inermis , and S. steindachneri ) were excluded due to missing phenotypes. For 321 genotypes, coding sequences were filtered to retain loci whose lengths were divisible by 322 three and with zero stop codons per sequence. Filtered nucleotide MSAs were translated 323 into amino acid alignments. Biallelic nonsynonymous sites were retained and encoded in 324 BIMBAM format: major allele = 0, minor allele = 2, and missing genotypes = “NA”. Variants 325 were selected with per-site missingness of less than 10% and a minor allele frequency 326 greater than 5%. 327 To account for phylogenetic relationships in phyloGWAS, two GRMs were constructed: 328 (i) A tree-based variance–covariance matrix, 𝐶 ∗, was derived from gene trees represented in 329 substitution units across 9706 loci (Hibbins et al., 2023). (ii) A genotype-based GRM, 𝐶 ""! ,330 directly from the variant matrix inferred above (Zhou and Stephens 2012). Both GRMs were 331 computed on the same set of 52 species. 332 Association tests were performed with linear mixed models in GEMMA (Zhou and 333 Stephens 2012) using each GRM in turn. A linear model (no GRM) was also fit for comparison. 334 

P-values from GEMMA likelihood-ratio tests were evaluated at a threshold of 1×10 -5 across 335 sites. 336

Trait 353 contributions were summed across all causal loci for each species. Finally, we added an 354 environmental noise term drawn from a normal distribution with mean zero and variance 355
