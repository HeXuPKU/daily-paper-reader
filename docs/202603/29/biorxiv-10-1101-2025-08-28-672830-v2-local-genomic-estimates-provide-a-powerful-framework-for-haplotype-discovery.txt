Running title: Local genomic estimates for haplotype discovery 8

Abstract 9

Quantitative trait loci (QTL) discovery studies on diversity panels or breeding populations typically 10 

use genome -wide association studies (GWAS) to estimate marker effects.  For plant and animal 11 

breeding applications, researchers increasingly recognize the potential benefits of identifying superior 12 

haplotypes (markers in linkage disequilibrium ; LD) rather than relying on single markers, as 13 

traditional approaches inefficiently account for cumulative signals from incomplete LD with QTL or 14 

split effects when multiple markers are in high LD with QTL. Using the genomic prediction 15 

framework, the local genomic estimated breeding values (localGEBV) method was developed in 16 

animal breeding and has been adopted in crop haplotype mapping studies; however, no study has 17 

thoroughly quantified the utility of this method or systematically compared outcomes to traditional 18 

GWAS approaches. Here, we characterized a strategy to group markers in chromosomal segments 19 

based on LD (haplotype blocks or haploblocks), computed localGEBV as a linear contrast of marker 20 

effects within each haploblock, and utili sed the variance of localGEBV to enhance QTL discovery 21 

compared to traditional GWAS. Marker effects for localGEBV were estimated with ridge -regression 22 

best linear unbiased prediction (rrBLUP) and BayesR, with results compared to two common GWAS 23 

approaches. Using the barley row -type trait, we demonstrated that localGEBV improved QTL 24 

discovery and phenotypic prediction compared to single markers. Furthermore, localGEBV results 25 

were robust to the choice of prior marker assumptions and blocking parameters, enabling flexibility in 26 

fine or broad -scale QTL mapping. Overall, our findings establish localGEBV as a haplotype -based 27 

strategy capable of leveraging localized genomic effects to improve QTL discovery and , potentially ,28 

genomic selection. 29 

Key words: local GEBV, linkage disequilibrium , GWAS, genomic selection, haplotype, haploblock 30

Introduction 31 

Understanding the genetic basis of trait variation is fundamental to advancing crop and 32 

livestock improvement programs. Identifying quantitative trait loci (QTL ) or genomic regions 33 

harbouring mutations that contribute to trait variation provides crucial insights into genetic 34 

architecture and enables targeted breeding strategies. Traditional genome -wide association 35 

studies (GWAS) detect QTL by testing associations between individual genetic markers and 36 

phenotypes using linear regression, while modern approaches utilize linear mixed models, 37 

Bayesian hierarchical models, or multi -stage methods. A significant association indicates the 38 

marker is in linkage disequilibrium (LD) with a causal mutation. 39 

The single -marker approach proves effective in populations with limited LD, such as human 40 

populations with large effective population sizes. However, this approach lacks precision in 41 

populations characterized by more extensive LD due to small effective population sizes – a42 

common feature of crop and livestock populations. In these populations, markers that are in 43 

LD often split the QTL effect when estimated simultaneously, and individual markers may 44 

capture only fragments of the total QTL effect through imperfect LD relationships 45 

(Meuwissen and Goddard, 2000; Marchini et al., 2004; Goddard, 2009; Zhang et al., 2010) .46 

This dilution of signal across multiple correlated markers reduces the power to detect causal 47 

variants. 48 

To address these limitations, chromosome segments in LD can be leveraged through 49 

haplotype -based approaches that capture the collective signal across multiple linked markers 50 

(Meuwissen and Goddard, 2000; The Bovine HapMap Consortium et al., 2009; Comadran et 51 

al., 2011; Joukhadar et al., 2017) . These methods group markers into haplotype blocks ,52 

typically defined as LD -based chromosomal segments (LD blocks or haploblocks) , and 53 

analyse the combined effects within each block. One promising haplotype -based method 54 

utilizes the variance of local genomic estimated breeding values (localGEBV), which 55 

represents the variance of haplotype effects within a block. This approach has demonstrated 56 

the ability to detect QTL where causal mutations are known in livestock populations (Fan et 57 

al., 2011; Kemper et al., 2015; Van Den Berg et al., 2019; Xiang et al., 2021) . Unlike 58 

traditional GWAS that tests markers individually, the localGEBV approach , derived from 59 

genomic prediction methods , begins with fitting all single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) 60 

simultaneously (Meuwissen et al., 2001) , counteracting shrinkage effects and the dilution of 61 

marker effects when multiple markers tag the same QTL. Theoretical advantages of the 62 

localGEBV approach include reduced susceptibility to false positives from population 63

stemming from the simultaneous fitting of all SNP rather than individual testing (Yang et al., 65

The localGEBV method has also shown promise in crop applications. Voss -Fels et al. (2019) 67 

first applied this approach in wheat to identify beneficial haplotypes for yield, though their 68 

focus was on breeding program improvements rather than QTL discovery methodology. 69 

Others have applied the same method to identify haplotypes important for disease resistance 70 

in barley and wheat (Tong et al., 2024; Roy et al., 2025; Vo Van -Zivkovic et al., 2025) , stay -71 

green and root traits in barley (Brunner et al., 2024; Alahmad et al., 2025; Aldiss et al., 2025) ,72 

and chickpea seed weight, flowering time, and plant height (Varshney et al., 2021; Akinlade 73 

et al., 2025) .74 

Despite these promising developments, critical knowledge gaps limit our understanding and 75 

application of haplotype -based QTL detection. No direct comparison between localGEBV 76 

and standard GWAS approaches has been conducted to empirically evaluate their relative 77 

performance. Previous applications in QTL discovery in livestock have relied on sliding 78 

window approaches , whereas in crop species, haplotype discovery focused on biologically 79 

meaningful LD -based block s. However, the effect of fundamental methodological aspects, 80 

including optimal marker effect priors and blocking parameters, remain poorly characterized 81 

for QTL discovery .82 

Here, we address these gaps through comprehensive evaluation of the localGEBV approach 83 

using a barley global diversity panel genotyped with 40K SNP, focusing on spike row 84 

morphology , a trait with complex genetic architecture distinguishing 2 -row from 6 -row 85 

varieties. We construct LD -based haploblocks, investigate localGEBV estimation using ridge 86 

regression best linear unbiased prediction (rrBLUP) and BayesR to account for different 87 

marker effect priors  (Meuwissen et al., 2001; Erbe et al., 2012) , and directly compare QTL 88 

detection capabilities against established multi -locus GWAS methods (FarmCPU and 89 

BLINK). Our findings demonstrate that haplotype -based localGEBV offers superior precision 90 

and power for QTL discovery in populations with extensive LD by detecting multiple known 91 

QTL compared to modern GWAS methods, providing a robust framework applicable across 92 

plant and animal breeding programs. 93

Barley accessions display distinct genetic differentiation for row -type phenotypes 95 

Principal component analysis revealed substantial genetic differentiation among the barley 96 

accessions, with PC1 and PC2 explaining 30.4% and 14.4% of variation, respectively. K-97 

means clustering identified three main genetic clusters (Figure 1A): Cluster 1 (313) 98 

comprised predominantly 2 -row accessions, while Clusters 2 (297) and 3 (180) were 99 

primarily composed of 6 -row accessions (Figure 1B and 1D ). 100 

LD analysis demonstrated high initial LD ( 𝑟 2 = 0.74 ) within distances <0.1 Mbp, followed 101 

by rapid decay to  𝑟 2 = 0.19  at 1 Mbp, with subsequent gradual decay thereafter (Figure 1C). 102 

LD -based haploblock construction reveals diverse genomic segmentation patterns 103 

The construction of chromosome segments using varying LD parameters generated distinct 104 

haploblock configurations. Relaxed parameters (lower  𝑟 2 threshold and higher marker 105 

tolerance , 𝑡𝑜𝑙 , which allowed some markers in the block to not meet the LD threshold )106 

generated fewer haploblocks total with more markers per haploblock , while stringent 107 

parameters (higher  𝑟 2 threshold, lower marker tolerance) produced more numerous 108 

haploblocks that were predominantly single -marker blocks (Supplementary Figure 1). For 109 

instance, at  𝑟 2 = 0.1 with zero marker tolerance, 3,738 haploblocks (447,691 possible 110 

haplotypes) were constructed, with 60.27% comprising single SNP markers. Increasing the  𝑟 2

111 

threshold to 0.5 resulted in 6,781 haploblocks (103,857 possible haplotypes), where 73.72% 112 

consisted of single SNP markers. 113 

Increasing marker tolerance substantially reduced haploblock count while expanding block 114 

size and possible haplotype numbers. At  𝑟 2 = 0.1 with marker  𝑡𝑜𝑙 = 3, only 1,096 115 

haploblocks were constructed (2,890,043 possible haplotypes). The largest observed 116 

haploblock (364 SNP markers spanning 299 Mbp on chromosome 5H) contained 374,192 117 

possible haplotypes. 118

Validation of localGEBV approach for mapping VRS1 gene in chromosome 2H 133 

To determine whether haploblock variance was influenced by segment size, we applied a 134 

generalized additive linear regression model , which revealed no meaningful linear 135 

relationship between haploblock variance and block size ( number of markers;  𝑅 2 = 0.07 , P <136 

2e -16 ; Figure 2 , Supplementary Figure 3 ). Instead, a significant and meaningful relationship 137 

was observed between haploblock variance and haplotype effects ( 𝑅 2 = 0.48 , P < 2e -16). 138 

We validated the localGEBV approach by examining the well -characterized VRS1 gene on 139 

chromosome 2H, which determines row -type phenotype (Komatsuda et al., 2007) . Marker 140 

effect estimation using rrBLUP showed relatively small effects distributed across the 141 

chromosome, with modest increase in the VRS1 region (Supplementary Figure 2). In contrast, 142 

BayesR attributed larger effects to SNP collocated with VRS1 compared to surrounding 143 

markers. Analysis of localGEBV variance across different LD parameters consistently 144 

identified haploblocks peaking at the VRS1 region (Figure 3A). Both rrBLUP and BayesR 145 

detected maximum variance at  𝑟 2 = 0.1 and marker tolerance , 𝑡𝑜𝑙 = 3, co -locating with 146 

VRS1 and with localGEBV variance of 0. 00 81 and 0.0 133, respectively (Figure 3A ; Table 1 ).147 

Closer examination of the highest variance haploblock in chromosome 2H identified 148 

progressive refinement from 49 SNP spanning 10.23 Mbp at  𝑟 2 = 0.1 to 4 SNP spanning 149 

0.88 Mbp at  𝑟 2 = 0.5 (Figure 3B) , denoting the trade -off between flexibility of block 150 

discovery power and fine -mapping resolution with choice in blocking parameters . BayesR 151 

consistently produced higher variance estimates at the VRS1 region compared to rrBLUP, 152 

likely due to its heterogeneous variance assumptions allowing larger effects for SNP in high 153 

LD with QTL. Additionally, rrBLUP identified dispersed regions of additional high variance 154 

haploblocks in chromosome 2H, particularly within 231.42 to 431.25 Mbp when using 155 

relaxed LD parameters. These regions diminished in BayesR at higher LD thresholds, 156 

consistent with its assumption that most SNP have no effect on the trait (Supplementary 157 

Figure 2). 158

Haploblocks demonstrate superior predictability compared to single markers 229 

Using a classical linear model and generalized linear model framework, we aimed to 230 

demonstrate that the discrete, categorical haplotype configurations (i.e., discretised 231 

localGEBV values ) had greater ability to predict row -type phenotype compared to discrete, 232 

categorical single marker genotype s. We first conducted principal component analysis on the 233 

marker effects from each method of estimating marker effects and conducting GWAS 234 

(FarmCPU, BLINK, rrBLUP, and Bayes R). Principal component analysis of marker effect 235 

estimates from all methods showed PC1 and PC2 explaining 73.93% of cumulative explained 236 

variance, with marker 2H:AVRIG03047 contributing most substantially (17.49%), followed 237 

by 4H:AVRIG07945 (5.64%; Figure 5A). Marker 2H:AVRIG03047 was part of the 238 

haploblock collocating with the VRS1 gene and identified as significant by all GWAS and 239 

haploblocking strategies (Table 1), whereas marker 4H: AVRIG07945 was only identified in 240 

haploblock b000636 using BayesR marker effects .241 

We further evaluated the regression of row -spike status on SNP genotype s or localGEBV 242 

haplotype configurations fit as categorical effects for row -spike phenotype prediction 243 

accuracy utilising both a linear probability model (LPM) and logistic regression in a 244 

generalized linear model (GLM) framework. Configurations were fit as categorical predictors 245 

to capture non -addi tive effects and to avoid utilizing the localGEBV and marker effect values 246 

trained on the whole population. This ensured that the predicted effects were specific to the 247 

sampled training set. The SNP -only model (m AVRIG03047) used the single most significan t248 

marker identified in the FarmCPU/BLINK, while t he other two models (rrBLUP .b000235 and 249 

BayesR.b00023 5), incorporate information from haplotype s within the 2H:b000235 (𝑟 2 =250 

0.1,  𝑡𝑜𝑙  = 3 ) simultaneously. The a verage of the f ive -fold cross -validation correlations 251 

between the predicted phenotype and actual phenotype in the test set were consistently higher 252 

for both significant markers and the high est variance haploblock compared to randomly 253 

selected markers or haploblocks (Figure 5B). Notably, both rrBLUP and BayesR achieved 254 

higher correlation values when using haploblock haplotype configurations as predictors 255 

(LPM = 0. 88 , GLM = 0.8 1; Root Mean Squared Error; RMSE LPM = 0. 07 , GLM = 1.78)256

downwards biased estimate because of the violation of ordinary least squares assumptions 259 

and should not be utilised to justify a better model fit than the logistic regression model. 260

Haplotype analysis reveals distinctive features of localGEBV 261 

We explored the relationship between haploblock variance and the effect of individual 262 

haplotypes (Figure 6). In general, as the magnitude of a localGEBV increased, the variance of 263 

the corresponding block was greater (Figure 6, Supplementary Figure 3). 264 

Detailed haplotype profiling of haploblock 2H:b000235 ( VRS1 region) at higher LD 265 

resolution ( 𝑟 ² = 0.5, 𝑡𝑜𝑙 = 3) revealed 4 SNP markers spanning 0.88 Mbp (651.54 to 266 

652.42 Mbp) with varying allele frequencies (Figure 7B). From these markers, 30 haplotypes 267 

were identified, with 13 occurring at frequencies ≥5 accessions. Two predominant haplotypes 268 

emerged as complete allelic reversals: hap1 (TTTT) associated primarily with 2 -row 269 

phenotype (252) and hap2 (AAAA) with 6 -row phenotype (239). 270 

Haplotype network analysis demonstrated that the direction of haplotype effects aligned with 271 

phenotypic expression: negative effects for hap1 is associated with 2-row and positive effects 272 

for hap2 is linked with 6-row (Figures 6, 7C); this aligns with the regression analysis in 273 

accurately predicting 2 -row or 6 -row phenotypes utilising the same haplotype effects (Figure 274 

5B). Hierarchical clustering based on similarity matrix scores differentiated distinct clusters 275 

corresponding to haplotype -phenotype associations , wherein Australian Grains Genebank 276 

(AGG) accessions carrying hap1 had 2-row spikes while AGG accessions with hap2 had 6-277 

row spikes (Figure 7D). 278

Discussion 327 

In this study, we demonstrated that localGEBV offers substantial improvements in QTL 328 

discovery compared to standard GWAS methods. By integrating LD information with 329 

genomic prediction models, the framework effectively partitions the genome into disjoint ed 330 

chromosome segments with estimated haplotype effects (localGEBV ) and haploblock 331 

variances . This allowed us to leverage local information in the genome enabling more 332 

comprehensive detection of genetic loci controlling complex traits. Furthermore, we assessed 333 

the impact different marker tolerance and LD threshold blocking parameters had on discovery 334 

to provide a general guideline on how to conduct blocking. 335 

Biological complexity of barley row -type phenotype as a model for QTL discovery 336 

The barley row -type phenotype represents an ideal model for testing QTL discovery methods 337 

due to its simple phenotype and semi -complex genetic architecture (Bull, 2015) . The 338 

morphological distinction between 2 -row and 6 -row phenotypes is determined by the fertility 339 

status of lateral spikelets. In 2 -row barley, only central spikelets are fertile and develop 340 

grains, while lateral spikelets remain sterile. In contrast, 6 -row barley exhibits fertility in both 341 

central and lateral spikelets, producing more grains per spike and significantly influencing 342 

yield component traits (Youssef et al., 2017) such as thousand kernel weight and kernel 343 

number. 344 

The genetic architecture underlying this trait involves a network of co -regulated genes ;345 

however, there is still little information on the exact mechanism (Zwirek et al., 2019) . While 346 

VRS1 on chromosome 2H serves as the most downstream genetic component (Komatsuda et 347 

al., 2007) , several other characterised genes regulate lateral spikelet fertility and overall spike 348 

morphology, including VRS2 in 5H (Youssef et al., 2017) , VRS3 (syn. INTERMEDIUM -A) in 349 

1H (Van Esse et al., 2017; Bull et al., 2017) , VRS4 (syn. INTERMEDIUM -E) in 3H (Koppolu 350 

et al., 2013; Koppolu et al., 2022) , and VRS5 (syn. INTERMEDIUM -C) in 4H (Ramsay et al., 351 

2011) . Contemporary 6 -row barley cultivars typically contain natural recessive vrs1 and vrs5 352 

alleles (Zwirek et al., 2019) .353 

Limitations of standard GWAS approaches 354 

Various GWAS methods remain the predominant method for QTL discovery and genetic 355 

architecture analysis but face significant challenges including spurious associations , large 356 

background noise, and limited power to explain complex trait variants (Wang and Xu, 2019) .357

QTL effect if they are only partially correlated with the QTL (Meuwissen and Goddard, 2000; 372 

Marchini et al., 2004; Goddard, 2009; Zhang et al., 2010) .373

recover any split signal like the localGEBV method would. However, w e employed a similar 384 

approach in a GWAS framework to compute the orthogonal amount of information each 385 

block contained to validate the use of haploblock variance as a useful metric in breeding and 386 

QTL discovery independent of GWAS . Utilizing this same approach, we were able to 387 

increase our power in a GWAS framework as evidence d by the detection of known QTL 388

Methodological advantages of localGEBV 412 

In contrast to standard GWAS, the localGEBV approach demonstrated superior detection 413 

power for known genes associated with barley spike row phenotype. This is because the 414 

localGEBV haploblocking method prevents overcorrect ing p-values as it accounts for non -415

et al., 2008 methods) . The localGEBV method also collates genomic signals that are truly 417 

dispersed because of LD in small to moderate effect loci , which maximizes power and 418 

simultaneously reduced background noise . This was especially true under the infinitesimal 419 

assumptions of rrBLUP, which tended to disperse the signal (e.g., Supplementary Figure 2). 420 

Thus , we likely controlled the number of false negatives by collating dispersed QTL signals 421 

when accounting for LD structure and preventing the overcorrection of p -values. We 422

A key advantage of the local GEBV approach beyond statistical power and accurately 456 

defining QTL regions is its flexibility in accommodating different genomic prediction models 457 

to estimate haplotype effects, as exemplified in Figure 4 and 5B. BayesR demonstrated 458 

exceptional precision in estimating large -effect QTL, particularly visible in the VRS1 region. 459 

This is not surprising as it aligns with the major role of VRS1 in the development of the six -460 

rowed spike phenotype controlled by a single recessive allele ( vrs1 ), in contrast to the 461 

dominant allele for the two -rowed phenotype (Komatsuda et al., 2007) . This advantage stems 462 

from the model's ability to accommodate various assumptions in estimating marker effects 463 

(Erbe et al., 2012; Moser et al., 2015; Wolc and Dekkers, 2022) , leading to improved 464 

genome -wide QTL discovery (Kemper et al., 2015; Van Den Berg et al., 2019; Xiang et al., 465 

2021) . Conversely, rrBLUP showed advantages in detecting candi date regions with multiple 466 

small -effect alleles that cumulatively form a significant signal (Figure 4 , Supplementary 467 

Figure s 2 and 4 ) and is suggestive of greater applicability for most quantitative traits with 468 

near -infinitesimal genetic architecture . However, these candidate regions could not be 469 

explicitly verified , because they were not known QTL , and thus more work is needed to 470 

determine if rrBLUP truly had greater power than BayesR for small to moderate effect QTL .471 

This model flexibility allows application without prior knowledge of the trait's genetic 472 

architecture, making it suitable for diverse breeding scenarios. For instance, this is evident in 473 

the nearby VRS1 region, where rrBLUP captured higher variance blocks (2H:b000210 ; Figure 474 

3, Supplementary Figure 2). This region was not found to co -locate with known genes or 475 

QTL, suggesting it could represent a potentially new QTL associated with row -type and 476 

requires further investigation to determine if it is a new QTL . Thus, our results indicate that 477 

the localGEBV method can capture different distributions of marker effects by utilising the 478 

infinitesimal assumptions found in rrBLUP .479 

LD parameters and genetic implications 480 

The precision of QTL discovery using localGEBV is directly influenced by LD structure 481 

within the population. Indirectly, it is also based on marker positions. While we had a high -482 

quality reference map, it may be prudent to ignore marker positions and use the LD patterns 483 

(genetic map) to form haploblocks if the reference is poor ly constructed . It is also worth 484 

noting that we utilized genome -wide blocking parameters, but parameters can also be defined 485 

at the chromosomal level. 486

Our analysis determined that while varying LD parameters affected haploblock size, they did 487

This finding aligns with previous studies concluding that no 'universal' or optimal haploblock 489 

construction method applied to breeding programs exists (Andrade et al., 2019; Difabachew 490 

et al., 2023; Weber et al., 2023) . Stringent LD ( 𝑟 2 = 0.5) produced smaller haploblocks 491 

(often comprised of a single marker) enabling fine mapping of specific genes , while relaxed 492 

LD ( 𝑟 2 = 0.1) generated larger haploblocks (Figure 3) independent of the genomic prediction 493 

method. The ability to choose parameters allows for flexibility and balancing the trade -offs 494 

between detection proportion and fine -resolution mapping when conducting QTL discovery 495 

studies . This is a clear advantage not currently found in most, if not all, GWAS methods 496 

without utilising different priors on the underlying genetic architecture. In c onjunction with 497 

likely lower power to detect low to moderate effect QTL based on our results, the area to 498 

search for QTL is often defined by genome -wide average LD patterns. The localGEBV 499 

method overcomes both challenges. 500 

The biological significance of haploblock size warrants consideration. Smaller haploblocks 501 

may signify high LD between markers and the QTL (Andrade et al., 2019) , which facilitates 502 

precise candidate gene identification for post -GWAS analysis (Clark, 2004) . On the other 503 

hand, l arger haploblocks likely capture important biological mechanisms including local 504 

epistasis, pleiotropy (Kouyos et al., 2007; Zhang et al., 2014; Jiang et al., 2018) , and adaptive 505 

mechanisms from balancing selection, mutation, and genetic drift (Barton, 2010) . We did not 506 

observe this in our case, but we utili sed a genetically intricate trait rather than fully 507 

Mendelian with substantial non -additive effects between a low number of loci .508 

Additionally, information on recombination hotspots at haploblock boundaries (Kim et al., 509 

2018; Epstein et al., 2023) can be valuable for breaking unfavourable linkage drag in 510 

breeding programs (Bohra et al., 2022) or stacking favourable haplotypes . Utilizing 511 

haploblocks provides an interesting advantage when introgressing new QTL (haplotypes) for 512 

a complex population structure. Utili sing localGEBV from haploblocks provide s a "genetic 513 

insurance policy" by co -selecting target genes with their local genetic background, thereby 514 

ensuring more predictable and stable trait expression when introgressed into new cohorts. 515 

The latter point is especially important in genetic improvement to maintain diversity and 516 

construct the “ultimate genotype” (Cole and VanRaden, 2011; Kemper et al., 2012; Hayes et 517 

al., 2024) . The latter suggests our results likely have implications in breeding programs, 518 

crossing design s, and genomic improvement. This is comparable to GWAS and genomic 519

Biological insights from localGEBV features 533 

The localGEBV approach provides deeper understanding of trait genetic architecture through 534 

two key features: 535 

Haploblock variance . Haploblocks with high variances (greater than the mean haploblock 536 

variance) were consistently associated with substantial haplotype effects, representing true 537 

biological signals rather than artifacts of segment size be cause size was not correlated with 538 

variance (Figure 2 and 6) . This variance metric offers advantages over conventional p-values 539 

in GWAS, which provides statistical significance but often lack informative biological 540 

meaning (Visscher et al., 2017) , may suffer from false associations (Yang et al., 2010; Wray 541 

et al., 2013) and do not provide a definitive region to explore QTL. In addition, GWAS may 542 

also only capture part of the QTL signal or share its effect with nearby markers in high LD 543 

with the QTL (Meuwissen and Goddard, 2000; Marchini et al., 2004; Goddard, 2009; Zhang 544 

et al., 2010) ; however, this is dependent on the prior assumptions of marker effects, towards 545 

which our approach is more robust . By shifting focus from marker significance thresholds to 546 

haploblock variance or testing haploblock variance for significance , the localGEBV approach 547 

diminishes the problems associated with multiple testing that also makes small -effect loci 548 

effectively undetectable . The reduction in stringency is because there are often far less 549 

haploblocks than markers, so less stringency on type I error control is needed. The reduction 550 

in the number of tests is appropriate, because markers are not independent and Bonferroni or 551

mutations affecting trait heritability (Sullivan et al., 2023) .555 

Haplotype effects . Haplotypes provide superior predictive ability compared to single markers. 556 

Our regression analyses demonstrated that the haplotype configurations within a particular, 557 

high -variance haploblock serve as better predictors of phenotypic traits than individual SNP 558 

genotype configurations . This highlights the importance of considering cumulative genetic 559 

effects in tightly linked regions (Meuwissen and Goddard, 2000) , rather than isolated markers 560 

as single markers can only capture a limited amount of genetic variation .561 

The choice of estimating SNP effects (Step 2) to calculate localGEBV provides flexibility of 562 

this approach. While BayesR was able to confirm known large effect VRS1 region (Figure 3 563 

and 7A; Supplementary Figure 2), complex traits will not have this type of architecture and 564 

rrBLUP or other similar infinitesimal models is a good default model. 565 

Lower -frequency alleles with considerable effect can be successfully captured as a single unit 566 

within the haploblock (Berdan et al., 2023) , enabling exploration of local allelic interactions 567 

and epistasis (Charlesworth, 1974) . The localGEBV method may help to capture these rare 568 

effects, but only if there are QTL and markers that have similar frequencies because LD is 569 

dependent on similar allele frequencies. 570 

Lastly, haplotype effects also signify directionality (Han and Pan, 2010) in relation to a 571 

reference haplotype, similar to the allele substitution effect of the alternative allele relative to 572 

the reference allele. Our analysis confirmed that negative -effect haplotypes were associated 573 

with 2 -row phenotypes and positive -effect haplotypes with 6 -row spikes (Figure 7A and 7D). 574 

Limitations of localGEBV 575 

The localGEBV me thod represents a promising leap forward from a single marker to LD -576 

based haplotype approach. However, the implementation we present here is based solely on 577 

additive genetic effects and aggregating dispersed QTL signals using LD. Regarding the 578 

latter, this means that haploblocks are based on historic selection, drift, and recombination 579 

patterns that influence the development and decay of LD (Slatkin, 2008) . However, if intense 580 

selection and drift were driving quick formation of LD, it could indicate that haploblock 581 

boundaries are not reflective of future recombination “hotspots .” In such a case, the 582

haploblock boundaries is determined on the specific population that is analy sed , its 586 

population structure, and recent selection history. 587 

Furthermore, while only additive genetic effects are considered here, non -additive genetic 588 

effects, such as dominance and epis tasis, could be incorporated into the calculations to 589 

compute local genomic estimated genotypic values (localGEGV) rather than localGEBV . It is 590 

conceivable that dominance effects could be “solved” for individual loci much in the sam e591 

way additive marker effects are derived from Genomic Best Linear Unbiased Prediction 592 

using dominance or genotypic value relationship matrices (e.g., Vitezica et al., 2017; Liu et 593

and localGEBV. Bayesian methodologies to directly compute dominance deviation and 597

interaction model (Bayesian or otherwise) remains an active area of exploration. 603 

Implications for crop breeding 604 

The localGEBV approach offers significant advantages in QTL discovery that could be used 605 

in genetic improvement strategies in crops. By exploiting LD blocks, breeders can determine 606 

both global and local genetic correlations between multiple traits (Visscher et al., 2017; 607 

Olasege et al., 2022) , identify sources of genetic control under pleiotropic influence 608 

(Watanabe et al., 2019) , and select favourable haplotype configurations. The approach 609 

effectively identifies compound -effect loci (Bevan et al., 2017) that influences complex traits. 610 

The approach is particularly valuable for traits that are difficult to phenotype; for example, 611 

fertility in barley is difficult to phenotype because fertility of lateral spikelets drives row -type 612 

morphology. Consequently , fertility can influence other yield components such as grain 613 

number and thousand kernel weight. 614

multi -trait selection in traditional genomic prediction frameworks, because multi -trait 620 

selection is expected to exhaust favourable genetic covariation between the traits, leaving 621 

only neutral o r unfavourable genetic covariation (Itoh, 1991) . The latter would make genetic 622 

progress for both traits increasingly more difficult over time (Itoh, 1991) .623 

In practice, linkage drag between haplotypes pose a challenging task in breeding. Haplotype 624 

boundaries signifying recombination hotspots can be broken down using targeted 625 

recombination approach. On the other hand, for a more deployable application, localGEBV 626 

information can be used to develop haplotype markers, especially for qualitative or 627

that assemble beneficial allele combinations and optimise genotype configurations (Hayes et 637

approaches (Cooper et al., 2009; Kemper et al., 2012; Villiers et al., 2024) , and improve 639 

genomic predictions (Schopp et al., 2017) in crop breeding programs. 640
