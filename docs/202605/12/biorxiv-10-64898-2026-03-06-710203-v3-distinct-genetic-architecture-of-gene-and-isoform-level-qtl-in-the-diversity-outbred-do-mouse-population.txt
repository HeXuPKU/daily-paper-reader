# Abstract 27 

Genetic association studies of mRNA abundance phenotypes link regulatory gene loci to 28 

mRNA abundance (quantitative trait loci; QTL). The majority of eQTL studies are limited 29 

to gene-level mRNAs and have not focused on mRNA isoforms. Here, we utilized a large, 30 

genetically diverse mouse population to map QTL for both gene and transcript isoform 31 

abundance. We identified largely overlapping sets of locally regulated mRNAs, which 32 

contrasted with the predominantly non-overlapping distally regulated mRNAs, particularly 33 

those influenced by sex and diet. Using allele-effect patterns from local QTL for protein-34 

coding gene-isoform pairs, we show that genetic variation drives allele-specific isoform 35 

usage, generating isoforms whose genetic signals diverge from their aggregated gene-36 

level effects through predominantly post-transcriptional mechanisms. We conducted 37 

pathway enrichment on distal mRNA hotspots and uncovered isoform-level pathways not 38 

detected with gene-level traits. We then applied a composite mediation approach at distal 39 

hotspots that compares gene-gene, isoform-isoform, and isoform-gene mediator models. 40 

By contrasting these causal models of transcriptional regulation, we identified unique 41 

associations between mRNA isoforms that were undetected at the gene level. We also 42 

characterized the influence of sex and diet on mRNA expression. Our data also suggest 43 

that sex and diet influence expression primarily through distal-acting gene loci. We 44 

integrated our QTL data with human genetic data, prioritizing effector genes in loci 45 

associated with metabolically relevant traits that suggest conditional dependence on sex 46 

and diet in humans. Overall, our findings highlight distinctive mechanisms of 47 

transcriptional regulation and emphasize the need to prioritize an isoform-level focus for 48

Genetic association studies link DNA variants to physiological and molecular trait 54 

outcomes (1). When we use mRNA as a quantitative trait, we can identify DNA variants 55 

that regulate mRNA abundance. These are termed expression quantitative trait loci 56 

(eQTL). eQTL studies conventionally prioritize gene-level estimates of mRNA expression. 57 

This approach overlooks a major source of transcriptomic complexity in eukaryotes: 58 

alternative splicing, which mediates the production of multiple distinct mRNA isoforms 59 

from a single gene, which can differ in structure and function (2,3). It also assumes a 60 

similar genetic effect for all isoforms from a given gene. Recent studies have shown that 61 

aggregating isoform-level abundances to gene-level estimates masks isoform-specific 62 

regulatory mechanisms in genetic association studies (4–6). 63 

Variations in isoform transcript structure and function provide an opportunity to discover 64 

genetic variants controlling alternative splicing. Thus, distinguishing between the genetic 65 

regulation of transcriptional and post-transcriptional mechanisms. Alternative pre-mRNA 66 

splicing has been established as a key mechanism through which genetic variation 67 

mediates complex trait outcomes (7–12). Transcript- (13,14) and event-based (15–17) 68 

approaches are commonly used to quantify alternative splicing as a molecular trait. While 69 

these approaches are more sensitive to post-transcriptional regulation, they, however, 70 

treat alternative splicing events as a set of independent local events, which can obscure 71 

how multiple coordinated changes in splicing shape full-length transcript output (18,19). 72 

mRNA isoform abundance measures provide a direct readout of alternative splicing 73 

mechanisms and can be associated with loci regulating their expression. 74

Advances in sequencing and analytical methods have enabled large-scale genome-wide 75 

association studies (GWAS) in both humans and model organisms. However, our ability 76 

to study genetic mechanisms underlying complex traits in humans remains limited by the 77 

practical challenges of establishing well-controlled, long-term, context-dependent studies 78 

in humans. This necessitates the use of genomic approaches that prioritize model 79 

organisms and allow for translatable findings in humans. 80 

We have adopted the Diversity Outbred (DO) mouse stock, originating from 8 inbred 81 

mouse strains (20), as a model system to study the genetic regulation of gene expression. 82 

Because of meiotic recombination during outbreeding, each DO mouse is genetically 83 

unique, and together, the DO mice offer the same level of genetic diversity as the entire 84 

human population (~40 million SNPs). With a large sample size, we associate 85 

physiological and molecular phenotypes with variable regions of the genome (21,22). This 86 

approach is based on the premise that key metabolic pathways and regulatory 87 

mechanisms are highly conserved between mice and humans (23–25). As a result, the 88 

identification of these genetic mechanisms in a mouse population allows for the 89 

translation of physiological outcomes in humans. For example, we previously 90 

demonstrated that QTL associated with insulin secretion in the DO mouse population 91 

corresponded to loci associated with diabetes risk in humans (26). In another study, we 92 

used these mice to identify microbe-host interactions associated with lipid production in 93 

the mouse gut and in humans (27). Several other studies using this heterogeneous 94 

mouse population have successfully delineated the genetic mechanisms underlying trait 95 

associations (28–36). 96

Isoform-level QTL (isoQTL) analysis has been relatively unexplored in the DO mouse 97 

population. Previous studies have investigated eQTL in islets (37), heart (38,39), brain 98 

(40), skeletal muscle (41), kidney (42), embryonic stem cells (43), and liver (44–48). Each 99 

DO mouse inherits a unique combination of the eight founder alleles in small haplotype 100 

blocks, and we can test whether differences in these haplotypes are associated with 101 

differences in the expression of a given mRNA across the population. For each significant 102 

locus, the effects of the eight founder alleles can be estimated to identify which founder 103 

haplotypes are associated with increased or decreased expression of the mRNA. These 104 

founder allele effect patterns capture the genetic architecture underlying mRNA 105 

expression and represent the basis of genetic mapping using this mouse population (22). 106 

However, the overreliance on gene-level estimates of the mRNA abundance follows the 107 

assumption that the genetic architecture for all isoforms of a given gene is the same. 108 

In this study, we utilized a large, DO mouse cohort (male and female) that were fed one 109 

of two extreme diets: a low-fat/high-carbohydrate (HC) diet or a low-carbohydrate/high-110 

fat (HF) diet. We performed QTL mapping to identify loci that were associated with the 111 

abundance of mRNAs (eQTL) and their isoforms (isoQTL) in the liver. We demonstrate 112 

the occurrence of distinct genetic architectures between genes and their isoforms. By 113 

comparing causal models of gene- and isoform-level associations, we identify unique 114 

associations between isoform-level mRNAs, undetected at the gene-level. Finally, we 115 

show that sex and diet also influence the genetic regulation of mRNA expression distinctly 116 

on gene- and isoform-level QTL. By integrating human GWAS data, we show that these 117 

findings are translatable to humans. We provide an interactive web resource that allows 118 

users to query our genetic data. 119

# Results 120 

## The genetic architecture of gene and isoform-level QTL in the 121 

## DO mouse cohort 122 

We performed RNA-seq on the liver from 1,157 (male and female) DO mice that were 123 

maintained on one of two diets: a low-fat/high-carbohydrate (HC) diet or a low-124 

carbohydrate/high-fat (HF) diet for 12 weeks ( Fig 1A, see Methods). This allowed us to 125 

quantify ~28,000 gene- and ~85,000 isoform-level mRNAs ( S1A Fig ). We then performed 126 

whole-genome scans using diet and sex as additive covariates, initially negating their 127 

influence, to identify ~23,000 and ~62,000 QTL and isoQTL, respectively (FDR< 0.1; LOD 128 

≥ 7.5; Fig 1B ; S1B Fig ; see Method). The majority of these QTL were characterized by 129 

local regulatory elements (~72%). As expected, local QTL identified at both the gene and 130 

isoform levels clustered near their target genes’ transcription start sites (TSS) ( Fig 1C ). 131 

That multiple isoforms can be generated from a gene raises the possibility that the greater 132 

number of QTL for isoform versus gene-level analyses reflects redundancy, i.e., several 133 

isoforms for a gene can co-map to the same locus as the gene from our gene-level 134 

analysis. To address this, we examined the number of unique genes identified from our 135 

eQTL (eGenes) and isoQTL (isoGenes) analyses. We found substantial overlap (88%) 136 

between locally regulated eGenes and isoGenes when compared to distal eGenes and 137 

isoGenes (44%) ( Fig 1D ). This demonstrated that we detected a local QTL for one or 138 

more isoforms of the majority of mRNAs in our study. 139

We next sought to identify likely drivers at the distally regulated QTL hotspots that enrich 232 

for physiological function. We performed mediation analyses to identify candidate drivers 233 

using bmediatR (51). Briefly, bmediatR utilizes a Bayesian model selection approach to 234 

infer a causal structure relating to the effect of the genotype at a QTL (X) on a target 235 

phenotype (Y) through a candidate mediator (M). It provides an advantage over other 236 

inference methods in that it is able to distinguish between complete mediation, partial 237 

mediation, and co-local effects, thereby limiting the detection of spurious mediators. 238 

Importantly, while complete mediators capture the full effect of the mediator on the trait, 239 

thereby making them truly causal, partial mediators are unable to fully account for the 240 

effect of the mediator on the trait. 241 

We considered only complete mediators under three models for our analyses. Under the 242 

first two models, we used gene- or isoform-level mRNA as the mediator of either gene-243 

level or isoform-level RNAs ( Fig 2C ). For the third model, we utilized isoform-level mRNA 244 

as the mediator of gene-level expression as the trait. This third model was largely driven 245 

by our discovery that isoforms from the same gene can demonstrate distinct genetic 246 

architecture, suggesting they will perform differently during mediation. Our results show 247 

an improved detection of driver-trait relationships when considering isoforms as 248 

mediators ( Fig 2D ). 249

## Sex and diet influence on gene and isoform QTL 308 

To identify sex and/or diet-dependent QTL, we performed QTL analysis that allowed sex 309 

or diet to function as interacting variables (see Methods). This identified 7,869 sex-310 

dependent and 4,424 diet-dependent QTL at the gene level and 18,218 sex-dependent 311 

and 11,478 diet-dependent at the isoform level (FDR< 0.1; S5A-B Fig ). However, in 312 

contrast to what we observed when sex and diet were additive covariates, ~29% and 313 

~71% of both sex and diet-dependent QTL were subject to local versus distal regulation, 314 

respectively ( Fig 3A ). This result demonstrated that sex and diet exert their influence on 315 

mRNA expression predominantly through distal-acting genetic loci. 316

# Discussion 441 

Genetic association studies have been applied to mRNA abundance to identify gene loci 442 

that control gene expression. We and others have carried out these studies in mouse 443 

intercrosses and, most recently, in the Diversity Outbred population. Studies on the 444 

transcriptomic regulatory landscape of the DO mouse population have generally not 445 

focused on isoform-level genetic associations (80). Here, we present the first QTL 446 

mapping analyses using isoform-level mRNA abundance to identify isoform-specific 447 

genetic associations in mouse liver. Our study is the largest genetic screen of the hepatic 448 

transcriptome conducted in the DO mouse population. 449 

Local genetic effects on mRNA expression traits were predominant in our study, 450 

consistent with previous reports of the DO mouse hepatic transcriptome (45,47). 451 

However, we identified differences in the local genetic architecture between genes and 452 

their transcript isoforms. These differences are attributable to diverging founder allele 453 

effects between genes and their transcript isoforms. 454 

QTL mapping in the DO population enables an estimation of the effect of each allele from 455 

each of the eight founder parent strains at each locus (22). These allele-effect patterns 456 

recapitulate the unit measures of the phenotype when centered at zero. Therefore, the 457 

estimated effect of each allele from the founder strains can be associated with an increase 458 

or a reduction in the expression of an mRNA at that locus. Our results here infer allele-459 

specific isoform expression that is masked when we aggregate mRNA expression at the 460 

gene level (81). Our findings here agree with early evidence in humans suggesting that 461

expression and structure (82). 463 

We demonstrated three distinct patterns of local genetic variation, characterized by allele-464

canonical isoforms. These results implicate alternative isoforms in the regulation of 471 

transcriptional/post-transcriptional modifications and mitochondrial biogenesis/function. 472 

A recent study evaluating the activities of transcription factor (TF) mRNAs reported 473 

widespread variation in the molecular functions of alternative TF isoforms relative to 474

## Diversity outbred mouse population and tissue collection 599 

A total of 1200 Diversity Outbred (DO) mice, 600 males and 600 females, across 7 600 

generations (gens. 41–47) were obtained from The Jackson Laboratory at 4–5 weeks of 601 

age (J: DO, JAX stock #009376) and maintained in the AAALAC-accredited University of 602 

Wisconsin-Madison Department of Biochemistry animal vivarium. Over the course of 603 

approximately 2 years, DO mice were received in waves of 100 mice, 50 males, and 50 604 

females. 100 mice from litter 2 of the gen. 41, 100 mice from litter 1 of the gen. 42, and 605 

100 mice from each of the litters 1 and 2 of generations 43–47 were obtained. Mice were 606 

maintained singly housed, on a 12-h light/12-h dark cycle (6 AM/6 PM), at a temperature 607 

range of 67–76 °C with a humidity range of 28–60%, and had ad libitum access to food 608 

and water. 609 

From arrival until 6 weeks of age, mice were fed a standard chow diet (Formulab Diet 610 

5008, LabDiet). At 6 weeks of age, half of the mice (half of the females and half of the 611 

males) were switched to a custom-made low-fat, high-carbohydrate (HC) diet (10.4% kcal 612 

from fat, 75.5% kcal from carbohydrate, 14.2% kcal from protein, caloric density 3.6 613 

kcal/g) (TD.200339, Envigo Teklad Diets) and the other half to a custom-made high-fat, 614 

low-carbohydrate (HF) diet (83.5% kcal from fat, 2.5% kcal from carbohydrate, 14.0% 615 

kcal from protein, caloric density 6.2 kcal/g) (TD.200625, Envigo Teklad Diets). Instead 616 

of cellulose, both diets contained a mix of 7 fiber sources: pectin, inulin, HI-MAIZE 260 617 

(resistant starch), resistant wheat starch (Fibersym), fructooligosaccharide (FOS), beta-618 

glucan, and glucomannan. The amount of fiber added to each diet was adjusted to be 619

## QTL Mapping 689 

Normalized expression values for each expression trait were “RankZ”-transformed before 690 

eQTL mapping. That is, expression values were converted to ranks, and then 691 

corresponding quantiles were found from the normal distribution. This is also known as 692 

“quantile-based inverse Normal transformation” or as transforming to “normal quantiles”. 693 

Genome scans were performed using a single-QTL linear mixed model with the scan1() 694 

function in the R/qtl2 package. Scans were carried out using data from the full set of 1157 695 

mice, the allele probabilities, the kinship matrix calculated using the “leave one 696 

chromosome out” (LOCO) method, and with generation_litter (i.e., wave) and sex*diet as 697 

additive covariates. 698 

We used permutation analysis to set the significance threshold for identifying significant 699 

eQTL and an FDR approach to account for multiple testing in the search across genes or 700 

transcripts. RankZ-transformation of data gives each trait the same marginal distribution 701 

and allows for the significance threshold obtained from the permutation of one trait scan 702 

to be used for all trait scans. 703 

We permuted one gene scan 1000 times and one transcript scan 1000 times using the 704 

scan1perm() function in the R/qtl2 package, calculated the significance threshold from 705 

each at a 10% significance level, and used the respective thresholds for all gene scans 706 

or all transcript scans. It is of note, however, that this significance threshold was indeed 707 

the same for both permutations, a LOD score of 7.5. Peaks at or above the significance 708

## SNP association 756 

We performed SNP association using the scan1snps() function in the R/qtl2 package and 757 

following the workflow at R/qtl2 User Guide for genome build GRCm39. A SNP or indel 758 

was considered for association analysis if it 1) was polymorphic amongst the DO founder 759 

strains, 2) had a genotype call in all DO founder strains and the genotype call was 760 

homozygous, and 3) had a high-confidence genotype call in at least 6 of the 8 DO founder 761 

strains. In brief, for each significant eQTL peak, we imputed all SNPs and indels that met 762 

our criteria within a 3 Mbp window centered around each peak and performed a scan to 763 

test for association between each variant and the relevant expression trait, using the 764 

same covariates that produced the relevant eQTL. Variants with the strongest 765 

associations were then identified by harvesting those variants with a LOD score within 766 

1.5 LOD of that of the variant with the highest LOD score. Consequences for variants with 767 

the strongest associations were predicted using Ensembl VEP (version 114). 768
