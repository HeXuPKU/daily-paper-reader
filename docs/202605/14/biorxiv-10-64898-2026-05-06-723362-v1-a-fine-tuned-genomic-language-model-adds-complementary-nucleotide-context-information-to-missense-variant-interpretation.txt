# A fine-tuned genomic language model adds complementary nucleotide-context information to missense variant interpretation 

Yaqi Su 1* † and Yu-Jen Lin 1* †

1Department of Molecular and Cell Biology, University of California, Berkeley, Berkeley, California, 94720, United States. *Corresponding author(s). E-mail(s): yaqisu@berkeley.edu; jenniferyjlin@berkeley.edu; 

†These authors contributed equally to this work. 

Abstract 

Missense variant interpretation remains a central challenge in clinical genomics. Missense pathogenicity predictors achieve strong performance, but many empha-size protein-level consequences or overlapping annotation priors. Whether genomic language models add non-redundant nucleotide-context signal to mis-sense interpretation remains unclear. Here, we systematically adapted genomic language models to ClinVar missense pathogenicity prediction across back-bone architectures, representation strategies, classifier heads, and adaptation regimes. In our analysis, variant-position embeddings consistently outperformed pooled sequence representations, multi-species pretraining provided the strongest backbone-level advantage, and low-rank adaptation generalized better than full fine-tuning. The resulting fine-tuned model, GLM-Missense, substantially outperformed zero-shot scoring from the same pretrained model. To test whether GLM-Missense contributes information beyond existing meth-ods, we built MetaMissense, an XGBoost ensemble combining GLM-Missense with AlphaMissense, ESM1b, REVEL, CADD, SIFT, and PolyPhen-2. GLM-Missense showed the lowest concordance with other predictors, retained the strongest partial association with pathogenicity after controlling for the other predictors, and ranked as the most informative non-ensemble input to MetaMis-sense. MetaMissense achieved the best performance in both cross-validation and held-out testing. Analyses of variants correctly classified by GLM-Missense but misclassified by several established predictors suggested two patterns. First, part of the GLM-Missense signal may reflect splice-relevant exonic context. Second, GLM-Missense appears to add value in settings where other predictors may

# 1 Introduction 1

Missense variants are among the most common classes of coding variation identified 2

in clinical sequencing, yet their interpretation remains a major challenge in human 3

genetics Majewski et al. (2011); Tennessen et al. (2012); McLaughlin et al. (2014). 4

Clinical classification of missense variants is central to molecular diagnosis and variant 5

prioritization Richards et al. (2015). ClinVar aggregates clinical assertions of variant 6

significance together with supporting evidence and has become a key resource for 7

variant interpretation Landrum et al. (2018, 2020). However, accurate classification 8

of previously uncharacterized missense variants remains an unsolved challenge Starita 9

et al. (2017). 10 

Most existing missense pathogenicity predictors were developed to model protein-11 

level consequences, typically through amino-acid sequence conservation, structural 12 

context, and predicted effects on protein function Ng and Henikoff (2006) This empha-13 

sis does not arise because missense variants originate in protein space, but because 14 

the encoded amino-acid change has historically been the most interpretable route 15 

from coding DNA variation to altered function. In protein space, pathogenic missense 16 

variants can disrupt stability, catalytic activity, binding interfaces, or other biochem-17 

ical functions, so these are natural targets for computational prediction (Stefl et al. 18 

2013). Current methods span several methodological classes. Supervised annotation-19 

based models such as REVEL and CADD integrate conservation with diverse genomic 20 

annotations Ioannidis et al. (2016); Kircher et al. (2014); Rentzsch et al. (2019). 21 

Classical sequence- and structure-informed predictors such as SIFT and PolyPhen-2 22 

estimate the functional impact of amino-acid substitutions using evolutionary con-23 

servation, physicochemical properties, and protein sequence or structural context Ng 24 

and Henikoff (2003); Adzhubei et al. (2010). More recently, protein language models 25 

and structure-informed methods, including ESM1b and AlphaMissense, have further 26 

improved performance by leveraging large-scale proteomic and structural priors Bran-27 

des et al. (2023); Cheng et al. (2023). Large protein resources such as UniProt have 28 

enabled self-supervised pretraining, and protein language models have shown strong 29 

transfer across structural and functional tasks, including zero-shot mutation-effect pre-30 

diction Bateman et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023); Meier et al. (2021). Together, these 31 

approaches have advanced missense prediction Rastogi et al. (2025). Nevertheless, 32 

independent benchmarking still reveals substantial residual error Rastogi et al. (2025). 33 

It therefore remains unclear whether the remaining error mainly reflects irreducible 34 

uncertainty or systematically missing information. 35 

A missense annotation does not guarantee a protein-only mechanism. Exonic sub-36 

stitutions can alter splicing by perturbing splice-adjacent sequence, exonic splicing 37

many existing methods focus primarily on protein-level effects and do not fully capture 50 

nucleotide-level context. 51 

Genomic language models provide a practical framework that could potentially 52 

address this gap by learning DNA-sequence representations. DNABERT first showed 53 

that self-supervised learning on genomic sequence can produce transferable represen-54 

tations for downstream genomic tasks Ji et al. (2021). Subsequent models expanded 55 

both tokenization strategies and model scale, such as the Nucleotide Transformer 56 

family Dalla-Torre et al. (2025). The models examined in this study differ in both 57 

pretraining corpus and architecture. Nucleotide Transformer 1 (NT-1) refers to the 58 

original 500M Nucleotide Transformer pretrained with masked language modeling 59 

on over 3000 human genomes. Nucleotide Transformer 2 (NT-2) refers to the multi-60 

species Nucleotide Transformer v2 pretrained on genomes across over 800 different 61 

species Dalla-Torre et al. (2025). Caduceus further broadened the architectural space 62 

by introducing a state-space genomic language model designed for long-range DNA 63 

context modeling Schiff et al. (2024). Caduceus-PS, the parameter-sharing (PS) imple-64 

mentation evaluated here, incorporates inductive biases such as reverse-complement 65 

symmetry and was pretrained on the human reference genome Schiff et al. (2024). Prior 66 

genomic language model studies showed that these models can perform well across 67 

multiple genomic prediction tasks and can score variants directly from sequence Bene-68 

gas et al. (2025). However, to our knowledge, no previous study has directly tested 69 

whether genomic language models contribute additional information beyond existing 70 

missense predictors in missense interpretation. 71 

Meta-predictors, or ensemble models, combine outputs or feature summaries from 72 

multiple existing predictors into a single score. Their main purpose is to aggregate 73 

partially complementary lines of evidence and improve overall predictive performance. 74 

Existing examples, such as REVEL, show that combining multiple predictor scores 75 

can improve missense prediction Ioannidis et al. (2016). Ensemble models are also 76 

useful for a second reason: they provide a natural framework for testing whether a 77 

new predictor adds information beyond what is already captured by the others. Nev-78 

ertheless, most benchmarking studies still focus on whether one method outperforms 79 

another on standalone accuracy metrics. Much less attention has been paid to whether 80 

a model contributes non-redundant signal that could help close the remaining gap 81

Effective fine-tuning is important to fully utilize genomic language models for mis-85

labels, recent studies suggest that zero-shot performance alone may not be suffi-87 

cient for accurate missense classification Benegas et al. (2025); Curtis (2025). These 88 

observations motivate task-specific adaptation of genomic language models for clinical 89 

missense prediction. Choosing an effective fine-tuning strategy is critical because clin-90 

ically labeled missense datasets are limited relative to the size of current pretrained 91 

genomic models. Downstream performance may therefore depend strongly on how the 92 

pretrained model backbone is adapted Veiner and Supek (2026); Feng et al. (2025). 93 

It remains unclear which combination of pretrained backbone, pretraining corpus, 94 

embedding extraction strategy, and fine-tuning strategy yields the most robust perfor-95 

mance for missense pathogenicity prediction. Parameter-efficient approaches such as 96 

low-rank adaptation (LoRA), which keep the pretrained backbone fixed and learn only 97 

low-rank updates, may be particularly useful when labeled missense data are limited 98 

relative to model size Hu et al. (2022). Rigorous evaluation is also essential because 99 

benchmarking of variant effect predictors is vulnerable to circularity and other sources 100 

of optimistic bias Grimm et al. (2015); Livesey and Marsh (2020, 2023); Rastogi et al. 101 

(2025). Chromosome-level holdout designs are commonly used as a more conservative 102 

alternative to random splits in sequence-based genomic prediction, because they reduce 103 

the risk of leakage from nearby genomic regions or linkage disequilibrium de Boer and 104 

Taipale (2024); Feng et al. (2025). 105 

Here, we systematically evaluated adapting genomic language models for mis-106 

sense pathogenicity prediction using curated ClinVar missense datasets. We compared 107 

NT-1, NT-2, and Caduceus-PS as representative genomic language model backbones 108 

and examined how pretraining corpus, embedding extraction strategy, classifier head, 109 

and adaptation regime influence downstream performance. Building on the best-110 

performing configuration, we developed GLM-Missense, a genomic language model 111 

fine-tuned for missense pathogenicity prediction. We then asked whether GLM-112 

Missense contributes information beyond existing missense predictors by constructing 113 

MetaMissense, an XGBoost-based ensemble model that combines GLM-Missense with 114 

AlphaMissense, ESM1b, REVEL, CADD, SIFT, and PolyPhen-2 Cheng et al. (2023); 115 

Brandes et al. (2023); Ioannidis et al. (2016); Kircher et al. (2014); Ng and Henikoff 116 

(2003); Adzhubei et al. (2010). In brief, variant-position embeddings outperformed 117 

mean-pooled sequence representations, multi-species pretraining provided the clearest 118 

backbone-level advantage, and LoRA generalized better than full fine-tuning under 119 

limited supervision. GLM-Missense substantially improved over zero-shot scoring from 120 

the same pretrained backbone. More importantly, GLM-Missense contributed comple-121 

mentary signal within the MetaMissense ensemble despite not being the top standalone 122 

predictor: it showed the lowest concordance with the other predictors, retained strong 123 

non-redundant association with the true label after accounting for the other predictor 124 

scores, and was among the most important features used by MetaMissense. Consistent 125 

with these findings, MetaMissense achieved the best overall performance compared 126

# 2 Results 136 

2.1 Variant-position embeddings and multi-species pretraining 137 

yield the best frozen-backbone configuration 138 

We first asked which frozen-backbone configuration provided the strongest baseline 139 

for clinical missense classification. In this setting, the backbone parameters were 140 

kept frozen and only a lightweight downstream classifier was trained. We compared 141 

three pretrained gLM backbones: NT-2, NT-1, and Caduceus-PS Dalla-Torre et al. 142 

(2025); Schiff et al. (2024). For each backbone, we evaluated multiple combinations of 143 

embedding extraction strategies and classifier heads to identify the optimal baseline 144 

configuration. (Fig. 1). 145 

Embedding choice had significant effect on performance. We first compared the 146 

hidden state of the token containing the variant, which we call the variant-position 147 

embedding , against mean pooling across the full input sequence. Each representation 148 

was paired with an MLP, a CNN, or a shallow Transformer head. For Caduceus-149 

PS, we first performed downsampling of the full sequence to account for memory 150 

constraint when paired with CNN or Tranasformer head. Variant-position embeddings 151 

consistently outperformed mean pooling in the models tested (Fig. 2). 152 

A natural follow-up is whether some intermediate representation such as averag-153 

ing the embeddings only within a small window around the variant, might provide 154 

additional local context without diluting the signal across irrelevant positions. To test 155 

whether nearby tokens added useful flanking context, we replaced full-sequence mean 156 

pooling in the frozen NT-2 + CNN model with mean pooling over 8-, 16-, or 32-token 157 

windows centered on the variant (Supplementary Fig. S1). Performance decreased 158 

as the window widened and even the best local-window model remained below the 159 

variant-position baseline. Thus, preserving the representation at the variant position 160 

was better than averaging across nearby tokens. 161 

Classifier-head choice had a smaller effect than embedding choice. In NT-2 with 162 

a variant-position embedding, the three classifier heads performed almost identically 163 

with validation AUROC ranged from 0 .869 to 0 .871 (Fig. 2). In NT-2 with mean pool-164 

ing, the spread across heads increased to ∆AUROC = 0 .027. This pattern suggests 165 

that when using suboptimal input representations, more sophisticated classifier archi-166 

tectures (e.g., CNN and Transformer) may partially compensate by learning to extract 167 

relevant features from the aggregated sequence information. The Caduceus-PS model 168

2.2 Parameter-efficient fine-tuning by LoRA retains the 184 

advantage of variant-position embeddings and improves 185 

generalization over full fine-tuning 186 

We next asked whether the design principles from the frozen setting persisted under 187 

low-rank adaptation (LoRA). In LoRA, the pretrained backbone remains fixed, and 188 

only a small set of low-rank parameters is added and trained Hu et al. (2022). To 189 

test this, we evaluated 54 LoRA configurations on NT-2. The sweep varied embedding 190 

strategy (variant-position and mean pooling), classifier head (MLP, CNN, Trans-191 

former), LoRA rank ( r ∈ { 8, 16 , 32 }), and learning rate (1 × 10 −5, 3 × 10 −5, 5 × 10 −5). 192 

To enable rapid iteration, these experiments used a reduced 5k-sample subset from 193 

the Clinvar earlier-release strict training set (Table 1). 194 

Variant-position embeddings remained the dominant design choice under LoRA. 195 

All 27 variant-position configurations outperformed all 27 mean-pooling configura-196 

tions. The worst variant-position model (MLP, r = 8, lr = 1 × 10 −5) achieved an 197 

AUROC of 0 .809 and an AUPRC of 0 .747. The best mean-pooling model (Trans-198 

former, r = 32, lr = 3 × 10 −5) reached only 0 .778 AUROC and 0 .719 AUPRC. This 199 

complete separation shows that embedding choice remained the strongest factor for 200 

parameter-efficient fine-tuning. 201 

The MLP classifier performed best overall among LoRA models with variant-202 

position embeddings (Table 1). The best CNN configuration reached an AUROC of 203 

0.840, compared with 0 .860 for the top MLP configuration. This pattern suggests that 204

2.3 Reference–alternate difference improves the allele-aware 233 

input representation 234 

Explicitly encoding the reference-to-alternate change improved the input representa-235 

tion. Earlier experiments used only the variant-position embedding from the alternate 236 

sequence ( alt ). This representation captures the local context of the alternate allele, 237 

but it does not explicitly encode the reference state. We therefore compared two 238 

richer inputs. The ref-alt representation concatenates the reference and alternate 239 

variant-position embeddings. The ref-alt-diff representation concatenates the reference 240 

embedding, alternate embedding, and their element-wise difference. 241 

Among the three input representations, ref-alt-diff performed best. It improved 242 

performance by ∆AUROC = 0 .008 and ∆AUPRC = 0 .023 over the alt baseline. 243 

By contrast, alt and ref-alt performed similarly (Supplementary Fig. S2). This result 244 

indicates that explicitly encoding the allelic change is more informative than providing 245 

the two allelic states without their difference. 246

2.5 GLM-Missense contributes complementary signal to an 264 

ensemble with the best performance in our benchmark 265 

We next asked whether GLM-Missense added information beyond established mis-266 

sense pathogenicity predictors. We computed pairwise absolute Kendall’s τ on the 267 

ClinVar later-release set among the seven predictor scores: GLM-Missense, AlphaMis-268 

sense Cheng et al. (2023), REVEL Ioannidis et al. (2016), CADD Kircher et al. 269 

(2014), ESM1b Brandes et al. (2023), SIFT Ng and Henikoff (2003), and PolyPhen-270 

2 Adzhubei et al. (2010) (Fig. 4A). GLM-Missense had the lowest average correlation 271 

with the other methods. Its pairwise |τ | values ranged from 0 .39 with SIFT to 0 .50 with 272 

REVEL. The remaining scores were more tightly clustered, with pairwise |τ | values 273 

mostly between 0 .51 and 0 .62. This pattern suggested that GLM-Missense captured 274 

signal that was less redundant with the other predictors. 275 

GLM-Missense retained the strongest association with the true label after account-276 

ing for the other predictor scores. Following the rationale proposed by Lin et al. 277 

(unpublished manuscript), we asked whether each method still tracked pathogenic-278

we computed the partial Spearman correlation between each predictor and the true 280 

label while controlling for the other six predictor scores (Fig. 4B). In this analysis, 281 

a larger positive value indicates that a method contributes useful information that is 282 

not already explained by the others. GLM-Missense had the highest value ( r = 0 .277), 283 

exceeding REVEL ( r = 0 .221), AlphaMissense ( r = 0 .192), ESM1b ( r = 0 .135), SIFT 284 

(r = 0 .047), and PolyPhen-2 ( r = 0 .033). CADD was close to zero and slightly negative 285 

(r = −0.046). These results show that the differences between GLM-Missense and the 286 

other methods were not simply random disagreement but potentially complementary 287 

signals. 288

3.1 Multi-species pretraining and variant-centered 397 

representations capture core biological properties of 398 

missense variants 399 

Multi-species pretraining was a crucial factor affecting performance in the frozen-400 

backbone setting (Fig. 2). In particular, NT-2 was pretrained on sequences from 850 401 

species spanning diverse phyla Dalla-Torre et al. (2025), whereas NT-1 was trained 402 

only on human sequences. This result is biologically plausible because evolutionary 403 

conservation is a central signal used in missense variant interpretation Cooper and 404 

Shendure (2011); Capriotti and Fariselli (2022). Positions preserved across species are 405 

often functionally constrained, so variants at those sites are more likely to be deleteri-406 

ous. Multi-species pretraining therefore allows NT-2 to encode patterns of evolutionary 407 

constraint that are not observable from human sequence alone. Together, these find-408 

ings suggest that pretraining corpus diversity is a key driver of performance in genomic 409 

missense prediction. 410 

Variant-position embeddings were consistently more informative than pooled 411 

sequence representations (Fig. 2). This result aligns with our understanding of mis-412 

sense variants. Many pathogenic missense variants act through local effects on residue 413 

stability, catalytic function, or molecular interfaces Gerasimavicius et al. (2020, 2022). 414 

In NT-2, the representation at the variant position already integrates surrounding 415 

sequence context through self-attention during pretraining Dalla-Torre et al. (2025). 416 

In contrast, mean pooling over thousands of tokens, many of which correspond to posi-417 

tions unrelated to the affected codon (e.g., intronic or intergenic regions), would likely 418 

dilute this variant-specific information. This pattern was observed across all classifier 419 

architectures, indicating that the key factor is the sequence representation rather than 420 

the downstream classifier. 421 

3.2 Low-rank adaptation preserves pretrained representations 422 

while enabling effective task adaptation 423 

LoRA was effective for adapting NT-2 to missense classification. It achieved the highest 424 

validation AUROC and AUPRC, whereas full fine-tuning overfit quickly (Fig. 3). This 425 

contrast is notable because the downstream dataset is small relative to the model 426 

size. With only 44k training examples for a 500M-parameter backbone, full fine-tuning 427 

grants the optimizer enough degrees of freedom to fit training-set-specific patterns that 428 

may not generalize, a phenomenon broadly termed catastrophic forgetting McCloskey 429 

and Cohen (1989); Kirkpatrick et al. (2017). NT-2 has likely already captured essential 430 

genomic features during pretraining on billions of nucleotides across 850 species Dalla-431 

Torre et al. (2025), including codon usage patterns and evolutionary constraints that 432 

are directly relevant to variant pathogenicity. Updating all parameters therefore risks 433 

overwriting these broadly useful representations in favor of spurious correlations in 434 

the comparatively small labeled set. 435 

LoRA reduces the risk of overfitting by constraining the update space. It freezes 436 

the pretrained weights W0 and learning only low-rank perturbations, ∆ W = BA , with 437

Our results therefore suggest that LoRA can leverage strong pretrained genomic 458

Fine-tuning was also substantially more effective than zero-shot scoring from the 460 

same NT-2 backbone (Fig. S4). This difference is expected because masked language-461

distribution, rather than whether the resulting variant is clinically pathogenic or 463 

benign Meier et al. (2021). In long genomic windows, this likelihood can reflect many 464 

sequence constraints, including local nucleotide composition, repetitive sequence, and 465 

other contextual patterns that are not specific to the functional consequence of a sin-466 

gle missense substitution. Fine-tuning therefore serves an essential role: it redirects 467 

the general sequence representation learned during pretraining toward the clinical 468 

classification boundary of interest. 469

model like GLM-Missense would not necessarily be expected to outperform current 481 

state-of-the-art predictors as a standalone classifier. 482 

GLM-Missense was valuable not simply as a standalone predictor, but as a source 483 

of information that remained useful after strong existing methods were already 484 

included. Its lower concordance with other predictor scores, retained association 485 

with pathogenicity after adjustment for those scores, and high feature importance 486 

in MetaMissense indicate that it contributed signal not fully represented by existing 487 

methods. The additional performance gain of MetaMissense, despite the strong per-488 

formance of current missense predictors, suggests that part of the remaining error 489 

in missense interpretation is still addressable. By learning directly from coding DNA 490 

sequence, GLM-Missense may help capture information that is underrepresented in 491 

protein-, conservation-, and annotation-centered predictors. 492 

The most plausible reason GLM-Missense adds non-redundant information is its 493 

input representation. Most current missense predictors may already capture protein-494 

level, conservation-based, structural, or annotation-derived signals. Many of these 495 

signals are also partially overlapping. In most cases, they are centered on the amino-496 

acid substitution or on curated genomic features. A coding single-nucleotide variant, 497 

however, is not only an amino-acid substitution. It is also a nucleotide substitution 498 

embedded in DNA and pre-mRNA sequence context. Coding sequence contains over-499 

lapping layers of information, including splice-site strength, exonic splicing regulatory 500 

elements, codon context, RNA stability, local RNA structure, and other regulatory fea-501 

tures Dalla-Torre et al. (2025); Presnyak et al. (2015); Wu et al. (2019); Sabarinathan 502 

et al. (2013); Fairbrother et al. (2004); Stergachis et al. (2013). Protein language mod-503 

els and protein-centered missense predictors do not directly model these layers because 504 

they operate after translation into amino-acid space. GLM-Missense instead operates 505 

directly on DNA sequence and can, in principle, represent both protein-coding and 506 

nucleotide-level constraints. 507 

This difference in input representation also has practical implications for vari-508 

ant interpretation. In many workflows, a variant annotated as missense is evaluated 509 

mainly with missense-specific predictors. That workflow implicitly treats the amino-510 

acid substitution as the main consequence of the variant. Our results suggest that 511 

this framing can be incomplete. A missense variant can also carry exonic sequence 512 

effects that are relevant to clinical interpretation, such as altered splice regulation 513 

or other local nucleotide-context changes. If those effects are not considered, part 514 

of the variant information may be missed or deferred to a separate downstream 515 

analysis. Genomic language models offer one way to address this gap by incorpo-516 

rating nucleotide-level context directly into missense prediction, either as standalone 517 

predictors or as components of meta-predictors such as MetaMissense. 518 

The GLM-Missense-correct subset provides a more specific view of where GLM-519 

Missense adds value beyond existing predictors. Variants correctly classified by 520 

GLM-Missense but misclassified by several established methods were enriched in sev-521 

eral distinct settings (Fig. 6). One of these settings points to a plausible biological 522 

mechanism. The others are contexts in which GLM-Missense appears less dependent 523 

on priors that other predictors may overweight. 524

Rare benign variants points to one context in which GLM-Missense appears to 541 

add value. Very rare benign variants often lack strong population-frequency support 542 

for a benign interpretation, even though they are not disease-causing. As a result, 543 

they can be over-prioritized by methods that use rarity as direct or indirect evidence 544 

for pathogenicity. This pattern is consistent with the CAGI 6 (Critical Assessment 545 

of Genome Interpretation) Annotate-All-Missense assessment, which identified very 546 

rare benign variants as a systematic challenge for predictors that incorporate allele 547

less dependent on rarity-related priors and can better distinguish rare benign variants 550 

from truly pathogenic ones (Fig. 6B). 551 

Benign variants in constrained genes highlight a second context in which GLM-552 

Missense differs from other predictors. Gene-level constraint is informative for variant 553 

interpretation, but it can also be overly coarse. A benign missense variant in a con-554 

strained gene may still lie in a locally tolerant region or produce a tolerated amino-acid 555 

substitution despite the gene’s overall intolerance to damaging variation. The CAGI 556

are disproportionately represented among pathogenic examples Rastogi et al. (2025). 559 

GLM-Missense is not immune to this issue, since it is also trained on clinical labels. 560 

However, its input representation does not directly encode gene-level annotations such 561 

as LOEUF or gene identity. This difference may allow the model to rely more strongly 562 

on local sequence context and positional information, which may help it distinguish 563 

tolerated variants in constrained genes from truly pathogenic variants (Fig. 6C). 564

# 4 Conclusion 595 

We systematically evaluated how genomic language models can be adapted for clinical 596 

missense variant pathogenicity prediction and identified a robust configuration for this 597 

task. The strongest setup combined the NT-2 backbone, variant-position embeddings, 598 

LoRA fine-tuning, an MLP classifier head, and a reference–alternate difference repre-599 

sentation. The resulting fine-tuned model, GLM-Missense, substantially outperformed 600 

zero-shot scoring from the same backbone. 601 

More importantly, GLM-Missense contributed non-redundant information beyond 602 

existing methods. GLM-Missense showed lower concordance with other predictors, 603 

retained the strongest partial association with pathogenicity after accounting for other 604 

predictor scores, and remained one of the most important inputs to the MetaMissense 605 

ensemble. These results indicate that GLM-Missense was not simply recapitulating 606 

protein-based, conservation-based, or annotation-based predictors. Instead, it provides 607 

complementary nucleotide-context signal that improved prediction when combined 608 

with other predictors. 609

5.2 Backbone-specific input sequence construction 662 

All sequence inputs were centered on the candidate missense variant so that the altered 663 

position occupied a fixed location after tokenization. Depending on the experiment, 664 

the model received either the alternate sequence alone or paired reference and alternate 665 

sequences processed separately and combined only at the classifier input. This design 666 

enabled consistent extraction of variant-position and local-context embeddings across 667 

backbones while allowing each model to operate at its native tokenization scheme and 668 

context length. 669 

Nucleotide Transformer v2 (NT-2). For each variant, we extracted an 11,999-670 

bp sequence centered on the variant position and tokenized it using 6-mer encoding 671 

with stride 6. This yielded 2,000 tokens, with the variant located at token index 672 

1,000. We used the pretrained Nucleotide Transformer v2 (NT-2, 500M parameters), a 673 

BERT-style model trained by masked language modeling on 850 genomes from diverse 674 

species Dalla-Torre et al. (2025). NT-2 produces a 1,024-dimensional embedding per 675

5,999-bp input window. 677

500. NT-1 produces a 1,280-dimensional embedding per token. 682

5.3 Embedding strategies, allele-aware inputs, classifier heads, 692 

and adaptation regimes 693 

We systematically explored four model components: embedding extraction strategy, 694 

allele-aware input representation, classifier head, and training regime. 695 

Embedding strategies. For frozen-backbone and LoRA experiments, we com-696 

pared several ways to derive the classifier input from backbone hidden states. 697 

Variant-position embedding extracts the embedding at the variant position (i.e., 1,024-698 

d at token index 1000 for NT-2, 1,280-d at token index 500 for NT-1, 512-d at 699 

token index 15,000 for Caduceus; see Fig. 1). Mean pooling averages hidden states 700 

across all tokens in the input sequence, with attention masking for proper handling 701 

of padding tokens. For Caduceus-PS, we applied a downsampled sequence represen-702 

tation in which the full 30 kb hidden-state sequence was reduced to 2,048 positions 703 

by adaptive average pooling, thereby reducing memory requirements for downstream 704 

CNN and Transformer heads. 705 

Local-window pooling. To test whether pooling over a narrow neighborhood 706 

around the variant could recover local context without full-sequence dilution, we evalu-707 

ated local-window mean pooling in the frozen NT-2 with CNN setting. In this analysis, 708 

hidden states were mean-pooled over symmetric windows of ±8, ±16, or ±32 tokens 709 

around the variant position. With 6-mer tokenization, these correspond to ±48, ±96, 710 

and ±192 nucleotides, respectively. 711 

Allele-aware input representations. Most frozen-backbone and LoRA exper-712 

iments used only the variant-position embedding from the alternate sequence ( alt ). 713 

In the final input-representation comparison, we also evaluated ref-alt , which concate-714 

nates the reference and alternate variant-position embeddings, and ref-alt-diff , which 715

difference. For NT-2, these inputs have dimensions 1,024 ( alt ), 2,048 ( ref-alt ), and 717 

3,072 ( ref-alt-diff ), respectively. 718

encoder. The MLP used either two hidden layers with dimensions 512 and 256, or 721

classification layer. ReLU activations and 10% dropout were applied between hidden 723 

layers. The CNN head used two one-dimensional convolutional layers that projected 724

padding 1, batch normalization, ReLU activation, and 10% dropout. The Transformer 726

defined as substitutions with Grantham distance greater than 150. The fraction of rad-866

To quantify how much of the GLM-Missense-correct signal was explained by these 869 

four features, we trained logistic-regression models to distinguish the GLM-Missense-870 

correct subset from the corresponding background. This analysis was performed 871 

separately for benign/likely benign and pathogenic/likely pathogenic variants. The 872 

outcome variable was a binary indicator of inclusion in the GLM-Missense-correct 873 

subset. Predictor variables were maximum SpliceAI delta score, log 10 (AF + 10 −8), 874 

LOEUF, and radical amino-acid-change status. 875 

Model fitting used stratified five-fold cross-validation. Within each training fold, 876 

missing feature values were median-imputed and continuous features were standard-877 

ized by z-score transformation before fitting the logistic-regression model. The fitted 878 

model was then used to predict the probability that each held-out variant belonged 879 

to the GLM-Missense-correct subset. Because the goal was to quantify explained 880 

deviance using calibrated probabilities, logistic-regression models were fit without class 881 

weighting. 882 

Explanatory power was quantified using cross-validated Bernoulli deviance 883 

explained ( D2), defined as 884

For each fold, the null model predicted only the GLM-Missense-correct subset rate 885 

observed in the corresponding training fold and did not use any variant-level features. 886 

Out-of-fold predictions from all folds were combined to compute the final model and 887 

null log-loss values. The reported percentage of deviance explained was calculated as 888 

100 × D2. The unexplained fraction was calculated as 100 × (1 − D2). 889
