ABSTRACT 

Accurate prediction of conversion from Mild Cognitive Im-pairment (MCI) to Alzheimer’s Diseases (AD) is essential for early intervention, however, developing reliable conver-sion predictive models is difficult to develop due to limited longitudinal data availability We evaluate TabPFN (Tabular Pre-Trained Foundation Network) against traditional machine learning methods for predicting 3 year MCI to AD conver-sion using the TADPOLE dataset derived from ADNI. Using multimodal biomarker features extracted from demograph-ics, APOE4, MRI volumes, CSF markers, and PET imaging, we conducted an experimental comparison across varying training set sizes (N=50 to 1000) and models including XG-Boost, Random Forest, LightGBM, and Logistic Regression. TabPFN achieved one the highest performance (AUC=0.892), outperforming LightGBM (AUC=0.860) and demonstrating advantages in low data settings. At N=50 training sam-ples, TabPFN maintained strong AUC while the traditional machine learning models struggles at small training sam-ples. These findings demonstrate that foundation models are promising for disease prediction in data limited scenarios, such as Alzheimer’s diseases. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

The number of Americans living with Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) is projected to reach 13.8 million by 2060[1], under-scoring the urgent need for improved early detection and in-tervention strategies. Machine learning models show promise for predicting disease progression, yet their development faces a fundamental challenge: limited high-quality train-ing data. Alzheimer’s datasets such as OASIS and ADNI suffer from class imbalances, missing longitudinal measure-ments, and incomplete patient histories—constraints that significantly reduce usable training samples[2]. This limi-tation is particularly acute for slowly progressing diseases like AD, where collecting sufficient longitudinal observations with complete neuroimaging and biomarker profiles requires years or decades. Traditional machine learning approaches for tabular medical data, such as gradient boosting meth-ods (XGBoost, LightGBM), typically require hundreds of training samples to achieve clinically acceptable accuracy[3]. In medical research where each labeled sample involves expensive neuroimaging acquisition, biomarker collection, and longitudinal follow-up, this data requirement creates a significant deployment barrier. Moreover, extensive hyper-parameter tuning and model development increases the need for resources and may require machine learning expertise that may be unavailable in clinical settings. Foundation mod-els pre-trained on large/synthetic datasets offer a potential solution[1]. TabPFN is a transformer-based model pre-trained on millions of synthetic tabular classification tasks that per-forms zero-shot learning without task-specific fine-tuning. Unlike traditional models that learn from scratch, TabPFN leverages pre-trained knowledge through in-context learning, potentially achieving strong performance with minimal train-ing samples. While TabPFN has shown promise on standard benchmarks, its effectiveness for medical prediction tasks with real-world constraints—missing values, temporal de-pendencies, and data leakage risks remains unexplored. This study investigates whether pre-trained foundation models can match or exceed traditional machine learning performance when training data is severely limited. 

2. MATERIALS AND EXPERIMENTS 2.1. Dataset and Preprocessing 

We utilized the TADPOLE dataset, derived from the Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI), a comprehen-sive longitudinal study containing clinical, imaging, and biomarker data from 1,737 participants[4]. The dataset in-cludes multiple visit observations spanning up to 10 years, with measurements of structural MRI volumes, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) biomarkers, PET imaging, cognitive assessments, and demographic information collected at each timepoint.

Our study focused on predicting conversion from Mild Cog-nitive Impairment (MCI) to Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) within a 3 year window. For each subject, we identified the first visit with an MCI diagnosis as the baseline timepoint, excluding any patient with an AD diagnosis at or before this baseline visit. Patients were labeled as converters if they received an AD diagnosis at any visit within 3 years of the baseline. Patients were labeled as stable only if they had at least one observed visit at or after the window confirming a non AD status (either remained MCI or reverted to cognitively nor-mal). Patients were excluded if they lacked an AD diagnosis after the window. We excluded cognitive test scores such as MMSE, CDRSB, ADAS-Cog, and FAQ from the feature set to prevent label leakage and artificially inflated performance, since these measures are closely tied to the diagnostic criteria for MCI and AD. Our final feature set comprised biomark-ers only, including demographics, MRI volumetrics, CSF biomarkers, and PET imaging. All preprocessing steps were applied consistently across models to ensure fair compari-son. Categorical variables such as sex were label-encoded, with encoders fit on training data and applied to test data to prevent information leakage. Missing values were imputed using median imputation based on training set statistics. All features were z-score normalized using training set means and standard deviations. 

2.2. Model Configuration 

We evaluated five models spanning traditional machine learn-ing methods and foundation model approaches. Logistic Re-gression served as a linear baseline, configured with a max-imum of 1000 iterations for convergence. Random Forest was configured with 200 trees and a maximum depth of 10 to balance model complexity with generalization. LightGBM and XGBoost, both gradient boosting frameworks, were con-figured with 200 estimators, a maximum depth of 6, and a learning rate of 0.05. We additionally evaluated a tuned XG-Boost model using Optuna with 50 trials and a Tree-structured Parzen Estimator sampler, optimizing over number of esti-mators, maximum depth (3-10), learning rate , subsample ra-tio, column sample ratio, and L1/L2 regularization parame-ters. TabPFN, our primary model of interest, is a transformer-based foundation model pre-trained on millions of synthetic datasets to perform in-context learning on tabular data. Un-like traditional models, TabPFN performs inference by treat-ing the training set as context and predicting on test sam-ples in a single forward pass. We used the publicly available TabPFN classifier with default parameters. Due to architec-tural constraints, TabPFN is limited to datasets with at most 1000 training samples and 100 features (we were well under these constraints for this data set). 

2.3. Experimental Design 

We conducted three experiments to evaluate model perfor-mance for MCI-to-AD conversion prediction. The first exper-iment assessed data efficiency by comparing models across training set sizes of 50, 100, 200, 500, and 1000 patients. For each sample size, we performed 10 bootstrap iterations with stratified sampling to maintain class proportions, train-ing each model on the sampled subset and evaluating on the remaining held out data.The second experiment evalu-ated overall model performance using holdout validation, comparing models based on their ability to distinguish MCI patients who convert to AD from those who remain stable. We report the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) as the primary metric, as it is threshold indepen-dent and robust to class imbalance. We additionally report balanced classification accuracy (BCA) [3], defined as the arithmetic mean of sensitivity and specificity, to assess clas-sification performance at the standard 0.5 decision threshold. The third experiment investigated the relationship between decision thresholds and classification performance. Initial results revealed that TabPFN achieved the highest AUC but the lowest BCA, prompting further analysis. We evaluated BCA across thresholds from 0.05 to 0.95 in increments of 0.01 for each model to identify optimal decision boundaries. All experiments used a fixed random seed for reproducibility. 

3. RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS 

Figure 1 presents the overall performance of all models on the holdout validation set. XGBoost achieved the highest AUC score of 0.901, followed closely by TabPFN at 0.892. Random Forest achieved 0.888, while LightGBM and Lo-gistic Regression performed comparably at 0.860 and 0.859 respectively. These results indicate that both tuned gradient boosting and foundation model approaches achieve strong ability for MCI-to-AD conversion prediction. However, classification performance measured by balanced accuracy revealed a different ranking. Logistic Regression achieved the highest BCA of 0.810, followed by XGBoost at 0.795. LightGBM and the Random Forest configuration achieved 0.728 and 0.690 respectively. TabPFN achieved the lowest BCA of 0.684 despite its strong AUC performance. This discrepancy between AUC and BCA for TabPFN motivated our further threshold analysis. Figure 2 presents model per-formance based off of varyinng training sample sizes. At the smallest training size of N=50, TabPFN achieved the high-est AUC of 0.796, outperforming XGBoost 0.765, Logistic Regression 0.777 and LightGBM 0.713. Though limited, the 8.3 percentage differences demonstrates TabPFN’s ability to extract predictive signal from limited training data. As train-ing size increased, the performance gap between TabPFN and traditional models narrowed. At N=500, all models converged toward similar AUC values ranging from 0.836 to 0.861, with TabPFN at 0.861 and LightGBM at 0.836. The BCA results across sample sizes revealed a consistent pattern: TabPFN’s balanced accuracy lagged behind other models regardless of training size. At N=50, Logistic Regres-sion achieved the highest BCA. This gap persisted across all sample sizes, with TabPFN consistently ranking among the lower performing models for BCA despite its strong AUC. The persistent discrepancy between TabPFN’s high AUC and low BCA prompted investigation into the model’s probability distributions. We evaluated BCA across decision thresholds from 0.05 to 0.95 for all models. TabPFN’s optimal thresh-old was 0.14, substantially lower than the standard 0.5. At this optimized threshold, TabPFN’s BCA improved from 0.68 to 0.82 shown in Figure 3. Our results demonstrate that TabPFN, a foundation model for tabular data, achieves competitive performance for predicting MCI to AD conver-sion, with particular advantages in data limited scenarios. At training sizes of 50-100 patients, TabPFN outperformed nearly all traditional machine learning methods at each train-ing size. This finding has direct clinical relevance for settings where large training cohorts are unavailable, such as early-phase clinical trials, newly established memory clinics, or studies of rare Alzheimer’s disease subtypes. The conver-gence of model performance at larger sample sizes aligns with theoretical expectations. Traditional gradient boosting methods are known to be highly effective given sufficient training data, and our results confirm that XGBoost with hyperparameter tuning achieves strong performance when data availability is not a constraint. The practical implication is that foundation models offer the greatest advantage pre-cisely in the scenarios where they are most needed—when data is scarce. Our threshold analysis revealed an important consideration for deploying foundation models in clinical settings. TabPFN’s predicted probabilities do not correspond directly to conversion risk in the way that traditionally cali-brated models’ outputs do. The optimal decision threshold of 0.14 indicates that TabPFN expresses relatively lower confi-dence in its predictions, with most outputs falling below 0.5 even for patients at elevated risk. This behavior may reflect the model’s pre-training on diverse synthetic datasets with varying class distributions, resulting in probability outputs that require task-specific calibration. From a clinical work-flow perspective, TabPFN’s low optimal threshold of 0.14 translates to a more sensitive screening approach by flagging patients as at-risk for conversion when the model assigns even modest probability scores. This behavior aligns well with the clinical context of MCI to AD prediction, where the cost of missing a true conversion typically outweighs the cost of additional monitoring for false positives. In practice, a clinician using TabPFN would identify a larger pool of at risk patients for closer follow-up, with the model’s strong rank-ing ability ensuring that true converters are prioritized within this pool. The key insight is that TabPFN’s raw probability outputs should be interpreted as relative risk scores rather than absolute conversion probabilities, and deployment re-quires establishing an appropriate decision boundary through validation on a representative patient cohort. 

4. CONCLUSIONS 

This study provides a systematic evaluation of TabPFN, a foundation model for tabular data, for predicting MCI-to-AD conversion using biomarker features from the TADPOLE dataset. Our results demonstrate that foundation models of-fer meaningful advantages in data-limited clinical scenarios while also revealing important practical considerations for their deployment. TabPFN achieved high performance at small training sizes, outperforming XGBoost and other tradi-tional methods when training data was limited to 50-100 pa-tients. This data efficiency advantage addresses a fundamen-tal bottleneck in clinical AI development, where acquiring longitudinal data with complete neuroimaging and biomarker profiles requires years of patient follow-up and substantial resources. At larger sample sizes, performance converged across models, confirming that foundation models provide the greatest benefit precisely where it is most needed in data-scarce settings) such as early-phase trials, newly established clinics, and rare disease subtypes. A key contribution of this work is identifying the threshold optimization require-ment for foundation model deployment. Despite achieving strong AUC, TabPFN exhibited poor balanced classification accuracy at the standard 0.5 decision threshold due to sys-tematically low probability outputs. This finding has direct implications for clinical deployment.Practitioners should not assume that foundation model probability outputs are directly interpretable as risk estimates without validation and thresh-old tuning on representative held out data. Several limitations warrant consideration. Our evaluation used a single dataset, and generalization to other Alzheimer’s cohorts requires fur-ther validation. The exclusion of cognitive test scores, while methodologically appropriate to avoid diagnostic overlap, may limit comparison with studies that include these fea-tures. Additionally, optimal thresholds are cohort specific and would require recalibration for different clinical populations. Future research directions include validation across diverse medical domains to establish whether TabPFN’s sample ef-ficiency advantages generalize beyond Alzheimer’s disease prediction. Investigation into why foundation models produce systematically conservative probability estimates on clinical data—and whether architectural modifications or pre-training strategies could address this—represents an important avenue for improving clinical applicability. The development of hy-brid approaches that leverage foundation models for initial risk stratification while using traditional methods or clinical expertise for refined assessment may combine the strengths of both paradigms. Our findings establish that foundation models represent a practical tool for clinical prediction in data-limited scenarios, provided that an appropriate thresh-old calibration is performed. The path forward involves thoughtful integration of these models into clinical AI work-flows—not as a direct replacement for traditional methods, but as a complementary approach that excels where data scarcity has historically limited model development. For settings where large training cohorts are unavailable, founda-tion models offer a viable path toward earlier deployment of clinical decision support tools.
