## Abstract 8

Standard genome-wide association studies (GWASs) are vulnerable to confounding factors, in-9

cluding stratification, assortative mating, and dynastic effects. Family studies such as sibling-10 

based GWAS (sib-GWAS) mitigate such confounding and are becoming the tool of choice for 11 

teasing apart direct genetic effects—causal effects of one’s genotype on one’s own phenotype— 12 

from other factors. However, due in part to their smaller sample sizes, sib-GWAS allelic effect 13 

estimates are substantially more variable than standard (i.e., population-based) GWAS esti-14 

mates. The quantification of this uncertainty is essential for many uses of sib-GWAS, including 15 

polygenic scoring, causal inference (e.g., Mendelian randomization), disentangling direct from 16 

indirect familial effects, and measuring assortative mating. Here, we investigate sources of un-17 

certainty in sib-GWAS allelic effect estimators. We study their impacts on the biases of three 18 

uncertainty measurement methods, including two that are commonly used and a new resampling-19 

based approach we propose. We find that heterogeneity in allelic effects or heteroskedasticity 20 

across families (e.g., due to variation in genetic backgrounds or environments) can bias existing 21 

methods, and that this bias is more severe for small samples and rare variants. In contrast, 22 

the resampling-based approach we propose is approximately unbiased under all scenarios we 23 

considered. We validate our theoretical predictions, as well as the importance of effect hetero-24 

geneity and heteroskedasticity, using simulations and empirical analysis in the UK Biobank. In 25 

sum, this study helps understand the sources of uncertainty in family-based genotype-phenotype 26 

association studies and provides a robust method to estimate uncertainty. 27

## Introduction 28 

Typical genome-wide association studies (GWASs) designs are influenced by population stratifi-29 

cation, by assortative mating, and by parental indirect effects 1. If the goal of the GWAS is to 30 

estimate the causal effect of one’s alleles on one’s own phenotype—i.e., direct effects—then these 31 

other influences represent confounding. In spite of the risk of confounding, GWAS was adopted 32 

by the biomedical research community, largely to the exclusion of family-based study designs such 33 

as linkage mapping 2 and transmission-disequilibrium tests 3. Although these family-based com-34 

petitors are much less susceptible to confounding than GWAS, GWAS studies entail much easier 35 

recruitment of participants and better statistical power 4. However, in the last ten years, there 36 

has been a resurgence of interest in family-based designs for genetic data 5–7 , driven, among other 37 

reasons, by the awareness of residual confounding in standard GWAS 8–12 , and sometimes with 38 

the specific motivation of studying indirect familial genetic effects 13–18 , assortative mating 19,20 

> 39

or natural selection acting on direct genetic variation 5,21,22 .40 

One influential family-based design is sibling-based GWAS, sometimes termed sib-GWAS. 41 

In a sib-GWAS, the genetic differences among siblings at a given locus are compared with phe-42 

notypic differences among them. This design, like its family-based forebears, is largely robust 43 

to confounding by population stratification, assortative mating, and dynastic (i.e., parental, 44 

grandparental, etc.) indirect genetic effects 23 . Although sib-GWAS-estimated effect sizes are 45 

less susceptible to bias than traditional GWAS, they are also less precise, both because typical 46 

sample sizes are smaller and also because they are more variable than traditional GWAS even at 47 

the same overall sample size. 48 

Despite their importance, there has been little study of the uncertainty of sib-GWAS-based 49 

effect estimates. Measures of uncertainty are especially important in analyses of highly polygenic 50 

traits, which often integrate signals (and consider signal-to-noise ratio) across numerous sites 24–28 .51 

The uncertainty in sib-GWAS genetic effect estimates arises from multiple sources that require 52 

special consideration. First, if true allelic effects are heterogeneous 29 due to gene-by-gene or 53 

gene-by-environment interactions that are structured with respect to family membership, then 54 

sampling variation will be larger than it would be in the absence of this heterogeneity. Second, 55 

variation not explained by the focal locus might be heteroskedastic across families, perhaps due 56 

to differences in genetic background or environmental exposures. Third, if an allele (or a binary 57 

phenotype) is rare, then there may be few sibling comparisons that are informative about the 58 

association. 59 

Here, we focus on the form of sib-GWAS in which there are exactly two individuals in each 60 

of n sibships (see Supplementary Material S6 for a generalization to larger families). In 61 

this case, the allelic effect can be estimated by regressing the n phenotypic differences between 62 

sibling pairs on the n differences between them in genotype at a focal locus. One way to es-63 

timate uncertainty in such a case, which we call the OLS method, is to use standard ordinary 64 

least squares theory that assumes homoskedastic residuals. However, such an approach will not 65 

accommodate heteroskedasticity from heterogeneity in true effect size or from differences among 66 

families (sibships) in genetic background or environmental exposures. A second approach, some-67 

times adopted in light of problems with the OLS method, is to use a permutation distribution 68 

to quantify uncertainty 7. However, this approach estimates uncertainty assuming that the true 69 

effect size is zero. A third approach is to use resampling methods to quantify uncertainty. Here, 70

Empirical sib-GWASs in the UK Biobank 198 

We sought to evaluate our theoretical results regarding the biases of the three variance estimators 199 

using empirical data from the UK Biobank. Because the block jackknife variance estimator is 200 

asymptotically unbiased, we used it as an empirical benchmark. Specifically, we calculated the 201 

three variance estimates using sib-GWASs we performed in the UK Biobank for 11 physiological 202 

traits and 6 social or behavioral traits in a sample of 17,353 sibling pairs. For each SNP, we 203 

calculated the ratio of the permutation (or OLS) variance estimate to the block jackknife variance 204 

estimate. Ratios greater than 1 are consistent with upward bias, and ratios less than 1 are 205 

consistent with downward bias, given that the block jackknife is asymptotically unbiased. 206

## Discussion 233 

Family-based studies provide estimates of direct genetic effects with minimal bias from population 234 

stratification or other confounding factors 1,9 . Although the biasing effects of stratification and 235 

assortative mating are largely removed by the family design 23 , the heterogeneity with which they 236 

are associated can still influence estimates of standard error. 237 

We studied the behavior of three estimators of the (squared) standard error of allelic effect 238 

estimates in family GWAS. We focused on a setting with exactly two siblings per family in 239 

the main text, although we generalize this in the Supplementary Material S6 . The first 240 

method, the usual OLS standard errors applied to a regression of within-sibship phenotypic 241 

differences on within-sibship genotypic differences, is biased in the presence of heteroskedasticity. 242 

We identify heterogeneity among families—including in genetic or environmental background— 243 

as an important driver of heteroskedasticity. The second method, using the standard deviation of 244 

the permutation distribution under the null, is biased when the null hypothesis is false, i.e., when 245 

the true allelic effect is nonzero. In contrast to these two estimators, block jackknife standard 246 

errors are approximately unbiased in the scenarios we studied and can be computed easily from 247

We focus on the block jackknife here rather than leave-one-out jackknifing or bootstrapping 251 

for ease of computation. Standard leave-one-out jackknifing requires estimating the statistic 252 

of interest—here, the estimated allelic effect— n times, one for each of the n datasets that can 253 

be formed by leaving out one of the original observations. (In our setting, the observations 254 

are sibships, and n is the number of sibships.) In contrast, for block jackknife, we provided a 255 

guideline ( Eq. 10 ) for choosing the number of block jackknife samples needed to achieve a given 256 

level of accuracy, managing a tradeoff between calibration and computational costs. 257 

This reduced cost is important in GWAS contexts, where both the sample size and the number 258 

of genomic sites for which estimates are required can be very large. 259 

The reason we focus on the jackknife over bootstrapping is more practical than computational 260 

per se : the jackknife is much easier to implement in PLINK 32 , the most widely used software 261 

for GWAS. Whereas neither bootstrap nor jackknife standard errors for sibling-based GWAS are 262 

implemented in PLINK , it is easy to run PLINK analyses while excluding subjects in a user-263 

provided list. Such analyses of subsets are the key ingredient of the jackknife and the basis for 264 

our analyses of UK Biobank data here. Were bootstrap methods designed for family data to be 265 

implemented directly in PLINK or other widely used software for GWAS, we expect that they 266 

would perform well. As shown in the Supplementary Material S5 , the clustered standard 267 

errors used in some recent family studies 5 also appear to be approximately unbiased, and they 268 

are computationally efficient, but they are not implemented in widely used software for family-269 

GWAS, most notably PLINK and snipar . In the meantime, resampling approaches like block 270 

jackknife are readily available. 271 

We focused here on quantitative traits. However, we did not assume normality of errors, 272 

and we expect most of our results to carry over to binary traits. That said, when studying 273 

binary traits, often researchers prefer to estimate effects using a generalized linear model, such 274 

as logistic 36 or probit regression 37 . Although our results do not translate directly to this setting, 275 

we expect block jackknife standard errors to perform well for GLM estimates as well. The 276 

issue perhaps requiring more serious investigation is ascertainment of families into a GWAS. 277 

Whereas we assume a population-based sample, studies of binary traits such as diseases often 278 

entail intensive sampling of cases 38,39 .279 

To understand the sources of genotype-phenotype associations, we will need to accurately 280 

quantify uncertainty in family GWAS, and we will need to be able to do this in workhorse 281 

software. Informed by our investigation of drivers of uncertainty in within-family GWAS, we 282 

argue that the block jackknife based measures of uncertainty are accurate, computationally 283 

efficient, and straightforward to compute with widely available tools. 284

Application to UK Biobank data 455 

We applied the three variance estimators to sib-GWAS data from the UK Biobank 35 . The 456 

cohort construction, phenotype preprocessing, and genotype quality control followed the pipeline 457 

described in Smith, Smith et al. 9 and are summarized here. Sibling pairs were identified from 458 

the relatedness information provided by the UK Biobank: pairs were retained if their kinship 459 

coefficient estimate fell between 0.1768 and 0.3536 , and their IBS0 value exceeded 0.0012 , as 460 

estimated by KING 41 . From each resulting family group, two siblings were selected, yielding 461 

17,353 White British full sibling pairs in which each individual belongs to exactly one pair. We 462 

analyzed 17 continuous phenotypes, spanning 11 physiological traits and 6 behavioral traits. 463 

Following Smith, Smith et al. 9, we residualized individuals’ phenotypes for age and sex. Sample 464 

sizes per trait ranged from 2,163 to 17,328 sibling pairs after removing pairs with missing data. 465 

Genotypic variants were filtered using plink2 42 . Specifically, variants with INFO score below 466 

0.8, genotype missingness above 0.05 (–geno 0.05 ), minor allele count of 5 or fewer ( –mac 5 ), 467 

non-biallelic sites ( –snps-only ), or Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium p < 10 −10 (–hwe 1e-10 ) were 468 

excluded, leaving 9,607,691 variants. We focused on a set of SNPs obtained by clumping on 469 

linkage disequilibrium ( r2 < 0.1, window = 100kb ), without thresholding on p-value. This pro-470 

cedure ensures that the analyzed SNPs span a broad, approximately unselected range of allelic 471 

effect sizes, allowing assessment of estimator behavior from null to strongly associated SNPs. 472 

Clumping was performed within the sibling cohort, leaving 504,858 SNPs for analysis. 473 

Each of the three variance estimators was then computed for every SNP-trait combination, 474 

using the procedure described below. To obtain the OLS variance estimates, we performed OLS 475 

regression of within-family phenotype differences on within-family genotype differences, following 476 

the additive model ( Eq. 3 ). The variance, ̂ Var OLS [ ˆβ0], was computed analytically from the RSS 477 

of this regression, using custom scripts in R43 .478 

To obtain the permutation variance estimates, we used the standard permutation procedure 479 

in PLINK1.9 , performing 1,000 replicates per SNP in which phenotype labels were randomly 480 

swapped within sibships. The variance of the resulting empirical distribution of allelic effect 481 

estimates serves as the permutation-based variance estimate. To rank SNPs by strength of 482 

evidence for a nonzero allelic effect in Figs. 3 (A-B), S7, S8 , we obtained p-values via adaptive 483 

permutation in PLINK1.9 with a minimum of 1,000 and a maximum of 1,000,000 permutations 484 

per SNP. 485
