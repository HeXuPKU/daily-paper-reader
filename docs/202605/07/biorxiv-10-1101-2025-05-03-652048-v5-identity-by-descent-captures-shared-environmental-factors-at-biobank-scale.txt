environmental exposures and cultural practices that persist across generations. Using 20 

genomic and electronic health record data from 13,143 individuals in the Biorepository 21

# 1 Introduction 41 

Genomic studies increasingly rely on large biobanks that link genetic, clinical, and environmen-42 

tal data (Buonaiuto et al., 2025; Belbin et al., 2021; Nait Saada et al., 2020; Caggiano et al., 43 

2023). How participants in these resources are grouped shapes which patterns of variation 44 

become detectable. Identity-by-descent (IBD) supports a grouping based on the genealogical 45 

relationships among individuals, inferred directly from the genome. 46 

IBD refers to DNA segments shared by two individuals due to inheritance from a common 47 

ancestor (Palamara et al., 2012). Recombination progressively fragments these segments 48

Close relatives, first and second degree, usually share more than just genetic material: their 50 

relationships are the foundation of many family systems (Itao and Kaneko, 2021) and typically 51 

reflect shared culture, social norms, and environments. These ties extend beyond close kin: 52 

even distant relatives can share cultural traits passed through generations of slowly evolving 53 

social norms, as observed by Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman (1981; 1986). 54 

To study population structure, the common practice of categorizing individuals into discrete 55 

genetic groups faces challenges that go beyond technical limitations. Such categorizations 56 

impose artificial boundaries on what is fundamentally a continuous spectrum of human genetic 57 

variation (Barbujani and Colonna, 2010; Dobzhansky, 1962), and depend on predefined 58 

reference populations whose composition is itself the product of sampling decisions (Rosenberg 59 

et al., 2002; Barbujani et al., 1997; Maples et al., 2013). IBD-based grouping addresses these 60 

limitations by recovering population structure directly from genealogical relationships among 61 

participants, without recourse to external reference panels (Browning et al., 2018; Browning 62 

and Browning, 2011; Ralph and Coop, 2013; Caggiano et al., 2023; Gilbert et al., 2022; Naseri 63 

et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2020; Nait Saada et al., 2020; Belbin et al., 2021; Shemirani et al., 64 

2026). Recent consensus statements from the National Academies of Sciences and editors of 65 

biomedical journals converge in this direction, calling for the differentiation of genetic ancestry 66 

from race and ethnicity and supporting reference-free frameworks where possible (National 67 

Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2023; Lewis et al., 2024). Self-reported 68 

race and ethnicity categories retain practical value in U.S. clinical settings, where researchers 69 

often argue they function as proxies for social and environmental factors such as residential 70 

context and access to care (Manski et al., 2023). 71 

In this study, we used data from the Biorepository for Integrative Genomics (BIG) cohort 72 

(Buonaiuto et al., 2025) to build IBD-based communities that integrate genomic, clinical, 73 

and environmental information, providing a scalable framework to study health disparities 74 

and the co-segregation of genealogy and environment at biobank scale. The BIG resource 75 

provides comprehensive electronic health records (EHR), neighborhood-level environmental 76

2.1 Capturing population structure using information from genomic 97 

segments shared identical by descent 98 

The BIG initiative has enrolled over 42,000 participants with linked EHRs and collected more 99 

than 15,000 biosamples ( Supplementary Figure S1 ). The cohort is predominantly pediatric 100 

(87% under 18 years), spanning ages from infancy to 90 years (mean = 8.4, median = 6.2) 101

To assess population structure, we performed identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis, which 104 

infers genetic relationships directly from shared genomic segments (Zhou et al., 2020). Total 105 

pairwise IBD was calculated as the sum of the length (cM) of all shared segments between 106 

individuals. The distribution of total IBD values followed an exponential decay ( Figure 1A ), 107 

indicating that distant relatives pairs far outnumber close kinships, consistent with previous 108 

studies (Caggiano et al., 2023; Nait Saada et al., 2020; Shemirani et al., 2021). 109 

We constructed an IBD network, where nodes represent individuals (n = 13,143) and edge 110 

lengths are inversely proportional to shared IBD. The network’s global density (0.018) and 111 

average degree (241.15) indicate a well-connected structure. Four main dense clusters are 112 

visible, surrounded by smaller peripheral groups ( Figure 1B ). Using the Leiden algorithm 113 

(Anuar et al., 2021), we applied two rounds of hierarchical community detection. The first 114 

iteration identified four major communities (C1–C4), two of which contained more than 115 

5,000 individuals. Subsequent iterations within each community revealed 17 subcommunities 116 

(Figure 1C ). 117 

The community classification corresponds well with the population structure revealed 118 

by PCA of autosomal genetic markers, with clear separation between communities ( Figure 119 

1D ). This concordance between discrete community assignments and continuous genetic 120 

variation, both inferred from the same genomic data, is consistent with communities reflecting 121 

non-arbitrary genealogical structure rather than artificial partitions. Despite this overall 122 

trend, we also notice individuals positioned between the main groups in the PCA space, 123 

as well as some individuals from one community who fall within the region predominantly 124 

occupied by a different community. As an example, C1 individuals in C2 space would be 125 

assigned to C2 based on their position in the PCA-based classification; however, the IBD-126 

based approach places them in C1 due to detected signals of relatedness (up to third degree), 127 

highlighting the additional power of relationship-based community detection over purely 128 

dimensional-reduction-based methods. 129

2.2 IBD communities reflect geographical ancestry with fine-scale 137 

substructure 138 

Having determined IBD-based communities, we next asked whether they corresponded to global 139 

ancestry previously inferred from reference populations (Buonaiuto et al., 2025). We found 140 

enrichment of EUR-inferred ancestry (91 %) in C1, AFR-inferred (49 %) with EUR–AFR 141 

admixed components (39 %) in C2, EUR–AMR admixed (51 %) with multi-component 142 

admixture (43 %) in C3, and EAS-inferred (81%) in C4 ( Figure 2A ). Community and 143 

ancestry, both inferred from the same genomic data, showed strong concordance (Cramér’s V 144 

= 0.81). 145 

We characterized the relationship between ancestry and network topology by computing 146 

each BIG individual’s connectivity (degree link strength) and betweenness (path-based cen-147 

trality). Stratification by ancestry revealed that individuals with predominantly EUR-inferred 148 

ancestry in C1, predominantly AFR-inferred in C2, and predominantly AMR-inferred in C3 149 

tend to act as hub–bridge nodes, mediating many connections and shortest paths ( Figure 150 

2B ). In contrast, individuals with multi-component ancestry inference exhibit lower connec-151 

tivity and betweenness, occupying more peripheral network positions. Those with multi-way 152 

(≥3 components) ancestry profiles consistently fall into low-degree, low-betweenness niches, 153 

while certain moderately connected nodes display elevated betweenness, acting as critical 154 

inter-community bridges. C4 communities were not analyzed due to their smaller size. 155

between BIG subcommunities and the HGDP-1kGP reference panels. The IBD sharing 157 

patterns align with the ancestry correlations we previously inferred ( Figure 2C ). However, 158 

the fine-scale subcommunity structure suggests subtle demographic events that shaped genetic 159 

diversity within broader continental groups. For example, subcommunity C3b showed a 160 

stronger relationship with the Puerto Rican population, characterized by its three-way ancestry 161 

pattern (Browning et al., 2018; Mas-Sandoval et al., 2023), while C3a was more closely 162 

associated with Peruvian and other AMR populations. This subdivision suggests multiple 163 

waves or routes of population movement and mixing within C3. Likewise, the connections 164 

within C2 (predominantly enriched in AFR-inferred ancestry) of C2g with Surui (HGDP 165 

population from America superpopulation) might indicate shared ancestry components that 166 

transcend simple continental divisions, possibly reflecting more recent demographic events. 167 

After characterizing community structure through genetic measures, we next examined 168 

how communities aligned with participants’ self-reported race and ethnicity ( Figure 2D-E ). 169 

Similar to what was observed for IBD sharing with reference populations, while community-170 

level patterns aligned with self-reported categories as expected for an urban U.S. cohort 171 

with documented residential segregation history (C1: 91% white, C2: 97% Black/African 172 

American, C4: 62% Asian), subcommunity analysis reveals important nuances. Most notably, 173 

C3 subcommunities diverge markedly. In terms of self-reported race, C3a is predominantly 174 

Other race (73%), whereas C3b shows a three-way split across Other race (33%), White 175 

(21%), and Black/African American (21%). In terms of self-reported ethnicity, individuals 176 

in C3a were more likely to self-identify as Hispanic or Latino (72.3%), while those in C3b 177 

predominantly self-report as Not Hispanic or Latino (69.6%). 178

2.3 IBD sharing reflects shared environments in the BIG cohort 186 

To elucidate the spatial distribution of genetic relationships, we examined whether subcom-187 

munities exhibit distinct geographical patterns and how these relate to IBD. Specifically, we 188 

geolocated BIG participants’ ZIP codes at the neighborhood level using publicly available spa-189 

tial polygons (Pebesma and Bivand, 2018), and then analyzed whether genetically determined 190 

subcommunities cluster geographically. We detected a strong association between genealogical 191 

and geographical distances (exponential fit R2 = 0 .969 , p-value < 0.0001, Figure 3A , Sup-192 

plementary Figure S3 ). Nearly half (49%) of close relatives (first- and second-degree) lived 193 

in the same neighborhood, while the remaining pairs typically resided 4-6 km apart. Third-194 

and fourth-degree relatives showed greater separation, though still within 20 km, with 34.3% 195 

sharing the same neighborhood. Beyond the seventh or eighth degree, geographic distance 196 

increased sharply, only 4.45% of eighth-degree or more distant relatives lived in the same 197 

neighborhood. 198 

We used ZIP codes to link communities to social determinants of health (SDoH), i.e., 199 

non-medical factors influencing health outcomes. All SDoH values were obtained from 200 

publicly available datasets. Complete results, including interactive visualizations and detailed 201 

methodology, are available on our dedicated website ( https://github.com/MarsicoFL/BIG. 202 

Communities ). We quantified SDoH similarity across subcommunities using mean SDoH 203 

and found that subcommunities within the same community shared highly similar social 204 

environments, while communities differed markedly from each other ( Figure 3B ). This 205 

pattern held even for C1, whose subcommunities maintained 96% SDoH similarity despite 206 

being the most geographically dispersed (median distance 88.6 km between individuals vs. 207 

18.9-22.1 km for C2 and C3, Supplementary Figure S4B ). Conversely, C2 exhibited the 208 

greatest social heterogeneity among its subcommunities while occupying a smaller geographic 209

2.4 Health condition patterns in subcommunities reflect differential 233 

environmental exposure 234 

Having assessed the differential environmental exposures across communities and subcom-235 

munities, we next examined whether these differences translate into disparities in disease 236 

incidence. We mapped the ICD-9/10 codes from the BIG electronic health records to PheCode 237 

categories and found overall distinct disease prevalence patterns across communities ( Figure 238 

4B ). Community-level analysis revealed differences in environmentally-influenced conditions 239 

(Tran et al., 2023; Eisenberg et al., 2007). 240 

In this Memphis cohort, C2 showed a particularly high prevalence of respiratory diseases 241 

(71.4%), infections (56.8%), and dermatological conditions (50.7%). In contrast, conditions 242 

with stronger genetic components (Huang et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2021), including neoplasms, 243 

neurological disorders, and congenital disorders, show more homogeneous distributions across 244 

communities. We evaluated disease prevalence across the aforementioned six categories at 245 

the subcommunity level. Although these categories are broad and warrant detailed case-246 

by-case analysis, we observed a clear pattern: Communities showed variable prevalence 247 

for environmentally-driven diseases but consistent prevalence for genetically-driven diseases, 248 

suggesting that differential environmental exposures influence disease risk ( Figure 4D ). In line 249 

with what has been described so far, C2 has the largest variance across subcommunities in many 250 

cases, especially in congenital disorders that appeared generally similar across communities, 251 

with some cases, C2g and C2i, exhibiting a lower prevalence of congenital conditions. 252 

Combining the population structure, environmental factors, and health conditions, we 253 

detected that fine particulate matter exposure was strongly associated with the respiratory 254 

disease burden across subcommunities: PM 2.5 explained most of the variation in respiratory 255 

prevalence (Spearman ρ = 0 .85 , R2 = 0 .90 ), whereas no meaningful association was observed 256 

for neurological disorders ( ρ = 0 .10 , R2 = 0 .02 ; Figure 4C ). 257 

Importantly, individuals across all subcommunities showed consistent healthcare access, 258 

with a median of 8.7 visits annually and 38 total visits per individual, suggesting that 259

2.5 Decomposing the sources of subcommunity health disparities 262 

Up to this point, we have established that IBD-defined communities reflect ancestry and 263 

geography, with subcommunities sharing environmental exposures and showing enrichment for 264 

specific health conditions. Now, we ask whether we can take a further step by disentangling the 265 

sources of these differences. Specifically: how much is attributable to measured environmental 266 

and socioeconomic factors, and how much remains unexplained? To answer this, we imple-267 

mented an interventional mediation analysis treating IBD subcommunity membership as the 268 

exposure, with age and sex as baseline covariates ( Figure 5A ). We focused on four pediatric-269 

relevant outcomes selected a priori based on epidemiological evidence linking environmental 270 

exposures (PM 2.5 , poverty) to disease risk: asthma (n=1,757), dermatitis (n=742), epilepsy 271 

(n=1,502), and influenza (n=3,378) ( Figure 5B ). This focused, hypothesis-driven design 272 

prioritizes statistical precision in the mediation decomposition over exploratory breadth. For 273 

each outcome, we specified a priori a parsimonious set of neighborhood-level environmental 274 

mediators: PM 2.5 air pollution and poverty (PPOV) rate for respiratory and dermatological 275 

conditions; obesity prevalence, smoking prevalence, and PPOV for infections. 276 

First, we estimated for each subcommunity the absolute difference of disease risk (risk 277 

difference, RD, or total effect) relative to subcommunity C1c, arbitrarily chosen as a reference, 278 

for comparative purposes. All nine C2 subcommunities exhibited a significantly elevated risk 279 

of disease prevalence for asthma (mean RD = +9.9 percentage points [pp]; range: 7.6–16.8 280 

pp), dermatitis (mean RD = +6.5 pp; range: 3.8–8.1 pp) and influenza (mean RD = +5.9 281 

pp; range: 3.9–11.0 pp), with 95% confidence intervals excluding zero ( Figure 5C ). Epilepsy 282 

showed an opposite pattern, with some C2 subcommunities exhibiting modestly lower risk 283 

near zero (mean RD = −2.6 pp; range: −6.7 to −0.7 pp). 284

explains only approximately one-third of the excess disease burden compared to two-thirds 290 

explained by IF. EME ranged from 2.7 to 4.2 pp for asthma, 1.3 to 2.2 pp for dermatitis, and 291

accounted for 33% of the total effect for asthma, 26% for dermatitis, and 28% for influenza. In 293 

contrast IF, the residual risk elevation not explained by measured mediators, was substantial: 294 

4.5 to 12.6 pp for asthma, 2.5 to 6.2 pp for dermatitis, and 2.3 to 9.1 pp for influenza. 295 

Subcommunity C2i exhibited the largest total and residual effects for asthma (TE = 16.8 296 

pp; EME = 12.6 pp; IF = 4.2 pp), while C2g showed the highest effects for influenza (TE = 297 

11.0 pp; EME = 9.1 pp; IF = 1.9 pp). On average, IBD-defined subcommunities retained 298 

more than two-thirds independent component (ranging from 67% for asthma to 74% for 299 

dermatitis) (Supplementary Figure S7 ). Overall, we concluded that the IF likely reflects a 300 

combination of genetic factors, unmeasured environmental exposures (e.g., diet, indoor air 301 

quality, healthcare utilization patterns), and social/cultural determinants that co-segregate 302 

with fine-scale IBD-based relatedness. 303

but not for influenza). Because self-reported race may be partly downstream of community 310

# 3 Discussion 342 

In this work, we have shown that IBD can be used to group individuals without relying on 343 

reference populations, and that in our cohort, the grouping is informative about environmental 344 

and population health factors. Our findings demonstrate that IBD-based clustering captures 345 

population structure at multiple scales, from continental ancestry patterns to fine-scale genetic 346 

subcommunities that reflect shared environmental exposures and social determinants of health. 347 

Beyond broad inter-community differences, we observed distinct health condition prevalence 348 

among subcommunities, suggesting that genetic relatedness correlates with both biological and 349 

environmental factors influencing disease risk. Importantly, incorporating IBD information 350 

into genotype-phenotype models accounts for both genetic similarity and shared environmental 351 

effects, providing a more comprehensive framework for association studies. Together, these 352 

results demonstrate the utility of considering both close and distant relationships between 353 

individuals for targeted public health interventions. 354 

Recent discussions have focused on the risks of conflating race and ethnicity with genetic 355 

ancestry (Kozlov, 2024; National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2023; 356 

Popejoy, 2021; Carlson et al., 2022; Lewis et al., 2022, 2024). Typically, genetic studies 357 

categorize individuals based on continental-level labels; yet such classifications often overlook 358 

actual genetic relatedness. Our previous work demonstrated instances where individuals from 359 

different self-reported racial categories were closely related (i.e., second degree) (Buonaiuto 360 

et al., 2025). Similarly, ancestry-based classifications can separate genetically related indi-361 

viduals into distinct groups, particularly in cases involving admixed ancestry (Mas-Sandoval 362 

et al., 2023). In contrast, IBD-based hierarchical community detection allows for grouping 363 

individuals by their direct genetic relationships without relying on externally imposed labels 364 

based on arbitrarily defined reference populations. Although self-reported race is frequently 365

4.7 Network, community detection and topology 531 

A genetic network was constructed in which each BIG participant was represented as a 532 

node, and edges were defined based on the total genome-wide IBD segment sharing. Edge 533 

weights were proportional to the total IBD shared between individuals. To build this network, 534 

pairwise IBD data were processed and filtered, excluding close-kin pairs following Dai et al. 535 

(2020). For visualization and exploratory analyses, the network was represented using the 536 

Fruchterman-Reingold layout. 537 

We evaluated the Louvain and Leiden algorithms for community detection (Anuar et al., 538 

2021). Both were implemented using a multilevel approach. This means that the algorithms 539 

were run iteratively, initially identifying broad-scale genetic structures and subsequently 540 

resolving increasingly finer-scale subcommunities. Comparative analysis revealed an Adjusted 541 

Rand Index (ARI) of 0.975, indicating high similarity between community assignments from 542 

both methods. However, we selected the Leiden algorithm due to its improved resolution, 543 

robustness, and ability to avoid disconnected communities, a known limitation of Louvain 544 

(Anuar et al., 2021). 545

4.11 Interventional effect analysis 646 

We estimated interventional direct and indirect effects (Pearl, 2001; VanderWeele and Vanstee-647 

landt, 2014; Vansteelandt and Daniel, 2017) for IBD subcommunity ( C; reference C1c)648 

on binary health outcomes ( H) using g-computation (Robins, 1986) on the risk-difference 649 

scale. Baseline covariates ( D) included age and sex (plus self-reported race in sensitivity 650 

analyses). Environmental mediators ( E) were selected a priori based on epidemiological 651 

evidence (Johnson et al., 2021; Mersha and Abebe, 2015) and assessed for collinearity prior 652 

to model specification: for asthma and dermatitis, E = {PM 2.5, PPOV }; for influenza, E =653 

{OBESITY , CSMOKING , PPOV }; for epilepsy, E = {UNEMPLOY , OBESITY , PPOV }.654 

The interventional decomposition adopted here partitions risk differences under identification 655 

assumptions that we cannot test from these observational data. IBD-defined community 656 

membership ( C) is a stable, genealogically determined characteristic that does not vary with 657 

outcomes, ruling out reverse association from H to C. Neighborhood exposures ( E) at ZIP-658 

code resolution were largely fixed during the period in which pediatric outcomes accumulated. 659 

Unmeasured factors, including individual mobility, indoor exposures, healthcare utilization, 660 

and cultural or behavioral characteristics, may confound the mediator-outcome relationship 661 

and are not addressed by our specification. We therefore interpret the resulting partition (TE, 662 

EME, IF) descriptively, as the decomposition of risk differences that holds under the specified 663 

DAG and identification assumptions; the E-value sensitivity analysis (VanderWeele and Ding, 664 

2017) characterizes the strength of unmeasured confounding required to overturn the observed 665 

patterns. 666 

Estimation used cross-fitted g-computation ( K = 4 folds). In each fold we fit (i) a 667 

logistic outcome regression q(C, D, E ) with natural splines for continuous covariates and factor 668 

indicators for categorical covariates, and (ii) a joint mediator model via sequential factorization 669 

(Imai et al., 2010) using Gaussian GLMs on automatically selected transformation scales (logit 670 

for bounded mediators, log for right-skewed, identity otherwise). 671
