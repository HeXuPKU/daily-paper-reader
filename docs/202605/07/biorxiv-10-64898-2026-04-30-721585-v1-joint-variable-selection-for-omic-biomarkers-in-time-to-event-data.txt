# Abstract 1

The incidence of the vast majority of neurodegenerative, cancer, and metabolic 2

diseases generally increases exponentially with age. In large-scale biobanks, link- 3

ing time-to-diagnosis information in electronic health records to multiple genomic 4

(“multiomics”) measures has the potential to reveal the genes and biological path- 5

ways involved in the disease onset and progression. To date, association testing has 6

commonly been conducted by testing one variable at a time using semiparametric 7

Cox proportional hazards (CoxPH) models, which ignores correlation structure and 8

increases the risk of false discoveries. To address these issues, we introduce a novel fully 9

parametric Bayesian computational method, vampW, based on the Vector Approxi- 10 

mate Message Passing framework applied to a Weibull model. vampW jointly models 11 

correlated features, while providing an interpretable hazard structure, producing a con- 12 

tinuous survival curve, and incorporating prior knowledge. In an extensive simulation 13 

study, we demonstrate that joint modeling of omics data and time-to-event outcomes 14 

with vampW, substantially reduces false discoveries in comparison to marginal testing 15 

and other forms of joint CoxPH models. In 53,018 individuals from the UK Biobank, 16 

vampW identifies 219 protein associations with 24 disease outcomes, most of which 17 

are not among the top marginal discoveries. We further correct protein levels for expo- 18 

nential age effects, identifying 1,308 associations and highlighting the sensitivity of the 19 

analysis to age-correction methodology. Our findings replicate in independent cohorts 20 

using different measurement technologies, within data from Iceland and a novel Gen- 21 

eration Scotland proteomics dataset. vampW also achieves significant improvement in 22 

the prediction of disease onset times: across 14 outcomes, it reduces the root mean 23 

squared error by over 32% and 26%, when compared to CoxPH variants and the 24 

deep learning approach DeepSurv, respectively, while maintaining predictive utility 25 

in minority populations. In summary, vampW offers accurate and interpretable vari- 26 

able selection and out-of-sample prediction within a single computational framework, 27 

making it a powerful tool for dissecting the genomic architecture of common complex 28 

disease onset. 29

# Introduction 32 

Aging is the primary risk factor for the vast majority of common chronic, noncommu- 33 

nicable diseases that cause illness and death worldwide. The relationship is generally 34 

exponential, where disease risk and incidence increase sharply with advancing age [1]. 35 

The potential genes and pathways involved in disease onset and progression can be 36 

identified by associating time-to-diagnosis information contained within electronic 37 

health records to multiple genomic (“multiomics”) measures [2, 3]. The prognostic 38 

biomarkers identified can then provide predictors of age at disease onset, facilitating 39 

patient screening programs and preventative medicine [2, 3]. 40 

To date, the two main tasks in time-to-event modeling of multiomics data— 41 

association testing and out-of-sample prediction of disease onset age—are almost 42 

always conducted using semiparametric Cox proportional hazards (PH) models. More 43 

precisely, association testing of disease onset age in genomic studies has predominantly 44 

been conducted with marginal CoxPH testing [2, 4, 5]. This corresponds to analyzing 45 

one genomic feature at a time and it ignores pervasive correlations among genomic 46 

measures [6], making it difficult to identify which proteins are most likely driving the 47 

observed patterns, with an increased risk of false discoveries [7]. For prediction tasks, 48 

penalized CoxPH models [8] are frequently employed [2, 9], enabling the simultaneous 49 

modeling of many markers while shrinking or zeroing out a subset of coefficients. Cross- 50 

validation is commonly used to select the optimal level of penalization and stability 51 

selection [10] can further be applied to reduce the impact of stochastic variability in 52 

model fitting when identifying associated predictors. In practice, this involves fitting 53 

multiple models with fixed hyperparameters and selecting only stable features—those 54 

that are included in a significant proportion of the models. The CoxPH model relies 55 

on a partial likelihood that ignores the baseline hazard and the exact spacing between 56 

events. While this makes semi-parametric Cox regression robust and widely applica- 57 

ble, it is statistically less efficient because it depends only on the ordering of event 58 

times rather than their absolute values. Hence, it does not provide direct predictions 59 

of absolute event times. Instead, it yields relative risk scores with respect to other 60 

individuals in the dataset. 61 

Here, we provide an alternative to semi-parametric models by introducing vampW, 62 

a joint estimation procedure based on Vector Approximate Message Passing (VAMP) 63 

[11, 12] that employs a fully parametric Weibull survival model. The methodologi- 64 

cal novelty of vampW lies in the introduction of Weibull channel denoisers and in 65 

handling right-censoring. Using large-scale proteomics data linked to electronic health 66 

records [3, 13, 14], we conduct extensive simulations and benchmarking analyses to 67 

demonstrate that joint modeling of time-to-event outcomes using vampW substantially 68 

reduces false-positive protein associations, as compared to conventional one-protein- 69 

at-a-time testing and other forms of CoxPH models. Applying vampW to 24 disease 70 

outcomes in the UK Biobank, we identify 1,308 and 219 protein–disease associations 71 

in models with and without exponential age correction, respectively, highlighting the 72 

sensitivity of protein–outcome associations to the choice of age-correction method. 73 

In addition to identifying several well-established biomarkers, such as APOE with 74 

Alzheimer, REN with hypertension and KLK3 with prostate cancer, we find novel 75

Within a single framework, vampW performs variable selection using Posterior 121

viously been used for these data [2, 4, 5], the key differences are that within vampW: 126 

(i) all proteins are modeled jointly, capturing combined effects and controlling for 127 

correlations among plasma protein abundances; (ii) an interpretable hazard structure 128 

is used where depending on the value of its shape parameter, the hazard rate can 129 

increase, decrease, or remain constant over time; (iii) a full-likelihood exploits the 130 

actual event times directly, capturing the probability of the observed data under the 131 

complete model specification; (iv) a continuous survival curve can generate long-term 132 

survival predictions because its parametric form is defined over the full time domain, 133 

whereas the Cox model paired with the Breslow estimator yields a piecewise-defined 134 

baseline hazard; and (v) prior knowledge or specific information can be incorporated 135 

within the Bayesian framework and posterior mean estimates are produced together 136 

with credible intervals, allowing for explicit quantification of uncertainty. 137 

## Simulation study in UKB PPP 138 

We begin by benchmarking vampW against several state-of-the-art variable selection 139 

approaches within a semi-synthetic simulation study. Using the 2,924 observed protein 140 

levels from the UKB PPP dataset measured in 53,018 individuals, we simulate artificial 141 

phenotypic outcomes in 24 scenarios (see Methods for details). The varied parameters 142 

are: (i) the proportion of variance explained by biological markers, (ii) the proportion 143 

of censored individuals, (iii) the phenotype distribution (Weibull, ExpGamma), (iv) 144 

the prior distribution of the slab part of the regression coefficients (Normal, Laplace), 145 

and (v) the ExpGamma distribution parameter. Each scenario is repeated 50 times to 146 

obtain independent realizations of the simulated data, and train/test splits are resam- 147 

pled at a 90%/10% ratio across these realizations. We evaluate vampW against several 148 

approaches: one-protein-at-a-time testing (CoxPH marginal); non-penalized CoxPH 149 

fitted to all markers jointly (CoxPH); joint LASSO-penalized CoxPH with cross- 150 

validation and stability selection (CoxPH LASSO); and the deep learning approach 151 

DeepSurv (see Methods for details). We assess the ability of each method to recover the 152 

simulated causal variables and the out-of-sample time-to-event observation prediction 153

# Discussion 357 

In this work, we develop the novel vampW algorithm for Bayesian variable selec- 358 

tion under the Weibull model. Applying our approach to the largest proteomics data 359 

available to date, we demonstrate its capability to jointly fit thousands of plasma 360 

proteomics markers across tens of thousands of individuals from the UK Biobank, 361 

revealing new disease biomarkers. Our simulation study shows that vampW out- 362 

performs state-of-the-art methods for variable selection, reducing false discoveries 363 

compared to marginal testing and improving the true positive rate relative to multi- 364 

ple forms of CoxPH models. When analyzing real health-related outcomes from the 365 

UK Biobank, vampW identifies 1308 and 219 associations between protein levels and 366 

human complex diseases, for models with and without exponential age correction. The 367 

models exhibit a 69% and 68% replication rate in an independent Icelandic cohort 368 

using a different technology, accounting for overlap in proteins and outcomes between 369 

the studies. The replication of our findings across different technologies is further 370 

supported by analysis in Generation Scotland proteomics data. 371

## vampW algorithm and its implementation 443 

To infer the parameters of Equations 5 and 6, we propose an algorithm that we call 444 

vampW, which is based on Vector Approximate Message Passing (VAMP) for gener- 445 

alized linear models [11, 12], where the outcome variable is observed through a noisy 446 

and non-linear Weibull channel. It adaptively estimates the prior-specific parame- 447 

ters Θ = {λ, (πi)Li=1 , (σ2 

> i

)Li=1 } via a Bayesian Expectation-Maximization procedure, as 448 

introduced in EM-VAMP [33]. vampW introduces a novel approach for handling cen- 449 

sored data, as well as denoisers derived specifically for Weibull link functions and the 450 

corresponding expectation-maximization updates. We highlight that no existing AMP 451 

method is able to address the challenge of censored data. 452 

As seen in Algorithm 1, vampW consists of two denoising steps and two (non- 453 

linear) minimum mean square error (MMSE) estimation steps, applied separately to 454 

the vector of effect sizes β and to the latent predictor. The denoising step accounts for 455 

the prior properties of the model when considering a noisy estimate of either β or the 456 

latent predictor, while the MMSE step further refines the estimates by taking the cor- 457 

relation structure among the columns in the design matrix X into account. The key 458 

feature is the so-called Onsager correction , which is added to ensure the asymptotic 459 

normality of the noise corrupting the algorithm’s estimates of β at every iteration, 460 

important for the subsequent feature selection. Following [16], we employ the Hutchin- 461 

son estimator [34] to evaluate the trace of the inverse of regularized Gram matrices, 462 

and we warm-start the conjugate gradient (CG) algorithm approximating the solution 463 

of the linear system (line 32 in Algorithm 1). The CG solver is initialized from the 464 

value at convergence in the previous iteration, which leads to reduced computational 465 

time. Compared to the gVAMP algorithm introduced by [16] for a linear model with- 466 

out censoring, vampW derives a non-linear Weibull model within the AMP framework 467 

and incorporates censoring information into the process (as detailed in the paragraph 468 

below), thus requiring two denoising steps and two non-linear MMSE estimation steps. 469 

A direct application of generalized EM-VAMP to real genomics, epigenetics and 470 

proteomics measures may lead to signal-estimation divergence, primarily due to viola- 471 

tions of right-rotational invariance in the data design matrix, one of the assumptions 472 

in the original EM-VAMP algorithm. To mitigate these stability issues, we perform 473 

damping of denoised signals, as seen on line 11 of Algorithm 1, where ρ ∈ (0 , 1) denotes 474 

the damping factor. This approach makes the algorithm take smaller steps when learn- 475 

ing β. In order to further stabilize convergence and improve prediction performance, 476 

we employ damping autotuning [35]: we iteratively reduce the damping by the factor 477 

cρ while the training prediction, using signal estimates from the denoising step ˆβ1, 478 

increases over the last iteration, measured by the concordance index (C-index), as seen 479 

in line 12 of Algorithm 1. Thus, in terms of model stability, although the data appli- 480 

cations described below have design matrices that violate the assumptions required 481 

for Bayes optimality of our algorithm, introducing damping and damping autotuning 482 

empirically results in strong predictive performance. The damping choices affect the 483 

convergence properties when searching over the parameter space, as too strong damp- 484 

ing may cause the algorithm to get stuck in local minima; and thus we propose a grid 485 

search of starting parameters which is facilitated by the efficient implementation of 486 

our algorithm. 487

## Replication in Generation Scotland proteomics 598 

Generation Scotland cohort 599 

We sought replication of a subset of the vampW findings in an independent dataset, 600 

Generation Scotland (GS). GS is an epidemiological study consisting of approximately 601 

24,000 volunteers from across Scotland, aged 17–99 years old at recruitment between 602 

2006 and 2011 [37]. Blood samples were collected via venepuncture during the baseline 603 

clinic appointment for over 20,000 volunteers, alongside health, cognitive, and lifestyle 604 

questionnaires. Participants consented to electronic-health records linkage to both 605 

primary and secondary care data, allowing analyses of incident disease. 606 

Mass spectrometry proteomics 607 

Circulating proteins in GS were measured from serum using a high flow-rate liquid 608 

chromatography tandem mass spectrometry, which generated data for 325 unique, 609 

high abundance proteins across 18,826 participants. The sample preparation process 610 

has been described before [38–41]. 5 μL of serum samples were added to a solution of 611 

50 μL of 8 M urea and 0.1 M ammonium bicarbonate at pH 8.0 to have the proteins 612 

denatured. Subsequently, the proteins were reduced using 5 μL of 50 mM dithiothreitol 613 

for 1 hour at 30 °C and alkylated with 5 μL of 100 mM iodoacetamide for 30 minutes in 614 

the dark. The sample was then diluted with 340 μL of 0.1 M ammonium bicarbonate to 615 

a concentration of 1.5 M urea. The next step was the trypsinisation of the proteins and 616 

200 μL of the solution was used, and the proteins were digested overnight with trypsin 617 

(12.5 μL, 0.1 μg/ μL) at 37 °C at a 1/40 trypsin/total protein ratio. The digestion was 618 

quenched with the addition of 25 μL of 0.1% v/v formic acid (FA). The peptides were 619 

cleaned up with C18 96-well plates, eluted with 50% v/v acetonitrile (ACN), dried 620 

by a vacuum concentrator and redissolved in 50 μL of 0.1% v/v formic acid (FA) for 621 

processing by liquid chromatography (LC)-MS. Raw data were analyzed by DIA-NN 622 

[42] as described previously [41]. For 224 proteins with <20% missingness (i.e., <20% 623 

of participants lacked a measured protein level), the missing values were imputed after 624 

processing using softImpute method (2.89% imputed values in total). The remaining 625 

101 proteins were subset to individuals with measured protein levels. All proteins were 626 

subsequently rank-based inverse normalised prior to downstream analysis. 627 

Disease diagnosis information and statistical analysis 628

tension, and prostate cancer) was obtained through linkage to primary and secondary 630

Initialization of vampW 679 

The vampW framework allows several parameters to be specified before running the 680 

algorithm. Some of these are estimated from the data, while others influence con- 681 

vergence behavior. In our simulation study, we intentionally initialize vampW with a 682 

mis-specified prior to demonstrate that the algorithm can adapt and learn the prior 683 

from the data. Following the prior definition in Equation 6, we initialize the slab part 684 

of the prior with unit variance and sparsity λ = 0 .3. While the majority of traits uti- 685 

lize a unit slab part variance, a smaller variance of 0 .01 is applied to specific diverged 686 

scenarios. In our analysis, we observe better stability of the algorithm when using only 687 

1 slab component ( L = 1). Furthermore, we initialize the α parameter in the Weibull 688 

model to 3 (Equation 5). We fix the damping factor at 0.3 and train the algorithm 689 

for 30 iterations. Based on our observations, this number of iterations is sufficient for 690 

the hyperparameters to stabilize. Finally, we choose the best iteration based on train 691 

concordance index after 5 warm up iterations. 692 

We initialize vampW for all 24 UK Biobank traits with a consistent prior struc- 693 

ture, setting the initial shape parameter α = 3 and sparsity rate λ = 0 .3 and only 694 

one mixture component in the slab part. While the majority of traits utilize a slab 695 

variance of 0 .01, a wider variance of 1 is applied to specific phenotypes such as hyper- 696 

tension and death. The global shrinkage parameter ρ is initialized between 0 .3 and 697

## Benchmarking methods 706 

We evaluate vampW against state-of-the-art survival analysis approaches, specifi- 707 

cally: (i) one-at-a-time marginal CoxPH testing (CoxPH marginal); (ii) non-penalized 708 

CoxPH fitted to all markers simultaneously (CoxPH); (iii) LASSO-penalized CoxPH 709 

[8] with cross-validation and stability selection (CoxPH LASSO); and (iv) DeepSurv 710 

[15]. One-protein-at-a-time testing (CoxPH marginal) is among the most commonly 711 

used approaches for variable selection. Joint models offer an alternative which fit each 712 

marker in the structural context of all other markers. Furthermore, a penalized model 713 

(CoxPH LASSO) enables joint selection of markers while shrinking or zeroing out some 714 

coefficients. Cross-validation is used to determine the optimal level of penalization, and 715 

stability selection is then applied to identify the most robust predictors and reduce the 716 

impact of stochastic variability. DeepSurv represents one of the most commonly used 717

from 10 −4 to 10 0. After selecting the optimal penalizer, we perform stability selection 738 

[10] by fitting 100 parallel penalized regressions, each trained on a random 75% subset 739 

of the data. We then retain only the stable variables appearing in at least 90% of the 740 

models. Finally, we fit a non-penalized CoxPH model from lifelines using only these 741 

stable features, estimating the baseline hazard via the Breslow method to obtain the 742 

final refined regression coefficients and their corresponding p-values. 743
