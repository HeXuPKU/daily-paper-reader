Abstract 

The Positional Burrows–Wheeler Transform (PBWT) is a fundamental data structure for the efficient representation and analysis of large-scale haplotype panels. For a panel of h sequences {S1, . . . , S h}

over m sites, a key operation is the ϕj (i) query, which returns the haplotype index immediately preceding Si in co-lexicographic order at site j. Efficient support for k iterative queries ϕ1, . . . , ϕ k is essential for haplotype matching and variation analysis. In this work, we introduce a simple and novel decomposition scheme that decomposes each haplotype row into sub-intervals, called refined segments , within which a haplotype’s co-lexicographic predecessor for the sites remains unchanged. We show that refined segments satisfy two key properties: (i) each segment [b, e ] associated with Si overlaps with at most a constant number of segments of 

Sϕe(i), and (ii) the total number of segments is bounded by O(˜ r + h), where ˜r denotes the number of runs in the PBWT. Building on this decomposition, we present two space–time tradeoffs for supporting k iterative ϕ queries: (i) a structure using O(( ˜r + h) log n) bits of space that answers k

iterative queries in O(log log w min (m, h) + k) time, where n = m · h, and (ii) a more compact structure using O(˜ r log h + h log n) bits of space that supports queries in O(k log log w h) time. Prior to our work, supporting these queries required O(( ˜r + h) log n) bits of space and O(k ·

log log w m) time. Our second tradeoff is expected to be effective in practice for modern genomic datasets, where the number h of haplotypes is typically much smaller than the number m of sites.

## 1 Introduction 

The management of large-scale genomic datasets is a central challenge in bioinformatics. A typical component of these resources is the haplotype panel, which captures genetic variation across a population. Such panels consist of h sequences (haplotypes) observed at m variant sites and are conventionally represented as an h × m matrix, where rows correspond to haplotypes and columns to genomic positions. Haplotype panels are used to support several common genomic applications, including the computation of matching statistics [10 , 3], the identification of minimal positional substring covers (MPSC) [ 3], and the search for 

set-maximal exact matches (SMEMs) [10, 3]. To address the computational demands of these applications, Richard Durbin introduced the Positional Burrows–Wheeler Transform (PBWT) [ 11 ]. The PBWT is a data structure inspired by suffix array techniques [ 17 ] and comprises two main components: the prefix array (PA) and the PBWT matrix itself. A ϕj (i) query returns the index immediately preceding i in the j-th column of the PA. These three operations together serve as the core primitives for the fundamental haplotype panel queries described earlier. Although the PBWT supports efficient matching, storing the full PA requires substantial memory for large datasets such as the UK Biobank [ 15 ]. To mitigate this, the μ-PBWT [ 10 ]was introduced, using run-length encoding (RLE) to reduce the space requirement from 

O(m · h) to O( ˜r) words, where ˜r denotes the number of runs in the PBWT matrix. However, this compression comes at a cost: steppings and ϕ queries are no longer constant-time, typically requiring predecessor or successor queries at each step [10]. Recently, Bonizzoni et al. [ 4] showed that forward and backward stepping can be sup-ported in constant time while preserving the O( ˜r) space bound. Their approach splits PBWT runs into at most 2 ˜r sub-runs and leverages fractional cascading [8 ]—a technique originally developed for computational geometry—to enable constant-time forward stepping and backward stepping between sites. This result motivates the search for an equally efficient solution for supporting ϕ queries in run-length compressed PBWT representations. 

Related Work. The ϕ query was originally introduced for a text T [1 ..n ] to support the construction of the permuted longest common prefix (PLCP) array [ 16 ]. For a text T [1 ..n ], it is defined as ϕ(SA [j]) = SA [j − 1] , where SA [1 ..n ] denotes the suffix array of T . Symmetrically, the query ϕ−1 is defined by ϕ(SA [j]) = SA [j + 1] .By combining a classical FM-index [ 12 ] with a sampled suffix array, ϕ queries can be supported in O(r + n/s ) words of space and answered in O(s log log n) time, where s is a sampling parameter and r is the number of runs in the Burrows–Wheeler Transform (BWT) [ 7] of T . The log log n factor in the query time stems from the complexity of LF-and FL-mapping operations within the FM-index [ 12 ]. This approach has been adopted to report all occurrences of a query pattern in a text [16]. Gagie et al. [ 14 ] revisited ϕ-queries in the context of the r-index, reducing the space to O(r)

words and the query time to O( log log w(n/r )) . In this setting, ϕ queries are utilized to locate matches sequentially once an initial suffix array value has been identified, demonstrating that the efficiency of ϕ queries is a critical bottleneck in compressed text indexing. Bonizzoni et al. 3

Nishimoto and Tabei [ 18 ] introduced the move structure to accelerate queries on the RLE-BWT. Brown et al. [ 5] subsequently established the the splitting theorem , generalizing this concept to compactly represent permutations using move structures. Since both ϕ and 

ϕ−1 queries can be supported via permutations, the move structure reduces the total running time for k iterative queries to O(log log w(n/r ) + k) while maintaining O(r)-word space. Cozzi et al. [ 10 ] extended ϕ-queries to the PBWT as defined above. Analogous to ϕ-queries on texts, which are used to report all occurrences of a query pattern, ϕ-queries in the PBWT are used to locate all SMEMs [ 10 ]. They observe that if a site j of a haplotype Si is not a run-top —that is, it shares the same symbol Si[j] as its co-lexicographic predecessor 

Sϕj (i) at that site—then i and ϕj (i) remain adjacent at site j of the PA, i.e., ϕj (i) = ϕj+1 (i)

(see Proposition 3). Exploiting this observation, the supplementary material of [ 10 ] briefly mentions that it is possible to use a successor data structure to answer ϕ-queries. For completeness, we outline the high-level idea of the approach by Cozzi et al. [ 10 ]. For each haplotype, we identify its all sites at which its entries appear as run-tops in the PBWT. Viewing the haplotype as an interval [1 , m], we then partition it into haplotype intervals 

at these sites. Across all haplotypes, the total number of such intervals is O( ˜r + h). The observation by Cozzi et al. implies that, for any haplotype interval [b, e ] of Si, all values ϕj (i)

are identical for b ≤ j ≤ e. Hence, it suffices to store ϕe(i) for each interval. To support queries, a successor data structure is built for each haplotype over the right endpoints of its intervals. Given a query ϕj (i), the structure identifies the interval of Si containing j via a successor query and returns the value stored for that interval. As a result, one obtains an 

O(˜ r + h)-word data structure that supports each ϕ-query in O(log log w m) time. ⌟

As discussed earlier, ϕ queries are used to locate occurrences of query patterns in texts and, analogously, SMEMs in the PBWT. Reporting multiple occurrences requires a sequence of consecutive applications of ϕ, i.e., ϕ1, ϕ 2, . . . , ϕ k. We call this process iterative ϕ queries .

## 3 Decomposition of Haplotype Intervals 

In this section, we describe the algorithm that divides haplotype intervals into refined segments that satisfy both the overlap and the total-size constraints. 

## 3.1 The Decomposition Algorithm 

We assume the lists L1, . . . , L h of haplotype intervals are given, each implemented as a linked list. To construct the refined segments, we maintain an array RS [1 .. h], where each RS [c]

is a linked list of disjoint segments for haplotype Sc. These lists are initially empty. The algorithm processes the Prefix Array ( PA ) column by column from j = 1 to m. Then, at each column j, we iterate through rows i = 1 to h and perform the following steps: 

Retrieval: retrieve the first interval [bc, e c] stored in Lc, where c = col j (PA )[ i].

Passive Split: if j = ec, remove [bc, e c] from Lc and append it to the tail of RS [c].

Active Split: otherwise, if i > 1 and [bc, j ] overlaps exactly d refined segments in RS [c′],where d > 1 is a predefined constant and c′ = col j (PA )[ i − 1] , then append [bc, j ] to the tail of RS [c], remove [bc, e c] from Lc, and insert [j + 1 , e c] at the head of Lc.An example of this decomposition process is shown in Figure 3. ## 4 Two Space-Time Tradeoffs for ϕ Queries 

We present two space-time tradeoffs for iterative ϕ queries, both based on the decomposition of haplotype intervals given by Theorem 14. We assume, in this section, that for all 1 ≤ i ≤ h,the lists RS [i] of refined segments—constructed according to Theorem 14 with d := 2 —are available. The two tradeoffs are summarized as follows: 

▶ Theorem 15. There exists a data structure using S(n, ˜r, h, m) bits of space that supports 

ϕ1, ϕ 2, . . . , ϕ k queries on PBWT in overall Q(n, ˜r, h, m, k ) time, where i) S(n, ˜r, h, m) = O((˜ r + h) log n) and Q(n, ˜r, h, m, k ) = O(log log w min( m, h) + k), or ii) S(n, ˜r, h, m) = O(˜ r log h + h log n) and Q(n, ˜r, h, m, k ) = O(k log log w h).

Henceforth, for any 1 ≤ c ≤ h and any refined segment [b, e ] ∈ RS [c], we call c the color 

of [b, e ]. If ϕe(c) > 0, we define the parent of [b, e ] to be the refined segment in RS [ϕe(c)] that contains e. Since the refined segments in RS [ϕe(c)] partition [1 , m] by Corollary 6, this parent segment is unique if it exists. As shown in Figure 2, the color of the refined segment [4 , 5]

High-Level Idea. Inspired by the move structure [ 18 ], we eliminate expensive successor queries by maintaining, for each refined segment, its relationship to its parent: the color c′,the endpoints [bc′ , e c′ ], and the index of the parent in RS [c′]. By the overlap constraint, it suffices at each iteration to scan only a constant number of refined segments (e.g., in RS [c′])to find the one containing j, assuming the queries are applied at site j. In the first trade-off, we store this relationship information explicitly, using Θ( log n) bits per segment: ⌈log m⌉ bits for the endpoints and ⌈log h⌉ bits for the color. This yields constant time per step after an initial successor search. In the second trade-off, we store the endpoints in a sparse bit vector (Lemma 2), reducing the space to O( log h) bits per segment. To recover the parent index, we encode the relative positions of right endpoints of all refined segments in a single array over 

{1, . . . , h}, using O( log h) bits per segment, and support rank and select queries via Lemma 1. This introduces a rank query at each step, increasing the time per step to O(log log w h).
