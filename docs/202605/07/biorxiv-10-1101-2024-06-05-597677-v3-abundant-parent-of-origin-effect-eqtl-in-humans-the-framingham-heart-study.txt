Abstract 5

Parent-of-origin effect (POE) is a phenomenon whereby an allele’s effect on a phe-6

notype depends both on its allelic identity and parent’s gender from whom the allele is 7

inherited, as exemplified by the polar overdominance in the ovine callypyge locus and 8

the human obesity DLK1 locus. Systematic studies of POE of expression quantitative 9

trait loci (eQTL) are lacking. In this study we use trios among participants in the 10 

Framingham Heart Study to examine to what extend POE exists for gene expression 11 

of whole blood using whole genome sequencing and RNA sequencing. For each gene 12 

and the SNPs in cis, we performed eQTL analysis using genotype, paternal, maternal, 13 

and joint models, where the genotype model enforces the identical effect sizes on pater-14 

nal and maternal alleles, and the joint model allows them to have different effect sizes. 15 

We compared models using Bayes factors to identify paternal, maternal, and opposing 16 

eQTL, where paternal and maternal effects have opposite directions. The resultant 17 

variants are collectively called POE eQTL. The highlights of our study include: 1) 18 

There are more than 2 , 000 genes harbor POE eQTL and majority POE eQTL are 19 

not in the vicinity of known imprinted genes; 2) Among 180 genes harboring opposing 20 

eQTL, 99 harbor exclusively opposing eQTL, and 58 of the 99 are phosphoprotein cod-21 

ing genes, reflecting significant enrichment; 3) Paternal eQTL are enriched with GWAS 22 

hits, and genes harboring paternal eQTL are enriched with drug targets. Our study 23 

demonstrates the abundance of POE in gene expression, illustrates the complexity of 24 

gene expression regulation, and provides a resource that is complementary to existing 25 

resources such as GTEx. We revisited two previous POE findings in light of our POE 26 

results. A SNP residing in KCNQ1 that is maternally associated with diabetes is a 27 

maternal eQTL of CDKN1C , not KCNQ1 . A SNP residing in DLK1 that showed pa-28 

ternal polar overdominance for human obesity is a maternal eQTL of MEG3 , offering an 29 

explanation for the baseline risk of homozygous samples through association between 30 

MEG3 expression and obesity. Finally, we advised caution on conducting Mendelian 31 

randomization using gene expression as the exposure. 32

# 1 Introduction 33 

Parent-of-origin effect (POE) is a phenomenon whereby an allele’s effect on a phenotype 34 

not only depends on its allelic identity, but also on the parent from whom the allele is 35 

inherited (DeChiara et al., 1991; Cockett et al., 1996). POE can be driven by factors such as 36 

sex bias in transmission of genetic variants (Tom´ e et al., 2011) and maternal genetic effects 37 

that influences the environment in which the offspring develops (Hager et al., 2008). The 38 

main driver, however, is genomic imprinting, which is a phenomenon where one of the two 39 

alleles at a locus is functionally silenced by methylation. Importantly, which parental copy of 40 

the allele being silenced is highly consistent for that gene across samples. Imprinting appears 41 

to play an important role in embryonic and placental development and social behavior (Reik 42 

and Walter, 2001; Garfield et al., 2011). Genes underpinning syndromic disorders such as 43 

Prader-Willi, Beckwtih-Weidemann, and Angelman show POE (Lawson et al., 2013). The 44 

same is also true for common disease phenotypes, such as breast cancer, diabetes, and 45 

cardiovascular disease (Kong et al., 2009; Hanson et al., 2013; Mozaffari et al., 2019). 46 

In genetic association studies, particularly genome-wide association studies (GWAS), the 47 

absence of POE is usually assumed implicitly. This assumption is largely out of convenience, 48 

as in most GWAS, the parent-of-origin of an allele is not directly observable, and not inferable 49 

due to lack of first and second degree relatives in the sample. Exceptions include an Icelandic 50 

cohort, with their extensively documented pedigrees and the abundance of closely related 51 

samples (Kong et al., 2009), a Hutterite pedigree (Mozaffari et al., 2019), the Framingham 52 

Heart Study with three-generations of participants (Kannel et al., 1979), and the biobank 53 

datasets, for which parent-of-origin can be inferred using distant relatives (Hofmeister et al., 54 

2022, 2025). Another contributing factor to the implicit assumption may be the presumed 55 

scarcity of documented POE in humans. Although POE is believed to be associated with a 56 

wide range of complex traits and diseases (Lawson et al., 2013), significant associations re-57 

ported in GWAS setting are of limited quantity. For example, in a Hutterite study, Mozaffari 58 

et al. (2019) examined 21 phenotypes and produced 18 significant associations with POE, 59 

fewer than one association per phenotype. 60 

Mouse studies demonstrated that, despite a limited number of imprinted genes, most 61 

phenotypes display POE, and loci that show POE were not enriched for known imprinted 62 

genes. Using reciprocal F1 crosses, Mott et al. (2014) showed that non-imprinted genes can 63 

generate POE by interactions with imprinted loci. Focusing on metabolic traits and using a 64 

mouse population at different levels of intercrossing, Macias-Velasco et al. (2022) identified 65 

a network comprised of three imprinted and six non-imprinted genes that show POE. A 66

Using whole genome sequencing and RNA sequencing data from the Framingham Heart 73 

Study, and taking advantage of its relative abundance of trios, we set to investigate to what 74 

extent and degree gene expressions is affected by parent-of-origin. There exist multiple 75 

studies on POE on gene expression (Zhabotynsky et al., 2019; Jadhav et al., 2019; Deng 76 

et al., 2020) or POE of methylation and gene expression jointly (Zink et al., 2018), but these 77 

studies are either underpowered due to small sizes or focus on analysis of allele specific gene 78 

expression instead of eQTL. A systematic study of POE eQTL is lacking and our study aims 79 

to fill this void. 80 

# 2 Results 81 

2.1 Overview of data processing 82 

We identified 1477 trios with whole genome sequencing (WGS) data and children within 83 

the trios having RNAseq data. The trios were selected from participants in the Framing-84 

ham Heart Study (FHS) who are predominantly of European descent. After routine QC of 85 

genotype data, for each trio, we first phased the child based on rules of Mendelian inher-86 

itance, which resolved all non triple heterozygous genotypes. (See Methods for details on 87 

Mendelian errors rate and how to handle them.) We then marked the remaining triple het-88 

erozygous genotypes as missing, which produced phased paternal and maternal haplotypes 89 

for each child in the trio. Next we imputed missing alleles into their paternal and maternal 90 

haplotype background, and the parental haplotype with a higher imputed allele dosage was 91 

assigned the reference allele, the other haplotype was assigned the alternative allele. Simu-92 

lation studies showed this mask and impute phasing to be highly accurate (Supplementary 93 

Table S1). Thus at each SNP, we have a genotype vector, a paternal vector, and a maternal 94 

vector. 95 

To process RNAseq data, we started from Transcript Per Million (TPM) values, and 96 

selected 16 , 824 genes that have < 5% missing values (i.e., 0 TMP). We then corrected for 97 

the GC content bias using an approach that is similar to the one used by EDAseq (Risso 98

2.2 Bayes factors as evidence for association 100 

For genetic association we used a linear mixed model, fitted by a novel and efficient method 101 

that is designed for analyzing multiomics datasets (Guan and Levy, 2024a). The genetic 102 

relatedness matrix (twice of kinship matrix) used for the linear mixed model was estimated 103 

by Kindred (Guan and Levy, 2024c). The covariates included age, sex, body mass index, 104 

and white blood cell composition. This study focuses on genetic association between gene 105 

expression and genetic variants in cis, defined as within 1Mb from transcription start site. 106 

We elected to use Bayes factors (Stephens and Balding, 2009) instead of p-values as the 107 

evidence of association for the following considerations. First, at many SNPs, paternal and 108 

maternal alleles have different allele frequencies, presumably due to sampling variation. P-109 

values are not comparable unless they have the same power, while the same power requires 110 

equal allele frequencies. On the other hand Bayes factors are comparable because they 111 

take into account power through prior specifications. Second, Bayes factors panelize genetic 112 

variants with small allele frequencies to reduce false positives (Guan and Stephens, 2011; 113 

Zhou and Guan, 2018). This is useful as we observed extremely low paternal or maternal 114 

allele frequencies. Third, Bayes factors are also convenient in comparing different alternative 115 

models, such as paternal effects versus genotype effect, a setting that is difficult for p-values. 116 

For each gene and its cis-SNP, we computed four Bayes factors: genotype Bayes factor 117 

(BF g), paternal Bayes factor ( BF 1), maternal Bayes factor ( BF 0), and joint Bayes factors 118 

(BF j ). When computing joint Bayes factor, we also computed paternal and maternal effect 119 

sizes and attached a p-value to test whether they differ significantly. Examples of test 120 

statistics are provided in Table 1. BF g, BF 0 and BF 1 are one degree of freedom tests, 121 

but BF j is a two degree of freedom test. Thus to achieve BF j > BF g, the paternal and 122 

maternal effect sizes have to differ significantly to compensate for the extra degree of freedom. 123 

Examples in Table 1 are chosen because their paternal and maternal effects differ significantly, 124 

and we have BF j >> BF g. Another useful observation is that BF 1 is correlated with |β1|,125 

and BF 0 is correlated with |β0|. But BF 1 and BF 0 are oblivious to the signs of β1 and β0,126 

and signs are informative and interesting in our context. 127 

We used log 10 BF = 4 as the significance threshold. If the prior odds for a cis-eQTL is 128 

1 out of 1000, this threshold gives the posterior probability of association (PPA) of 0 .91. If 129 

the prior odds for a cis-eQTL is 1 out of 100, this threshold gives the PPA of 0 .99. 1 − P P A 130 

is the Bayesian counterpart of the local false discovery rate (c.f. Soloff et al., 2024). We 131

2.4 NECAB3 : opposite paternal and maternal effects 156 

The existence of eGenes whose eQTL show opposite paternal and maternal effects is intrigu-157 

ing. We focus on the example of NECAB3 to examine further details. Figure 2 left panel 158 

shows that all eQTL of NECAB3 have opposite paternal and maternal effects. Consequently, 159 

the joint Bayes factors BF j ’s are much larger than their corresponding genotype Bayes fac-160 

tors BF g’s (right bottom). We select as an example SNP rs4911348 , which is different from 161 

the sentinel eQTL shown in Figure 1, and looked into details of its association with the gene 162 

expression of NECAB3 (phenotype). Three panels of boxplots (right top) show that geno-163 

type has no association with the phenotype, and both paternal alleles and maternal alleles 164 

are associated with the phenotype, but in opposite directions. Opposite paternal and ma-165 

ternal effects were also observed in Hutterite POE study, where ten associations of opposite 166 

effects across nine traits were reported (Mozaffari et al., 2019). 167 

2.5 POE eQTL are abundant 168 

Next we looked beyond the sentinel eQTL and examined all significant eQTL. We noted that 169 

there are 15 , 893 eQTL from 14 , 733 SNPs and 1 , 824 eGenes that are insignificant for the 170 

SNP test, but significant for the paternal, or maternal, or joint test. That is, log 10 BF g < 4, 171 

but either log 10 BF 1 > 4, or log 10 BF 0 > 4, or log 10 BF j > 4. In other words, these eQTL 172 

cannot be detected without analyzing POE, which implies that POE can contribute to 173 

recover missing heritability. 174 

The minor allele frequencies of the POE eQTL are balanced. Supplementary Figure S4 175 

empirically demonstrates that our findings are not disproprtionately driven by low-frequency 176 

variants and provide a more complete picture of the genetic architecure underlying these POE 177 

signals. 178

made the following observations: 1) The median distance to transcription start site (TSS) for 203

2.7 POE eGenes are abundant 220 

There are 1 , 188 eGenes harboring paternal eQTL and 1 , 209 eGenes harboring maternal 221 

eQTL. Their union contains 2 , 139 eGenes harbor imprinting eQTL, and their intersection 222 

contains 258 eGenes harboring both paternal and maternal eQTL. There are 4 , 940 eGenes 223

2.8 eGenes that harbor exclusively imprinting eQTL 230 

There are 129 eGenes (set G1) that harbor exclusively paternal eQTL, and 139 eGenes 231 

(set G0) that harbor exclusively maternal eQTL. Naturally, G1 contain many bona fide 232 

imprinted genes with paternal expression such as FAM50B, NDN, SGCE, SNRPN, SNURF 233 

and PEG10 . Remaining genes in G1 are candidates for imprinted genes with paternal expres-234 

sion, for example CCR9 and NCOA2 . G0 contain bona fide imprinted genes with maternal 235 

expression such as GRB10, ZNF331 and MEG3 . The remaining in G0 are candidates for 236 

imprinted genes with maternal expression, for example COA8 and ZNF888 .237 

The Supplementary Material provides a full list of the candidate imprinted genes from 238 

the eQTL analysis. Note however, that not all imprinted genes have eQTL, therefore this 239 

list is incomplete. Moreover, a gene on the list is considered behaving like an imprinted gene, 240 

but may not be a bona fide imprinted gene. For example, a gene that has no cis-eQTL on 241 

its own, may “acquire” a cis-eQTL through gene-gene interaction with a putative imprinted 242 

gene such that the cis-eQTL is in fact its trans-eQTL. 243 

We used G1 and G0 as proxies for paternal and maternal imprinted genes, and used 244 

cytoBands these genes occupies as proxies for imprinted regions to examine to what extend 245 

paternal and maternal eQTL locate in the same cytoBands as these proxy imprinted genes. 246 

There are 811 autosome cytoBands in humans, 765 of them larger than 1 Mb, and the median 247 

size is 3 .2 Mb (Cheung et al., 2001). The 129 proxy paternal imprinted genes occupy 89 248 

cytoBands (set CPI ), while 15 , 576 paternal eQTL occupy 445 cytoBands (set CPe ), among 249 

them 9 , 947 (or 64%) were not in the cytoBands set CPI . On the other hand, the 139 proxy 250 

maternal imprinted genes occupy 96 cytoBands (set CMI ), and 14 , 783 maternal eQTL occupy 251 

465 cytoBands (set CMe ), among them 7 , 987 (or 54%) were not in the cytoBand set CMI .252

3.2 Polar overdominance and rs1802710 345 

Heterozygous overdominance is a pattern of inheritance such that both homozygous AA and 346 

BB have the same baseline trait value, while heterozygous AB has a higher trait value. Polar 347 

overdominance (Cockett et al., 1996) introduces asymmetry into overdominance to separates 348 

AB into two groups according to parent-of-origin of A (or B), such that AB with paternal A 349 

(or B) has a high trait value, while AB with maternal A (or B) has the baseline trait value. 350 

Wermter et al. (2008) studied trios of extremely obese offspring and identified rs1802710 351 

in exon 5 of DLK1 , homologous to the ovine callipyge locus (Cockett et al., 1996), whose 352 

allelic transmission pattern was consistent with polar overdominance: frequent transmission 353 

of the paternal C allele to obese children, but the relative risk for carriers of the homozygous 354 

CC genotype was not increased compared to the reference TT genotype. 355 

Our eQTL results show that DLK1 has no significant eQTL, but the maternal C allele 356 

of rs1802710 reduces expression of MEG3 , 53Kb downstream of DLK1 . Both MEG3 and 357 

DLK1 are imprinted with MEG3 maternally expressed and DLK1 paternally expressed. 358 

The expression of MEG3 was shown to be significantly higher in the obese group (Danesh-359 

moghadam et al., 2021). Therefore the maternal C allele is associated with reduced risks of 360 

obesity, which balances out the increased risk conferred by paternal C allele, thus offering an 361 

explanation for the baseline risk of homozygous CC samples in (Wermter et al., 2008). Since 362 

rs1802710 is not an eQTL of any gene in any tissue according to GTEx data, our study is 363 

critical to link rs1802710 with MEG3 , albeit not in the adipose tissue. 364

3.3 Mendelian randomization 365 

Mendelian randomization (MR) refers to the random allocation of alleles at the time of 366 

gamete formation. Observational epidemiology studies use MR to infer the causal effect 367 

of an exposure on an phenotype (Smith and Ebrahim, 2003), as if the random allocation 368 

of alleles is comparable to a randomized clinical trial. An important assumption for MR, 369 

among many other important assumptions, is that the phenotype conferred by a specific 370 

genetic variant is homogeneous in the population, and exchangeable between paternally and 371 

maternally inherited alleles. POE is a direct violation of this assumption (Bochud et al., 372 

2008). 373 

There is a growing interest in using gene expression as an exposure to perform MR across 374 

various traits (Porcu et al., 2019; van der Graaf et al., 2020). If an exposure has eQTL with 375 

POE, ignoring the parent-of-origin will bias prediction of a subset of samples. If the bias is 376 

severe it will change the ranking of the exposure. Consequently, it either compromises the 377 

power of the MR or leads to a false conclusion of a causal relationship between the exposure 378 

and the phenotype. 379 

Our study demonstrates the abundance of POE in gene expressions, and we therefore 380 

advise caution when conducting MR using gene expression as the exposure. We suggest 381 

checking the list of POE SNPs and eGenes we provided in the Supplementary Material and 382 

exclude those that show POE towards the exposure. The same suggestion is also applicable to 383 

computing polygenic risk scores, and imputing gene expression such as PrediXcan (Gamazon 384 

et al., 2015) and a Bayesian method motivated by it (Qi et al., 2018). 385

4.2 Inference of parent of origin 402 

For each trio, we first phase all markers that are not triple heterozygous using rule of 403 

Mendelian inheritance, where for the child in each trio we obtain paternal and maternal 404 

haplotypes with triple heterozygous markers whose phase are unresolved. We mark those 405 

triple heterozygous markers as missing. For markers that show Mendelian incompatibility, 406 

we assume child’s genotype is correct and mark as missing if it is heterozygous. Using a 407 

hidden Markov model that was designed to model haplotype variation (Guan, 2014), we 408 

impute missing markers in each haplotypes to obtain dosage estimates (between 0 and 1). 409 

Then for each marker that are marked as missing, we compare imputed dosages between 410 

paternal and maternal haplotypes, and assign the large dosage as 1 and small dosage as 0. 411 

We thus obtain paternal and maternal haplotypes with no missing data. We simulated trios 412 

using haplotypes from 1000 Genomes project, and investigated the accuracy of the phasing 413 

by mask and imputation approach described above. The phasing is highly accurate, the 414 

error rate is less than 1 out of 200 triple heterozygous SNPs (Supplementary Table S1). 415
