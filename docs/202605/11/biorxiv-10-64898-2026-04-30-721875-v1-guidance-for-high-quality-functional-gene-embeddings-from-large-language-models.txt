Abstract 25 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to generate gene embeddings,  yet systematic 26 

benchmarks of  prompting  strategies and practical guidance for obtaining biologically meaningful 27 

representations remain limited . Here we present GEbench, a n evaluation framework for assessing 28 

LLM -derived  gene embeddings across  different  tasks , prompting strategies , and LLM  architectures .29 

GEbench revealed that embedding quality depends primarily on whether the input text contains explicit 30 

functional information, rather than  on  sparse gene identifiers  or model size . Identifier -based 31 

embeddings showed weak biological organization, whereas embeddings derived from functional 32 

descriptions consistently achieved stronger functional separation and predictive performance. Notably, 33 

Self -Des, which extracts embeddings from model -generated gene function descriptions,  enable d34 

locally deployable LLMs to generate high -fidelity representations  that approach the quality of expert -35 

curated databases . Genome -scale analyses further supported these findings, indicating that explicit 36 

functional descriptions are an effective design principle for generating high -quality gene embeddings 37 

from LLMs .38

Keywords : gene embeddings,  large  language  models,  functional genomics,  prompting strategy 40

Main 42 

Large language models (LLMs) are emerging as a promising source of gene representations for 43 

downstream genomics analyses, including pathway inference, network modeling, and functional 44 

prediction 1-4. Unlike embeddings derived from curated annotations or task -specific neural 45 

architectures, LLM -based embeddings can convert textual biological knowledge into a shared 46 

representation space with broad applicability. However, current studies vary substantially in model 47 

architecture, input formulation, and evaluation strategy, making it difficult to determine which inputs 48 

produce embeddings that best reflect gene function 5-7. In practice, guidance on obtaining high -quality 49 

functional gene embeddings from LLMs remains limited. 50 

A central limitation is the lack of a standardized, biologically grounded evaluation framework. 51 

Many existing assessments rely on isolated downstream tasks or ad hoc metrics, which can obscure 52 

the factors that determine embedding quality and complicate comparisons across models and 53 

prompting strategies. In particular, current studies often do not distinguish whether improved 54 

performance arises from richer biological information in the input text or from model -specific 55 

technical choices. To address this gap, we developed GEbench, a compact benchmark designed to 56

GEbench integrates three complementary tasks: unsupervised gene clustering, supervised pathway 58

modules representing canonical biological programs: allograft rejection, apical junction, E2F targets, 64 

fatty acid metabolism, and TGF -β signaling  (Supplementary  Fig.  S1). These modules served as a 65 

common reference for comparing embedding strategies across models. 66

Results 68 

We applied GEbench  to embeddings generated by both encoder - and decoder -based language models. 69 

Encoder models included SBERT 11 , BioBERT 12 , and the cloud -based  Qwen -Enc  embedding service. 70 

Decoder models  contained  locally deployed models , including  Qwen -7B, Mistral -7B, and Llama -8B. 71 

To determine how input formulation affects functional representation quality, we compared six input 72 

strategies: Ensembl, Symbol, NCBI, Cloud -Des, Prompt, and Self -Des. Ensembl and Symbol represent 73 

sparse identifier inputs. NCBI corresponds to expert -curated gene summaries. Cloud -Des denotes 74 

functional descriptions generated by a cloud -based  full -parameter  LLM  service . Prompt refers to 75 

embeddings extracted directly from the full instruction prompt containing the gene symbol, whereas 76 

Self -Des denotes embeddings extracted from a functional description generated by the same local 77 

model. This design allowed us to distinguish the effects of sparse identifiers, external descriptive text, 78 

and self -generated functional descriptions within a unified evaluation framework. 79 

Embeddings derived from sparse identifiers showed weak functional organization across all tested 80 

models. In UMAP space, Ensembl - and Symbol -based embeddings remained diffuse and overlapping, 81 

with poor separation of the benchmark functional groups ( Fig. 1b –g). This pattern was confirmed 82 

quantitatively by adjusted Rand index (ARI), where identifier -based embeddings performed near 83 

random expectation in the clustering benchmark ( Fig. 2a ). Thus, sparse identifiers alone were 84 

generally insufficient for generating functionally informative gene embeddings. Although gene 85 

symbols may trigger latent biological knowledge in LLMs,  this  knowledge was not consistently 86 

encoded  in a representation space that preserved functional structure. 87

In contrast, embeddings derived from text containing explicit functional descriptions showed 101 

substantially stronger biological organization. Both NCBI and Cloud -Des inputs yielded compact, 102 

well -separated clusters corresponding to known biological programs ( Fig. 1b –g). This advantage 103 

extended across all three evaluation tasks. In pathway prediction, description -based inputs consistently 104 

achieved higher balanced accuracy than identifier -based inputs (Fig. 2b ). In PPI prediction, 105 

embeddings derived from explicit functional descriptions also showed markedly improved 106 

performance, with AUROC values reaching 0.80 –0.86 across decoder -based models ( Fig. 2c ). Across 107 

models and tasks, the strongest determinant of embedding quality was therefore not  the  identifier type 108 

or model scale, but whether the input text explicitly described gene function. 109 

We next asked whether a practical local strategy  in decoder -based models  (Qwen -7B, Mistral -7B, 110 

and Llama -8B ) could  reproduce  this advantage without relying on external curated resources.  In these 111 

locally deployed models,  Prompt modestly improved performance relative to Symbol, indicating that 112 

the inclusion of functional instruction text can partially enrich representation quality. However, Self -113 

Des consistently outperformed Prompt across all evaluated decoder models. In the clustering task, 114 

Self -Des achieved markedly higher ARI values and approached the performance obtained with NCBI 115 

and Cloud -Des ( Fig. 2a ). Similar gains were observed in pathway prediction and PPI prediction ( Fig. 116 

2b,c ). These results indicate that local decoder -based models benefit strongly when embeddings are 117 

derived from a stand -alone gene -function statement rather than from prompt text alone. 118 

To determine whether this guidance generalized beyond the benchmark gene set, we extended the 119 

analysis to 18,954 human protein -coding genes. Genome -scale embeddings derived from functional 120 

descriptions showed stronger agreement with Gene Ontology Biological Process similarity and higher 121 

cluster -level functional coherence than embeddings derived from Ensembl, Symbol, or Prompt inputs 122 

(Fig. 2d –f). The relative ordering of input strategies was consistent with the benchmark results, 123 

indicating that the conclusions derived from GEbench generalized to a genome -wide setting. 124

Discussion 143 

Our results provide a clear practical message for generating functional gene embeddings from LLMs. 144 

Sparse identifiers such as Ensembl IDs and gene symbols should not be used alone when the goal is to 145 

obtain biologically meaningful functional representations. Although LLMs may contain latent 146 

knowledge linked to these identifiers,  this  knowledge is not consistently  encoded or retrieved  in an 147 

embedding space that preserves functional structure. By contrast, inputs containing explicit functional 148 

descriptions consistently yield stronger biological organization and better predictive performance 149 

across models and tasks. 150 

The comparison between Prompt and Self -Des further clarifies the source of this improvement. 151 

Prompt embeddings are extracted from input text that still contains the instructional frame together 152 

with the gene symbol. Self -Des, instead, uses a direct functional description generated by the model 153 

itself as the embedding input. The superior performance of Self -Des therefore suggests that the benefit 154 

does not arise simply from  increasing text length , but from using text with clearer biological  semantics .155 

In practical terms, transforming sparse identifiers into standalone functional statements emerges as a 156 

simple yet effective local strategy for improving decoder -based gene embeddings. 157 

Together, these analyses provide practical guidance for generating high -quality functional gene 158 

embeddings from LLMs. Across models and tasks, embedding quality depended primarily on whether 159

Methods 165 

Model  selection and  deployment 166 

Six language models were selected for embedding evaluation, representing both encoder - and decoder -167 

based architectures. Encoder models included Sentence -BERT (SBERT), BioBERT, and the  cloud -168 

based full -parameter  Qwen text -embedding -v4 (Qwen -Enc). Decoder models included Qwen2.5 -7B -169 

Instruct (Qwen -7B), Mistral -7B -Instruct -v0.3 (Mistral -7B), and Llama -3.1 -8B -Instruct (Llama -8B). 170 

Additionally, the  cloud -based  Qwen -Turbo model was employed specifically for generating semantic 171 

descriptions of gene functions (Qwen -Des).  Embedding dimensions varied by model architecture: 172 

SBERT (384), BioBERT (1,024), Qwen -7B (3,584), and both Mistral -7B and Llama -8B (4,096). For 173 

Qwen -Enc, we utilized the default dimension of 1,024. Regarding deployment, SBERT, BioBERT, 174 

Qwen -7B, Mistral -7B, and Llama -8B were obtained from ModelScope 175 

(https://www.modelscope.cn/models) and  deployed on a local server equipped with an Apple M 4 chip 176 

(Mac Studio). Local inference was accelerated using the Metal Performance Shaders (MPS) backend 177 

to ensure computational efficiency and reproducibility.  The Qwen -Enc and Qwen -Turbo models were 178 

accessed via the Alibaba Cloud DashScope API .179 

To ensure deterministic and reproducible text generation, a standardized parameter configuration 180 

was applied for both D -Prompt and Qwen -Des descriptions. The temperature parameter was set to 0.0 181 

to maximize output consistency, and a fixed seed value of 42 was utilized to eliminate stochastic 182 

variability. The maximum token limit was set to 256 to constrain response length. Manual inspection 183 

confirmed that outputs under these settings were conceptually stable across repetitions .184

Software  environment and  embedding  extraction 186 

Gene embeddings were extracted via a standardized computational pipeline implemented in Python 187 

3.10 .16 , leveraging transformers (v4.45. 3) and torch (v2. 6.0). The extraction protocol was executed 188

2.  Layer  selection and  feature  capture: During the forward pass, the output_hidden_states parameter 193

analysis. 196 

3.  Aggregation via  masked  mean  pooling: To condense variable -length sequence information into a 197 

singular fixed -length vector, we applied masked mean pooling. Th e process calculated the 198 

arithmetic mean across all valid token embeddings, explicitly excluding padding tokens via the 199 

attention_mask. 200 

4.  Numerical  stabilization: To ensure computational stability during batch processing, a clamping 201 

factor of  1 × 10 −9 was applied to the mask summation. Th is  safeguard prevent ed  division -by -202 

zero errors during the averaging step, ensuring consistent embedding normalization. 203

Prompting  strategies and  input  formulations 205 

Human protein -coding genes were acquired from the Ensembl database (GRCh38.p14).  The biomaRt 206 

package 13  was used to convert gene symbols to Ensembl IDs.  For symbols with multiple mappings, a 207 

single identifier was randomly selected. We filtered the dataset to exclude genes lacking a208 

corresponding gene symbol, yielding 19,477 protein -coding genes. Subsequently, we queried the 209 

NCBI Gene database and retrieved gene summaries for 18,954 of these genes. To ensure consistency 210 

across all analyses, the final study cohort was restricted to these 18,954 genes for which both Ensembl 211 

IDs and NCBI gene summaries were available.  To evaluate the impact of input modality on embedding 212 

quality, we designed six distinct prompting strategies categorized into identifier -based, external -213 

knowledge -based, and LLM -generated inputs: 214 

1.  Identifier -based  inputs (Ensembl ID  and  Gene Symbol): To establish a baseline, we generated 215 

embeddings using raw identifiers. For Ensembl IDs, unique gene identifiers (e.g., 216 

ENSG00000141510 ) were used as direct inputs. For Gene Symbols, the standard HGNC symbol 217 

(e.g.,  TP53 ) was used as the sole input token. 218 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted May 4, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.04.30.721875 doi: bioRxiv preprint

2.  External  knowledge  input (NCBI): To assess embeddings derived from expert -curated text, we 219 

retrieved full gene summaries from the NCBI Gene database. The complete abstract text served 220 

as the input for the embedding models. 221 

3.  Cloud -generated  description ( Cloud -Des): To evaluate the utility of high -quality synthetic text, 222 

we generated functional descriptions using the full -parameter Qwen -Turbo model via the Alibaba 223 

Cloud API. We employed a standardized instruction:  “Describe biological functions of the [Gene 224 

Symbol] in one sentence. ” The resulting generated text was then used as the input for the 225 

embedding models. 226 

4.  Prompt  input : This strategy evaluates the model's internal knowledge without a distinct generation 227 

step. The model is provided with the context instruction  “Describe biological functions of the 228 

[Gene Symbol] in one sentence, ” and the embedding is extracted directly from this prompt context. 229 

5.  Self -generated functional descriptions  (Self -Des ): We developed a two -stage strategy to decouple 230 

semantic generation from representation learning. In the first  stage , the locally deployed LLM 231 

(Qwen -7B, Mistral -7B, or Llama -8B) generates a functional description using the standardized 232 

instruction. In the second  stage , this self -generated description is fed back into the model as a 233 

clean input to generate the final embedding. This protocol aligns the semantic content with the 234 

model ’s native representation space. 235

Construction of  ground  truth  gene  sets 237 

Gene sets representing known biological pathways were obtained from the Molecular Signatures 238 

Database (MSigDB), specifically leveraging four curated collections: Hallmark gene sets, KEGG 239 

pathways, Reactome pathways, and Gene Ontology (GO) Biological Process terms. All analyses 240 

utilized  Homo sapiens -specific annotations, which were preprocessed as follows: (i) gene identifiers 241 

were standardized to HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee (HGNC) symbols; (ii) pathways 242 

containing fewer than 20 or more than 500 genes were excluded to maintain biological relevance and 243 

statistical power. A binary gene -pathway association matrix was constructed , and iterative 244 

unsupervised clustering was applied to define functionally coherent and non -overlapping gene 245 

modules .246 

The analytical pipeline  of  the binary association matrix , implemented in Seurat (v5.1.0) 14 , began 247 

with data standardization and dimensionality reduction via principal component analysis (PCA). The 248

top 30 principal components were retained to construct a k -nearest neighbor graph.  Identification of 249 

major gene  module s with distinct functional space was  performed  through three sequential steps:  (1) 250 

Initial Module Definition: Unsupervised Louvain clustering 15  was applied to the binary association 251 

matrix to delineate broad functional modules. Module coherence and statistically significant 252 

enrichment were confirmed via UMAP visualization and enrichment heatmap analysis.  (2)  Module 253 

Refinement: The top five modules exhibiting the highest biological relevance were subjected to refined 254 

Louvain clustering to resolve distinct biological subclusters. These subclusters were validated based 255 

on significant enrichment for specific Hallmark gene sets.  (3)  Ground Truth Curation: The top five 256 

most distinct clusters were extracted from the refined subclusters and designated as the ground truth 257 

gene sets. Each cluster was annotated with its most significantly enriched Hallmark pathway to define 258 

its nomenclature. 259 

All clustering and enrichment analyses were performed using standardized protocols for MSigDB 260 

pathway data. Functional enrichment analysis was conducted with clusterProfiler (v4.12.0) 16  using 261 

hypergeometric tests against the Hallmark gene set collection. P  values were adjusted for multiple 262 

testing using the Benjamini -Hochberg procedure ( false discovery rate (FDR)  < 0.05). The most 263 

significantly enriched Hallmark term was assigned as the cluster label. All analyses were performed in 264 

R (v4.4.1) with Seurat (v5.1.0), clusterProfiler (v4.12.0), and msigdbr (v7.5.1) .265

Benchmark of  gene  clustering 267 

Gene embeddings were processed using the Seurat (v5.1.0) pipeline. After loading the embedding 268 

matrix, data were normalized and scaled, followed by PCA for dimensionality reduction (30 269 

components retained). K -means clustering (k  = 5) was applied directly to the PCA coordinates. For 270 

visualization, Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) was performed using the first 271 

30 principal components. In the resulting two -dimensional plots, genes were color -coded by their 272 

ground -truth pathway annotations. UMAP axes were adjusted per embedding type to ensure consistent 273 

visualization scales. Clustering performance was quantified by computing the adjusted Rand index 274 

(ARI) between the  k-means cluster assignments and the ground truth pathway labels using the scikit -275 

learn (v1.5.2) Python package 17 .276

Pathway prediction was formulated as a supervised multi -class classification task. For each gene 279 

embedding set, a  random  forest classifier (100 decision trees) was trained. Prior to model fitting, 280 

embeddings were standardized via z -score normalization and their dimensionality was reduced to 30 281 

components via PCA. Model performance was evaluated using a robust 3 x5-fold repeated cross -282 

validation scheme. Given potential class imbalance across pathway categories, performance was 283 

primarily assessed using  balanced  accuracy, which averages the recall across all classes to provide an 284 

unbiased estimate .285

Benchmark of Protein –Protein Interaction (PPI) Prediction 287 

A high -confidence human PPI network was constructed by integrating data from three primary 288 

databases: STRING (v12.0; interactions with a combined score  ≥ 700) 18 , BioGRID (v4.4.248; 289 

physical interactions only) 19 , and IntAct (v1.0.4; all interactions) 20 . Data were restricted to Homo 290 

sapiens (Taxon ID 9606). Interactions were defined as unique pairs of HGNC gene symbols, and self -291 

interactions were excluded. The final positive dataset, comprising 1,238,984 unique interactions, was 292 

formed by taking the union of pairs from these sources .293 

PPI prediction was framed as a binary classification problem. Individual proteins were 294 

represented by their corresponding gene embeddings. For each candidate protein pair, a feature vector 295 

was constructed by computing the element -wise product of the two embedding vectors. Features were 296 

standardized, and dimensionality was reduced to 30 components via PCA. A Random Forest classifier 297 

(100 decision trees) was trained to discriminate true interacting pairs from non -interacting pairs; 298 

negative examples were randomly sampled from non -observed protein pairs. Model performance was 299 

evaluated using a 3 x5-fold repeated cross -validation scheme and reported  as  the Area Under the 300 

Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUROC) and the Area Under the Precision -Recall Curve 301 

(AUPRC) .302

Genome -scale benchmark of human protein -coding genes 304 

Embedding quality was assessed through two complementary approaches: semantic similarity 305 

comparison  and functional clustering . (1)  Semantic Similarity Comparison: The biological consistency 306 

of embeddings was evaluated by comparing embedding -based similarity with ontology -based semantic 307
