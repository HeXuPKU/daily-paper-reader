# Abstract 9

Representation lea rning is an emerging paradigm for deriving phenotypes from complex 10 

measurements (e.g., imaging) for genetic discovery. However, the learning dynamics of deep 11 

neural networks, especially the evolution of representations during training, while of interest in 12 

representation learning, were insufficiently investigated in the context of genetic discovery. In 13 

this study, using a 3D convolutional autoencoder trained on T1 -weighted brain MRIs UK 14 

Biobank participants, we show that its learning trajectory forms an epo ch -stratified landscape of 15 

brain morphology heritability. Different training epochs capture distinct genetic architectures at 16 

comparable heritability levels. Overall, ensembling across informative checkpoints identifies 17 

more genomic risk loci than the conv entional single -checkpoint approach. Interpretability 18 

analysis reveals that epoch -specific loci, including MAPT and MCPH1, map onto biologically 19 

coherent and distinct neuroanatomical signatures, identified at different stages of the training 20 

process. Our results establish learning dynamics as a novel axis for genetic discovery using 21 

unsupervised deep learning and have practical implications for any architecture that saves 22 

multiple checkpoints during training. 23 

# Introduction 24 

Neuroimaging genetics aims to iden tify genetic variants that affect brain structure and function. 25 

It combines genome -wide association studies (GWAS) with brain imaging phenotypes. Large -26 

scale efforts using UK Biobank and ENIGMA have identified hundreds of genomic loci 27

To overcom e these limits, unsupervised deep learning models provide data -driven alternatives 37 

for extracting brain imaging phenotypes. This is especially true for autoencoders  (Hinton & 38 

Salakhutdinov, 2006) . The se models compress entire MRI volumes into low -dimensional latent 39 

representations, without using anatomical priors. As a result, they can find features that reflect 40 

the full complexity of brain morphology. When used as endophenotypes for GWAS, such 41 

unsuper vised representations have identified novel genomic loci not detected by traditional IDPs 42 

(Bonazzola et al., 2024; Patel et al., 2024) , demonstrating their value for genetic discovery. 43 

A key overlooked question is how an autoencoder's genetic signals evolve during training. 44 

Standard practice selects a single checkpoint, usually the one with the lowest validation loss, 45 

while discarding other representations, assuming that superior reconst ruction quality means 46 

greater genetic sensitivity. This assumption is rarely tested. Neural networks are known to 47 

undergo systematic changes in their internal representations across training  (Rahaman e t al., 48 

2019) , but whether these dynamics affect genetic discovery is unknown. Different training 49 

phases might encode distinct aspects of heritable brain morphology, in which case discarding all 50 

but one checkpoint would leave genuine genetic signals on the table. 51 

Here, we systematically characterize the learning dynamics of a 3D convolutional autoencoder 52 

for genetic discovery and introduce TRACE (TRaining -dynamics Analysis via Checkpoint 53 

Ensemble), an epoch -ensembling strategy that leverages the entire train ing trajectory. We trained 54 

the model on T1 -weighted brain MRIs from the UK Biobank, saving checkpoints at each of 85 55 

epochs, and performed GWAS on 128 -dimensional embeddings from an independent cohort of 56 

22,962 individuals. The contributions are threefold: (1) we show that the learning trajectory 57

# Results 76 

# Learning dynamics reveal divergent trajectories of 77 

# reconstruction, prediction, and heri tability. 78 

We trained a 3D convolutional autoencoder with a 128 -dimensional bottleneck (138.12 million 79 

parameters) on 6,130 T1 -weighted brain MRIs from the UK Biobank. We saved checkpoints 80 

after each of 85 epochs (Fig. 1a –b). During training, we used three metrics to assess 81 

representations: reconstruction loss, brain volume prediction, and SNP heritability (Fig. 1c). 82 

Reconstruction loss dropped rapidly in the first 10 epochs, then declined gradually until epoch 83 

85, indicating progressive refinement of voxel -level image detail (Fig. 2d). Figure 2a –c shows 84 

example reconstructions and absolute difference maps for two subjects at epochs 3 (early), 37 85 

(mid), and 83 (late). Even at epoch 3, despite high reconstruction loss, the autoencoder preserves 86 

global features such as cortical topology, ventricular geometry, and hemispheric structure (Fig. 87 

2a –c). The absolute difference maps demonstrate that reconstruction errors remain diffuse and 88 

small throughout training. Across all epochs, the two subjects remain visually d istinct, and 89 

morphological differences between subjects exceed reconstruction errors within each subject 90 

(Fig. 2a –c), confirming that embeddings preserve individual morphology from the earliest 91 

epochs. 92 

To assess whether the learned embeddings capture anato mically meaningful information, we 93 

predicted 139 image -derived phenotypes (IDPs) that represent regional gray matter (GM) 94 

volumes. These GM volumes were measured independently by the UK Biobank imaging 95 

pipeline. We used 5 -fold cross -validated ordinary leas t squares (OLS) regression on the 128 -96 

dimensional embeddings (n = 2,000 subjects). Even at epoch 0 —after one complete pass through 97 

the training data —mean prediction R² across regions was 0.112. This suggests that, from the 98 

start, the learned representation from the convolutional architecture captures some spatial 99 

structure of brain morphology. By epoch 84, the mean prediction R² across regions reached 100 

0.352. Mean R² rose steeply to 0.305 by epoch 10, representing 80% of the total improvement in 101 

just 12% of the training. It then reached 0.341 by epoch 40 and plateaued at 0.352 by epoch 84 102 

(Fig. 2e; Supplementary Fig. 2; Supplementary Table 5). The best -predicted region was the 103 

Intracalcarine Cortex (R² = 0.554). This shows that 128 unsupervised dimensions can explain 104

For heritability analyses on epochs that contributed independent genetic information, we first 115 

identified 13 informative epochs (0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9, 18, 37, 38, 46) using a data -driven 116 

criterion (see Methods: Epoch selection and ensembling for full details). SNP heritability (h²) —117 

the proportion of phenotypic variance attributable to common genetic variants (Yang et al., 118 

2011) —was then estimated for each of the 128 embedding dimensions at these 13 epochs using 119 

GCTA (Fig. 2f; Supplementary Fig. 1; Supplementary Table 1). Heritability was substantial at all 120 

epochs: mean h² = 0.464 at epoch 0 (after one complete pass through the training data), rising to 121 

h² ≈ 0.60 –0.61 at epochs 1 –9 (the highest values), and declining gradually to h² ≈ 0.56 –0.57 at 122

epoch used in the conventional approach (0.585, SD = 0.134; see below). 126 

These three metrics exhibit distinct trajectories during training. R econstruction loss continues to 127 

improve, IDP prediction performance plateaus around epoch 40, and heritability peaks early, 128 

then slightly declines. This divergence is a crucial observation —demonstrating that the model's 129 

ability to capture heritable variati on does not align with its fidelity in reconstruction. Rather, the 130 

autoencoder's learning dynamics establish a temporally structured landscape where genetic 131 

sensitivity evolves independently of image quality. Within this landscape, the regional specificity 132 

of IDP prediction gain corresponds to the regional specificity of epoch -specific genetic 133 

discovery; epochs that acquire MAPT sensitivity also demonstrate disproportionate improvement 134 

in encoding temporal lobe structures affected by MAPT. 135

# The training tr ajectory reveals a temporally structured 145 

# landscape of genetic discovery 146 

After showing that reconstruction quality and heritability do not track each other across training, 147 

we next asked whether genetic signals captured also shift across training. We perfor med GWAS 148 

on 128 -dimensional embeddings at each of the 85 training epochs using TorchGWAS (Zhao et 149 

al., 2026)  , a GPU -accelerated linear regression pipeline. This pipeline was applied to the 150 

discovery co hort (n = 22,962 White British individuals). For each epoch, we applied minimum 151 

P-value (minP) aggregation across the 128 dimensions to obtain a single genome -wide 152 

significance profile, using a Bonferroni -adjusted threshold of 5 × 10−8/128 per dimension. 153 

The number of genome -wide significant SNPs varied substantially across epochs. While most 154 

epochs contributed significant associations, certain epochs yielded disproportionately more 155 

unique discoveries, defined as SNPs not detected at any earlier epoch. App lying a criterion of at 156 

least 100 unique SNPs per epoch, we identified 13 informative epochs: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9, 18, 157 

37, 38, and 46 (Fig. 3c). These informative epochs collectively span the entire training trajectory, 158 

encompassing the initial post -optimization state (epoch 0) through early learning (epochs 1 –9), 159 

mid -training (epoch 18), and late training (epochs 37 –46). 160 

The epoch -pair heatmap of shared significant SNPs revealed that early epochs (0 –9) share a 161 

largely overlapping set of associations, consistent with their similar heritability profiles. In 162 

contrast, later epochs (37 and beyond) exhibit a distinct set of associations that partially diverge 163 

from early -epoch findings (Fig. 3b). The later -epoch signal is primarily driven by the 164 

chr17q21.31 locus (lead SNP rs62062271, P = 9.75 × 10−12 ), a region of approximately 1.4 Mb 165 

encompassing MAPT and several co -localized genes, including KANSL1 and CRHR1, with the 166 

lead SNP falling within the MAPT gene body, a well -established locus associated with tau 167 

pathology and neurodegeneration. This locus is notably absent from early -epoch GWAS despite 168 

their higher overall heritability (h² ≈ 0.61 vs 0.57; Fig. 3a, Fig. 2f). 169 

This divergence between heritability and locus discovery reflects the autoencoder's learnin g170 

dynamics across training. Early training captures coarse, globally distributed morphological 171

# Biological validation of epoch -specific sensitivity: the MAPT 184 

# locus 185 

To tes t if epoch -specific loci are genuine biological signals or just statistical artifacts, we 186 

performed anatomical mapping of the MAPT locus. The MAPT locus was the strongest example 187 

of epoch -specific detection. MAPT encodes the tau protein, which shows pathol ogical 188 

aggregation in a clear neuroanatomical sequence. Neurofibrillary tangles start in the entorhinal 189 

cortex and extend from the temporal cortex to the parietal and frontal association areas (Vogel et 190 

al., 2020). If the autoencoder accurately captures MA PT -related brain morphology at later 191 

epochs, the relevant voxels should localize to temporal lobe structures. 192 

193 

We developed a four -stage locus mapping pipeline to answer a key question. Which brain 194 

regions does the autoencoder use when its representation b ecomes sensitive to the MAPT locus? 195 

First, to isolate the representational shift associated with MAPT detection, we trained an L1 -196 

penalized logistic regression classifier. This classifier distinguished embeddings from MAPT -197 

negative epochs (0 –36) from those of MAPT -positive epochs (60 –83) using 500 randomly 198 

selected subjects per epoch. This process identified directions in embedding space that carry 199 

MAPT -relevant information. 200 

201 

Second, to generate voxel -level maps indicating which brain locations drive the c lassifier’s 202 

MAPT -positive prediction, we computed Integrated Gradient (IG) attribution maps through the 203 

full pipeline (MRI to encoder to embedding to classifier) for 30 subjects at each of 24 MAPT -204 

positive epoch checkpoints. Results were averaged across ep ochs to get one saliency map per 205 

subject. 206 

207 

Third, to identify voxels consistently salient across subjects rather than those driven by 208 

individual variation, we performed a voxel -wise one -sample t -test across 30 subjects, applying 209 

FDR correction (α = 0.05). The resulting group -level saliency map revealed broad cortical 210 

involvement, with especially strong signal in inferior and lateral temporal regions at 211 

inferotemporal slices (Fig. 4a). 212

# Har nessing learning dynamics through epoch ensembling 294 

TRACE constructs its ensemble by first selecting epochs that each contribute at least 100 295 

genome -wide significant SNPs not detected at any earlier epoch —a data -driven criterion that 296 

identified 13 informati ve epochs (0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9, 18, 37, 38, 46; Fig. 3c) —and then 297 

aggregating their GWAS summary statistics across all 128 embedding dimensions via minP, 298 

retaining the smallest p -value observed for each SNP across the full set of 1,664 epoch –299 

dimensio n combinations. To quantify the advantage of this approach, we compared TRACE 300 

against UDIP ((Patel et al., 2024) ), an established single -checkpoint method that uses the same 301 

autoencoder architecture bu t selects only the best validation epoch (lowest reconstruction loss) 302 

for GWAS —the standard practice in deep learning -based neuroimaging genetics. Both methods 303 

use minP aggregation across 128 embedding dimensions, and loci were annotated using FUMA. 304 

Epoch ensembling identified 90 genomic risk loci (Supplementary Table 2), compared with 78 305 

from UDIP (Supplementary Table 3). Using interval -based overlap on the same chromosome, 54 306 

loci were shared between the two methods, 36 were unique to the epoch ensemble, and 24 were 307 

unique to UDIP (Fig. 5b). Critically, neither approach strictly dominates the other: the two 308 

methods access overlapping but distinct regions of the genetic landscape, reflecting their 309 

different selection criteria. All 36 ensemble -unique loci re ached genome -wide significance with 310 

strong P -values (many P < 4.88 × 10−14 ), confirming they are genuine associations rather 311 

than artifacts of the more conservative ensemble threshold. 312 

To assess the robustness of the ≥100 unique SNP criterion, we repeate d the analysis using 313 

thresholds of 50 and 200 unique SNPs. When we applied a threshold of 50 unique SNPs, we 314 

obtained 19 informative epochs at an adjusted Bonferroni threshold of 2.06 × 10−11 . Using a 315 

higher threshold of 200 unique SNPs resulted in 8 infor mative epochs, with a Bonferroni 316 

threshold of 4.88 × 10−11 . These analyses discovered 86 and 90 genomic loci, respectively. In 317 

comparison, the primary threshold identified 90 loci. Together, these results demonstrate that the 318 

main conclusions are robust acro ss alternative epoch selection criteria (Supplementary Figure 4; 319 

Supplementary Table 7). 320

The 24 UDIP -unique loci —present in UDIP but absent from the epoch ensemble —deserve 328 

explicit attention, as they reveal the trade -off inherent in epoch selection. The 13 informative 329 

epochs were selected by a data -driven criterion (≥100 unique SNPs per epoch), which favors 330 

epochs that introduce many novel associations relative to all prior epochs. The validation -loss -331 

optimal UDIP epoch is selected by a fundamentally different criterion —reconstruction qual ity —332 

and carries its own genetic profile not necessarily captured by any of the 13 informative epochs. 333 

These are not missing signals but signals with a different discovery path: the UDIP epoch 334 

maximizes reconstruction fidelity, which may preferentially enco de certain morphological 335 

features whose genetic architecture is not among the most temporally novel. Epoch ensembling 336 

and single -checkpoint approaches therefore have complementary biases, and the full landscape 337 

of brain morphology heritability likely requi res both strategies. Denser epoch sampling or 338 

adaptive selection algorithms that explicitly optimize for UDIP -complementary coverage could 339 

reduce this gap in future work. 340 

The MAPT locus (chr17q21.31) was detected by both methods through overlapping genomic 341 

intervals, but the epoch -resolved analysis provides additional insight: MAPT is only detectable at 342 

later epochs (37+), not at early epochs. This means UDIP detects MAPT because its best 343 

validation epoch is sufficiently late, whereas epoch ensembling expli citly reveals the epoch -344 

specificity of this detection. Early -epoch GWAS with higher h² (≈0.61) fail to detect MAPT, 345 

suggesting that heritability and locus discovery can diverge. 346

# Discussion 367 

368 

This study demonstrates that the learning dynamics of an unsupervised autoencoder generate a 369 

temporally structured landscape of heritable variation in brain morphology. As training 370 

progresses, the model captures different sets of loci at each stage, with l imited overlap between 371 

early and later epochs. This means the learning trajectory explores complementary regions of the 372 

genetic landscape, rather than repeatedly detecting the same signals. The central finding is that 373 

this dynamic can be exploited. Epoch e nsembling across 13 informative checkpoints identifies 374

90 genomic loci, while the conventional single -checkpoint approach yields only 78. Notably, 36 375 

loci are unique to the ensemble and 24 to the single checkpoint, both at identical mean 376 

heritability (h² = 0.585). The two approaches thus exhibit complementary strengths —each 377 

captures signals missed by the other. The ensemble, however, accesses a broader region of the 378 

genetic landscape. The fundamental advantage of ensembling lies in broader, more diverse 379 

gen etic coverage, not in increased heritability; this is a direct result of the learning dynamics. 380 

381 

These findings challenge the implicit assumption that the best -reconstructing checkpoint is 382 

optimal for genetic analysis. Specifically, reconstruction loss, an atomical prediction accuracy, 383 

and SNP heritability do not track one another across training: reconstruction loss improves 384 

continuously, IDP prediction plateaus at approximately epoch 40, heritability peaks early and 385 

then declines modestly, and genomic loci continue to emerge at later epochs even as the other 386 

two metrics stabilize. This pattern is consistent with the progressive feature refinement observed 387 

broadly in deep learning  (Rahaman et al., 2019) , in which networks tend to first capture coarse, 388 

global structure before resolving finer details — a transition our results suggest has direct 389 

consequences for genetic discovery. Whether epoch -specific genetic architectures reflect 390 

frequency -domain special ization or other aspects of the loss landscape geometry remains an open 391 

question. 392 

393 

The MAPT locus mapping provides biological validation of genetic sensitivity at specific 394 

training epochs through two independent lines of evidence. First, the MAPT genetic s ignal 395 

appears only at late training epochs. Second, when we compute attribution maps (Integrated 396 

Gradients distinguishing early - from late -epoch embeddings across representational dimensions), 397 

the saliency localizes to the temporal lobe, the site of tau pa thology. The classifier was trained on 398 

overall representational change, not MAPT -specific effects, so temporal lobe enrichment in these 399 

maps is an independent anatomical signal. The dominance of the temporal fusiform cortex and 400 

inferior temporal gyrus matc hes the pattern of tau pathology described by Braak staging  (Vogel 401 

et al., 2020) . The simultaneous emphasis on the inferior frontal gyrus reflects the frontotemporal 402 

pattern seen in frontotemporal dem entia  (Rascovsky et al., 2011; Strang et al., 2019) . The 403 

convergence of these two independent forms of evidence — genetic and anatomical — at the 404 

same epochs shows that epoch -specific loci are real biological signals, not artifacts. This pattern 405

Checkpoint ensembling has been explored in cardiac MRI phenotyping  (Bonazzola e t al., 2024) .411 

However, the learning dynamics perspective we present here —where we systematically 412 

characterize how genetic sensitivity evolves across training epochs —is, to our knowledge, novel 413 

in neuroimaging genetics. The complexity of the brain's structu ral variability (differences in 414 

brain shapes or structures across individuals) and its polygenic genetic architecture make this 415 

domain well -suited to reveal epoch -dependent sensitivity at different training times. Our 416 

approach complements existing multivar iate GWAS methods, such as MOSTest  (van der Meer et 417 

al., 2020) . Those methods aggregate across phenotypic dimensions (combining multiple traits), 418 

while we aggregate across training stages (combining r esults from different points in model 419 

training). 420 

421 

The practical implications extend beyond our specific autoencoder. Any unsupervised deep 422 

learning model used for genetic phenotyping undergoes learning dynamics that may create 423 

epoch -specific sensitivity to different genetic architectures. Our results suggest it is useful to 424 

systematically characterize these dynamics, rather than relying solely on validation loss for 425 

checkpoint selection. This strategy could improve genetic discovery across a range of 426 

archit ectures, such as variational autoencoders  (Kingma et al., 2019)  and transformers  (Vaswani 427 

et al., 2017) , and across imaging modalities, including diffusion MRI and functional MRI. The 428 

framework is architecture -agnostic and only requires that multiple checkpoints be saved during 429 

training. 430 

431 

Several limitations should be noted. First, our analysis used 13 of 85 available epochs, selected 432 

by a data -driv en criterion (≥100 unique SNPs). Sensitivity analyses with thresholds of 50 and 433 

200 unique SNPs yielded 86 and 90 loci, respectively, confirming robustness of the primary 434 

result. The 24 UDIP -unique loci reflect the complementary bias of reconstruction -qual ity -based 435 

checkpoint selection. Combining selection criteria or denser epoch sampling could improve joint 436

hours and remains the most straightforward path to tighter spatial maps. Third, the KS 439 

enrichment analysis is descriptive and does not provide formal significance testing for individual 440 

regions. Future work could employ p ermutation -based enrichment. Fourth, our study uses only 441 

T1 -weighted structural MRI from the UK Biobank's White British cohort. Generalizability to 442 

other imaging modalities, ancestries, and cohorts will require external validation. Fifth, IDP 443 

prediction us ed OLS regression. Nonlinear methods might reveal more anatomical information in 444 

the embeddings. 445 

446 

In summary, the learning dynamics of unsupervised deep learning models provide a window into 447 

the genetic architecture of brain morphology. The training trajec tory forms a temporally 448 

structured landscape: different epochs capture distinct heritable variation, and no single 449 

checkpoint fully represents this landscape. Epoch ensembling thus offers a principled way to 450 

harness these dynamics, achieving broader geneti c coverage at identical heritability. Together, 451 

these findings establish learning dynamics as a new axis of investigation in deep learning -based 452 

neuroimaging genetics. 453

# Dataset 455 

We used T1 -weighted brain MRI data from the UK Biobank  (Alfaro -Almagro et al., 2018; 456 

Littlejohns et al., 2020; Sudlow et al., 2015) . MRIs were acquired primarily on Siemens Skyra 457 

3T scanners with a 32 -channel RF head coil at 1 × 1 × 1 mm resolution. Preprocess ing followed 458 

the UK Biobank imaging pipeline: defacing, brain extraction (FSL BET), bias field correction 459 

(FSL FAST), and affine registration (12 degrees of freedom) to MNI152 standard space using 460 

FSL FLIRT. Intensity values were z -score normalized, exclud ing background voxels. 461 

Quality control used UK Biobank metrics: inverted contrast -to -noise ratio (field 25735) and 462 

T2/T1 discrepancy (field 25736). Images within the top 95% of quality were retained. Only one 463

# 3D convolutional autoencoder 469 

We employed a 3D convolutional autoencoder to learn 128 -dimensional representations of 470 

endophenotypes from whole -brain T1 -weighted MRIs. The architecture consists of an initial 471 

convolutional block, four encoder blocks, a 128 -dimensional fully connected latent space, four 472 

decoder blocks, and a final convolutional block, totaling 138.12 million parameters. 473 

Each encoder block comprises a 3D max -pooling layer (kernel size = 2) followed by two 3D 474 

convolutional layers (kernel size = 3, stride = 1), batch normalization, and LeakyReLU 475 

activation. The decoder mirrors the encoder using 3D transposed convolutions (st ride = 2, kernel 476 

size = 2). Skip connections were intentionally omitted to ensure full information compression 477 

into the latent space. The model processes entire MRI volumes (182 × 218 × 182) at native 478 

resolution, allowing each latent dimension to encode wh ole -brain information. 479 

# Training 480 

The model was trained as a voxel -wise regression task minimizing mean squared error (MSE) 481 

between input and reconstructed images, with a brain mask excluding non -brain voxels from the 482 

loss computation. We used the AdamW opti mizer with the OneCycleLR learning rate scheduler 483 

(L. N. Smith, 2018) . Training was conducted for 85 epochs on a single NVIDIA A100 -SXM -484 

80GB GPU, with each epoch (training + validation) taking approximately 30 minutes. 485 

Checkpoints were saved at every epoch to enable systematic analysis of the training trajectory. 486 

# Genome -wid e association study 487

# Epoch selection and ensembling 495 

To select informative epochs for ensembling, we identified epochs that contributed ≥100 496 

genome -wide significant SNPs not detected at any earlier epoch. This data -driven criterion 497 

yielded 13 informative epochs: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9, 18, 37, 38, and 46. For each informative 498 

epoch, the minimum P -value (minP) across 128 dimensions was computed for every SNP, and 499 

results were aggregated across the 13 epochs using a second -level minP, with a Bonferroni -500 

corrected significance threshold of 5 × 10−8/ (128 × 13 ) = 3.00 × 10−11 , accounting for both 501 

embedding dimensions and selected epochs. This threshold is more conservative than the per -502 

epoch threshold app lied to UDIP ( 5 × 10−8/128 = 3.91 × 10−10 ), ensuring the ensemble 503 

comparison is conservative against false positives. Genomic risk loci were annotated using 504 

FUMA  (Watanabe et al., 2017) .505 

We used SNP count s rather than locus counts to retain sensitivity to association signals that have 506 

not yet been consolidated into annotated loci. We defined novelty sequentially relative to all 507 

prior epochs. This approach directly measures marginal information gain without requiring prior 508 

knowledge of the embedding geometry. 509

# SNP heritability estimation 512 

SNP heritability (h²) was estimated for each of the 128 embedding dimensions at each of the 13 513 

informative epochs using GCTA v1.94  (Jiang et al., 2019) . We used the fastGWA mixed linear 514

genomic relationship matrix (GRM) without performing genome -wide SNP scanning. 516

# IDP prediction 522 

We assessed the anatomical information encoded in the 128 -dimensional embeddings by 523 

predicting 139 regional grey matter (GM) volum es from the UK Biobank imaging data (fields 524 

25782 –25920): 96 cortical, 15 subcortical, and 28 cerebellar volumes, derived from the Harvard –525 

Oxford atlas. For each epoch (0 –84) and each GM volume, we performed 5 -fold cross -validated 526 

OLS regression (n = 2,000 subjects), standardizing embeddings and volumes within each 527 

training fold to prevent data leakage. Prediction accuracy was quantified as R² (squared Pearson 528 

correlation between predicted and actual values). 529 

# MAPT locus mapping 530 

Epoch classification . We trai ned an L1 -penalized logistic regression classifier to distinguish 531 

MAPT -negative (epochs 0 –36) from MAPT -positive (epochs 60 –83) embeddings. For each 532 

labeled epoch, 500 subjects were randomly selected, yielding 30,500 training samples (61 epochs 533 

× 500 subje cts). Features were standardized, and the classifier was fit with an 80/20 stratified 534 

train/test split. 535 

Integrated Gradients attribution . For 30 randomly selected subjects, we computed voxel -536 

level attribution maps using Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan e t al., 2017) through the full 537 

prediction pipeline: T1 MRI → autoencoder encoder → 128 -dim embedding → standard 538 

scaler → classifier logit. The baseline was the average of 30 'brainless' images (with 539 

foreground voxels replaced by the background mean). Attrib ution maps were computed at 540

each of 24 MAPT -positive epoch checkpoints (n_steps = 50) and averaged across epochs, 541 

yielding one saliency map per subject. We note that the classifier was trained to distinguish 542 

early - from late -epoch embeddings across all rep resentational dimensions, not specifically on 543 

MAPT. Accordingly, the resulting attribution maps reflect the aggregate representational 544 

shift between early and late training epochs — the voxels that drive 'late -epoch' classification 545 

— rather than a direct a ttribution of MAPT genetic effects to specific voxels. 546 

Statistical analysi s. Saliency maps were stacked into a 4D array (30 × 182 × 218 × 182) and 547 

subjected to a voxel -wise one -sample t -test (H₀: mean saliency = 0), with background voxels 548 

masked out. P -val ues were corrected using Benjamini –Hochberg FDR at α = 0.05. 549 

Regional enrichment . We quantified enrichment of the MAPT saliency signal in anatomical 550 

regions using a KS enrichment analysis analogous to Gene Set Enrichment Analysis 551 

(Subramanian et al., 2005) . Voxels covered by the Harvard –Oxford cortical atlas (48 regions) 552 

and subcortical atlas (11 structures), thresholded at 50%, were ranked by t -statistic. For each 553 

region, a running enrichment score wa s computed by walking down the ranked list, incrementing 554 

when a voxel belongs to the target region and decrementing otherwise. The KS score is the 555 

maximum of this running sum. Left and right hemispheres were merged. 556 

# Locus comparison 557

based overlap: two loci are considered "shared" if they are on the same chromosome 559 

and their genomic intervals [start, end] overlap (i.e., start₁ ≤ end₂ and start₂ ≤ end₁). 560 

Loci present in only one method were classified as "unique" to that method. 561
