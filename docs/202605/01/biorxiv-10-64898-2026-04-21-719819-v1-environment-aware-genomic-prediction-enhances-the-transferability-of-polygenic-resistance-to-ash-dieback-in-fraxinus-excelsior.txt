Abstract 27 

Ash dieback caused by Hymenoscyphus fraxineus threatens European ash ( Fraxinus excelsior 28 

L.) across its range, yet natural populations retain heritable, polygenic variation in disease 29 

response. A major challenge for genomic prediction in long-lived trees is reduced transferability 30 

across heterogeneous environments, where genotype-by-environment ( G×E ) interactions may 31 

influence phenotypic expression. Here, we combined nationwide sampling across Poland (320 32 

trees from 107 populations), whole-genome SNP data, and climate-derived predictors to test 33 

whether modelling environmental similarity and G×E can improve the prediction of ash 34 

dieback severity, quantified using a synthetic tree damage index ( Syn ). Environmental 35 

ordination identified a primary hydroclimatic gradient as a key driver of Syn (PC1 env : β = 0.45 36 

± 0.14, p = 0.0016), although broad-scale environmental predictors explained only a modest 37 

proportion of phenotypic variance. Genome-wide association analyses revealed substantial 38 

additive genetic signal (SNP-based heritability h²SNP = 0.63; extreme-phenotype h²SNP = 0.81) 39 

and identified 414 suggestive loci (p < 1 × 10⁻⁵), consistent with a broadly polygenic 40 

architecture of resistance, but with pronounced local enrichment of association signals in two 41 

candidate regions on chromosomes 2 and 4. In genomic prediction, trait-enriched SNP panels 42 

consistently outperformed random panels across marker densities. Predictive ability reached r43 

≈ 0.89 in internal validation for a 500-SNP panel and remained robust ( r ≈ 0.80) in an 44 

independent external validation set (n = 64). Incorporating G×E in a multi-kernel framework 45 

yielded modest but consistent gains over main-effect models, particularly under environmental 46 

extrapolation, with REML variance partitioning supported a non-zero interaction component 47 

(V G×E ≈ 14.9% and 20.9%). Our results demonstrate that ash dieback resistance is predictably 48 

polygenic and that accounting for environmental heterogeneity enhances the robustness and 49 

transferability of genomic prediction, supporting environment-aware selection and assisted 50 

migration strategies for European ash restoration. 51

1 Introduction 58 

Tree diseases are increasingly recognized as major drivers of biodiversity loss, reduced 59 

productivity, and ecosystem disruption in forests worldwide (Boyd et al., 2013; Fisher et al., 60 

2012; Santini et al., 2013; Seaton et al., 2025). Over recent decades, global trade, species 61 

introductions, and climate change have accelerated the emergence and spread of invasive 62 

pathogens and pests (Guégan et al., 2023; Montgomery et al., 2023). Well-known examples, 63 

such as Dutch elm disease caused by Ophiostoma spp. (Brasier, 1991; Gibbs, 1978), sudden 64 

oak death caused by Phytophthora ramorum (Rizzo & Garbelotto, 2003), chestnut blight caused 65 

by Cryphonectria parasitica (Anderson & Anderson, 1913; Barr, 1978), white pine blister rust 66 

caused by Cronartium ribicola (Geils et al., 2010; Sniezko et al., 2024), ash dieback caused by 67 

Hymenoscyphus fraxineus (T. Kowalski) Baral, Queloz, and Hosoya (Baral et al., 2014), and 68 

the emerald ash borer ( Agrilus planipennis ) (Poland & McCullough, 2006) illustrate how 69 

biological invasions can rapidly decimate ecologically dominant tree species, with cascading 70 

consequences for biodiversity and stability of ecosystems. Consequently, developing resilient 71 

tree populations has become a central goal in forest management and biodiversity conservation. 72 

Genomic prediction (GP) has emerged as a promising framework for accelerating the 73 

identification of individuals with desirable traits, including disease resistance (Alemu et al., 74 

2024). By leveraging genome-wide marker information, GP enables early estimates of breeding 75 

value without extensive phenotyping (Meuwissen et al., 2001). In major crops, GP has been 76 

widely applied to complex polygenic traits related to yield stability and pathogen resistance 77 

(Biswas et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2020; Tomar et al., 2021; Xiang et al., 2024). In long-lived 78 

forest species such as Eucalyptus , Pinus taeda , Populus deltoides , and increasingly Fraxinus 79 

excelsior , GP has shown strong potential for predicting growth, wood quality, and disease 80 

resistance (Cappa et al., 2019; Doonan et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025; Meger et al., 2024; Resende 81 

et al., 2012c; Resende et al., 2017; Stocks et al., 2019). However, predictive accuracy often 82 

declines when models are transferred across contrasting environments or years (Bartholomé et 83 

al., 2016; Grattapaglia, 2022; Lenz et al., 2017), highlighting the need to account for 84 

environmental heterogeneity to improve predictive robustness and transferability. 85 

Phenotypic expression of adaptive traits, including resistance to pathogens, reflects both 86 

genetic and environmental influences. Environmental heterogeneity spanning climatic 87 

gradients, soil conditions, stand structure, and local pathogen pressure can modulate trait 88 

expression and generate genotype-by-environment ( G×E ) interactions (El-Soda et al., 2014). 89 

From ecological and evolutionary perspectives, G×E can contribute to the maintenance of 90

ongoing environmental change (Aitken et al., 2008; Fontanari et al., 2023; Mátyás et al., 2018). 92 

In genomic prediction, ignoring G×E may reduce model transferability across environments 93 

and bias inference on genetic effects (Crossa et al., 2022; Millet et al., 2019; Nguyen et al., 94 

2023; Rogers & Holland, 2022). Recent methodological advances address this challenge by 95 

modelling marker-by-environment interactions or incorporating environmental covariates 96 

through multi-environment and reaction-norm GP models (Costa-Neto et al., 2021; Crossa et 97 

al., 2022; Jarquín et al., 2014; Raffo et al., 2022). These approaches can improve predictive 98 

performance under heterogeneous conditions, particularly for traits influenced by climatic or 99 

biotic stress (Cuevas et al., 2023; Fois et al., 2021; Millet et al., 2019; Rogers & Holland, 2022). 100 

Despite this progress, G×E -informed GP remains underexplored in forest trees, even though 101 

long generation times, broad ecological distributions, and strong local adaptation make them 102 

especially relevant systems for studying environment-dependent genetic effects. 103 

The European ash ( Fraxinus excelsior L.) provides a useful system for evaluating the 104 

contribution of G×E to genomic prediction. Once widespread and ecologically important across 105 

Europe, F. excelsior has undergone dramatic declines due to the invasive ascomycete H. 106 

fraxineus (T. Kowalski) Baral, Queloz, and Hosoya, the causal agent of ash dieback (Baral et 107 

al., 2014; Kowalski, 2006). Since its emergence, ash dieback has spread rapidly across the 108 

continent, causing extensive mortality, altering forest structure, and affecting associated 109 

biodiversity (George et al., 2022; McKinney et al., 2014; Pautasso et al., 2013). Although 110 

susceptibility is widespread, natural populations show substantial variation in disease severity, 111 

with a small proportion of individuals displaying partial or near-complete resistance (Enderle 112 

et al., 2015; Kjær et al., 2012; Lobo et al., 2014; Muñoz et al., 2016; Pliūra et al., 2011; Stener, 113 

2013). Recent studies indicated that resistance is heritable and polygenic, involving numerous 114 

small-effect loci (Meger et al., 2024; Muñoz et al., 2016; Stocks et al., 2019). At the same time, 115 

resistance expression varies across environmental gradients, likely reflecting differences in 116 

climate, microhabitat, pathogen pressure, and tree vigor (Havrdová, 2017; Lobo et al., 2014; 117 

Marçais et al., 2016). Together, these observations suggest that G×E may influence resistance 118 

phenotypes and shape evolutionary responses under continued pathogen pressure. 119 

Although previous genomic studies have identified candidate loci and demonstrated the 120 

potential of genomic prediction for ash dieback, most models have not explicitly accounted for 121 

environmental heterogeneity, limiting transferability and obscuring environment-dependent 122 

genetic effects. Here, using a nationwide sampling design spanning over broad climatic and 123

to evaluate how environmental context influences prediction accuracy in F. excelsior . This 125 

framework allows genetic main effects, environmental similarity, and their interaction to be 126 

modelled explicitly, providing a direct test of transferability under heterogeneous conditions. 127 

Specifically, we: (i) identify candidate SNP–trait associations for ash dieback severity using 128

severity; (iii) test whether adding environmental similarity contributes to predictive 130 

performance; and (iv) evaluate whether modelling genotype-by-environment interactions via 131 

combined genomic and environmental kernels improves accuracy under environmental 132 

extrapolation and spatial transfer. Our results aim to inform environment-aware selection, 133 

breeding, and conservation strategies for European ash. 134 

2 Materials and Methods 135 

2.1 Plant sampling 136 

In 2021, a nationwide survey of F. excelsior health status was conducted in Poland under 137 

the coordination of the Forest Gene Bank in Kostrzyca. Local foresters identified ash trees that 138 

remained free of visible ash dieback symptoms within stands heavily affected by H. fraxineus .139 

These records were compiled into an internal list of candidate symptom-free trees and used to 140 

define sampling locations for the present study. 141 

We selected 107 populations across Poland to capture broad environmental variation, 142 

including climatic gradients and site heterogeneity (Fig. 1; Table S1). In total, 320 mature trees 143 

were sampled. Where possible, three individuals per population were collected following a 144 

consistent scheme that included contrasting health phenotypes: two symptom-free trees and one 145 

severely diseased tree. Geographic coordinates were recorded for all sampled individuals. 146 

Leaf material was collected in June–July 2022. Leaves were placed in paper envelopes, 147 

transported on ice, and stored at −80 °C until DNA extraction. 148

2.2 Phenotypic assessment 151 

Tree health was quantified using the synthetic tree damage index ( Syn ), which integrates 152 

crown defoliation and crown vitality (Dmyterko & Bruchwald, 1998). For each tree, crown 153 

defoliation (Def, %) was visually estimated, and crown vitality was scored following Roloff’s 154 

classification (Roloff, 1988, 2001). Vitality scores ( Vit ) were assigned on an ordinal scale from 155 

0 to 3, where 0 indicates an undamaged, vigorous tree; 1 a weakened tree; 2 a damaged tree; 156 

and 3 a severely damaged, declining tree. The index was calculated as: 157 

𝑆𝑦𝑛 = 0.03 x Def x Vit 2

158 

Syn values were treated as a continuous measure of ash dieback severity, with higher 159 

values indicating more severe crown damage and reduced vitality. Based on Syn , individuals 160 

were assigned to four damage classes: Class 0 (Syn ≤ 0.5; undamaged), Class 1 (0.5 < Syn ≤ 161 

1.5; slightly damaged), Class 2 (1.5 < Syn ≤ 2.5; moderately damaged), and Class 3 (Syn > 2.5; 162 

strongly damaged). Phenotypic data for all individuals are reported in Table S1. 163

2.3 Environmental variables 164 

To characterize local environmental conditions at each sampling location, we compiled 165 

spatially explicit climatic, drought-related, edaphic, and topographic predictors for the 166 

geographic coordinates of all sampled trees. 167 

Climatic variables were obtained from the CHELSA v2.1 bioclimatic dataset at 30″ (~1 168 

km) spatial resolution (Brun et al., 2022; Karger et al., 2017). We extracted all 19 bioclimatic 169 

variables ( BIO1 –BIO19 ) describing long-term temperature and precipitation regimes. To 170 

capture additional aspects of near-surface climate, we used the CHELSA-W5E5 daily datasets 171 

(1981–2016), which provide bias-corrected fields derived from the WFDE5 and ERA5-Land 172 

reanalyses (Karger et al., 2023). From these data, we derived mean relative humidity ( hurs ), 173 

surface shortwave radiation ( rsds ), daily maximum air temperature ( tasmax ), and daily 174 

minimum air temperature ( tasmin ). 175 

Longer-term water-balance anomalies and vegetation status were represented using 176 

CHELSA drought indices v2.1 (Chen et al., 2024, 2025). We extracted the 12-month 177 

Standardised Precipitation Index ( SPI12 ) (Edwards, 1997; Mckee et al., 1993), the 12-month 178 

Standardised Precipitation–Evapotranspiration Index ( SPEI12 ) (Vicente-Serrano et al., 2010), 179 

and a quality-adjusted NDVI-based greenness metric ( qkndvi ) (Chen et al., 2024, 2025). 180 

Edaphic properties were obtained from SoilGrids 2.0 at 250 m resolution (Poggio et al., 181 

2021) and included soil pH in water ( phh2o ), soil organic carbon ( soc ), and sand/silt/clay 182 

fractions in the 0–5 cm soil layer. Topographic variables were derived from the Shuttle Radar 183 

Topography Mission ( SRTM ) digital elevation model at 30 m resolution (Farr et al., 2007), 184 

including elevation (m a.s.l.), slope (°), and aspect (°). 185 

Environmental predictors were first screened for association with Syn by calculating 186 

Pearson correlations between each predictor and Syn (cor.test in R; R Core Team, 2024). 187 

Predictors with p < 0.05 were retained. Within this subset, we assessed collinearity and removed 188 

redundancy by retaining one representative variable from each highly correlated group (| r| > 189 

0.7) (Dormann et al., 2013). The resulting non-collinear predictor set was centred and scaled 190 

(mean 0, SD 1) and summarized using principal component analysis (PCA; prcomp in R; R 191 

Core Team, 2024). The first two environmental principal components were retained as synthetic 192 

covariates ( PC1 env and PC2 env ). Ordinations were visualized using ggplot2 (Wickham, 2016). 193 

This screening step was used to reduce dimensionality and collinearity among predictors; 194 

accordingly, the resulting environmental axes should be interpreted primarily as predictive 195

2.4 Genomic data 204 

Total genomic DNA was extracted from leaf tissue using a modified CTAB protocol 205 

following Wang et al. (2019). DNA quantity and quality were assessed using a Quantus 206 

Fluorometer (Promega, USA) and agarose gel electrophoresis. Whole-genome sequencing 207 

(WGS) was used to obtain genome-wide SNP data for all individuals. Libraries were prepared 208 

using the TruSeq PCR-free kit (Illumina; ~350 bp insert size) and sequenced by Macrogen 209 

Europe (Amsterdam, Netherlands) on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform to generate 150 bp 210 

paired-end reads. 211 

Raw reads were adapter- and quality-trimmed using Trimmomatic v0.33 (Bolger et al., 212 

2014) with a 4-bp sliding window and a minimum Phred score of 30; reads shorter than 36 bp 213 

were discarded. Trimmed reads were aligned to the F. excelsior reference genome 214 

FRAX_001_PL (GCA_019097785.1; NCBI; Meger et al. 2024) using BWA-MEM with default 215 

parameters (Li & Durbin, 2009). Alignments were converted to BAM format, sorted, and 216 

indexed using SAMtools v0.1.19 (Li et al., 2009). Variant calling was performed in GATK v3.5 217 

(DePristo et al., 2011). SNPs were called per sample with HaplotypeCaller (minimum Phred-218 

scaled confidence threshold = 30) and combined into a multi-sample VCF for joint genotyping. 219 

Variants were filtered using GATK VariantFiltration and SelectVariants with thresholds QD < 220 

2.0, MQ < 40.0, MQRankSum < −12.5, and ReadPosRankSum < −8.0, followed by additional 221 

filtering in VCFtools v0.1.16 (Danecek et al., 2011). We retained biallelic SNPs genotyped in 222 

≥95% of individuals (site call rate ≥ 0.95), with minimum read depth ≥10 and minor allele 223 

frequency (MAF) > 0.01. For analyses requiring approximately independent markers, linkage 224 

disequilibrium pruning was performed in PLINK v2.0 using r² > 0.5 (Chang et al., 2015). 225

2.5 Population structure and relatedness 226 

Population genetic structure was assessed by principal component analysis (PCA) using 227 

the quality-filtered, LD–pruned SNP dataset. PCA was performed in PLINK v2.0 (Chang et al., 228 

2015), and eigenvectors and eigenvalues were visualized in R (R Core Team, 2024) using 229 

ggplot2 (Wickham, 2016). The proportion of genetic variance explained by each axis was 230 

calculated from eigenvalues, and the number of informative components was evaluated using 231 

the broken-stick criterion (Jackson, 1993). To test whether sampled populations differed 232 

significantly in multivariate genotype space, we performed a permutation-based multivariate 233 

analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) using adonis2 in vegan (Oksanen, 2020) on Euclidean 234 

distances among individuals, with 999 permutations. Pairwise genomic relatedness was 235 

estimated using a centered genomic relationship matrix (GRM) computed in GEMMA v0.98 236 

(following the method of VanRaden, 2008) from the same filtered and LD-pruned SNP dataset 237 

(Zhou & Stephens, 2012). Kinship coefficients were visualized as a clustered heatmap using 238 

pheatmap in R (Kolde, 2019). 239 

2.6 Genome-wide association analysis (GWAS) 240 

Genome-wide association analyses were conducted using a univariate linear mixed 241 

model (LMM) implemented in GEMMA v0.98 (Zhou & Stephens, 2012), with the Syn analyzed 242 

as a continuous trait. For each SNP, the genotype at the focal marker was fitted as a fixed effect, 243 

and a random polygenic effect was modelled using the GRM (Section 2.5), thereby accounting 244 

for genome-wide additive covariance and background relatedness. Fixed-effect covariates 245 

included an intercept, the first two genetic principal components (PC1 gen and PC2 gen ; Section 246 

2.5), and PC1 env (Section 2.3) to control for residual population structure and broad-scale 247 

environmental heterogeneity. Association significance was assessed using Wald tests, and SNP-248 

based heritability was quantified as the proportion of phenotypic variance explained by the 249 

random polygenic effect (PVE) estimated from the fitted LMM. 250 

Association tests were carried out on the full set of quality-filtered biallelic SNPs 251 

without LD pruning in order to retain local haplotype structure and maximize power to detect 252 

association signals. In addition to the analysis of the full dataset, including all individuals, a 253 

complementary extreme-phenotype GWAS was performed using only trees from the two most 254 

contrasting damage classes (Class 0: undamaged; Class 3: strongly damaged), applying the 255 

same LMM specification and covariates to increase phenotypic contrast. Given the moderate 256 

sample size and the expected polygenic architecture of ash dieback resistance, a suggestive 257 

genome-wide significance threshold of p < 1 × 10⁻⁵ was used to prioritize candidate loci for 258

2.7 Multilocus genetic patterns at suggestive GWAS loci 277 

To characterize multilocus genetic patterns underlying ash dieback severity beyond 278 

single-locus GWAS signals, we conducted post-GWAS analyses based on SNPs exceeding the 279 

suggestive significance threshold in the full-dataset GWAS (p < 1 × 10⁻⁵; 414 SNPs). To avoid 280 

overrepresentation of tightly linked variants and to focus on approximately independent 281 

genomic signals, we applied LD pruning in PLINK v2.0 (Chang et al., 2015). SNPs were pruned 282 

based on pairwise LD (r² > 0.5) within sliding windows, retaining a single representative SNP 283 

from each LD block. The resulting LD-pruned panel of 142 suggestive loci was used in 284 

subsequent multilocus analyses. 285

2.7.2 Allele-frequency shifts between extreme damage classes 304 

To test whether suggestive loci showed systematic differences in allele frequency 305 

between phenotypic extremes, we compared effect-allele frequencies between Class 0 and Class 306 

3 individuals using an LD-pruned set of suggestive loci (Section 2.7). Genotype dosages (0, 1, 307 

2 copies of the effect allele) were used to compute effect-allele frequency in each class (mean 308 

dosage/2). Allele-frequency shifts were quantified as Δp = pClass3 − pClass0 ; positive Δp 309 

values indicate that effect alleles are more frequent in strongly damaged trees. To assess 310 

concordance between allele-frequency shifts and GWAS effect estimates, we tested the 311 

relationship between Δp and GWAS β using Pearson correlation. Analyses were performed in 312 

R v4.4.2 (R Core Team, 2024) using custom scripts. 313 

2.7.3 Genotype–phenotype relationships at nonsynonymous candidate variants 314 

To evaluate genotype–phenotype associations for coding candidates, we performed 315 

single-locus analyses for all missense SNPs detected among the suggestive loci prior to LD 316 

pruning. For each missense SNP, additive genotypes (0/1/2 effect alleles) were analyzed using 317 

linear regression with Syn as the response variable. Models were fitted in R v4.4.2 using lm ,318 

including covariates PC1 gen , PC2 gen , PC1 env , and population to control for structure and 319 

environmental heterogeneity. Effect estimates, standard errors, and p-values were extracted 320 

from model summaries. 321

2.7.4 Polygenic signal at suggestive GWAS loci 322 

To assess whether cumulative effects of suggestive loci jointly explained variation in 323 

Syn , we computed polygenic scores (PGS) using all suggestive SNPs (p < 1 × 10⁻⁵) prior to LD 324 

pruning. For each individual, a raw score was calculated as the sum of effect-allele dosages 325 

weighted by GWAS regression coefficients (β) and standardized to mean 0 and SD 1. 326 

Associations between PGS and Syn were examined using Pearson correlation and linear models 327 

adjusting for PC1 gen , PC2 gen , PC1 env , and population. 328 

To quantify discrimination between phenotypic extremes (Class 0 vs Class 3), receiver 329 

operating characteristic (ROC) analyses were performed using PGS as the predictor, and the 330 

area under the ROC curve (AUC) was computed using pROC (Robin et al., 2011). Because 331 

SNP selection and effect-size weights were derived from the same discovery dataset, these 332 

polygenic score analyses were interpreted as descriptive summaries of within-sample 333 

multilocus signal rather than as independent measures of predictive transferability. 334 

2.7.5 External validation of multilocus structure 335

2.8 Genomic prediction models 360 

Genotypes were encoded as allele dosages (0, 1, or 2 copies of the alternative allele), 361 

consistent with an additive model (Meuwissen et al., 2001). Missing genotypes were imputed 362 

strictly within each training partition using a k-nearest-neighbours (KNN) approach 363 

implemented in the VIM package (Kowarik & Templ, 2016), following the general principle of 364 

training-only imputation. Genotype matrices were centred and scaled (mean 0, SD 1) using 365 

parameters estimated from training data only, and the same transformation was applied to 366 

validation data. All preprocessing, kernel construction, and cross-validation procedures were 367 

implemented in R v4.4.2 (R Core Team, 2024). GWAS-informed SNP rankings were 368 

recalculated within each training partition only, and the resulting top-N marker sets were then 369 

applied to the corresponding validation data to minimize information leakage. 370 

To evaluate the effects of marker density and marker selection strategy on predictive 371 

performance, models were fitted using SNP panels of increasing size (100, 200, 500, 1,000, 372 

2,000, 5,000, 10,000, 25,000, and 50,000 markers). Two complementary strategies were used 373 

to construct marker panels. First, GWAS-informed panels were generated by ranking SNPs 374 

according to association strength with Syn (Wald test p-values) and retaining the top N markers 375 

at each density level (rankings derived from the GWAS performed in this study; Section 2.6). 376 

Second, random panels of identical size were generated by sampling SNPs at random from the 377 

genome-wide LD-pruned dataset. 378

2.8.4 Cross-validation and model evaluation 414 

Predictive performance was evaluated under three partitioning schemes designed to 415 

assess baseline accuracy, environmental extrapolation, and spatial transfer. Model 416 

transferability was additionally assessed using an independent external validation set 417 

comprising 64 individuals (Table S9). 418 

First, random cross-validation (CV) was used as a baseline: individuals were randomly 419 

assigned to five folds, models were trained on four folds (80%) and evaluated on the remaining 420 

fold (20%), and the procedure was repeated across 10 independent random partitions. Second, 421 

leave-one-environment-cluster-out cross-validation (LOECO) was used to test environmental 422 

extrapolation: individuals were assigned to five environmental clusters using k-means 423 

clustering based on PC1 env (Section 2.3), and in each fold, one cluster was withheld for 424 

validation while the remaining clusters served for training. Third, leave-one-geographic-425 

cluster-out cross-validation (LOGCO) was used to assess spatial transfer: individuals were 426 

assigned to five spatial clusters using k-means clustering based on geographic coordinates, and 427 

in each fold, one cluster was withheld while the remaining clusters were used for model 428 

training. 429 

All preprocessing and model-building steps were performed within the training data of 430 

each fold, as described above, and the resulting parameters were applied to the corresponding 431 

validation data. For internal cross-validation based on continuous Syn values, predictive ability 432 

was quantified as Pearson’s correlation coefficient ( r) between observed and predicted values. 433 

Results were summarized as mean ± SD across the 10 replicates for random CV and across the 434 

five holdout folds for LOECO and LOGCO. For the external validation set, predicted Syn values 435 

were converted to binary classes using a threshold of 1.5 (healthy: Syn ≤ 1.5; damaged: Syn > 436 

1.5), and agreement with observed health status was quantified using Pearson’s correlation (phi 437 

coefficient). Because the external dataset differed in phenotypic scale and structure from the 438 

discovery dataset, this analysis was interpreted primarily as a test of predictive transferability 439 

rather than as a direct replication of within-dataset performance. 440

3.1 Phenotypic variation in ash dieback severity 456 

The synthetic tree damage index ( Syn ) spanned a wide range of values (0.075–2.93), 457 

with an average of approximately 1.1, indicating moderate overall damage among the sampled 458 

individuals. The distribution of Syn appeared multimodal, reflecting substantial variation in ash 459

of individuals were undamaged (Class 0, Syn ≤ 0.5), 45.6% were slightly damaged (Class 1, 0.5 461

4.1 Environmental modulation of ash dieback severity 776 

The primary hydroclimatic gradient ( PC1 env ) was significantly associated with ash 777 

dieback severity, although the environmental gradients considered explained only a small 778 

proportion of the observed phenotypic variation overall (4%). This is consistent with the view 779 

that disease expression in European ash is environmentally structured across multiple spatial 780 

scales: regional climate sets the broad epidemiological background, while stand structure, host 781 

density, topography, and microclimatic buffering influence how strongly that background is 782 

expressed as visible damage in individual trees (Combes et al., 2024; Cracknell et al., 2023; 783 

Enderle et al., 2019; Fuchs et al., 2024; Raykova, 2025). Accordingly, studies across Europe 784 

have repeatedly shown that ash dieback tends to be most severe in moist, sheltered 785 

environments and in stands with high ash abundance, whereas more open or drier conditions 786 

are often associated with reduced disease severity, likely owing to lower humidity, reduced 787 

inoculum build-up, and fewer opportunities for infection and lesion development (Erfmeier et 788 

al., 2019; Fuchs et al., 2024; George et al., 2022; Klesse, 2021). 789 

Inspection of the underlying variables suggests that this signal was driven mainly by 790 

winter temperature, cold-season precipitation, and long-term summer water balance. In 791 

particular, more serious damage was associated with milder winter minima (BIO6) and wetter 792 

cold seasons (BIO19), a pattern broadly consistent with the idea that relatively moist and 793 

thermally moderate conditions may favour pathogen persistence, inoculum build-up, and 794 

infection pressure (Combes et al., 2024; Marçais et al., 2023). By contrast, lower values of 795 

SPI12_08, indicating a stronger cumulative summer moisture deficit, were also associated with 796 

greater damage, suggesting that host water stress may contribute to symptom severity in 797 

infected trees (Pliura et al., 2016). Precipitation seasonality (BIO15) contributed to the broader 798 

environmental structure, but its biological interpretation is likely less direct, as it may reflect 799 

the temporal redistribution of water availability rather than a single mechanistic driver of 800

4.2 Polygenic architecture and multilocus signals of resistance 810 

Several lines of evidence from our study support a broadly polygenic architecture of ash 811 

dieback resistance in F. excelsior . First, SNP-based heritability was high in both GWAS settings 812 

(h²SNP = 0.63 in the full dataset and 0.81 in the extreme-phenotype analysis), indicating that a 813 

substantial proportion of variation in disease severity is attributable to additive genetic effects 814 

captured by genome-wide markers. Although SNP-based heritability from mixed-model GWAS 815 

is not directly equivalent to narrow-sense heritability estimated from pedigree- or progeny-816 

based designs, our estimates fall within the broader range reported for ash dieback-related traits. 817 

Under family, progeny, and clonal test designs, heritability of crown dieback and related 818 

symptoms is often moderate (approximately 0.37–0.53) (Kjær et al., 2012; Liziniewicz et al., 819 

2022; Lobo et al., 2015; Muñoz et al., 2016; Pliura et al., 2016; Pliūra et al., 2011; Seidel et al., 820 

2025), but may be substantially higher in some cases, including estimates approaching or 821 

exceeding 0.7 (Pliūra et al., 2011; Seidel et al., 2025). By contrast, heritability estimated under 822 

heterogeneous natural stand conditions may be very low, indicating that environmental noise 823 

can strongly reduce the detectable additive genetic signal (Wohlmuth et al., 2018). 824 

Second, the genomic signal detected here was distributed broadly across the genome 825 

rather than concentrated in a single major-effect locus. In the full-dataset GWAS, 414 826 

suggestive SNPs were identified across nearly all assembled chromosomes. with the strongest 827 

enrichment was observed on chromosome 2, followed by chromosome 4 and, more weakly, 828 

chromosome 16. The extreme-phenotype GWAS recovered a similar, though not identical, 829 

pattern, again with pronounced enrichment on chromosomes 2 and 4. Thus, resistance appears 830 

clearly polygenic but not uniformly diffused, with a genome-wide background of dispersed 831 

signal complemented by a limited number of more prominent regions of enrichment. This 832 

pattern is consistent with a polygenic architecture in which both diffuse small-effect variation 833

4.3 Genomic prediction and marker prioritization 912 

Prediction performance was consistently higher for GWAS-informed SNP sets than for 913 

random SNP sets of the same size across all tested panel sizes, indicating that genomic 914 

prediction of ash dieback severity in F. excelsior is driven primarily by trait-relevant multilocus 915 

signal rather than by background genomic covariance captured by untargeted markers. Our 916 

results further show that predictive performance depends not only on which markers are used, 917

4.4 Genotype-by-environment interactions contribute to predictive transferability 941 

Genomic prediction in forest trees often achieves high apparent accuracy when training 942 

and test sets are closely related, yet performance commonly declines when models are applied 943 

across families or populations, and also across years, sites, or deployment zones (Beaulieu et 944 

al., 2014; Lauer et al., 2022; Lenz et al., 2017; Resende et al., 2012c). This pattern suggests that 945 

predictive ability in many datasets is partly contingent upon realized relatedness and underlying 946 

family or population structure; consequently, accuracy generalizes poorly as genetic distance 947 

between training and target sets increases (Beaulieu et al., 2014; Lenz et al., 2017; Resende et 948 

al., 2012c). Reduced transferability is typically attributed to a combination of factors, including 949 

differences in linkage disequilibrium between markers and causal variants across populations, 950 

shifts in allele frequencies and effect sizes, and genuine genotype-by-environment interactions 951 

that alter realized genetic architecture across environments (Beaulieu et al., 2014; Lauer et al., 952 

2022; Resende et al., 2012a; Resende et al., 2012b; Slavov et al., 2025). Taken together, these 953 

findings motivate prediction frameworks that explicitly model environment-dependent genetic 954 

effects rather than assuming that marker effects remain constant across populations and 955 

environments. 956 

In our dataset, environmental similarity alone had limited predictive value, but its signal 957 

was consistently detectable: among single-predictor models, BIO19 and SPI12_08 performed 958 

best, and PC1 env captured the strongest environmental signal. This indicates that broad-scale 959 

hydroclimatic context contributes measurably to crown damage, even if it explains only a small 960 

fraction of overall phenotypic variance. Such modest performance of environment main effect 961 

prediction models is unsurprising in natural stands (Hennon et al., 2020; Holden et al., 2020), 962 

where disease severity integrates fine-scale, spatially structured processes—including local 963 

inoculum pressure, microclimatic buffering, stand structure, and host physiological status. 964 

These factors are only partially captured by coarse-gridded climate layers (Bütikofer et al., 965

most relevant for deployment across heterogeneous landscapes. 1019 

4.5 Implications for breeding and conservation 1020 

From an applied perspective, our results support genomic prediction as a promising tool 1021 

for ash breeding, restoration, and conservation, particularly when based on GWAS-informed 1022 

marker panels and interpreted in an environment-aware framework. The strong additive genetic 1023 

signal and the high performance of prioritized SNP panels suggest that resilient material can be 1024 

identified more efficiently than by phenotypic screening alone. At the same time, the improved 1025 

performance of G×E -aware models under extrapolative validation indicates that deployment 1026 

decisions should account for environmental context—incorporating tools such as climate niche 1027 

mapping and assisted migration frameworks—rather than relying solely on genomic ranking 1028 

derived from a single set of sites (Carroll & Boa, 2024; Seidel et al., 2025). 1029 

These findings also suggest that moderate, trait-enriched SNP panels may be more 1030 

useful operationally than very large marker sets, as they captured the bulk of the transferable 1031
