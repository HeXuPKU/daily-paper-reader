Abstract 14 

Single-cell Assay for Transposase Accessible Chromatin (scATAC-seq) is increas-15 

ingly used at population scale to study how genetic variation shapes chromatin 16 

accessibility. Method development is limited by the lack of flexible simulation 17 

tools with known ground truth. Here, we present simPIC, a fast, memory-18 

efficient framework for simulating realistic single-cell ATAC-seq count data 19 

across individuals and populations. simPIC models cell groups, batch effects, 20 

and genotype-dependent accessibility variation, enabling controlled evaluation of 21 

population-scale methods, including chromatin accessibility quantitative traits 22 

locus (QTL) mapping. Across multiple datasets and cell types, simPIC closely 23 

matches real data distributions while scaling to cohort sizes impractical for 24 

current tools. 25 

Keywords: single-cell ATAC-seq, single-cell genomics, software, simulation 26

The epigenetic template governing lineage-specific programs is shaped by a com-28 

plex landscape of cis-regulatory elements. Single-cell Assay for Transposase-Accessible 29 

Chromatin sequencing (scATAC-seq) has become foundational for mapping chromatin 30 

accessibility at single-cell resolution, enabling the study of regulatory interactions and 31 

transcriptional control [1]. By using Tn5 transposase to preferentially insert sequenc-32 

ing adapters into accessible genomic regions, scATAC-seq provides detailed maps of 33 

cell-specific regulatory landscapes. This approach provides critical insights into the 34 

interplay between chromatin structure and gene expression [1, 2]. However, extract-35 

ing robust biological insights from scATAC-seq remains computationally challenging; 36 

the data is characterised by extreme sparsity and high-dimensional feature spaces. 37 

Consequently, most analytical strategies to date have focused on clustering cells into 38 

discrete groups or aggregating accessibility signals across genomic peaks. While effec-39 

tive in many contexts, these approaches may obscure the biological variability present 40 

at the single-cell level. New data analysis approaches are needed, but the computa-41 

tional infrastructure for scATAC-seq data analysis remains underdeveloped relative 42 

to rapid advances in experimental techniques [3]. Notably, flexible and biologically 43 

realistic simulation frameworks are lacking, which constrains rigorous evaluation and 44 

benchmarking of novel computational methods. 45 

As experimental designs transition toward larger cohorts and multi-condition stud-46 

ies, the requirement for rigorous methodological validation has become acute. In the 47 

absence of comprehensive experimental “gold standards” where regulatory events are 48 

validated a priori simulation provides a practical solution by generating synthetic 49 

datasets with known ground truth under controlled settings. Such frameworks enable 50 

objective assessment of whether a method can faithfully recapitulate empirical data 51 

distributions, preserve latent biological signals, and maintain computational scalabil-52 

ity, which are core criteria used in systematic benchmarking studies [4]. While the 53 

transcriptomics field utilizes mature, parametric simulators such as Splatter [5], and 54 

its population extension splatPop [6], which provide transparent parametric models 55 

calibrated to real data, analogous tools for scATAC-seq remain fragmented across 56 

several distinct methodological approaches. 57 

Existing scATAC-seq simulators fall into two broad categories, each with distinct 58 

strengths and limitations. The first comprises tools purpose-built for scATAC-seq. 59 

SCAN-ATAC-Sim [7] derives synthetic profiles by downsampling bulk chromatin 60 

accessibility data, masking cell-type-specific heterogeneity. simATAC [8] generates 61 

bin-by-cell count matrices rather than peak-by-cell matrices, produces pseudo-bulk 62 

profiles, and does not adequately capture the extreme sparsity characteristic of single-63 

cell data [9]. EpiAnno is constrained by a strict dependency on prior knowledge of 64 

highly accessible peaks, restricting its use to datasets with established reference anno-65 

tations [9]. simCAS [10] uses a deep-learning embedding strategy that reproduces the 66 

high-level properties of real data but requires extensive parameter calibration and 67 

utilises a mechanistically opaque architecture, which limits the user’s ability to pre-68 

cisely specify biological variables. scReadSim [11] simulates reads rather than count 69 

matrices directly. It learns local genomic distributions from aligned reads and can gen-70 

erate realistic count matrices; however, it requires raw BAM files as input, which are 71

The second category comprises general-purpose multi-modal simulators that 76 

include scATAC-seq as one of several supported data types. scDesign3 [12] uses a 77 

copula-based statistical framework to model multivariate single-cell data across RNA, 78 

ATAC, and spatial modalities; it offers broad distributional flexibility but is not 79 

tailored to the discrete count structure of paired insertion counts (PIC) quantified 80 

scATAC-seq data [13] and does not support population-scale simulation with geno-81 

type effects. scMultiSim [14] is a mechanistic simulator guided by gene regulatory 82 

networks (GRN) and cell-cell interactions, whose primary strength is benchmarking 83 

multi-omics integration and GRN inference methods; it similarly lacks support for 84 

inter-individual genetic variation. DiTSim [15] uses diffusion-transformer architectures 85 

to learn and reproduce the global distributional structure of real scATAC-seq datasets 86 

and demonstrates strong marginal fidelity; however, as a deep-generative model, it 87 

does not expose an interpretable parametric structure and does not directly support 88 

user-specified experimental designs such as batch structure or caQTL. 89 

Across existing scATAC-seq simulation frameworks, methods typically do not 90 

simultaneously provide an interpretable and calibrated generative model of count data, 91 

flexible experimental design, and population-scale simulation with explicit genetic 92 

effects. This limitation is increasingly consequential as functional genomics shifts 93 

toward population-scale studies that require modelling of inter-individual variation in 94 

chromatin accessibility. As such, including caQTL effects is essential for evaluating 95 

methods that aim to identify genetic drivers of regulatory variation and complex traits. 96 

To address the need for realistic and efficient simulation of single-cell chromatin 97 

accessibility data, we developed simPIC, an R/Bioconductor package that gener-98 

ates peak-by-cell count matrices based on PIC, a recently introduced standardized 99 

approach for quantifying scATAC-seq signal [16]. PIC quantifies scATAC-seq signal 100 

by counting the number of Tn5 insertion events within each peak per cell, yield-101 

ing a count structure constrained by the underlying diploid biology. The resulting 102 

counts are well suited to probabilistic modelling of sparsity and accessibility. simPIC 103 

learns key data properties from a reference dataset and uses these calibrated param-104 

eters to generate synthetic count matrices under user-specified experimental designs. 105 

The simPIC framework supports multiple cell types, experimental batch effects, and 106 

through integration with the splatPop framework [6] enables population-scale simu-107 

lation with caQTL effects, a capability that is not supported in existing scATAC-seq 108 

simulation frameworks. This integration is crucial for mimicking the multidimensional 109 

heterogeneity present in real single-cell studies, where technical artifacts, diverse cellu-110 

lar identities, and inter-individual genetic variation all shape the observed chromatin 111 

landscape [17]. simPIC is well documented and freely available through Bioconduc-112 

tor, providing researchers with a flexible, scalable, and biologically grounded tool for 113 

simulating realistic scATAC-seq datasets (Figure1). 114

# 2 Results 115 

2.1 The simPIC model framework 116 

simPIC is a modular R/Bioconductor package for simulating synthetic scATAC-seq 117 

datasets that capture the key biological and technical features observed in real data. 118 

The simPIC framework supports two main simulation modes: (a) simulating one 119 

or more cell types within a single sample, optionally with multiple experimental 120 

batches; and (b) simulating population-scale data with inter-individual genetic varia-121 

tion via integration with splatPop [6] (Figure 2, Supplementary Table S1). All output 122 

is returned as a SingleCellExperiment object, enabling easy integration with down-123 

stream tools. The simPICcompare() function, provides diagnostic plots comparing 124 

real and simulated data across key metrics: library size, peak accessibility, and spar-125 

sity, enabling rapid model calibration. Together, these features support user-defined 126 

scATAC-seq simulations for method evaluation and hypothesis testing under explicit 127 

generative assumptions. 128 

simPIC offers four peak-mean models—gamma, Weibull, lognormal-gamma (ln-129 

gamma), and Pareto; which differ in the shape of the marginal peak accessibility 130 

distribution they impose. Across a benchmark of 25 cell types from six datasets (Table 131 

1) spanning multiple scATAC-seq technologies and species, simPIC with ln-gamma 132 

peak means consistently achieved the best distributional accuracy and is therefore the 133 

recommended default. All four variants complete simulations in under five minutes for 134 

the cell types evaluated here; runtime differences between variants are small relative 135 

to differences between simPIC and competing tools (Section 2.5). 136 

To contextualise simPIC within the broader simulation landscape, we compared 137 

it with four tools capable of producing count-level scATAC-seq data: simCAS [10], 138 

scDesign3 [12], scMultiSim [14], and DiTSim [15]. These tools differ substantially in 139 

their required inputs, modelling frameworks, and computational demands (Supplemen-140 

tary Tables S2-S3). Briefly, scDesign3 is a multi-modal simulator using Generalized 141 

Additive Models for Location, Scale, and Shape (GAMLSS) and copula models that 142 

requires cell-type annotations; scMultiSim is a mechanistic simulator guided by GRN 143 

and cell-cell interactions, targeting RNA and ATAC jointly; and DiTSim is a diffusion-144 

transformer-based deep learning simulator requiring cell-type labels and GPU access. 145 

Unlike these tools, simPIC requires only a count matrix as input, is the only simu-146 

lator in this comparison that supports population-scale and caQTL simulation, and 147 

provides built-in diagnostic functions. 148

We show a representative cell type from Cusanovich dataset: Kidney Podocytes. 163 

All four simPIC variants closely reproduced the log library size distribution of real 164 

data (KS = 0.06–0.09), while scDesign3 showed moderate deviation (KS = 0.38), 165 

simCAS performed similarly (KS = 0.04), and DiTSim deviated more substantially 166 

(KS = 0.39). scMultiSim showed the largest discrepancy (KS = 1.00), reflecting a 167 

systematic mismatch in both scale and shape (Figure 3a). A similar pattern was 168 

observed for log peak mean: simPIC variants maintained low KS distances (0.08–0.18), 169 

with simPIC default ln-gamma being lowest (KS = 0.08), comparable to scDesign3 170 

(KS = 0.09) and DiTSim (KS = 0.09), while simCAS deviated more substantially (KS 171 

= 0.38), and scMultiSim again showed maximal divergence (KS = 1.00) (Figure 3b). 172 

For cell sparsity, a defining feature of scATAC-seq data, simPIC consistently achieved 173 

the lowest KS values (0.05–0.08), with simCAS performing comparably (KS = 0.05) 174 

and scDesign3 showing moderate deviation (KS = 0.37). In contrast, DiTSim and 175 

scMultiSim both showed KS = 1.00, indicating a failure to capture the characteristic 176 

high-sparsity structure of real data (Figure 3c). 177 

Density-based comparisons reinforced these findings.DiTSim and scMultiSim are 178 

not shown in the density plots because they produced degenerate or near-degenerate 179 

discrepancies for key metrics in this benchmark setting: scMultiSim had a KS dis-180 

tance of 1 for peak means, and DiTSim had a KS distance of 1 for cell sparsity. We 181 

therefore omitted these two methods from the density and density-difference panels 182 

to improve visual resolution among the remaining competing methods. For log library 183 

size, simPIC variants broadly overlapped the real-data distribution, whereas scDesign3 184 

produced a narrower distribution centred near the middle of the observed range. The 185 

corresponding density-difference plot showed a positive deviation near the distribution 186 

centre and negative deviations in the shoulders, consistent with under-representation 187 

of library-size heterogeneity (Figure 3d and 3g). For log peak mean, simPIC closely 188 

followed the real density across most of the distribution, with modest deviations near 189 

zero; simCAS showed a stronger negative deviation at the lowest peak means and a 190 

broader positive deviation at intermediate values (Figure 3e and 3h). We also note 191 

that simPIC simulates peaks conditionally independently given peak-wise and cell-192 

wise parameters, it does not explicitly model peak–peak covariance (co-accessibility). 193 

Consistent with this design choice, we observe differences in the tails of the peak–peak 194 

correlation distribution between simulated and real data (Supplementary Figure S2 195 

and Supplementary Table S5). For cell sparsity, simPIC reproduced the high-sparsity 196 

mode characteristic of real scATAC-seq data, with residual discrepancies concentrated 197 

in the extreme high-sparsity tail; scDesign3 produced a sharper and shifted sparsity 198

2.3 simPIC outperforms competing simulators across 230 

quantitative metrics 231 

We quantified simulator performance by computing composite ranks from 232 

four distributional similarity measures: Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD), 233 

Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), and 1 −234 

Pearson Correlation Coefficient (PCC). Rankings were computed per cell type and 235 

aggregated to obtain an overall composite rank (lower is better). A Friedman test, 236 

treating cell type as a blocking factor confirmed highly significant differences in per-237 

formance across simulators (Figure 4a). Across 25 cell types, all four simPIC variants 238 

consistently achieved lower (better) composite ranks than DiTSim and scMultiSim 239 

(Wilcoxon signed-rank tests, all FDR < 0.001; Figure 4b), demonstrating a clear 240 

and consistent performance advantage. Among simPIC variants, simPIC(ln-gamma) 241 

achieved the lowest median overall composite rank, and ranked first across all six 242

Simulating multiple cell types with differential accessibility 262

Simulating batch effects across cell groups 273 

Batch effects were simulated using peak-wise, batch-specific multiplicative offsets to 274 

expected peak accessibility (peak means) values. In a two-batch, two-group design 275 

(nGroups = 2, group.probs = c(0,5,0.5), nBatches = 2), we generated two batches 276 

with equal distribution of cells from each group, specifying batch-level offsets while 277 

holding biological group structure constant. The simulated data showed separation 278 

in lower-dimensional space by both biological group and batch identity (Supplemen-279

2 (Supplementary Figure S6b), confirming coherent batch-associated structure. A 283 

peak detection rate shift plot (Batch 1 versus Batch 2) showed systematic feature-284 

level deviations from the diagonal within each group, consistent with the peak-wise 285 

multiplicative batch model (Supplementary Figure S6c). 286

# 3 Discussion 384 

The shift of single-cell technologies such as scATAC-seq to population-scale stud-385 

ies has created unprecedented opportunities for dissecting the genetic regulation of 386 

chromatin accessibility, but has also introduced new analytical challenges. Simulation 387 

tools are essential for method development because they enable controlled data gen-388 

eration under defined assumptions and study designs. The R/Bioconductor package 389 

simPIC provides a flexible and interpretable framework that captures key distribu-390 

tional properties of scATAC-seq data. Across 25 cell types from six datasets, simPIC 391 

with ln-gamma peak means achieved the highest overall fidelity to empirical distribu-392 

tions while remaining computationally tractable, whereas alternative methods required 393 

hours to days or became infeasible at scale. By estimating parameters directly from 394 

empirical data and allowing users to control group structure, batch, and genetic effects, 395 

simPIC balances realism with usability. Through integration with splatPop [6], simPIC 396 

further supports population-scale simulations with explicit genetic effects, a capability 397 

not supported by existing scATAC-seq simulators. 398 

Empirical benchmarking strategies based on orthogonal information, such as motif 399 

enrichment, transcription factor binding from ChIP-seq, or concordance with paired 400 

scRNA-seq, are valuable for validating specific aspects of scATAC-seq analyses but 401 

typically provide indirect proxies rather than explicit locus-by-cell ground truth. Simu-402 

lation addresses this limitation by generating datasets with known structure. simPIC 403 

generates peak-by-cell count matrices with explicitly defined cell-group labels, effect 404 

sizes, batch structure, and genetic effects, enabling controlled evaluation of analytical 405 

methods. These capabilities are particularly important for population-scale analyses 406 

where ground truth is not directly observable in empirical datasets and experimental 407 

designs and confounding factors are difficult to disentangle. Consistent with this ratio-408 

nale, differential accessibility method rankings derived from simPIC-simulated data 409 

across 25 cell types were concordant with those reported in real-data benchmarks [24] 410 

using real scATAC-seq data with orthogonal validation. These results provide support-411 

ing evidence that simPIC can serve as a useful tool for method benchmarking beyond 412 

the simulation setting. We note that this concordance is task-specific and depends on 413 

the fidelity of the generative model; simulation-based rankings should not be expected 414 

to transfer universally to all real datasets. 415 

A defining strength of simPIC is its ability to model genetic effects at popula-416 

tion scale, making it uniquely suited for benchmarking caQTL mapping approaches. 417 

By simulating ground-truth causal variants, effect-size distributions, and realistic 418 

association patterns, simPIC enables systematic evaluation of association methods 419 

under controlled conditions. Researchers can use simPIC to assess statistical power 420

5.2 simPIC simulation framework 525 

The simPIC simulation framework has four key components: (i) estimating parame-526 

ters from empirical data at both single sample and population levels, (ii) simulating 527 

cell groups and batches for a single sample, (iii) modelling peak means with genetic 528 

effects across a population, and (iv) simulating single-cell counts for individual and 529 

population-scale data. 530 

5.2.1 Parameter estimation 531 

a) Single-sample parameter estimation 532 

Single-sample simulation requires three key parameters estimated from a user-supplied 533 

peak-by-cell count matrix or SCE object using the simPICestimate() function: library 534 

size, peak-mean, and cell sparsity. Library size is modelled using a log-normal distri-535 

bution, with parameters mean( μ) and standard deviation ( σ). Peak-mean is computed 536 

after normalizing each cell to the dataset’s median total count. Peaks with zero counts 537 

across all cells are removed (i.e., peaks for which every single entry is zero) before 538 

parameter estimation. A lognormal-gamma mixture distribution is then fitted to the 539 

non-zero peak means using the fitdistrplus package [28] (Supplementary Note 1). This 540 

estimates the gamma shape ( α) and rate ( β), the lognormal location ( μ) and scale 541 

(σ), and the mixture weight w. Alternative distributions from the generalized gamma 542

5.2.2 Simulating cell-groups and batches for single sample 592 

simPIC simulates samples with multiple cell groups by defining distinct cell groups 593 

where peaks exhibit differential accessibility. Consistent with previous reports that 594 

approximately 13% of peaks are differentially accessible in single-cell ATAC-seq data 595 

[31], a multiplicative differential accessibility (DA) factor is assigned to each peak i and 596 

applied to its baseline mean ( λi). DA factors for differentially accessible peaks are sam-597 

pled from a log-normal distribution; non-differentialy accessible peaks are assigned a 598 

factor of one. The number of groups and cell membership probabilities are user-defined, 599 

as are parameters governing the probability of DA and the magnitude and direction 600 

of DA factors per group. The resulting SCE object contains group assignments and 601 

corresponding DA factors for each cell. 602 

Batch effects represent technical variation introduced during sample handling, 603 

preparation, or processing that can obscure true biological signals. simPIC models 604 

these effects by applying multiplicative scaling factors to peak accessibility values 605 

across cells within each batch. Specifically, for each peak i and batch b, a peak-wise scal-606 

ing factor ωbi is drawn from a log-normal distribution, ωbi ∼ LN (μb, σ b), and applied 607 

to all cells assigned to batch b. These factors scale the expected peak means (i.e., the 608 

base mean λ′ 

> i,j

for cells in batch b is multiplied by ωbi ), thereby inducing a systematic 609 

batch-specific shift in peak accessibility rather than unstructured per-cell noise (Figure 610

5.2.3 Simulating peak means with genetic effects across a 620 

population 621 

To simulate population-scale single-cell ATAC-seq data, we created a wrapper around 622 

splatPop extending the simPIC framework [6] by adapting splatPop’s gene-based 623 

model to chromatin accessibility peaks. For each peak i, the population-wide accessi-624 

bility mean ( λi) was sampled from a ln-gamma mixture distribution. This distribution 625 

is parameterised by the gamma shape ( α) and rate ( β), the lognormal location ( μ), 626 

and scale ( σ) and the mixture weight w. Peak-specific variability ( σi) was sampled 627 

from a gamma distribution, with an optional scaling factor ( s) to adjust overall vari-628 

ability. Baseline accessibility for each peak in each individual ( λi,j ) was drawn from 629 

a normal distribution centered on ( λi) and scaled by ( σi). When empirical data were 630 

provided to the splatPopSimulate() function, these sampling steps were bypassed and 631 

empirical means and variances were used directly. 632 

caQTL effects are simulated using real genotype and peak annotation data from 633 

Xiong et. al [22], including VCF and GFF files. Parameters including the proportion 634 

of accessible peaks associated with caQTLs (caPeaks), the minor allele frequency of 635 

the associated SNPs (caSNPs), and the distance between each caPeak and its linked 636 

SNP were defined to reflect the population structure of the reference dataset. caQTL 637 

effect sizes ( ωi) are sampled from a gamma distribution with parameters αc and βc638 

and applied to peak accessibility based on genotype ( Gi,j , encoded as 0, 1, or 2). 639 

The framework also supports group-specific caQTLs affecting defined cell groups, and 640 

condition-specific effects tailored to specific cohorts. 641 

Differential accessibility (DA) between groups or conditions is simulated by scaling 642 

peak accessibility using factors drawn from a log-normal distribution with parameters 643 

(μda , σ da ). Separate parameters for cell groups and cohorts allow independent control 644 

of these effects and the proportion of peaks assigned negative DA factors (reduced 645 

accessibility) can be specified to control directional heterogeneity across the simulated 646 

dataset. 647

5.4 Evaluation metrics 698 

Simulation performance was evaluated across three key characteristics: library size, 699 

peak mean, and cell sparsity. For each matched real–simulated pair, real and simulated 700 

value vectors were first aligned by quantile on a common grid of n = 1024 points, 701 

four error metrics were then computed from the aligned vectors: Median Absolute 702 

Deviation (MAD), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), 703 

and 1 minus Pearson Correlation Coefficient (1 −PCC). Together these metrics capture 704 

both central tendency and tail behaviour of distributional discrepancy. 705 

To complement the quantitative metrics, we computed two-sample Kol-706 

mogorov–Smirnov (KS) statistics for each property. The KS statistic is defined as 707 

D = sup x |Freal (x) − Fsim (x)|, where F denotes the empirical cumulative distribu-708 

tion function. KS values were computed per cell type for each tool and property, and 709 

summarised across tools and properties in a summary heatmap (Figure 3j). 710 

Distributional agreement was further assessed visually using kernel density esti-711 

mates for each property computed separately for real and simulated data. Density 712 

difference plots were constructed by subtracting the real kernel density estimate from 713 

the simulated estimate on a common evaluation grid, with positive values indicat-714 

ing regions where the simulator overestimates density and negative values indicating 715 

underestimation. Library size and peak mean distributions were plotted on a log 1p 716 

scale; cell sparsity was plotted on a proportion scale. These plots are shown in Figure 3. 717 

To aggregate performance across properties and metrics, a composite rank score 718 

was computed for each method within each matched cell type. Within each dataset ×719 

cell type × property × metric combination, methods were ranked by ascending error 720 

value (lower error = rank 1). The composite rank for each method within each cell 721 

type was then defined as the mean rank across all three properties and all four metrics, 722 

yielding 12 rank values per cell type. The distribution of composite rank across cell 723 

types per method is shown in Figure 4a. 724 

To assess whether differences in composite rank between simPIC variants and 725 

competing methods were statistically supported, each simPIC variant was compared 726 

against each competing tool using matched cell types only. For each pairwise compar-727 

ison, paired differences in composite rank were computed per cell type. The median 728 

paired difference, bootstrap 95% confidence interval for the median, Wilcoxon signed-729 

rank test p-value, and Benjamini–Hochberg (BH) adjusted FDR were computed for 730 

each comparison. A negative median paired difference indicates better performance of 731 

the simPIC variant relative to the comparator. Results are shown in Figure 4b. 732 

To assess whether simulated data preserves global peak–peak dependence, we 733 

additionally computed pairwise peak–peak correlations on TF–IDF transformed acces-734 

sibility for the top 2,000 variable peaks (after filtering peaks detected in ≥ 10 cells). 735 

Distributions of signed and absolute correlations |r| between real and simulated data 736 

were compared, with tail behaviour summarised using the 95th/99th percentiles of 737 

|r| and the number of peak pairs with |r| > 0.3 and |r| > 0.5 (Supplementary Fig. S2; 738 

Supplementary Table S5). 739

5.6 caQTL mapping analyses 759 

We simulated a larger dataset based on parameter estimates from library 5 of the 760 

microglia cell type from the Xiong et.al, dataset [22]. The simulation included 100 cells 761 

per individual for 83 samples, matching the real dataset, within a single batch. caQTL 762 

effects were assigned to 70% of the simulated peaks. Single-cell counts were normal-763

and quantile normalized to a standard normal distribution [33]. caQTL mapping was 765

included SNP genotype as a fixed effect, the top 15 principal components from chro-768 

matin accessibility PCA as fixed effects to control unwanted variation, and a kinship 769

structure. Associations with empirical feature p-values below 0.005 were considered 771 

significant. Manhattan plots showing the full chromosome 22 and regional 400 kb 772
