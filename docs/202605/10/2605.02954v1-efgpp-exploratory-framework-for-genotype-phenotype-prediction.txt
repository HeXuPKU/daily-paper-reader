# SUMMARY 8

Predicting complex phenotypes from human genetic data is increasingly constrained not by data 9

availability, but by how best to prioritize and combine heterogeneous sources of signal. Here, we 10 

present EFGPP, a systematic framework for generating, ranking, and integrating heterogeneous 11 

genetic data representations for genotype–phenotype prediction. Using UK Biobank data from 12 

733 individuals, we evaluated migraine prediction by combining genotype-derived features, prin- 13 

cipal components, clinical and metabolomic covariates, and PRS generated from two migraine 14 

GWAS and three depression GWAS using PLINK, PRS ICE -2, A NNO PRED , and LDAK-GWAS. 15 

The best individual representation achieved a test AUC of 0.644, while structured multimodal 16 

integration improved performance to 0.688 in a migraine-focused configuration and 0.663 in 17 

a cross-trait configuration incorporating depression-derived inputs. No single genetic modality 18 

matched the covariates-only baseline (AUC 0.639), although genotype features outperformed 19 

PRS in isolation and depression-derived PRS remained competitive. These results position 20 

EFGPP as a reproducible proof-of-concept framework for systematic data prioritization and mul- 21 

timodal integration in genotype–phenotype prediction. 22

# INTRODUCTION 25 

Genotype-phenotype prediction involves predicting traits and diseases based on genetic datasets 1,2 .26 

It has diverse applications, including understanding disease mechanisms 3, personalized medicine 4,5 ,27 

case-control classification, disease treatment responses 6, and exploring the interplay between 28 

genetic diseases 7,8 . 29 

Data from various biological, environmental 9, and phenotypic dimensions are gathered and 30 

incorporated into a coherent framework for robust predictive modeling 10 . These data sources 31 

include, but are not limited to, genotype data obtained by selecting top single nucleotide poly- 32 

morphisms (SNPs) using p-value thresholding 11 from genome-wide association studies (GWAS). 33 

Covariates encompass medical conditions, metabolite levels, sex, and other demographic or clin- 34 

ical variables that may influence phenotypic expression 12 . Principal component analysis (PCA) 35 

is applied to the genotype data to account for population stratification and reduce dimension- 36 

ality 13 . Functional annotations (FA) provide insights into the biological relevance of genetic 37 

variants, assisting in prioritizing SNPs for prediction models 14 . GWAS offers summary statis- 38 

tics highlighting associations between genetic variants and traits, serving as a foundation for 39

identifying significant SNPs 15 . Polygenic risk scores (PRS) 16,17 , derived from GWAS data, ag- 40 

gregate the effects of multiple genetic variants to estimate an individual’s genetic predisposition 41 

to specific traits or diseases. These data sources are combined to create a feature set, and 42 

machine learning (ML) or deep learning (DL) algorithms are employed for phenotype predic- 43 

tion 10,18 . Researchers have further enhanced prediction performance by integrating multiple 44 

GWAS 19,20 for one phenotype, leveraging GWAS from related phenotypes 21 and populations 22 , 45 

and combining multiple PRS 23 . For instance, combining GWAS data from migraine and depres- 46 

sion enables the development of multi-trait PRS, capturing shared genetic architectures between 47 

related conditions. To address the wide variety of data representations and integration strategies 48 

available for genotype–phenotype prediction, we developed EFGPP, an exploratory framework 49 

that systematically generates, prioritizes, and integrates heterogeneous datasets. The frame- 50 

work first constructs individual datasets from covariates, genotype-derived matrices (weighted 51 

or unweighted, annotated or non-annotated), principal components, and polygenic risk scores 52 

(PRS) generated using PLINK 24 , PRS ICE -2 25 , A NNO PRED 26 , and LDAK-GWAS 27 . These in- 53 

dividual datasets are then benchmarked and ranked according to predictive performance and 54 

stability, after which the strongest representatives from each category are carried forward for 55 

multimodal integration. The framework supports multiple GWAS from the same or related phe- 56 

notypes, incorporates functional annotation, and enables structured comparison of genetic and 57 

non-genetic inputs within a unified modelling workflow. In this way, EFGPP serves as a prac- 58 

tical decision framework for identifying which data representations and combinations are most 59 

informative for case–control prediction under limited-sample conditions. Figure 1 summarizes 60 

the overall workflow of EFGPP. 61 

Despite this progress, there remains limited practical guidance on how to systematically eval- 62 

uate, rank, and combine these heterogeneous inputs in a reproducible way, particularly in mod- 63 

estly sized datasets where exhaustive exploration can quickly introduce redundancy and overfit- 64 

ting. The field now has many possible genetic representations, PRS tools, and source GWAS, 65 

but no simple framework for deciding which combinations are worth carrying forward for a given 66 

phenotype under realistic data limitations. This gap is especially consequential for complex traits 67 

with moderate polygenic signal, where the marginal contribution of each data modality is small 68 

and selection decisions have a disproportionate impact on out-of-sample performance. Migraine 69 

provides a useful test case in which to address this problem directly. It is a common and clinically 70 

heterogeneous neurological disorder with a substantial polygenic component, and it frequently 71 

co-occurs with depression. Beyond epidemiological comorbidity, genetic studies have identified 72 

overlapping polygenic architecture and shared loci between migraine and psychiatric traits in- 73 

cluding depression 28 , suggesting that cross-trait information may contain predictive signal not 74 

captured by migraine-only models. At the same time, related-trait integration is not guaranteed 75 

to improve performance, making migraine and depression a stringent setting in which to test 76 

a framework designed to prioritize informative inputs rather than simply accumulate features. 77 

Here, we present EFGPP, an exploratory framework for genotype–phenotype prediction that 78 

addresses this practical model-development problem: given multiple GWAS, PRS tools, geno- 79 

type representations, functional annotations, and covariates, which combinations are most worth 80 

carrying forward for a given phenotype? Framed in this way, EFGPP is best viewed as a de- 81 

cision framework for systematic data prioritization in genotype–phenotype modelling and as a 82 

foundation for larger-scale benchmarking, external validation, and future biological refinement. 83 

2Figure 1: Overview of the EFGPP workflow. The framework proceeds through six main stages. (1) Data sources: heterogeneous inputs are assembled from the target cohort and external discovery resources, including genotype data, covariates, principal components, func-tional annotations, and GWAS summary statistics. (2) Dataset generation: multiple individual datasets are constructed, including genotype-derived, covariate, PCA, and PRS-based repre-sentations. (3) Ranking and pruning: each dataset is benchmarked across machine-learning models and ranked according to predictive performance, generalization, and stability, while re-dundant representations are removed. (4) Category selection: the strongest retained datasets are grouped into representative families such as covariates, PCA, genotype, and PRS. (5) Multi-modal search: shortlisted datasets are systematically combined to evaluate whether integrated representations improve predictive performance beyond any single modality. (6) Final evalu-ation: the best individual and multimodal models are compared using cross-validated perfor-mance metrics to quantify the gain from structured data integration.

## EFGPP constructs and prioritizes heterogeneous genotype–phenotype rep- 85 

## resentations 86 

We first used EFGPP to generate and evaluate heterogeneous representations for migraine 87 

prediction, including covariates, principal components, genotype-derived matrices, and PRS de- 88 

rived from migraine and depression GWAS. In the migraine-focused configuration, 174 candidate 89 

datasets were generated and reduced to 62 non-redundant representations after similarity-based 90 

pruning. In the expanded cross-trait configuration, 975 candidate datasets were reduced to 489 91 

non-redundant representations. Following fold-wise model benchmarking, validation-based filter- 92 

ing, and composite-score ranking, 11 datasets from the migraine-focused configuration and 16 93 

datasets from the cross-trait configuration were carried forward for multimodal integration. This 94 

staged reduction illustrates the main purpose of EFGPP: to make large representation spaces 95 

tractable while preserving modality diversity for downstream prediction. 96 

Across individual datasets, the strongest single-representation performance was observed for 97

## Single-modality analyses show that no genetic representation is sufficient 116 

## alone 117 

To quantify the independent predictive contribution of each data modality, we conducted an abla- 118 

tion analysis across all individual datasets generated by EFGPP, averaging performance across 119 

five cross-validation folds and reporting 95% confidence intervals (Table 2). The covariates- 120 

only model, comprising 135 NMR metabolomic biomarkers and comorbid condition indicators, 121 

4achieved the highest test AUC among all single-modality configurations (AUC = 0.639, 95% CI 122 

[0.494, 0.784]), establishing a strong non-genetic baseline. No genetic modality alone matched 123 

this baseline. To reduce information leakage during this large representation search, all data- 124 

dependent processing and model-selection steps were performed within each fold before held- 125 

out test evaluation (Figure 2). Training and validation subsets were used for dataset construction, 126 

model fitting, hyperparameter comparison, ranking, pruning, and multimodal combination selec- 127 

tion, whereas held-out test subsets were reserved exclusively for final fold-level performance 128 

evaluation. 129

## PRS tool performance provides broader benchmarking context 177 

To contextualise the PRS results obtained within EFGPP, we draw on findings from a companion 178 

study 29 in which 46 PRS tools were benchmarked across seven binary UK Biobank pheno- 179 

types under harmonised preprocessing and cross-validation conditions, using the same 733- 180 

participant cohort and covariate structure employed here. In that study, the best-performing tool 181 

for migraine was XPBLUP (full-model AUC = 0 .629 ), followed by GEMMA-LMM (AUC = 0 .629 ) 182 

and GEMMA-LM (AUC = 0 .628 ), with PRSice-2 achieving AUC = 0 .549 under the same full- 183 

8model configuration. These values provide an externally validated single-tool ceiling for migraine 184 

PRS prediction in this cohort and confirm that the best individual PRS dataset within EFGPP 185 

(test AUC = 0 .521 for PLINK-derived migraine PRS in the ablation analysis) is consistent with 186 

the range reported across tools in the broader benchmark. 187 

Critically, the best multimodal combination in EFGPP Configuration 1 (test AUC = 0 .688 ) 188 

exceeded the best single-tool full-model result from the 46-tool benchmark (AUC = 0 .629 ) by 189 

+0 .059 AUC units, demonstrating that structured multimodal integration provides a measurable 190 

improvement beyond what any single PRS tool can achieve in isolation, even after covariate 191 

adjustment. Depression-derived PRS contributed competitive predictive signal for migraine pre- 192 

diction across all four tools evaluated; among all PRS configurations, LDAK-GWAS-depression 193 

achieved the strongest result (AUC = 0.564, 95% CI [0.520, 0.608], ∆ = −0.075 ), outperform- 194 

ing every migraine-derived PRS, consistent with documented genetic overlap between the two 195 

traits 28 . 196 

## Structured multimodal integration improves migraine prediction 197 

Single-modality analysis showed that no genetic representation alone matched the covariates- 198 

only baseline, although genotype-derived matrices were consistently more competitive than PRS 199 

in isolation. Structured integration improved performance beyond these individual inputs. In the 200 

migraine-focused configuration, the best multimodal combination achieved a mean test AUC 201 

of 0.688, compared with 0.644 for the strongest individual representation and 0.639 for the 202 

covariates-only baseline. In the cross-trait configuration incorporating depression-derived in- 203 

puts, the best multimodal model achieved a mean test AUC of 0.663. These results indicate that 204 

EFGPP can identify complementary data representations that improve prediction under limited- 205 

sample conditions, while also showing that related-trait data must be empirically prioritized rather 206 

than added indiscriminately. 207 

We created dataset combinations and trained each using the stacked artificial neural network 208 

framework described in STAR Methods. For Configuration 1, 11 selected datasets yielded 1,012 209 

candidate combinations of 2–5 datasets before filtering; after discarding combinations with mean 210 

validation AUC below 0.50, 427 unique combinations remained. For Configuration 2, 16 selected 211 

datasets yielded 6,868 candidate combinations before filtering, of which 366 passed the valida- 212 

tion threshold. Results for all combinations are available on GitHub ( Configuration1/ResultsML1.csv ,213 

Configuration2/ResultsML2.csv ). 214 

Systematic evaluation of multimodal combinations confirmed that structured integration of 215 

heterogeneous genetic representations improves migraine prediction beyond any single data 216 

modality (Tables 3–5). In Configuration 1, which restricted inputs to migraine-derived sources, 217 

two three-dataset combinations tied for the highest mean test AUC. The most parsimonious was 218 

the combination of covariates, PCA, and a weighted non-annotated migraine genotype matrix 219 

(mean test AUC 0.688 ± 0.109 , 95% CI [0 .552 , 0.824] , ∆ = +0 .049 ); a second combination of co- 220 

variates and two weighted non-annotated migraine genotype matrices at different SNP thresh- 221 

olds achieved a comparable test AUC of 0.685 ± 0.127 (95% CI [0 .527 , 0.843] , ∆ = +0 .046 ). A 222 

five-dataset combination of covariates, PCA, and three genotype matrices reached 0.683 ± 0.064 223 

with a narrower confidence interval, suggesting improved stability when additional complemen- 224 

tary representations are included. Notably, no PRS dataset appeared in the top-performing 225 

Configuration 1 combinations; weighted non-annotated genotype matrices from migraine GWAS 226 

were the dominant genetic representation across all top models, suggesting that in this set- 227 

ting, directly modelled SNP-level genotype data captures more predictive signal than aggregated 228 

PRS scores. Across all top Configuration 1 combinations, covariates were consistently retained 229 

alongside genotype-derived features, reinforcing the conclusion that clinical and metabolomic 230 

9covariates capture phenotype-proximal signal that complements purely genetic predictors. 231

The EFGPP workflow is designed to support reproducible representation prioritization across 268

# DISCUSSION 276 

This study presents EFGPP as a practical framework for prioritizing and integrating heteroge- 277 

neous inputs for genotype–phenotype prediction. Rather than introducing a new PRS algorithm, 278 

the framework addresses a common but under-served modelling problem: when multiple GWAS, 279 

PRS methods, genotype representations, annotations, and covariates are available, which inputs 280 

are most informative to carry forward, and which combinations generalize best? In this proof- 281 

of-concept application to migraine prediction, EFGPP identified a best individual representation 282 

with a test AUC of 0.644 and improved performance to 0.688 through structured multimodal 283 

integration in a migraine-focused configuration, with a cross-trait configuration reaching 0.663. 284 

Framed in this way, the main contribution of the study is methodological and practical: it offers a 285 

reproducible strategy for reducing a large space of plausible data representations into a smaller 286 

set of better-supported modelling choices. 287 

Several findings from the single-dataset and integrated analyses are particularly informative. 288 

First, no single genetic representation dominated across all settings. The covariates-only model 289 

established a strong non-genetic baseline (AUC 0.639), and no single genetic modality exceeded 290 

it. Among the single-modality genetic representations, genotype-based features were more com- 291 

petitive than PRS, with the strongest depression-derived genotype representation reaching AUC 292 

0.620 and the best migraine-derived genotype representations reaching AUC 0.605. By contrast, 293 

the strongest migraine-derived PRS reached AUC 0.521, while the strongest overall PRS rep- 294 

resentation was depression-derived LDAK-GWAS at AUC 0.564. These findings suggest that, 295 

in this cohort, directly modelled SNP-level genotype information retained more predictive signal 296 

than aggregated PRS scores when each modality was evaluated in isolation. 297 

Second, the top multimodal models were generally parsimonious, and adding more datasets 298 

did not guarantee improved generalization. In Configuration 1, the best-performing model com- 299 

bined only three inputs – covariates, PCA, and a weighted non-annotated migraine genotype 300 

matrix – and achieved the strongest overall performance (AUC 0.688). A slightly more complex 301 

five-dataset model showed somewhat narrower variability but did not surpass this mean test 302 

AUC. This is an important result in its own right: in modestly sized cohorts, disciplined feature 303 

prioritization may matter more than raw feature volume. It also explains why no PRS representa- 304 

tion appeared in the strongest Configuration 1 combinations, despite PRS remaining biologically 305 

relevant and competitive in some single-modality settings. 306 

The cross-trait analyses provide an additional biologically and methodologically relevant sig- 307 

nal. Migraine and depression are well known to co-occur, and accumulating evidence supports 308 

partial overlap in their biology and genetic architecture 28 . Within this context, the observation 309 

that depression-derived PRS contributed to competitive migraine prediction models is plausible 310 

and strengthens the rationale for testing related-trait information within a structured framework. 311 

In particular, the best Configuration 2 model combined migraine-derived genotype features with 312 

depression-derived LDAK-GWAS PRS and achieved AUC 0.663. Importantly, these results 313 

should not be interpreted as showing that cross-disorder data are universally beneficial; rather, 314 

they suggest that related-trait inputs can be useful when they are empirically prioritized and 315 

selectively integrated. That distinction matters, because naive feature expansion can increase 316 

apparent training performance without improving out-of-sample prediction. 317 

The broader benchmarking context is also informative. In a companion harmonized study of 318 

46 PRS tools evaluated in the same 733-participant UK Biobank cohort, the best single-tool full- 319 

model migraine AUC was 0.629. Against that benchmark, the best multimodal Configuration 1 320 

12 result in EFGPP (AUC 0.688) represents a gain of +0 .059 AUC units, indicating that structured 321 

multimodal integration can improve upon the ceiling achieved by individual PRS tools alone. At 322 

the same time, the absolute gains observed here remain modest: relative to the covariates-only 323 

baseline, the best multimodal model improved test AUC by +0 .049 in Configuration 1 and +0 .024 324 

in Configuration 2. This pattern is consistent with the incremental and complementary nature of 325 

the signal being integrated and underscores that the value of EFGPP lies primarily in systematic 326 

data prioritization rather than in producing large absolute performance gains. 327 

The current study should therefore be interpreted as a proof-of-concept rather than a clinically 328 

deployable prediction model. The sample size is modest, the performance gains are incremental, 329 

and the strongest integrated models include non-genetic covariates that may capture phenotype- 330 

proximal or comorbidity-related information in addition to inherited risk. Accordingly, the present 331 

study is best viewed as demonstrating a workflow for representation selection and multimodal 332 

benchmarking, not as establishing a migraine risk predictor ready for translational use. Stronger 333 

claims would require larger cohorts, broader benchmarking against contemporary PRS methods, 334 

more formal nested model selection, calibration analyses, and external validation in independent 335 

datasets. 336 

Several limitations define the next stage of development. The framework explores a large 337 

combinatorial space, and although ranking and category-based reduction make this tractable, 338 

the search process remains computationally intensive. Performance may depend on the avail- 339 

able discovery GWAS, the ancestry composition of both discovery and target data, the selected 340 

SNP thresholds, and the specific feature categories retained after prioritization. More broadly, 341 

PRS and related genetic predictors remain sensitive to discovery sample composition and often 342 

show reduced portability across settings, emphasizing the need for cautious interpretation and 343 

external validation. Future work should therefore focus on extending the framework across phe- 344 

notypes, ancestries, and larger cohorts; incorporating stronger contemporary PRS baselines; 345 

formalizing nested selection; and improving reproducibility through clearer reporting of each de- 346 

cision point in the prioritization pipeline. 347 

Taken together, our results support a simple conclusion: for complex trait prediction, the key 348 

challenge is often not data scarcity, but data prioritization. EFGPP provides a structured way 349 

to evaluate multiple GWAS-derived and genotype-derived representations, identify those with 350 

the strongest evidence of utility and stability, and test whether they contribute complementary 351 

predictive information when combined. In migraine, this strategy improved performance beyond 352 

any single representation and showed that selectively integrated cross-trait information can add 353 

value. More broadly, the framework offers a practical foundation for future studies seeking to 354 

move from opportunistic feature assembly toward systematic, evidence-based construction of 355 

genotype–phenotype prediction models. 356 

# Limitations of the study 357 

This study has several limitations. First, the analysis was performed in a modestly sized UK 358 

Biobank cohort of 733 individuals, including 53 migraine cases and 680 controls, which limits 359 

the precision and generalizability of the performance estimates. Second, external validation in 360 

an independent cohort was not performed, and the results should therefore be interpreted as 361 

proof of concept rather than evidence of clinical deployability. Third, calibration analysis was 362 

not performed, and the framework was not evaluated as a clinical risk prediction model. Fourth, 363 

although the fold-wise design reserved held-out test subsets for final evaluation, the large rep- 364 

resentation search remains computationally intensive and should be further evaluated in larger 365 

cohorts. Fifth, the analysis focused on migraine and depression-derived cross-trait inputs; future 366 

work should test additional phenotypes, ancestries, PRS tools, and external datasets. 367

## Method details 416 

EFGPP framework overview 417 

EFGPP is a reproducible framework for generating, prioritizing, pruning, and integrating hetero- 418 

geneous genotype–phenotype data representations. The framework was designed to address a 419 

practical model-development question: given multiple GWAS, PRS tools, genotype representa- 420 

tions, functional annotations, principal components, and covariates, which data representations 421 

and combinations should be carried forward for phenotype prediction under limited-sample con- 422 

ditions? 423 

The workflow comprised six main stages: heterogeneous data-source assembly, candidate 424 

dataset generation, individual dataset benchmarking, redundancy pruning and category-level 425 

ranking, multimodal combination testing, and final held-out evaluation. Candidate representa- 426 

tions included covariates, principal components, genotype-derived SNP matrices, GWAS-weighted 427 

SNP matrices, functionally annotated SNP matrices, weighted and annotated SNP matrices, and 428 

PRS-derived representations. 429 

Six base data sources were used to construct the candidate representation space: GWAS 430 

summary statistics, target-cohort genotype data, phenotype labels, covariates, linkage disequi- 431 

librium reference data, and functional annotations. GWAS summary statistics were obtained 432 

from the GWAS Catalog. Genotype, phenotype, covariate, and metabolomic data were obtained 433 

from UK Biobank. Linkage disequilibrium reference data were obtained from LDAK, and func- 434 

tional annotation files were obtained from AnnoPred. PRS representations were generated us- 435 

ing PLINK, PRSice-2, AnnoPred, and LDAK-GWAS. These inputs were processed into covariate, 436 

PCA, genotype-derived, annotated, weighted, weighted-and-annotated, and PRS-based dataset 437 

families before downstream benchmarking, pruning, ranking, and multimodal integration. 438 

For each GWAS source, genotype-derived datasets were constructed by selecting SNPs 439 

at predefined thresholds and representing them as unweighted, GWAS-weighted, functionally 440 

annotated, or weighted-and-annotated matrices. PCA representations were used to capture 441 

population-structure-related variation, while covariate representations included clinical and metabolomic 442 

variables. PRS-derived representations were generated separately for each GWAS–tool combi- 443 

nation. These candidate datasets formed the input space for downstream benchmarking, prun- 444 

ing, ranking, and multimodal integration. 445 

All data-dependent processing steps were performed within the fold-wise evaluation design. 446 

Training and validation subsets were used for dataset construction, model fitting, hyperparame- 447 

ter comparison, ranking, pruning, and multimodal combination selection. Held-out test subsets 448 

were reserved exclusively for final fold-level performance evaluation and were not used to guide 449 

dataset selection, model choice, or combination search. 450 

16 Phenotype definition and covariates 451 

Migraine case–control status was defined using UK Biobank phenotype information. The final 452 

analysis cohort contained 53 migraine cases and 680 controls. Covariate representations in- 453 

cluded 135 NMR metabolomic biomarkers and comorbid condition indicators. These covariates 454 

were used both as an individual non-genetic baseline and as candidate inputs for multimodal 455 

integration. 456 

GWAS summary statistics 457 

Five GWAS summary statistic files were used to construct phenotype-matched and cross-trait 458 

representations. Two GWAS files were migraine-derived and three were depression-derived. 459 

Migraine-derived GWAS were used to construct phenotype-matched genetic representations, 460 

while depression-derived GWAS were included to evaluate whether cross-trait genetic signal 461 

could contribute to migraine prediction. The GWAS summary statistics were obtained from the 462 

GWAS Catalog and corresponded to accessions GCST90038646, GCST90043745, GCST90038650, 463 

GCST90101808, and GCST005839 30–33 . GWAS files were quality controlled by retaining SNPs 464 

with minor allele frequency (MAF) > 0.01 and imputation information score (INFO) > 0.8, while 465 

removing ambiguous and missing variants 34,35 . 466 

Genotype-derived representations 467 

Genotype-derived datasets were constructed from UK Biobank genotype data using GWAS- 468 

informed SNP selection. Genotype quality control was applied using thresholds of MAF > 0.01 , 469 

Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium P > 1 × 10 −6, genotype missingness ≤ 0.1, individual missingness 470 

≤ 0.1, and relatedness cutoff ≤ 0.125 36,37 . SNPs were selected at predefined thresholds and 471 

converted into genotype matrices using P -value thresholding 38 . 472 

For weighted genotype matrices, SNP dosages were weighted using GWAS-derived effect 473 

estimates. For annotated matrices, selected SNPs were intersected with functional annota- 474 

tion information obtained from AnnoPred. These procedures generated compact and expanded 475 

genotype-derived representations across GWAS sources, SNP thresholds, weighting status, and 476 

annotation status. 477 

Principal component analysis 478 

Principal component analysis was used to generate PCA-based representations from genotype 479 

data. PCA representations were included to account for population-structure-related variation 480 

and to test whether broad genetic structure alone contributed predictive information for migraine 481 

status. 482 

Polygenic risk score generation 483 

PRS representations were generated using four PRS tools: PLINK, PRSice-2, AnnoPred, and 484 

LDAK-GWAS. These tools were selected to represent distinct PRS method families and were 485 

interpreted in the context of our companion harmonized benchmark of 46 PRS tools across UK 486 

Biobank phenotypes 29 . PRS were generated separately for each GWAS source and tool com- 487 

bination. Migraine-derived PRS represented phenotype-matched genetic risk, while depression- 488 

derived PRS represented cross-trait genetic signal. These PRS-derived datasets were evaluated 489 

both as individual representations and as candidate inputs for multimodal integration. 490 

17 Configuration design 491 

Two main configurations were evaluated. Configuration 1 focused on migraine-derived inputs and 492 

represented the migraine-focused search space. Configuration 2 expanded the search space by 493 

incorporating depression-derived GWAS and PRS inputs to evaluate cross-trait contribution. 494 

In Configuration 1, 174 candidate datasets were generated and reduced to 62 non-redundant 495 

representations after similarity-based pruning. In Configuration 2, 975 candidate datasets were 496 

generated and reduced to 489 non-redundant representations. Following fold-wise benchmark- 497 

ing, validation-based filtering, and category-level ranking, 11 datasets from Configuration 1 and 498 

16 datasets from Configuration 2 were selected for multimodal combination testing. 499 

Fold-wise evaluation design 500 

The full dataset was partitioned into five stratified folds while preserving the migraine case– 501 

control ratio. For each fold, training, validation, and held-out test subsets were defined. Train- 502 

ing and validation subsets were used for dataset construction, model fitting, hyperparameter 503 

comparison, dataset ranking, pruning, and multimodal combination selection. The held-out test 504 

subset for each fold was reserved exclusively for final fold-level evaluation. 505 

To reduce information leakage during the large-scale search procedure, all data-dependent 506 

steps were performed within each fold using only the training and validation subsets. These 507 

steps included GWAS filtering, genotype quality control, SNP selection, functional annotation, 508 

PCA derivation, PRS generation, model fitting, hyperparameter comparison, dataset ranking, 509 

pruning, and multimodal combination selection. The held-out test subset was used only once 510 

for final fold-level evaluation after all within-fold selection decisions had been completed, and 511 

no test-set information was used to guide dataset prioritization, model choice, or combination 512 

search. 513 

This design should be interpreted as a stratified five-fold held-out validation framework with 514 

within-fold train/validation/test splitting, rather than strict nested cross-validation. 515 

Individual dataset benchmarking 516 

Each retained individual dataset was evaluated using 12 classification machine-learning algo- 517 

rithms. These included standard classifiers, ensemble models, gradient-boosting methods, and 518 

neural-network-based models. Models included logistic regression, decision tree, random for- 519 

est, support vector machine, Naive Bayes, k-nearest neighbours, gradient boosting, XGBoost, 520 

LightGBM, CatBoost, multilayer perceptron, and artificial neural network classifiers. 521 

Models were optimized via hyperparameter tuning where applicable, configured to handle 522 

class imbalance, and evaluated using training, validation, and held-out test performance across 523 

folds. For each dataset, the top-performing model was selected based on validation performance 524 

and the composite score. Datasets with mean validation AUC greater than 0.6 were retained for 525 

downstream prioritization and multimodal combination testing. Using this criterion, 49 datasets 526 

passed the validation threshold in Configuration 1 and 355 datasets passed the validation thresh- 527 

old in Configuration 2. 528 

Redundancy pruning 529 

To reduce redundancy in the representation space, candidate datasets were compared using 530 

similarity-based pruning based on the Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic 39 . Highly similar datasets 531 

were removed before downstream ranking and multimodal testing. This step was used to make 532 

18 the large representation space tractable while preserving diversity across major representation 533 

families, including covariates, PCA, genotype-derived features, and PRS-derived features. 534 

Category-level ranking and composite score 535 

Retained datasets were grouped into categories to preserve representation diversity while re- 536 

ducing the multimodal search space. Categories included covariates, PCA, genotype-derived 537 

representations stratified by weighting and annotation status, and PRS representations stratified 538 

by GWAS file and PRS tool. 539 

The retained datasets within each category were ranked using a composite score that in- 540 

corporated validation AUC, train-validation generalization gap, training stability, and validation 541 

stability: 542 

Composite score = 0 .25 × Validation AUC norm 

+ 0 .25 × Train–validation gap norm 

+ 0 .25 × Train stability norm 

+ 0 .25 × Validation stability norm . (1) Top-ranked representatives from each category were carried forward for multimodal combi- 543 

nation testing. This category-based approach was used to avoid selecting many near-duplicate 544 

datasets from the same representation family and to preserve modality diversity during integra- 545 

tion. 546 

Single-modality ablation analysis 547 

Single-modality ablation analysis was performed using individual dataset results generated within 548 

the EFGPP framework. For each of the five cross-validation folds, the best-performing machine- 549 

learning model was selected per dataset based on validation AUC. 550 

Individual datasets were grouped into mutually exclusive categories according to data type, 551 

GWAS source phenotype, PRS tool, and genotype annotation and weighting status. For each 552 

category, the best-performing dataset was identified by mean test AUC across folds. Perfor- 553 

mance was summarized using mean training AUC, validation AUC, test AUC, standard deviation, 554 

95% confidence interval, and the difference in test AUC relative to the covariates-only baseline. 555 

Multimodal integration 556 

Multimodal integration was performed after category-level prioritization. Selected datasets were 557 

combined systematically to test whether integrated representations improved prediction beyond 558 

individual modalities. For Configuration 1, 11 selected datasets were used to generate candidate 559 

combinations of 2–5 datasets. For Configuration 2, 16 selected datasets were used to generate 560 

candidate combinations of 2–5 datasets. 561 

Each dataset combination was evaluated using a stacked artificial neural network framework. 562 

In this framework, each input dataset was processed as a separate representation branch, and 563 

learned representations were combined before final classification. Candidate combinations with 564 

low validation performance were filtered before final comparison. The final multimodal models 565 

were evaluated on held-out test subsets across folds. 566 

For Configuration 1, 1,012 candidate combinations were generated before filtering, and 427 567 

unique combinations remained after discarding combinations with mean validation AUC below 568 

19 0.50. For Configuration 2, 6,868 candidate combinations were generated before filtering, and 569 

366 passed the validation threshold. Results for all multimodal combinations are available in the 570 

EFGPP GitHub repository. 571 

Cross-trait analysis 572 

Cross-trait analysis was performed by incorporating depression-derived GWAS and PRS repre- 573 

sentations into Configuration 2. These inputs were evaluated to determine whether related-trait 574 

genetic signal improved migraine prediction. Depression-derived PRS and genotype-derived 575 

representations were assessed both as individual representations and as components of multi- 576 

modal combinations. 577 

The cross-trait analysis was interpreted as a test of representation prioritization rather than 578 

a claim that related-trait inputs should always improve prediction. Depression-derived PRS 579 

showed evidence of transferable signal, but the best cross-trait multimodal configuration did not 580 

exceed the best migraine-focused multimodal configuration, indicating that related-trait data must 581 

be empirically prioritized rather than added indiscriminately. 582 

PRS benchmarking context 583 

PRS results from EFGPP were interpreted alongside a companion benchmark of 46 PRS tools 584 

across seven binary UK Biobank phenotypes using harmonized preprocessing and cross-validation 585 

conditions. This comparison was used to contextualize the magnitude of PRS-only performance 586 

in the same cohort and to determine whether structured multimodal integration exceeded the 587 

performance range observed for individual PRS tools. 588
