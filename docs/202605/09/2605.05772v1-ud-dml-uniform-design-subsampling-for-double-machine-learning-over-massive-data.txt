Abstract 

Double machine learning (DML) delivers valid inference on low-dimensional causal pa-rameters while permitting flexible nuisance estimation, but its computational cost becomes prohibitive once cross-fitted learners must be trained on massive observational data. Ap-plying DML to a uniformly drawn subsample alleviates this burden, yet such a reduction disregards the geometry of the covariate space and can exacerbate treated–control imbal-ance as well as overlap deficiency. We propose Uniform Design Double Machine Learning (UD-DML), a design-based subsampling strategy for average treatment effect (ATE) estima-tion. UD-DML first constructs a low-discrepancy skeleton in a PCA-rotated covariate space under the mixture-discrepancy criterion, and then assigns, to each skeleton point, the near-est treated and control units via KD-tree search. The resulting matched subsample is, by construction, both representative of the full covariate distribution and balanced across treat-ment arms; cross-fitted DML is subsequently applied to it. We establish discrepancy-based guarantees for representativeness and balance, and prove that the UD-DML estimator is √r-asymptotically normal under mild conditions, where the selected subsample size r ≪ n.The dominant nuisance-fitting cost is thereby reduced from the n-scale to the r-scale. Monte Carlo experiments show that UD-DML attains lower RMSE, narrower confidence intervals and more reliable coverage than uniform subsampling, with the largest gains in low-overlap and misspecified regimes. An application to a large observational dataset further demon-strates its practical feasibility. 

Keywords: Causal Inference, Double Machine Learning, Massive Data, Uniform Design, Sub-sampling. 

# 1 Introduction 

Modern observational datasets are increasingly produced at massive scale in a wide range of causal applications, including comparative effectiveness studies based on electronic health records and claims data, policy optimization on digital platforms using large-scale user logs, and population-level evaluation from linked administrative records (Stuart et al., 2013; Chetty and Hendren, 2018; Luo et al., 2024). In such settings, the objective extends beyond pre-diction: valid estimation and inference for causal effects are required on samples whose size commonly lies between 10 5 and 10 7. This massive-data regime poses a fundamental computa-tional challenge for classical causal inference procedures, since many modern estimators require repeated fitting of treatment and outcome models on very large samples, often compounded by cross-validation, repeated sample splitting or cross-fitting, and bootstrap-based uncertainty 1

quantification (Zivich and Breskin, 2021; Kosko et al., 2024). Consequently, procedures that are statistically well understood in moderate samples may become prohibitively expensive in large observational studies, raising the central question of how to scale classical causal inference procedures to massive datasets without sacrificing inferential validity. Among contemporary approaches to causal inference with observational data, double/debiased machine learning (DML) has emerged as a leading framework for valid inference on low-dimensional causal parameters (Chernozhukov et al., 2018). The framework couples two key ingredients: Neyman-orthogonal scores (Bickel et al., 1993; Newey, 1994; Bang and Robins, 2005), which are locally insensitive to first-order nuisance-estimation error, and cross-fitting, which decouples nuisance learning from target-parameter evaluation and thereby controls over-fitting bias. This construction accommodates flexible machine-learning estimators for nuisance components—such as the propensity score and the conditional outcome regressions—while re-taining root-n inference on the target causal estimand under mild rate conditions. The same orthogonal-learning paradigm has since been extended to richer causal targets, including condi-tional and heterogeneous treatment effects (Semenova and Chernozhukov, 2021; Nie and Wager, 2021); see Kennedy (2024) for a broader review of semiparametric and doubly robust machine-learning-based causal inference. Despite these statistical advantages, standard DML does not scale automatically to massive datasets. Its computational burden is driven by the repeated estimation of nuisance func-tions across cross-fitting folds: when the nuisance components are learned by flexible nonlinear methods, refitting these models across folds can be prohibitively expensive on very large sam-ples. A natural remedy is to replace full-sample nuisance learning with estimation on a smaller working sample. If this reduction is carried out by naive uniform subsampling, however, a non-trivial statistical difficulty arises—the selected subset need neither preserve the covariate geometry of the full data nor maintain adequate treated–control balance or overlap. In obser-vational settings, such deficiencies can destabilize nuisance estimation and, in turn, degrade the performance of orthogonal-score-based inference (Rosenbaum and Rubin, 1983; Lunceford and Davidian, 2004). Subsampling is therefore a natural route towards scalable causal inference, but these observations indicate that simple random reduction is unlikely to suffice. Motivated by this computational tension, a substantial literature has developed principled subsampling strategies for large-scale inference. One major strand is model-based optimal sub-sampling, which assigns non-uniform inclusion probabilities or deterministic selection rules so as to optimize a model-specific efficiency criterion, typically an asymptotic-variance or information-based objective (Yao and Wang, 2021; Yu et al., 2024). Under this rationale, once a working model and target estimator are specified, one preferentially retains the observations that are most informative for the chosen inferential task. The idea has proved effective across a range of models, including linear and generalized linear regression (Wang et al., 2019; Ai et al., 2021), logistic regression (Wang et al., 2018; Wang, 2019), quantile regression (Wang and Ma, 2021), additive hazards models (Zuo et al., 2021), and quasi-likelihood estimation (Yu et al., 2022). At the same time, the strength of this line of work is also its chief limitation: its performance is inherently tied to the working model and the estimator for which the rule is optimized. In prob-lems such as observational causal inference, where the nuisance structure is typically complex and learned flexibly rather than specified a priori, this model dependence can erode robust-ness. Such considerations have motivated a complementary strand of research on geometric or model-free subsampling. Unlike model-based optimal subsampling, which is tailored to a prespecified model and estimator, the geometric strand seeks subsets that are globally representative of the full co-variate distribution and space-filling in the design space. Rooted in uniform design (UD) and quasi-Monte Carlo methodology (Fang et al., 2000; Niederreiter, 1992; Dick et al., 2013), recent developments based on the generalized empirical F -discrepancy, the mixture discrepancy, and related UD constructions (Zhou et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) have shown 2that such approaches can be substantially more stable and more robust to model misspecifica-tion than naive random sampling (Yang et al., 2025). More closely related to the present paper are the recent causal subsampling methods of Su et al. (2022); Su (2025), which extend the subsampling paradigm to causal-effect estimation through staged nuisance estimation and cali-bration. Taken together, these contributions demonstrate that carefully designed subsampling can substantially reduce computational cost while improving robustness relative to naive uni-form subsampling. However, none is designed to construct a geometrically representative and treatment-balanced working sample for observational DML, and thus none directly addresses the instability that arises when poor overlap and treated–control misalignment undermine cross-fitted nuisance learning and the subsequent orthogonal-score inference. Motivated by these gaps, we propose Uniform Design Double Machine Learning (UD-DML), a design-based subsampling framework for scalable causal inference. UD-DML inte-grates uniform-design ideas with the DML paradigm, simultaneously addressing computational reduction, covariate representativeness, and treated–control balance. The method operates in a retained PCA-rotated covariate space. A low-discrepancy skeleton is first constructed to capture the dominant geometry of the full sample; for each skeleton point, the nearest treated and control observations are then identified through efficient nearest-neighbour search (Bentley, 1975, 1990). The resulting working sample is substantially smaller than the original data while remaining representative of the full covariate distribution and balanced across treatment arms. Standard cross-fitted DML is subsequently performed on the selected original observations. Our contributions are fourfold. First, we develop a novel subsampling algorithm for large-scale ATE estimation that couples uniform-design-based geometric coverage with orthogonal-score-based causal inference. Second, we establish a discrepancy-based theory showing that the selected subsample inherits favourable representativeness and treated–control balance properties within a kernel framework. Third, under mild regularity conditions, we prove that the UD-DML estimator is asymptotically normal, thereby supporting valid Wald inference. Fourth, extensive simulations demonstrate that UD-DML consistently outperforms naive uniform subsampling in both statistical efficiency and inferential stability, most notably in low-overlap regimes and under nuisance-model misspecification, while a real-data application further illustrates its practical feasibility for large-scale observational analysis. The remainder of this article is organized as follows. Section 2 first reviews the notation and frameworks for causal inference and DML, and then details the UD-DML algorithm, its implementation, as well as its theoretical properties. Section 3 presents our extensive simulation studies, comparing UD-DML to benchmarks across various data-generating processes. Section 4 applies our proposed method over a real dataset to demonstrate its practical properties. Section 5 concludes the paper with a brief discussion.

2.1 Causal Inference and Double Machine Learning 

Consider n i.i.d. observations Oi = ( Yi, W i, Xi), i = 1 , . . . , n , where Y ∈ R denotes the observed outcome, W ∈ { 0, 1} the binary treatment assignment, and X ∈ Rp a vector of pre-treatment covariates. We work within the potential-outcomes framework (Rubin, 1974): for each unit i,

For moderate p, this step is inexpensive relative to repeated ML fitting on the full sample. A naive implementation would scan, for each of the rp skeleton points, every treated and every control observation in the rotated space, leading to an overall cost of order O(rpnq ). To circumvent this, we construct two spatial in-dices—specifically the cKDTree from the scipy.spatial library—one for the treated arm and one for the control arm, and query them separately. For exact matching without replacement, each query returns a candidate set of nearest neighbours that is expanded adaptively until the nearest available unit is found. In low-to-moderate retained dimensions q, the average query cost is approximately logarithmic in the arm size, so the total matching cost is O(rpq log n)once the indices have been built. This stage applies standard K-fold cross-fitting only to the selected original observations, so its computational cost depends on r, not on n. Denote this cost by CDML (r, p, K ). up to a lower-order inference cost. By contrast, UD-DML replaces this full-sample training burden by the much smaller term CDML (r, p, K ) and adds only a quasi-linear preprocessing overhead in n. Therefore, when r ≪ n and nuisance fitting is the dominant computational bottleneck, UD-DML can be viewed as replacing a full-data learning problem by a preprocessing step on all n observations followed by a learning problem on only r selected observations. To build learner-specific intuition, the computational advantage of UD-DML is clearest when nuisance functions are fitted by nonlinear learners commonly used in causal inference, such as random forests or gradient-boosted trees—both of which typically exhibit nonlinear effects and higher-order interactions on observational data. For tree-ensemble learners, the training cost on a sample of size m is typically of order CDML (m, p, K ) ≈ cK,T m log m, where 

cK,T depends on the cross-fitting scheme, the number of trees, and other tuning parameters. Full-data DML therefore costs cK,T n log n, whereas UD-DML replaces this with O(pn log n) + 

cK,T r log r up to lower-order terms. When r ≪ n, the reduction from n log n to r log r in the nuisance-fitting stage substantially outweighs the one-time O(pn log n) preprocessing cost. 8The effect is even more pronounced for costlier nonlinear learners—for example, kernel-based methods whose training cost scales quadratically or cubically with the sample size. In large-scale regimes with complex nonlinear nuisance structure, UD-DML can therefore be substantially more efficient than full-data DML while preserving the design-induced representativeness and balance properties required for valid inference in Section 2.4.

3.4 Results and Analysis 

Figure 1 summarises the first experiment, reporting RMSE, CI Coverage, and CI Width as functions of the subsample size r. Both UD-DML and UNIF-DML improve monotonically with 

r, yet the gap between them is scenario-dependent and widens sharply as overlap deteriorates. In the high-overlap scenario OBS-1 the two curves are nearly indistinguishable, whereas in the moderate-overlap OBS-2 and low-overlap OBS-3 scenarios the UD-DML curves lie strictly and substantially below those of UNIF-DML in both RMSE and CI Width across the entire grid; the empirical coverage of the nominal 95% Wald interval remains close to the target for both methods. The underlying mechanism is as follows. In the poor-overlap regimes illustrated in Figure 2, a non-trivial fraction of the UNIF subsample falls in regions with extreme propensity scores, 16 contributing large inverse-probability weights to the AIPW score and thereby inflating its vari-ance. UD-DML, by construction, redirects the sampling budget towards the informative region of the covariate space and attenuates the tail contributions that drive the variance of the orthog-onal score. The practical consequence is that, at any given sample size, UD-DML yields sharper point estimation and tighter yet still valid confidence intervals; the gains therefore materialise as improved inferential efficiency rather than as undercoverage, and they grow precisely in the regimes where naive subsampling is most costly. Table 2 reports the scalability experiment, in which the subsample size is fixed at r ∈{1000 , 5000 } and the full-data size grows from n = 10 5 to n = 5 × 10 5. Statistically, the UD-DML RMSE is essentially flat in n at each level of r, while the UNIF-DML RMSE likewise remains roughly constant in n but at a higher level. The full-data estimator FULL-DML continues to improve as n grows, from RMSE 0 .008 at n = 10 5 to 0 .004 at n = 5 × 10 5 under OBS-3, confirming its role as the inferential gold standard. Computationally, the wall-clock cost of FULL-DML scales with n, rising from 5 .4s at n = 10 5

to 28 .7s at n = 5 × 10 5 under OBS-3; UD-DML and UNIF-DML remain in the one-to-three-seconds regime regardless of n. This accords with the complexity decomposition in Section 2.3: the RMSE of a subsample estimator is governed by the selected sample size rather than by n,whereas the iterative nuisance-fitting burden of FULL-DML scales with n. UD-DML inherits the former property for accuracy and incurs the latter only through an O(pn log n) preprocessing step, so its dominant cost is CDML (r, p, K ). Accordingly, at n = 5 × 10 5 and r = 5000, UD-DML attains an RMSE within a small multiple of FULL-DML while running roughly an order of magnitude faster and remaining markedly more accurate than UNIF-DML. The iterative nuisance-fitting burden is thus effectively transferred from size n to size r ≪ n without sacrificing too much inferential validity. Whenever at least one of the outcome model bm and the propensity score be is estimated correctly by LightGBM, both methods deliver RMSEs of comparable magnitude and coverage close to the nominal 95% level across all three DGPs. This confirms that the unified misspecification of 

bm and be on X(5) alone does not violate the doubly-robust structure of the AIPW score. The separation between UD-DML and UNIF-DML emerges under (Wrong, Wrong), in which both nuisance components are fitted on X(5) and the AIPW correction collapses analytically to the subsample treated–control mean difference. OBS-1 remains benign for both methods, whereas UNIF-DML collapses on OBS-2 and OBS-3: its RMSE inflates to 0 .191 and 0 .147, and coverage drops to 0 .03 and 0 .14, respectively. UD-DML retains nominal coverage on OBS-3 (RMSE 0 .036, coverage 0 .95) but degrades to (0 .082 , 0.67) on OBS-2. Under (Wrong, Wrong), the bias of the uncorrected mean-difference estimator is driven entirely by the residual treated–control distributional discrepancy within the selected sample. UNIF-DML inherits the full-sample confounding pattern and therefore collapses whenever the population is strongly unbalanced. UD-DML, by contrast, aligns bPS1,X and bPS0,X along the re-tained PCA directions; its residual bias is contributed only by functionals of X lying outside the span of that balance. The three DGPs differ precisely in how much such residual structure sur-vives. OBS-1, with additive linear g, ∆ and e, is entirely captured by the PCA-anchored balance. OBS-3 is driven by odd or mean-zero nonlinear terms (tanh X(1) , sin πX (1) , X(6) X(7) , ( X(6) )3,cos X(7) ) over a symmetric bimodal covariate, so these contributions cancel once the treated and control subsamples share the same cluster composition and independent-noise marginals. In OBS-2, by contrast, the propensity score contains −0.3( X(2) )2 and 0 .4 sin( X(6) ), while the CATE is the pure cross-moment 0 .5X(1) X(2) ; the confounding and effect heterogeneity are car-ried by second-order and interaction functionals that are only partially controlled by linear marginal balance in the PCA-rotated space, leaving a non-negligible residual bias that pushes coverage down to 0 .67 while still yielding an RMSE less than half of that of UNIF-DML. The practical implication is that UD-DML buys substantial robustness against the joint challenge of 17 misspecification and poor overlap; the regimes in which it remains vulnerable are precisely those in which the causal signal is carried by higher-order moments not controlled by the marginal geometry in Theorem 2. Figure 3 reports the overlap-gradient experiment on the tunable OBS-3 variant, in which the propensity-coefficient multiplier c controls the severity of treated–control separation. As 

c increases from near-perfect overlap towards extreme confounding, the RMSE of UNIF-DML grows monotonically and substantially, whereas that of UD-DML remains essentially flat across the entire gradient; empirical coverage stays close to the nominal level for both methods. The underlying mechanism is that, as c grows, the full-sample propensity distribution accumulates increasing mass near the boundaries of [0 , 1], so uniform subsampling over-represents units with highly variable inverse-probability weights and inflates the asymptotic variance of the AIPW score. UD-DML, by anchoring the selection to a low-discrepancy skeleton with explicit treated–control balance, suppresses precisely these tail contributions irrespective of c. The advantage of UD-DML is therefore an increasing function of overlap deficiency: the method delivers its largest efficiency gains in the observational regimes where uniform subsampling is least reliable, while maintaining stable runtime and valid coverage. The supplementary diagnostics in Figures 4 and 5 offer direct empirical support for the theoretical results and further clarify the experiments above. Figure 4 shows that the stan-dardised UD-DML estimates on OBS-3 track the standard-normal reference line almost exactly, corroborating the √r-asymptotic normality established in Theorem 3 and confirming that the valid-coverage pattern observed throughout Tables 2–3 and Figures 1–3 arises from a genuinely Gaussian sampling distribution of bθUD rather than from opportunistic interval width. Fig-ure 5 reports the covariate-wise standardised mean differences between the treated and control groups within each selected subsample: the UD-selected subsample concentrates the SMDs tightly around zero, while the UNIF-selected subsample retains substantial treated–control im-balance for several covariates, showing that uniform subsampling fails to deliver a balanced working sample. This direct empirical counterpart of Theorem 2 explains why UD-DML main-tains low RMSE and valid coverage under poor overlap and nuisance misspecification, whereas UNIF-DML deteriorates: the design-induced balance operates upstream of the downstream in-ferential metrics and constitutes the common mechanism behind the efficiency, scalability, and robustness patterns documented in the preceding experiments. 

# 4 Real Data Application 

Having established the finite-sample advantages of UD-DML by simulation, we now illustrate its practical utility on a large-scale observational dataset. Specifically, we apply UD-DML to estimate the causal effect of maternal smoking on infant birth weight using the 2021 US Natality records, thereby demonstrating the method’s ability to deliver statistically efficient and computationally tractable inference under realistic conditions. 

4.1 Dataset and Preprocessing 

We apply UD-DML to the 2021 US Natality micro-data file released by the National Center for Health Statistics (National Center for Health Statistics, 2022) and available from the CDC VitalStats portal. The file records every live birth registered in the 50 states and the District of Columbia during the 2021 calendar year ( N ≈ 3.67 million records), together with detailed maternal, paternal, and infant characteristics drawn from the standard birth certificate. Our causal question of interest is the average treatment effect of maternal smoking during preg-nancy on infant birth weight. This relationship has been extensively investigated in perinatal epidemiology (Almond et al., 2005; Walker et al., 2009; Pereira et al., 2017), and serves as a natural large-scale benchmark for the present study: the treated group is small and unevenly 18 distributed in the covariate space, yielding a genuinely low-overlap regime. Formally, for each birth record i = 1 , . . . , n , we define the treatment, outcome, and covari-ates as follows. The treatment indicator records whether the mother smoked during pregnancy (CDC field CIG REC ): Wi = 1 if reported “Yes” and Wi = 0 if reported “No”. The outcome

The covariate vector Xi ∈ R10 contains: maternal age ( MAGER ), maternal education ( MEDUC ), prenatal-care month ( PRECARE ), number of prenatal visits ( PREVIS ), infant sex ( SEX ), marital status ( DMAR ), paternal age ( FAGECOMB ), gestational diabetes ( RF GDIAB ), gestational hyperten-sion ( RF GHYPE ), and the number of prior preterm births ( PRIORTERM ). 4.2 Experimental Settings 

The real-data experiment pursues two complementary goals, mirroring the simulation evidence of Section 3 but on a single fixed observational dataset. First, we quantify the sampling variabil-ity and inferential precision of UD-DML relative to UNIF-DML at a single practically motivated subsample size r = 5000, treating the FULL-DML estimate on all n observations as the ref-erence point. Second, we trace how the RMSE relative to FULL, the Monte Carlo standard error of the estimator, and the wall-clock runtime evolve along an increasing subsample ladder 

r ∈ { 1000 , 2500 , 5000 , 10000 , 25000 }, so as to confirm that the simulation conclusions carry over to a genuinely massive low-overlap observational sample. Variability assessment and inference are conducted via the nonparametric bootstrap. We fix 

B = 100 bootstrap replications; for each b = 1 , . . . , B , we draw n rows with replacement from the cleaned analysis sample to obtain ( X(b), W (b), Y (b)), and re-run UD-DML and UNIF-DML on this bootstrap sample for every r in the subsample grid. A common bootstrap seed pool is shared by both methods so that the comparison is paired at every ( b, r ) cell. The FULL-DML reference is computed once on the original sample and serves as the gold-standard target. All three estimators (UD-DML, UNIF-DML, and FULL-DML) share the cross-fitted DML backbone introduced in Section 2.3, with K = 5 folds to allow for more granular nuisance estimation. Unless stated otherwise, the nuisance functions bmg and be are fitted by LightGBM with the hyperparameters specified in Section 3, and the estimated propensity scores are clipped to [0 .01 , 0.99]. UD-DML is implemented with the same settings as in simulation. 

4.3 Results and Analysis 

The bootstrap behaviour of the three estimators at r = 5000 is visualised in Figure 6, and the subsample-size scaling behaviour across r ∈ { 1000 , 2500 , 5000 , 10000 , 25000 } is reported in Figure 7. We begin with the canonical-size comparison. The FULL-DML reference returns a 19 scaled ATE bθFULL = −0.01701, corresponding on the original scale to an average causal change in birth weight of −134 .7,g. This magnitude is consistent with the classical literature on US natality data (Kramer, 1987; Abrevaya, 2006; Cattaneo, 2010). At the canonical subsample size, UD-DML produces a bootstrap mean of −0.01572 ( −124 .4,g, an absolute deviation of about 10,g from the FULL anchor) with Monte Carlo standard devia-tion 0 .00237, whereas UNIF-DML yields a bootstrap mean of −0.01925 ( −152 .4,g, a deviation of about 18,g) with Monte Carlo standard deviation 0 .01051. Translated into RMSE relative to the FULL reference, UD-DML is substantially more accurate than UNIF-DML. The same conclusion is evident in the left panel of Figure 6, where the UD-DML box concentrates tightly around bθFULL while the UNIF-DML box spans a region almost an order of magnitude wider along the ATE axis. Beyond the canonical slice, the subsample-size scaling experiment shows that the UD-DML advantage is uniform across the entire r-grid and not an artifact of any particular r. As the left panel of Figure 7 indicates, UD-DML attains a lower RMSE-to-FULL than UNIF-DML at every subsample size, and the relative gap widens at the smaller values of r where propensity-score overlap stress is more severe. The middle panel of Figure 7 isolates the variance component that drives this gain: the bootstrap Monte Carlo standard deviation of UD-DML lies strictly below that of UNIF-DML across the entire ladder. This is precisely the empirical signature of Theorem 2: the design-induced treated–control balance of the UD subsample suppresses the inverse-propensity tail contributions to the AIPW score, so that the resulting estimator inherits a substantially smaller asymptotic variance than its naive uniform counterpart. The empirical-CDF panel in Figure 6 conveys the same conclusion in distributional form: the UD-DML absolute-error curve lies entirely to the left of the UNIF-DML curve on the logarithmic scale, and therefore stochastically dominates it. The same comparison may be recast in computational terms. FULL-DML on the cleaned sample of n = 2 ,846 ,543 births takes 189 .9s per call, whereas UD-DML at the canonical sub-sample size takes 15 .4s (a 12 × speed-up) and UNIF-DML takes only 1 .1s (a 177 × speed-up). Importantly, the right panel of Figure 7 confirms that the UD-DML runtime remains strictly below the FULL-DML horizontal reference even at the largest considered sample size (30 .7s at 

r = 25000), so UD-DML is usable across the entire grid without ever crossing the cost fron-tier set by full-sample DML. Weighing the modest computational premium of UD-DML over UNIF-DML against its order-of-magnitude reduction in both RMSE and bootstrap dispersion, UD-DML clearly sits on the favourable side of the accuracy–cost frontier on this benchmark. Taken together, the natality experiment reproduces, on a genuine large-scale observational sample, the qualitative pattern established in simulation in Section 3. Naive random subsam-pling is statistically unstable, and full-sample DML is computationally heavy; UD-DML offers a principled middle ground that is statistically close to FULL-DML, computationally close to UNIF-DML, and substantially more efficient than UNIF-DML in both bias and variance. 

# 5 Conclusions 

This paper has addressed a problem at the intersection of modern causal inference and mas-sive observational data. While double machine learning delivers valid and flexible inference on causal estimands, its cross-fitted machine-learning machinery becomes computationally pro-hibitive at full-sample scale. The natural alternative of running DML on a uniformly drawn subsample is computationally attractive but, as we have shown, statistically fragile: uniform subsampling disregards the covariate geometry and offers no protection against treated–control imbalance, producing unstable estimates precisely in the low-overlap regimes that are common in observational studies. To resolve this tension, we have introduced UD-DML, a design-based subsampling framework that treats the choice of the working sample as a statistical design problem rather than as a 20 probabilistic reduction. UD-DML first constructs a low-discrepancy skeleton in a PCA-rotated covariate space under the mixture-discrepancy criterion, and then assigns, to each skeleton point, the nearest treated and control units via KD-tree search. The resulting matched subsample is, by construction, both representative of the full covariate distribution and balanced across treatment arms, and is subsequently plugged into the standard cross-fitted DML pipeline without any further methodological change. On the theoretical side, we have established discrepancy-based guarantees that translate the UD construction into deterministic control of two distinct geometric errors: a global represen-tativeness error governed by the generalised empirical F -discrepancy of the UD skeleton, and a within-subsample treated–control balance error governed by the common matching radius in the rotated space. Building on these design properties and on the Neyman-orthogonality of the AIPW score, we have proved that the UD-DML estimator is √r-asymptotically normal under the standard DML nuisance-rate conditions, where r ≪ n is the selected subsample size, so that Wald inference based on the selected sample remains asymptotically valid. The empirical evidence reinforces and refines these theoretical statements. Our simulation study spans three observational DGPs of increasing complexity (OBS-1 to OBS-3), a unified misspecification protocol in which all wrong components are estimated on a single uninforma-tive covariate X(5) , and four experiments targeting subsample-size efficiency, scalability in n,preservation of double robustness under nuisance misspecification, and sensitivity along a tun-able overlap gradient. Across all four dimensions, UD-DML delivers lower RMSE, narrower confidence intervals, and more reliable coverage than UNIF-DML, with the largest gains in the low-overlap and jointly-misspecified regimes. The supplementary Q–Q and SMD diagnostics further confirm that these gains stem from a genuinely Gaussian sampling distribution of the estimator and from the design-induced treated–control balance, rather than from opportunistic interval width. The natality application on the CDC 2021 micro-data reproduces the same qualitative pattern on a genuine observational dataset: UD-DML is statistically close to full-data DML, computationally close to uniform subsampling, and substantially more efficient than uniform subsampling in both bias and variance. Several directions remain open for future research. First, the current implementation relies on KD-tree nearest-neighbour matching in the retained PCA-rotated space, which is effective when the working dimension q is low to moderate but is eroded by the curse of dimension-ality when p ≫ r or when the relevant signal is genuinely high-dimensional; extending the UD construction to learned low-dimensional embeddings is a natural next step, for example, using sufficient dimension reduction, representation learning, or random projections. Second, the present framework is tailored to a binary treatment via 1:1 paired matching; generalis-ing the geometric matching principle to continuous or multi-valued treatments, for instance by anchoring the skeleton to a dose–response grid, would broaden its applicability to policy-evaluation problems. Third, hybrid strategies that combine UD-DML’s geometric balance with the variance-minimising ideas of model-based optimal subsampling could further improve effi-ciency, particularly when partial prior information on the nuisance structure is available. Fi-nally, embedding UD-DML into richer causal targets beyond the ATE, including conditional average treatment effects, distributional effects, and dynamic treatment regimes, would extend its scalable design-based perspective to the broader semiparametric and machine-learning-based causal-inference literature.
