Abstract 

Causal effect estimation from observational data requires careful adjustment for confounding. Classical estimators such as inverse probability weighting and augmented inverse probability weighting are effective under favorable model specification, but may become unstable when treatment assignment and outcome mechanisms are complex, non-linear and high-dimensional. Machine learning and representation-learning approaches improve flexibility, yet joint training can allow outcome-related information to influence treatment-side representations, which is unde-sirable from a causal perspective. We propose MOCA (Modular One-way Causal Attention), a transformer-based framework that separates treatment and outcome modeling through modular design, and makes confounder adjustments with a one-way attention process. A cutting-feedback strategy implemented with gradient detachment further prevents outcome loss from updating the treatment module. This design preserves directional information flow while retaining the representation power of transformer architectures for causal inference. Across multiple simulated scenarios, including linear, nonlinear, heavy-tailed, hidden-confounding, and high-dimensional settings, MOCA showed competitive or improved performance relative to IPW, AIPW, X-learner, TARNet, and DragonNet. We further illustrate the method on Infant Health and Development Program dataset and Dehejia–Wahba dataset for real world benchmarks. These results suggest that modular attention with one-way information flow provides a promising and interpretable direction for causal inference with modern deep learning models. 

Keywords: causal inference; transformer; cross-attention; modular learning; treatment effect estimation; cutting feedback 

# 1 Introduction 

In observational studies, treatment assignment is typically not randomized, leading to the pres-ence of confounders that affect both treatment assignment and outcomes. Many modern causal methodologies are built on the Rubin potential outcomes framework [Rubin, 2005]. A central quantity of interest is the average treatment effect (ATE). Classical approaches can perform well when the models are correctly specified. Propensity score methods like inverse probability weighting (IPW) [Robins et al., 2000] and doubly robust estimators such as augmented inverse probability weighting (AIPW) [Bang and Robins, 2005] are widely used approaches for confounding adjustment. 1

> arXiv:2604.23107v1 [stat.ML] 25 Apr 2026

However, parametric causal estimators often rely on restrictive assumptions, and cannot capture complex patterns such as nonlinear effects, or high-dimensional covariates. To address these limi-tations and give conditional average treatment effect (CATE) estimates, machine learning-based approaches, such as the T-learner and X-learner have been introduced [Künzel et al., 2019]. Besides, representation-learning approaches such as TARNet [Johansson et al., 2016] and DragonNet [Shi et al., 2019] have embedded the problem of causal inference within a deep learning framework consisting of representation learning paired with two potential outcome heads. Yet joint representation learning may allow for outcome information to affect treatment repre-sentations, which is undesired in causal estimation. To address these challenges, we draw inspiration from recent advances in transformer architectures and cross-attention mechanisms with a modular framework. Transformers [Vaswani et al., 2017] have demonstrated remarkable ability to capture complex dependent structures through attention-based representation learning, while cross-attention provides a natural mechanism for directional information transfer between modules. Building on these ideas, we propose a modular causal modeling framework in this article. In our framework, the treatment branch is designed to learn propensity-related representations, while the outcome branch receives treatment-side information only through one-way cross-attention. This two-step design reflects the causal role of treatment modeling as an adjustment mechanism for confounding. We also incorporate a cutting-feedback strategy through gradient detachment, preventing the outcome loss from propagating backward to affect the treatment module. Our proposed method, MOCA (Modular One-way Causal Attention ), preserves treatment modeling independence while still allowing the outcome module to adapt to complex response surfaces. In this way, the framework retains modern machine learning advantages while maintaining the structural requirements of causal inference and supporting interpretable treatment effect estimation. 

# 2 Problem Setup and Causal Estimand 

We consider an observational dataset consisting of n independent units: {(Xi, T i, Y i)}ni=1 , where 

Xi ∈ Rp denotes the observed covariates, Ti ∈ { 0, 1} is the binary treatment indicator, and Yi is the observed outcome. Under the potential outcomes framework, each unit i has two potential outcomes, Yi(0) and Yi(1) , corresponding to the outcomes that would be observed under control and treatment [Rubin, 2005]. The following assumptions are made. 1. Consistency: the observed outcome equals the corresponding potential outcome under the treatment received: Yi = Yi (Ti), implying no other version of treatment; 2. Ignorability (unconfoundedness): (Yi(0) , Y i(1)) ⊥Ti | Xi. That is, conditional on the observed covariates, treatment assignment is independent of the potential outcomes, which implies no unobserved confounding; 3. Positivity: 0 < P (Ti = 1 | Xi = x) < 1 for all x in the support of X, which ensures that each covariate profile has a positive probability of receiving either treatment level. For a unit i with covariate vector Xi = xi, a natural goal is to evaluate its individual level treatment effect (ITE), τi := Yi(1) − Yi(0) . However, τi is fundamentally unobservable, since for each unit we only observe one potential outcome. We instead focus on the conditional average treatment effect (CATE), τ (x) := E{Y (1) − Y (0) |

X = x}. Although τ (x) is a population-level quantity, it serves as the optimal target for individual-level prediction. In particular, let ˆτ (Xi) denote an estimator of τi. The mean squared error (MSE) 2at Xi = xi can be decomposed as

3.5 Modularization and Cutting Feedback Rules 

Cutting feedback originates from modular Bayesian analysis, where unwanted reverse feedback is removed during posterior updating [Frazier and Nott, 2025]. In our work, we mimic this principle through a two-step estimation procedure. There are four rules [Liu and Goudie, 2025] used here to ensure a meaningful modular mechanism. 

3.5.1 Rule 1: Self-contained modules 

This rule requires the model to be decomposed into distinct modules, where each module is self-contained; that is, internally coherent and functionally separable from the rest of the system (Figure 2). We have introduced two modules: a treatment module and an outcome module. Formally, we write ΨT = ( X, T ; θS,T , θ L,T ), ΨY = ( X, T, Y ; θS,Y , θ L,Y ). The treatment module produces an intermediate representation H(T ), which acts as frozen information passed to the downstream outcome module. The overlap between the two modules is ΨT ∩Y = {X, T ; H(T )}. Since {X, T }

is the observed dataset and H(T ) is the frozen representative information, each module remains self-contained in the sense that it is defined by its own data inputs and corresponding parameters. 

3.5.2 Rule 2: Module ordering 

The direction of information flow is explicitly defined from the treatment module to the outcome module. In other words, the treatment module is treated as the parent module, and the outcome module is treated as the child module. Hence, our method satisfies Rule 2 by explicitly imposing the directed modular order ΨT ≺ ΨY .

3.5.3 Rule 3: Cutting feedback 

This rule states that inference for the parent module should be based only on its own module. In our work, we implement this idea through stop-gradient. Specifically, the outcome model is written as

3.5.4 Rule 4: A cut-style downstream update 

In modular Bayesian analysis, this rule refers to posterior updating under a cut distribution. In our machine learning framework, we instead propose a cut-style downstream update for the parameters 6in the outcome module. Step 1 learns the treatment module, and Step 2 learns the outcome module conditional on the treatment representation. This is directly analogous to the logic of cut inference in modular Bayesian analysis: pcut (θT , θ Y | data ) = p(θT | treatment data ) · p(θY | θT , outcome data ).

4.2 Machine Learning Based Causal Effect Estimation 

Meta-learning approaches adapt supervised learning algorithms to estimate treatment effects. The T-learner fits two separate outcome models, using the control and treated samples respectively, and estimates the treatment effect by ̂ τ (x) = ̂ μ1(x) −̂ μ0(x).

This approach is simple and flexible, but may suffer when one treatment group has limited sample size. The X-learner improves upon the T-learner by first imputing pseudo-treatment effects and then learning treatment effect functions separately in each group, often achieving better performance under treatment imbalance [Künzel et al., 2019]. In our analysis, we will compare to X-learner since it considers the treatment adjustments. Representation learning methods for causal inference seek to learn latent covariate representations that are useful for estimating both treatment assignment and potential outcomes. TARNet and DragonNet are two examples. Let Φ( X) denote a learned representation of the covariates. 4.3 Transformer for Tabular Data 

Transformer and attention-based architectures have recently shown strong performance in tabular modeling because they can flexibly capture nonlinear dependencies and higher-order feature interac-tions [Wang et al., 2025]. Such flexibility is potentially valuable for causal inference, however, the application of transformers in causal inference is still a new attempt. For example, utilizing Trans-formers to capture complex, long-range dependencies among time-varying confounders [Melnychuk et al., 2022]. Most existing causal models typically do not emphasize directional information flow between treatment and outcome representations, nor do they explicitly implement cutting feedback to prevent outcome-side gradients from updating treatment-side representations. These limitations motivate architectures that preserve the representational power of attention while introducing mechanism-aware structure.

# 5 Simulation Study 

To comprehensively evaluate the performance of the proposed method, we conducted six simulation studies under multiple data-generating mechanisms. Including MOCA with its one-way and two-way version, there are 7 methods compared in the simulation. IPW and AIPW are well-established methods in classical causal inference, and AIPW is especially attractive due to its doubly robust property. X-learner represent machine-learning-based approaches that have shown competitive performance in treatment effect estimation. TARNet and DragonNet are representation-learning methods and provide important neural-network-based benchmarks for comparison. All simulations and model training were conducted in Python (3.11.4). The proposed model was implemented in PyTorch (version 2.5.1+cu121), with NumPy (version 2.4.3) and Pandas (3.0.1) used for data generation and numerical computation. Random seeds with different seeds for different samples were fixed to ensure reproducibility. We considered two sample-size settings: 100 and 1,000 observations in each of the training, validation, and test sets.

6.1 Simulation Results 

The simulation results suggest that MOCA is competitive for ATE estimation across all considered settings, and that the one-way attention version generally performs better than the two-way version [Table 1]. This supports the overall effectiveness of MOCA and highlights the value of one-way attention and cutting feedback. Under nonlinear, t-distribution, and high-dimensional settings, MOCA outperform traditional methods such as IPW and AIPW with less outliers [Figure 3-4]. IPW and AIPW may have the estimated propensity scores become extremely large or small [Rosenbaum and Rubin, 1983], leading to unstable weights, increased bias, more outliers, and ultimately larger RMSE [Li et al., 2019]. In these scenarios, MOCA is not only competitive with other machine learning approaches, but also clearly improves upon traditional estimators. Under violations of ignorabillity through the presence of hidden confounders, MOCA also shows advantages when the sample size is small. In particular, its bias is generally smaller than that of competing methods, and the one-way version again performs better than the two-way version. This suggests that when the model is misspecified and information is incomplete, it is important to prevent mutual contamination between the treatment module and the outcome module. However, when the sample size increases to 1000, none of the methods can estimate the ATE well [Table S1, Figure S1-S2]. Therefore, although MOCA shows some robustness to ignorability violation in small samples, valid estimation still fundamentally relies on identifiable assumptions being satisfied [VanderWeele and Arah, 2011]. For CATE estimation, this is one of the main advantages of machine learning based approaches [Table S2-S3, Figure S3-S4]. Because MOCA, X-learner, TARNet, and DragonNet can all produce individual-level predictions of the potential outcomes, it is natural to further assess their ability to estimate CATE. Overall, MOCA is effective for CATE estimation in most scenarios. TARNet, and DragonNet, which rely on learned representations, perform worse than the meta-learning frameworks X-learner under heavy-tailed t-distributed data. Shared representations and trained with squared-error loss can be less stable under heavy-tailed outcomes [Fan et al., 2021]. MOCA, on the other hand, still provided a relatively comparable result. Overall, MOCA performs better than traditional methods such as IPW and AIPW under nonlinear, high-dimensional, t-distributed, and even hidden-confounding settings, while remaining competitive with other machine learning based methods. At the same time, the use of one-way attention and cutting feedback helps reduce potential information pollution and preserves the methodological rationale of the model within the causal inference framework. 

6.2 Real Data Results 

Two open-sourced real datasets are used here [Table 2]. We estimated uncertainty for MOCA by repeatedly fitting the model and combining the resulting ATE estimates using a Rubin-style pooling rule [Barnard and Rubin, 1999]. IHDP refers to the Infant Health and Development Program dataset, which comes from a randomized experiment conducted between 1985 and 1988 to study the effect of home-based intervention on infants’ cognitive test scores [Louizos et al., 2017]. There are 747 observations with 608 controls and 139 treatments. This version contains the treatment indicator, the observed outcome yfactual , the counterfactual outcome ycfactual , the mean potential outcomes under control and treatment ( μ0 and μ1), and 25 covariates. Among these, X1–X6 are continuous variables, while X7–X25 are binary variables. A counterfactual outcome included in this dataset, so that we can show the ATE bias directly. The IHDP results show that MOCA has 11 the smallest ATE bias of 0.006. We have a pooling estimated ATE of 3.991 and a 95% confidence interval of (3.69, 4.29). DW refers to the Dehejia–Wahba dataset, which is a subset of the Lalonde data [Dehejia and Wahba, 1999]. It contains 445 observations with 185 treatments and 260 controls. Typically, we expected ATE is a positive number, and the doubly robust method AIPW can be a good reference [Iacus et al., 2019]. The variables include the treatment indicator treated, age, education, race, marital status, no-degree status, earnings in 1974 and 1975, post-treatment earnings in 1978, and unemployment indicators u74 and u75. Among these, re78 is usually treated as the post-treatment outcome. However, this dataset does not have a truth for reference. In this case, we will report only the ATE instead. The ATE based on doubly robust method AIPW is 784.214 with a 95% confidence interval of (-1746.59, 3315.02), which is a potential reference for other methods. MOCA pooling estimates ATE to be 672.201 and a 95% confidence interval of (-388.15, 1732.55), which is closest to the AIPW method. 

# 7 Discussion 

In conclusion, this paper introduces a modular causal transformer framework that combines represen-tation learning, one-way cross-attention, and cutting feedback for treatment effect estimation. The proposed design aims to preserve the usefulness of treatment information for confounding adjustment while avoiding undesirable reverse influence from outcome learning to treatment representation. Empirical results show that this framework can provide competitive and often improved performance, particularly in settings with nonlinear covariate effects, treatment effect heterogeneity, and complex dependency structures. At the same time, several limitations should be acknowledged. First, although the proposed model showed favorable empirical performance, it remains more computationally intensive than conventional estimators such as IPW, AIPW. Transformer-based architectures require tuning of multiple hyperparameters and may be sensitive to optimization settings, sample size, and training stability. In relatively simple settings, the added complexity of the model may not translate into substantial improvement over simpler approaches. Richer architectures can better capture complex structure, but may also overfit or underperform when the true mechanism is simple or when the available sample size is limited [Hastie et al., 2009]. Second, the validity of the MOCA still relies on standard identification assumptions such as consistency, ignorability, and positivity. The model cannot fully correct for hidden confounding when important variables are entirely unobserved. Third, although the one-way modular design helps reduce feedback contamination, it still relies on the correctness of treatment module. In addition, the hard cutting of feedback, while conceptually attractive for protecting the treatment mechanism, may be overly restrictive in some settings [Carmona and Nicholls, 2020]. This suggests that the optimal degree of modularization may vary across applications.
