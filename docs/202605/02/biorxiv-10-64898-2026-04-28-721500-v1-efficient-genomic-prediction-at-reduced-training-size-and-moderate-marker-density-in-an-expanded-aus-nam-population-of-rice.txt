Abstrac t26 

Genomic selection (GS) can accelerate genetic gain in crops, but its effectiveness depends on 27 

training population design and marker density. Nested association mapping (NAM) populations 28 

provide a structured framework that captures broad allelic diversity within a controlled genetic 29 

background. Here, we evaluated genomic prediction (GP) and genome -wide association study 30 

(GWAS) performance in an expanded aus -NAM population of rice comprising 1,818 recombinant 31 

inbred lines across 14 families and 11 agronomic traits, using genotyping -by -sequencing (GBS) 32 

markers and projected whole -genome sequence variants. Prediction accuracy plateaued at 33 

moderate marker densities (~20k SNPs) and with training populations of ~500 lines (~40 –60% of 34 

the available pool), with tra it heritability emerging as the strongest determinant of predictive 35 

performance rather than model choice or marker density. In contrast, GWAS resolution continued 36 

to improve with increasing marker density, enabling detection of additional loci, including a 37 

chromosome 12 locus associated with heading date, while consistently recovering well -38 

characterized genes such as EARLY HEADING DATE 1 (Ehd1) and SEMIDWARF 1 (SD1) .39 

These contrasting patterns indicate that GP reaches near -optimal performance once genome -40 

wi de variation is adequately represented, whereas GWAS benefits from higher marker density 41 

through improved locus resolution. The present study establishes a benchmark for implementing 42 

breeding programs involving japonica/indica crosses using GP in a single environment. 43

Background 48 

Meeting rising global food demand requires advances in breeding methodologies, as 49 

current rates of genetic gain remain insufficient to close the gap between crop production and 50 

projected demand  (Khush 2013; Duan et al. 2024; Voss -Fels et al. 2019) . Genomi c selection 51 

(GS), also referred to as genomic prediction (GP), has emerged as a promising strategy to 52 

accelerate breeding by predicting the genetic merit of individuals using genome -wide markers 53 

(Pérez and de los Campos 2014; Crossa et al. 2017) . Although GS has demonstrated success in 54 

specific contexts, including drought -tolerant maize and improved parental selection in oil palm 55 

(Gaffney et al. 2015; Aleri et al. 2026; Beyene et al. 2015; Kwong et al. 2017) , its broader and 56 

consistent implementation remains variable and often limited by factors such as population 57 

design, training set composition, and genotype -by -environment int eractions  (Bartholomé et al. 58 

2022;  Rice and Lipka 2021 ).59 

Rice ( Oryza sativa L.), a staple crop for more than half of the world’s population, represents a 60 

critical target for improving breeding efficiency under diverse and changing environments 61 

(Fukagawa and Ziska 2019; Du an et al. 2024) . Many agronomic traits in rice, including heading 62 

date, plant height, yield components, amylose content, and biomass, are complex quantitative 63 

traits controlled by numerous loci and strongly influenced by environmental conditions  (Nguyen 64 

et al. 2023; Lu et al. 2025) . Conventional breeding relies on extensive multi -environment 65 

phenotyping, making selection time -consuming and costly  (Guimarães 2009) . The effectiveness 66 

of GP in addressing these challenges depends on factors such as training population design, 67 

marker density, genetic architecture, and genotype -by -environment interactions  (Crossa et al. 68 

2017; Norman et al. 2018) . Structured populations, particularly nested association mapping 69 

(NAM), address this need by combining controlled genetic structure with broad allelic diversity. 70 

In our previous study, we developed an aus -NAM population consisting of multiple recombinant 71 

inbred line families derived from crosses between diverse aus donors and the common parent 72

Despite progress in rice GP, few studies have systematically disentangled the contributions of 79 

marker density, training population size, and model choice to prediction accuracy within the same 80 

structured population and trait set, information that is essent ial for designing cost -effective 81 

implementation strategies. Here, we address this gap using the expanded aus -NAM population 82 

across 11 agronomic traits and SNPs ranging from 1k to 56k markers, identifying the practical 83 

thresholds at which prediction accurac y saturates and showing that these thresholds differ 84 

fundamentally from those that govern GWAS resolution. We further show that the same 85 

population and marker data simultaneously support improved GWAS resolution — and that the 86 

divergent response of predict ion and mapping to increasing marker density has a practical 87 

consequence: a two -tiered genotyping strategy that makes full use of the NAM framework without 88 

requiring all lines to be genotyped at maximum density. 89

Genotyping and SNP proc essing 104 

Genomic DNA was extracted from approximately 5 cm of leaf tissue per line using a modified 105 

Dellaporta method (Dellaporta et al. 1983). Samples were oven -dried at 53 °C overnight before 106 

extractions. DNA quality and quantity were assessed by agarose g el electrophoresis (0.6% TBE) 107 

and the Quantiflour dsDNA system (Promega, USA). 108 

Genotyping -by -sequencing (GBS) libraries were prepared following established protocols (Poland 109 

et al. 2012; Furuta et al. 2017). Briefly, 200 ng of genomic DNA per sample was do uble -digested 110 

with KpnI and MspI enzymes (New England Biolabs, USA), ligated to barcode adapters, 111 

multiplexed, purified (QIAquick PCR purification kit, Qiagen), PCR -amplified, and sequenced on 112 

an Illumina platform (Poland et al. 2012; Furuta et al. 2017; R eyes et al. 2022). 113 

Raw sequence reads were processed using the TASSEL -GBS pipeline v5 (Glaubitz et al. 2014) 114 

and aligned to the Oryza sativa Nipponbare reference genome (IRGSP -1.0; (Kawahara et al. 115 

2013)). SNPs were filtered using the following criteria: m inor allele frequency (MAF) ≥ 0.02, 116 

minimum locus coverage ≥ 0.3, missing rate ≤ 0.5, and heterozygosity ≤ 0.125. Only SNPs 117 

polymorphic between parental lines and monomorphic within each parent were retained. Missing 118

were homozygous and concordant with the same parental allele, all parental WGS -derived SNP 134

flanking anchor markers were heterozygous, miss ing, or discordant — indicating a recombination 136 

breakpoint or insufficient marker coverage — were left as missing rather than assigned 137 

probabilistically. This procedure yielded a projected panel of approximately 56k SNPs per family, 138 

which was subsequently merged across families and used alongside the GBS dataset for GP and 139 

GWAS analyses at varying marker densities. To evaluate the effect of marker density, marker 140 

sets of approximately 1k, 2k, 5k, 10k, 20k, and 40k SNPs were generated from the 56k panel and 141 

are presented as marker density panels. 142

SNP heritability 143 

Marker -based heritability (h2) was estimated using the 10k genotype panel employed in GP. 144 

Genomic relationship matrix (GRM) was constructed using the rrBLUP v.4.6.3 framework 145 

(Endelman 2011). Varianc e components were estimated using a mixed model with family fitted 146 

as a fixed effect and additive genomic effects fitted as a random effect. SNP heritability was 147 

calculated as h²=Vg/(Vg+Ve), where Vg is the additive genetic variance captured by markers, an d148 

Ve is the residual variance. 149 

Population structure 150 

Genotypes were encoded as −1, 0, and 1, for homozygous reference, heterozygous, and 151 

homozygous alternative states, respectively, with missing genotypes retained as NA. Principal 152 

component analysis (PCA) w as performed using pcaMethods v.2.2.0 (Stacklies et al. 2007), and 153 

the leading components were used to account for population structure. 154 

Genome -wide association analysis 155 

GWAS results were visualized within a defined genomic window centered on the lead SNP (±2 156 

Mbp) to enable fine -scale interpretation of association signals. SNP significance was plotted as 157 

−log ₁ ₀ (p -value) against physical position, and linkage disequilibrium (LD; r²) between each SNP 158 

and the lead variant was calculated from genotype data and used to color -code markers, 159 

highlighting local haplotype structure around the peak. 160 

To capture clusters of association signals beyond single -marker significance, a loca l score 161 

approach based on the Lindley process was applied to transformed statistics (−log ₁ ₀ (p -value) − 162 

ξ), where ξ was empirically defined (Mercier and Daudin 2001). The cumulative score was 163 

computed along genomic coordinates to identify contiguous interva ls enriched for association 164 

signals, which were used to delineate candidate QTL regions (Bonhomme et al. 2019). SNP -wise 165

Genomic prediction 172 

GP was performed using four algorithms: ridge regression best linear unbiased prediction 173 

(rrBLUP), reproducing kernel Hilbert spa ce (RKHS), Bayesian B (BayesB), and Bayesian LASSO 174 

(BL), implemented in BGLR v1.1.4 (Pérez and de los Campos 2014) and the rrBLUP package 175 

(Endelman 2011). Marker genotypes were encoded as −1, 0, and 1, representing homozygous 176 

reference, heterozygous, and h omozygous alternative alleles, respectively. Prediction accuracy 177 

was evaluated using 5 -fold cross -validation with 10 repetitions, and calculated as the Pearson 178 

correlation between observed and predicted values. Results are reported as mean ± standard 179 

error .180 

Effect of training population size 181 

To evaluate the effect of training population size, a repeated k -fold cross -validation scheme was 182 

implemented. For each trait, individuals were partitioned into five folds, with one fold used as the 183 

validation set (~2 0%) and the remaining folds forming the training pool (~80%). 184 

Within each training pool, subsets corresponding to 20%, 40%, 60%, 80%, and 100% of the 185 

available individuals were sampled proportionally by family to preserve population structure. 186 

Models were trained on each subset and evaluated on the corresponding validation fold. 187

controlled genetic background (Supplemental Fig. 1). Substant ial phenotypic variation was 197

Discussion 288 

The ext ended aus -NAM population is a suitable platform for studying GP 289 

The aus -NAM population is structured to address a central challenge in rice GP: capturing 290 

sufficient allelic diversity for broad applicability while maintaining the controlled genetic 291 

background that enables accurate genomic modeling. By deriving all famil ies from crosses with 292 

the same recurrent parent, T65, the design generates genome -wide relatedness that supports 293 

the estimation of marker effects across families, while the diversity contributed by 14 aus donors 294 

ensures that the training population samples a broad range of haplotypes relevant to adaptive 295 

traits. This design enables extensive phenotypic variation while minimizing the confounding 296 

effects of population structure, supporting both accurate prediction and reliable mapping. The 297 

observed clustering by family, with partial overlap among groups, reflects the balance between 298 

relatedness and diversity that underpins the utility of NAM populations for quantitative genetic 299 

studies  (Gage et al. 2020) .300

dominates the population, which is favorable for genome -wide estimation of marker e ffects. 314 

GP accuracy is efficiently captured at moderate training population sizes 315 

GP accuracy increased with training population size across all traits and models, with most 316 

predictive gain achieved at moderate training sizes of ~40 –60% of the available pool (~500 lines; 317 

Fig. 2b). Beyond this point, accuracy gains were marginal, indicat ing diminishing returns from 318 

additional phenotyping effort. This pattern is consistent with previous findings in structured 319 

populations, where training population composition rather than size alone determines prediction 320 

performance  (Spindel et al. 2015; Yu et al. 2008) .321 

The efficiency of prediction at reduced training sizes reflects the haplotype structure inherent to 322 

NAM populations. Because all families share T65 as a common parent, key haplotype blocks and 323 

the underlying linkage disequilibrium structure are represented across families even at moderate 324

Moderate marker density of ~20k SNPs is sufficient for GP 335 

Prediction accuracy increased from low to moderate marker densities but plateaued at 336 

approximately 20k SNPs, beyond which additional markers contributed minimal gains (Fig. 2a). 337 

This plateau is consistent with saturation of linkage disequilibrium b etween markers and causal 338 

variants, such that markers beyond this density capture redundant rather than novel genomic 339 

information  (Yang Xu et al. 2018) . Traits with higher heritability reached this pla teau earlier and 340 

at higher absolute accuracy, whereas traits with lower heritability showed consistently low 341 

accuracy regardless of marker density, indicating that the accuracy ceiling for these traits is set 342 

by phenotypic quality rather than genotypic res olution. For traits such as DTH and CL, the 343 

accuracy gain from 10k to 40k SNPs was less than 0.05 correlation units —a difference unlikely 344 

to translate into meaningful improvement in selection decisions and not worth the additional 345 

genotyping cost in most b reeding programs. 346

Differences in prediction accuracy across traits were context -dependent (Fig. 2). Although rrBLUP 348 

showed lower prediction accuracy under reduced training popu lation sizes, the Bayes based 349

under these conditions. In contrast, when evaluated across marker densities, rrBLUP performed 351 

comparably to other models. The lower performance of rrBLUP under reduced training population 352 

sizes likely reflects differences in model assumptions. rrBLUP applies uniform shrinkage of 353 

marker effects based on an infinitesimal model  (Daetwyler et al. 2010) , whereas Bayesian models 354 

allow variable shrinkage and can better accommodate loci with larger effects  (Meuwissen et al. 355 

2001; Habier et al. 2011) . Despite this, rrBLUP remains robust and pe rforms comparably to more 356 

complex models when genome -wide relationships are effectively captured  (Endelman 2011; de 357 

Los Campos et al. 2013) . Trait -specific patterns further showed that traits with higher heritability 358 

exhibited higher prediction accuracy, whereas traits with lower heritability showed consistently 359 

lower accuracy across conditions. In practical terms, the choice between rrBLUP and Bayesian 360 

models matters most when training data are sc arce; once the training population reaches 361 

moderate size, the simpler model performs adequately, and the additional computational cost of 362 

Bayesian approaches is difficult to justify. 363

GWAS performance improved substantially with increasing marker density (Figs. 3g –h). Higher 365 

marker densities increased the number of detected QTL. This reflects enhanced genome -wide 366 

coverage and LD tagging, enabling detection of moderate -effect loci that may be missed at lower 367 

densities  (Gage et al. 2020) . This pattern reflects improved genome -wide LD tagging at higher 368 

densities, enabling detection of moderate -effect loci that are missed whe n marker coverage is 369 

sparse  (Gage et al. 2020) . Importantly, major loci including Ehd1 , SD1 , and FZP were consistently 370 

detected across all marker densities, confirming that the underlying genetic signals are robust 371

Known genes underlie major GWAS signals 383 

Local GWAS analyses provided additional resolution by leveraging LD structure and cumulative 384 

associa tion signals to refine candidate intervals  (Mercier and Daudin 2001) . This approach 385 

enabled clearer delineation of key loci, including Ehd1 , HGW , and SD1 , and improved 386 

interpretation of association pea ks within biologically relevant genomic regions. For example, 387

time variati on  (Lee et al. 2022) . These results support a genetic architecture in which complex 390 

traits are controlled by a combination of major loci and multiple moderate -effect components that 391 

can be resolved thr ough increased marker density and localized analysis. 392

In practical rice breeding, population breeding is gaining popularity over pedigree breeding. The 394 

primary difference between these m ethods lies in the generation at which selection is initiated. 395 

GP can be naturally integrated into population breeding, as described by  Reyes et al.  (2022) . The 396 

present study establishes a benchmark for implementing breeding programs involving 397 

japonica/indica crosses; notably, traits other than DTH were evaluated using a single plant per 398 

line, a nd we characterized the impacts of marker density, population size, and heritability on GP 399 

accuracy. Our results indicate that moderate marker densities and optimized training population 400 

sizes are sufficient for accurate genomic prediction, whereas higher marker densities are required 401 

to enhance GWAS resolution and candidate gene discovery. Although the aus -NAM population 402 

utilized only aus varieties as diversity donors and evaluation was done in a single environment, 403 

the potential of GP in practical breedin g was demonstrated. These findings provide a foundation 404 

for integrating GP with complementary approaches, including artificial intelligence, speed 405 

breeding, and multi -omics, to accelerate the development of climate -resilient crop varieties  (Cobb 406 

et al. 2019; Mishra et al. 2018; Crossa et al. 2017; Alemu et al. 2024; Gunundu et al. 2023) .407
