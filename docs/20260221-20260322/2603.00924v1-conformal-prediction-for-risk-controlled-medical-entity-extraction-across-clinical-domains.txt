# Conformal Prediction for Risk-Controlled Medical Entity Extraction Across Clinical Domains 

Manil Shrestha and Edward Kim 

Department of Computer Science, Drexel University, Philadelphia, USA 

{ms5267, ek826}@drexel.edu 

Abstract. Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used for medical entity extraction, yet their confidence scores are often miscali-brated, limiting safe deployment in clinical settings. We present a con-formal prediction framework that provides finite-sample coverage guar-antees for LLM-based extraction across two clinical domains. First, we extract structured entities from 1,000 FDA drug labels across eight sec-tions using GPT-4.1, verified via FactScore-based atomic statement eval-uation (97.7% accuracy over 128,906 entities). Second, we extract radi-ological entities from MIMIC-CXR reports using the RadGraph schema with GPT-4.1 and Llama-4-Maverick, evaluated against physician anno-tations (entity F1: 0.81 to 0.84). Our central finding is that miscalibration direction reverses across domains: on well-structured FDA labels, models are underconfident, requiring modest conformal thresholds ( τ ≈ 0.06 ), while on free-text radiology reports, models are overconfident, demand-ing strict thresholds ( τ up to 0.99). Despite this heterogeneity, conformal prediction achieves target coverage ( ≥ 90% ) in both settings with man-ageable rejection rates (9–13%). These results demonstrate that calibra-tion is not a global model property but depends on document structure, extraction category, and model architecture, motivating domain-specific conformal calibration for safe clinical deployment. 

Keywords: Large Language Models · Entity Extraction · Conformal Prediction. 

## 1 Introduction 

Extracting structured entities from unstructured medical text is critical for drug safety surveillance and clinical coding, and LLMs have shown strong perfor-mance on these tasks [11, 14]. However, LLM softmax probabilities are poorly calibrated [6, 7], providing no reliability guarantees for clinical deployment. Conformal prediction (CP) addresses this by converting raw scores into ac-ceptance decisions with finite-sample coverage guarantees under only an ex-changeability assumption [2]. CP has been applied to medical imaging [4], pathol-ogy coding [13], and EHR surveillance [5]. In our prior work [10], we applied CP to EHR extraction across 10,000 visits with thresholds validated against expert consensus [3]. Separately, LLM extraction has advanced on FDA labels [14] and

## 2 Methods 

We apply conformal prediction to two extraction tasks using GPT-4.1 [1] and Llama 4 Maverick (17B parameters, 128 experts) [12]. Both models return per-token log-probabilities. The pipeline has four steps. 

Step 1: Extraction with Token Confidence. The LLM extracts entities from eight FDA label sections (indications, adverse reactions, drug interactions, contraindi-cations, warnings, pregnancy, pediatric use, geriatric use) and from radiology reports following the RadGraph schema [8]: anatomy (ANAT) and observations with three uncertainty levels: definitely present (OBS-DP), definitely absent (OBS-DA), and uncertain (OBS-U). ## 3 Results and Discussion 

FDA Label Extraction. We extracted 110,664 entities from 1,000 FDA drug labels across eight sections using GPT-4.1, achieving 97.7% overall fact-score accuracy (per-section range: 74.3% for Pediatric Use to 100% for Geriatric Use). Calibration curves (Figure 1) reveal that GPT-4.1 is underconfident on FDA labels: predicted confidence ˆp consistently falls below empirical accuracy. ECE ranges from 0.022 (Adverse Reactions) to 0.211 (Pediatric Use). Adverse Reac-tions, the largest category (63,442 entities), is nearly perfectly calibrated ( τ =0.003 ). Pediatric Use is the sole exception, with both poor calibration and lower accuracy, likely due to sparser and less standardized safety language. At α = 0 .10 , conformal prediction achieves 88.5% to 90.8% coverage with 9.2% to 11.5% rejection rates across all sections. The model’s underconfidence is advantageous here, keeping conformal thresholds modest, in contrast to our prior EHR work [10] where overconfidence forced near-maximum thresholds. 

RadGraph Entity Extraction. We evaluated extraction on 100 MIMIC-CXR reports against test set of RadGraph gold annotations. With 5-shot prompting from dev set, GPT-4.1 achieves entity F1 of 0.826 and relation F1 of 0.502. Few-shot examples are critical: zero-shot recall drops 14.6 points and relation F1 nearly halves. In stark contrast to FDA labels, both GPT-4.1 and Llama 4 Maverick are 

overconfident on radiology reports (Figure 2), assigning near-certain probabili-ties to incorrect extractions. Llama achieves lower ECE than GPT-4.1 across all categories except OBS-U, which remains the most challenging for both models due to inherent ambiguity in hedging language. For OBS-DA, Llama requires ˆτ > 

0.999 , reflecting already high accuracy on negation predictions (ECE = 0.048). Rejection rates of 8.9% to 13.4% confirm that conformal filtering flags a man-ageable fraction for review while maintaining target coverage (Table 1).

Implications and Conclusion. Our central finding is that LLM calibration direction reverses across clinical domains: underconfident on structured FDA la-bels, overconfident on free-text radiology reports. Conformal prediction adapts to both regimes automatically, with thresholds that are lenient where models are conservative and strict where they are overconfident. A key limitation is the re-quirement for token-level log-probabilities, which frontier models (GPT-5 family, Claude Opus 4.6) do not currently expose. Future work includes extending guar-antees to black-box models via surrogate confidence estimation and comparison with post-hoc calibration baselines.
