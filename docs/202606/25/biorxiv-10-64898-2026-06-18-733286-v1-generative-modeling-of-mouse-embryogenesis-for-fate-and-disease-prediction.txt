Abstract 

Embryonic development is orchestrated by complex gene regulatory networks, and learning reg-ulatory dynamics from developmental data could allow us to understand, predict, and ultimately engineer cell fates. Here we introduce Navigo (https://github.com/aristoteleo/Navigo-release), a bi-ologically grounded generative modeling framework that learns a developmental vector field by integrating flow matching at the population level with RNA kinetics modeling at the molecular level. Navigo accurately maps developmental trajectories across lineages on a mouse embryogene-sis scRNA-seq atlas spanning 43 time points and comprising 12.4 million cells. Applied to cardiac development, Navigo enables disease modeling by mechanistically resolving regulatory networks that distinguish congenital heart disease subtypes. Navigo also predicts perturbation effects in a zero-shot manner, as validated on independent in vivo data from six knockout genotypes without perturbation-specific training, uncovering lineage-specific gene-compensation mechanisms. More-over, Navigo guides rational cell-fate engineering, exemplified by fibroblast reprogramming analy-ses, including identifying pro-fibrotic barriers to cardiac fates and evaluating hundreds of pairwise transcription factor combinations for neuronal fate, each consisting of one bHLH factor and one POU factor. Overall, Navigo provides a generalizable AI platform for perturbation-effect prediction, dis-ease modeling, and rational cell-fate engineering, advancing toward AI-based virtual embryos for developmental biology and regenerative medicine.

## Introduction 1

Embryonic development represents one of nature’s most remarkable transformations, where a single fer-2

tilized cell orchestrates a precisely choreographed sequence of cellular decisions to generate the extraor-3

dinary diversity and complexity of a multicellular organism. This process is governed by gene regulatory 4

networks (GRNs) operating through molecular interactions between regulators and their target genes, 5

which collectively coordinate cell fate decisions, tissue morphogenesis, and organ formation [1, 2, 3]. 6

Deciphering the regulatory principles underlying normal development addresses fundamental questions 7

in developmental biology [4, 5], provides the foundation for interpreting how regulatory disruptions 8

lead to congenital diseases [6, 7, 8], predicting the effects of genetic and regulatory perturbations, and 9

rationally manipulating developmental programs to engineer desired cellular outcomes for regenerative 10 

medicine [9, 10]. Together, these goals motivate the emerging vision of a predictive virtual embryo: a 11 

generalizable in silico system that can be queried and perturbed to understand developmental mecha-12 

nisms, predict disease-associated perturbation outcomes, and guide cell-fate engineering [11, 12]. 13 

Recent advances in single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) have generated unprecedented tem-14 

poral atlases of embryonic development [13, 14, 15, 16], profiling millions of cells across dozens of 15 

developmental time points from gastrulation through organogenesis to birth and capturing cellular states 16 

across diverse lineages and tissue types (Figure 1a,b). However, these snapshots capture only the out-17 

comes of development at sampled time points. They reveal what cellular states exist at each measured 18 

moment but do not reveal how cells transition between states, what regulatory mechanisms drive these 19 

transitions, or how the system would respond to perturbations. Addressing these questions requires 20 

learning the underlying generative process itself: a continuous dynamical system that captures how reg-21 

ulatory networks drive cellular state evolution over time, encodes the regulatory logic governing lineage 22 

decisions, and enables counterfactual simulation of developmental outcomes under unobserved pertur-23 

bations. Generative modeling [17, 18] provides this capability by capturing the data-generating process, 24 

transforming static observations into a dynamic, predictive, and programmable framework for modeling 25 

embryogenesis (Figure 1c). 26 

Current computational approaches for modeling developmental processes face distinct limitations. 27 

Early trajectory inference methods, including Monocle [19, 20] and Diffusion Pseudotime [21], re-28 

construct pseudotemporal ordering and branching structures from single-cell transcriptomes but do not 29 

explicitly model the underlying cellular dynamics. Methods based on RNA velocity [22, 23] cap-30 

ture molecular-level dynamics but do not explicitly model temporal information, while trajectory map-31 

ping [24] methods map population-level patterns but lack biological grounding in the molecular mech-32 

anisms driving transitions, limiting their ability to provide mechanistic insight. Neural-ODE-based ap-33 

proaches [25] can model continuous dynamics, but they can be computationally intensive and are not 34 

directly constrained by transcriptional kinetics. To address these gaps, we introduce Navigo, a biolog-35 

ically grounded generative modeling framework that learns developmental vector fields by integrating 36 

flow matching at the population level with RNA kinetics modeling at the molecular level. At the popula-37 

tion level, Navigo employs flow matching [26] to efficiently learn continuous vector fields from discrete 38 

temporal snapshots, enabling the model to capture smooth developmental trajectories across the en-39 

tire cellular state space. At the molecular level, Navigo explicitly models transcriptional kinetics [27] 40 

through differential equations governing RNA transcription, splicing, and degradation, grounding the 41 

learned dynamics in the mechanistic processes that drive gene expression changes. This integration en-42 

ables Navigo to capture both the temporal trajectories of development and the molecular mechanisms 43 

underlying cell state transitions, thereby providing a scalable and generalizable foundation for disease 44 

modeling, perturbation-effect prediction, and rational cell-fate engineering. 45 

We validate Navigo on a mouse embryogenesis scRNA-seq atlas [13] spanning embryonic day 8.5 46 

(E8.5) to birth and comprising 12.4 million cells across 43 time points and 26 major trajectories (Fig-47 

ure 1b). Navigo accurately maps developmental trajectories across lineages, achieving superior per-48 

formance in interpolating cellular states between observed time points. The learned generative model 49

## Results 71 

Navigo Learns Dynamic Vector Fields from Temporal Atlas of Mouse Embryogenesis via 72 

Generative Modeling 73 

Modeling embryogenesis from single-cell temporal atlases poses two fundamental challenges (Fig-74 

ure 1d). First, embryogenesis and developmental transitions are governed by high-dimensional non-75 

linear regulatory mechanisms that are infeasible to measure directly given the current technologies. 76 

Models must therefore learn biologically meaningful transition dynamics driven by underlying gene 77 

regulatory networks (GRNs), rather than merely fitting arbitrary mathematical transformations between 78 

cell state distributions at discrete time points. Second, the destructive nature of single-cell sequencing 79 

eliminates ground-truth cell-to-cell correspondences across time points, making it impossible to directly 80 

observe individual cellular trajectories or lineage relationships. This requires models to infer develop-81 

mental paths from population-level distributions without access to true cell pairings. 82 

To address these challenges, Navigo employs a generative modeling framework that learns the con-83 

tinuous developmental vector field from temporal snapshots through joint modeling of molecular and 84 

population dynamics. At the molecular level, Navigo captures transcriptional dynamics using differen-85 

tiable RNA velocity models (Figure 1e). The framework predicts biologically interpretable kinetic pa-86 

rameters from smoothed unspliced and spliced RNA counts: transcription rates ( α), splicing rates ( β), 87 

and degradation rates ( γ). Transcription rates are modeled as functions of mature mRNA abundance, re-88 

flecting the principle that gene expression is regulated by upstream regulatory proteins, for which mature 89 

mRNA counts are used as a proxy for protein abundance [32, 33]. This design makes transcription rates 90 

both cell-specific and gene-specific, capturing cell-state dependent regulatory dynamics, while splic-91 

ing and degradation rates remain gene-specific parameters, following previous studies [23, 32]. These 92 

kinetic parameters then define the velocity field through RNA kinetic equations, enabling the model 93 

to learn cell-specific regulatory dynamics that drive developmental transitions through a biologically 94 

grounded biophysical model of RNA transcription, splicing and degradation. 95

Navigo Accurately Maps the Mouse Embryogenesis Process 109 

A practical generative model of embryogenesis must learn the underlying regulatory process generating 110 

the observed data, rather than merely fitting static temporal snapshots. This distinction is critical because 111 

experimental scRNA-seq atlases have inherent limitations: data are collected at discrete time intervals 112 

with inevitable technical noise and sampling variability that can obscure true biological signals, partic-113 

ularly for cell types that are hard to capture. A model that genuinely captures the generative process 114 

should be able to infer biologically meaningful cellular states at unobserved intermediate time points 115 

(interpolation) and distinguish genuine developmental dynamics from technical artifacts (denoising). 116 

We therefore evaluated Navigo on these two tasks (Figure 2a), which assess whether the learned vector 117 

field represents a faithful reconstruction of the continuous regulatory dynamics governing embryonic 118 

development. 119

We then assessed the computational scalability of Navigo compared to other methods. As shown 140 

in Figure 2f, Navigo demonstrates superior scalability characteristics. TIGON [25], which employs 141 

a Neural-ODE framework operating in a relatively low-dimensional space with 10 features, requires 142 

substantially longer training time on large datasets. In contrast, Navigo with flow matching shows 143 

substantially better scalability while operating in a much higher-dimensional space (3,902 genes). This 144 

difference is critical because excessive dimensionality reduction inevitably results in information loss 145 

that compromises the model performance, as demonstrated by the inferior performance of TIGON in 146 

Figure 2b-d. The computational advantage of flow matching stems from its ability to directly regress the 147 

vector field through simulation-free objectives during training, whereas Neural-ODE methods require 148 

solving ordinary differential equations during both forward and backward passes. 149 

We next evaluated the denoising capability of Navigo by testing whether the model can recover 150 

true cellular states when technical artifacts cause under-representation of specific populations. For this 151 

task, we employed forward simulation to generate denoised cell populations ( Methods ). We designed 152 

two complementary evaluation settings (Figure 2g). In the simulated setting, we randomly selected X153 

cell type-time point pairs and retained only Y % of cells from each selected pair to simulate controlled 154 

noise scenarios across diverse developmental contexts. Specifically, we set X = 100 and Y = 20 in 155 

our experiments. In the real setting, we identified instances where certain cell types showed anoma-156 

lously low proportions at particular developmental stages, likely reflecting technical capture difficulties 157 

or biological noise. 158 

In the simulated setting, we first evaluate the agreement between the predicted denoised cell type 159 

proportions and the ground truth proportions without added noise. As shown in Figure 2h, the predicted 160 

proportions are highly correlated with the ground truth cell type proportions, with a high Pearson corre-161 

lation indicating that our model can effectively recover cell type proportions that are highly consistent 162 

with the ground truth. In Figure 2i, we further evaluate the marker gene prediction capability of Navigo 163 

(Methods ) by assessing how well the marker genes derived from the predicted cell population align with 164 

the marker genes derived from the ground truth cell population. Navigo achieves significantly higher 165 

F1 scores compared to the noised population, demonstrating its effective denoising performance. The 166 

performance comparisons under other hyperparameters for X, Y are shown in Supplementary Figure 6. 167

To evaluate whether the development vector field accurately captures lineage-specific GRNs, as shown 190 

in Figure 3a, we first extracted regulatory networks directly from the learned vector fields by computing 191 

the Jacobian matrix, which quantifies how changes in mature RNA counts Sj influence transcription 192 

rates αi as Jij = ∂α i 

> ∂S j

. This Jacobian element Jij represents the regulatory strength of Gene j on Gene 193 

i. The inferred regulatory network was then compared against ChIP-seq experimental data from the 194 

ChIP-Atlas [44] database, which measures transcription factor (TF) binding across diverse cell lineages 195 

and serves as experimental reference for validation. As shown in Figure 3b, Navigo consistently out-196 

performed existing methods (CellOracle and Dynamo) in recovering known TF-target relationships as 197 

measured by AUROC scores ( Methods ), with statistical significance confirmed by Wilcoxon matched-198 

pairs tests ( p-value < 0.05). These results demonstrate that Navigo effectively learns the regulatory logic 199 

across different cell types. 200 

We next applied this validated framework to investigate the gene regulatory mechanisms underlying 201 

developmental diseases by analyzing CHD [45] (Figure 3c), structural abnormalities of the heart that de-202 

velop during fetal development and represent the most common type of birth defect. CHDs encompass 203 

diverse subtypes including atrial septal defects (ASDs), ventricular septal defects (VSDs), outflow tract 204 

(OT) defects, heterotaxy, functional single ventricle, and obstructive lesions. Although genes associ-205 

ated with different CHD subtypes are known to involve distinct developmental processes, how different 206 

CHD genes affect specific regulatory pathways and networks to cause particular defect types remains 207 

incompletely understood. 208

Navigo Predicts Genetic Perturbation Outcomes Without Training on Specific Knockout 283 

Data 284 

A key promise of learning developmental dynamics is the ability to perform in silico genetic perturba-285 

tions, creating “virtual embryos” to predict how genetic knockout (KO) reshapes cell fates and gene ex-286 

pression programs without requiring costly and time-consuming animal work, gene editing and scRNA-287 

seq experiments. We evaluated Navigo’s ability to predict genetic KO outcomes without training on any 288 

knockout data using an independent scRNA-seq dataset of mouse embryonic gene expression following 289 

genetic KO (Figure 4a). This dataset [29] includes six KO genotypes, generated via standard gene-290 

editing and breeding or tetraploid aggregation, and profiled by single-cell sequencing at E13.5 alongside 291 

wild-type controls. 292 

To assess zero-shot KO prediction, we simulated KO by replacing each KO gene’s expression in 293 

all wild-type cells with mg i, where gi is the mean non-zero expression of gene i in wild-type cells, 294 

and m < 0 is a hyperparameter controlling the KO strength. The negative intervention value was 295 

used to represent a sustained inhibitory signal during simulation, following the perturbation strategy 296 

used in Dynamo [23]. We then applied Navigo’s ODE-based inference to predict developmental gene 297 

expression changes and identified the most affected genes by comparing the predicted KO and WT 298 

profiles ( Methods ). These predictions were validated against ground-truth DEGs, determined using a 299 

Wilcoxon rank-sum test with Benjamini-Hochberg correction (FDR < 0.05 ). Detailed statistics for 300 

different cell types and KOs are shown in the Supplementary Figure 13-15. 301 

As demonstrated in Figure 4b, this prediction task poses several challenges. First, the two datasets 302 

originate from independent studies, creating a data gap that tests model generalization. Second, analysis 303 

of ground-truth DEGs revealed substantial variance across KO-lineage combinations, with a substantial 304 

proportion of combinations producing relatively few DEGs. Third, a temporal mismatch exists: while 305 

actual KOs occur at embryogenesis onset, data availability and computational constraints limit our sim-306 

ulations to nearby time points around the E13.5 profiling stage. Specifically, we used five time points 307 

(E12.5, E12.75, E13.0, E13.25, and E13.5) to simulate KO impacts and averaged the predicted gene 308

Gene expression changes following KO vary across developmental lineages: some cell types exhibit 310 

substantial expression changes, while others remain relatively stable due to compensatory mechanisms. 311 

We assessed whether Navigo captures this heterogeneity by examining the correlation between predicted 312 

mean absolute expression changes and the number of ground-truth DEGs. As shown in Figure 4c and 313 

Supplementary Figure 16a, we observed a positive correlation across gene-lineage combinations (each 314 

dot represents one combination). This demonstrates that the model effectively prioritizes combinations 315 

likely to produce observable experimental outcomes, with lineage-specific correlations shown in Sup-316 

plementary Figure 16c. 317 

We next benchmarked Navigo against existing methods across different developmental lineages for 318 

Gli2 KO. We evaluated prediction performance using Top-50 precision, defined as the percentage of 319 

ground-truth DEGs recovered among the top 50 genes ranked by predicted expression change magni-320 

tude. As shown in Figure 4d, Navigo consistently outperformed both the random baseline and CellOr-321 

acle. For the random baseline, we randomly sampled a set of genes with the same size as the ground-322 

truth DEG set for each gene-lineage combination and computed Top-50 precision against this random 323 

set. Notably, Navigo achieved higher prediction precision on gene-lineage combinations with stronger 324 

perturbation signals. Similarly, we also observed that Navigo achieved higher prediction precision in lin-325 

eages with stronger perturbation signals, as measured by the number of ground-truth DEGs (Figure 4e, 326 

Supplementary Figure 16b). To assess sensitivity to the perturbation hyperparameter m, which controls 327 

the magnitude of simulated gene expression reduction upon KO, we evaluated Navigo’s performance 328

We next analyzed the factors underlying lineage- and gene-dependent variability in KO responses. 348 

We hypothesized that, within a gene family, the magnitude of the experimental KO response depends 349 

on the perturbed gene’s relative regulatory contribution compared with its paralogs in a given lineage. 350 

To test this idea, we performed systematic KO simulations for all genes in each family and quantified, 351 

for each lineage, a family-normalized score for each member’s predicted regulatory contribution. We 352 

then compared these simulation-derived scores with DEG counts measured in the corresponding ex-353 

perimental KO datasets. In the Gli family (Figure 4i), the lineage-specific pattern of predicted Gli2 354 

contribution was concordant with the pattern of DEG counts across lineages in the experimental Gli2 355 

KO data. Specifically, lineages in which Gli2 was predicted to contribute more strongly than Gli1 /Gli3 356 

showed larger experimental KO responses (more DEGs), whereas lineages in which Gli2 was predicted 357 

to play a weaker relative role showed smaller responses. Thus, although the simulated contribution score 358 

and experimental DEG count are different quantities, their agreement across lineages indicates that the 359 

model captures biologically meaningful differences in Gli2 dependence. In the Scn family (Figure 4j), 360 

Scn9a consistently showed the highest predicted regulatory contribution relative to Scn10a and Scn11a 361 

across all examined lineages. Consistent with this prediction, experimental double KO of Scn10a/11a 362 

resulted in minimal DEGs, suggesting that Scn9a buffers their loss. Together, these observations support 363 

the idea that the balance of regulatory contribution among paralogs determines KO response magnitude. 364 

Beyond explaining response magnitude, we investigated which specific downstream genes are sensi-365 

tive to KO by examining regulatory directionality among family members. We hypothesized that genes 366 

co-regulated in the same direction by family members would be more sensitive to gene KO perturba-367 

tions than genes subject to opposing regulation. Using Navigo, we simulated Gli1 /Gli3 double KO and 368 

Gli2 KO separately and classified predicted Gli effectors into “Same Directions” (concordant regulation 369 

by Gli2 versus Gli1 /Gli3 ) and “Different Directions” (opposing regulation) categories (Figure 4k, left). 370 

Hypergeometric enrichment analysis revealed significant enrichment of experimental Gli2 KO DEGs in 371 

the “Same Directions” category across lineages (Figure 4k, right). This pattern is consistent with dosage 372 

sharing in redundant gene regulation, where loss of one family member reduces combined regulatory 373 

output below a functional threshold, making expression changes more likely to manifest [91]. Con-374 

versely, effectors in the “Different Directions” category tend to remain buffered, as the opposing regula-375 

tory activities of remaining family members can partially compensate for the loss [92]. Supplementary 376 

Figure 18 shows similar enrichment patterns for simulated single KOs of Gli1 and Gli3 . Together, these 377 

results suggest that Navigo captures interpretable regulatory logic linking two complementary aspects of 378 

gene family compensation to KO outcomes: the balance of regulatory activity among paralogs may in-379 

fluence response magnitude, while regulatory directionality may determine which specific downstream 380 

genes are most affected. 381

Fibroblasts can be reprogrammed into specific cell types by overexpression of key TFs in cell culture. 392 

As shown in Figure 5a, we computationally simulated this process by setting a candidate TF’s expression 393 

to a high value and performing forward inference to predict the resulting gene expression changes. We 394 

then compared the direction of these predicted changes with that of the known DEGs distinguishing the 395 

target cell type from fibroblasts, and used the resulting directional accuracy as a proxy for reprogram-396 

ming potential, reasoning that TFs with stronger reprogramming potential should induce transcriptional 397

in Figure 5b, both AUROC and AUPRC scores were consistently high across all lineages, demonstrat-404

subsets for each cell type, further confirming the robustness of our predictions. 407

## Discussion 487 

In this paper, we introduce Navigo, a biologically grounded generative modeling framework that learns 488 

predictive developmental dynamics from static snapshots of embryogenesis. This framework addresses 489 

a fundamental challenge in developmental biology: while single-cell technologies have generated un-490 

precedented atlases profiling millions of cells across development [13, 14, 15, 16], these data capture 491 

discrete snapshots rather than the continuous dynamical system that generates cell-fate transitions. Un-492 

derstanding these dynamic regulatory processes is essential for building generalizable AI platforms that 493 

can model developmental diseases, predict the consequences of genetic and regulatory perturbations, 494 

and guide rational cell-fate engineering for regenerative medicine. 495 

Navigo advances this vision by integrating flow matching at the population level with RNA kinetics 496 

modeling at the molecular level, learning continuous dynamics that govern cell state transitions during 497

Despite these advances, several limitations suggest important directions for future improvement. 511 

First, while our use of metacells substantially reduces computational cost compared with modeling indi-512 

vidual cells, and flow matching is more efficient than neural ODEs [108, 109], Navigo still requires sub-513 

stantial training time. Developing more scalable model architectures and optimization strategies would 514 

improve efficiency and enable broader adoption of generative modeling approaches in developmental bi-515 

ology. Second, our perturbation prediction is based on zero-shot inference and validated on observational 516 

knockout data without direct supervision from perturbation experiments. Although this demonstrates the 517 

model’s ability to learn meaningful regulatory relationships, fine-tuning or explicitly training on pertur-518 

bation datasets could further improve prediction accuracy, particularly for subtle or context-dependent 519 

perturbation effects. Third, due to data availability, our analysis focuses on development from E8.5 520 

onward [13], missing earlier critical stages of embryogenesis, including pre-implantation development 521 

and gastrulation. Training models on datasets spanning the full developmental timeline would enable 522 

a more holistic understanding of embryogenesis. Ideally, such datasets should be generated using uni-523 

fied experimental platforms to minimize batch effects and ensure comparability across stages. Fourth, 524 

although Navigo models developmental state transitions in transcriptional space and succeeds in many 525 

applications, it does not explicitly account for population dynamics such as cell proliferation, division, 526 

and death, nor does it incorporate lineage information or ancestor–descendant relationships. These pro-527 

cesses are fundamental to embryonic development and may shape both the directionality and abundance 528 

of emerging cell states. Integrating lineage tracing and explicit population dynamics into the modeling 529 

framework could improve the biological realism of the learned developmental trajectories and strengthen 530 

mechanistic interpretation. 531 

Several promising directions emerge for advancing predictive and programmable modeling of devel-532 

opmental processes. First, scaling generative models across multiple species including human, mouse, 533 

and other model organisms could reveal conserved regulatory principles and evolutionary discrepancies 534 

in developmental programs [110, 111]. Comparative analysis across species would uncover universal 535 

gene regulatory mechanisms that are essential for development while identifying lineage-specific adap-536 

tations that generate phenotypic diversity. Second, establishing active learning frameworks [112] that 537 

create feedback loops between computational predictions and genetic perturbation experiments could 538 

iteratively refine perturbation prediction accuracy while systematically validating and discovering novel 539 

regulatory mechanisms during development. Third, incorporating spatial information into the modeling 540 

framework from technologies like spatial transcriptomics [113, 114, 115, 116] would capture the criti-541 

cal interplay between gene regulation and spatial organization during development, enabling models to 542 

predict not only cellular identities but also tissue architecture and morphogenesis. Fourth, integrating 543 

multi-modal and multi-omics measurements [117, 118, 119] such as chromatin accessibility and DNA 544 

methylation would provide a more complete mechanistic understanding of how regulatory programs 545 

orchestrate cell fate specification during embryo development. 546

The transcriptomic atlas spanning the entire embryogenesis process contains tens of millions of cells. 668 

While this comprehensive dataset provides rich characterization of developmental dynamics, directly 669

challenge, we adopt a metacell strategy that aggregates similar cells to reduce data size while preserving 671 

biological heterogeneity. Metacells have been widely applied in single-cell analysis tasks including 672 

trajectory inference and differential expression analysis [125, 126, 127]. 673 

We construct metacells separately for each combination of embryo, cell type, and time point. For 674 

any given embryo at a specific time point, if the number of cells belonging to a particular cell type 675 

(nc = |Xci |) is smaller than a threshold N (set to N = 100 ), these cells are retained without aggregation 676 

to preserve information from rare cell populations. Otherwise, we perform K-means clustering with 677 

K = N + ⌈ nc−Nm ⌉, where the hyperparameter m is set to 50. This adaptive clustering strategy ensures 678 

that rare cell types are preserved while abundant populations are appropriately downsampled. Using 679 

this approach, the dataset size is reduced by approximately 20-fold, achieving substantially improved 680 

computational efficiency while maintaining representation of rare cell populations. 681 

Baselines 682 

Dynamo 683 

Dynamo [23] reconstructs continuous velocity vector fields by modeling RNA transcription, splicing, 684 

and degradation kinetics. The method estimates RNA velocities from spliced and unspliced counts 685 

and performs vector field reconstruction in reduced-dimensional space. The reconstructed vector field 686 

enables differential geometry analysis including Jacobian, acceleration, and curvature calculations for 687 

inferring gene regulatory relationships and cellular dynamics. 688 

Moscot 689 

Moscot [24] is a scalable optimal transport framework that maps cells through time and space using 690 

multi-omics single-cell data. For temporal analysis (moscot.time), the method estimates cellular trajec-691 

tories by solving Wasserstein-type optimal transport problems between consecutive time points. The 692 

framework supports multimodal data through shared latent representations and achieves linear time and 693 

memory complexity through low-rank approximations of the coupling matrix, enabling application to 694 

atlas-scale datasets. 695 

TIGON 696 

TIGON [25] simultaneously infers cell velocity and population growth by solving a dynamic unbalanced 697 

optimal transport problem based on Wasserstein-Fisher-Rao distance. The method models cellular dy-698 

namics through a continuity equation with a growth term, where the velocity field describes instanta-699 

neous changes in gene expression and the growth term captures population changes due to cell division 700 

and death. TIGON uses neural networks to parameterize both velocity and growth fields, and learns 701 

trajectories by minimizing the combined Wasserstein and Fisher-Rao metrics through neural ordinary 702 

differential equations. This formulation provides an unbiased approach to learning cell transitions. 703 

CellOracle 704 

CellOracle [128] integrates GRN modeling with in silico perturbation to predict changes in cell identity 705 

following TF perturbations. The method constructs cell-type-specific GRN configurations using regular-706 

ized linear regression models on multi-omics data, combining scRNA-seq with scATAC-seq to identify 707 

accessible promoter and enhancer regions. CellOracle simulates TF perturbations by propagating ex-708 

pression shifts through the inferred GRN via iterative signal propagation, then converts the simulated 709 

gene expression changes into transition probability vectors in low-dimensional embedding space. 710
