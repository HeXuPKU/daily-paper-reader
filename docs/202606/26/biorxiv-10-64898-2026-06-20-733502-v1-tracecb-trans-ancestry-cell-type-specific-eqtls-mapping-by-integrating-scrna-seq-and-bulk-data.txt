## Abstract 7

Mapping cell-type-specific expression quantitative trait loci (ct-eQTLs) is essential for inter-8

preting disease-associated variants, yet studies in underrepresented populations are hindered 9

by limited statistical power. Here, we present traceCB, a statistical framework that enhances 10 

ct-eQTL mapping in target ancestries by integrating summary statistics from single-cell and 11 

bulk-tissue eQTL studies across diverse populations. By explicitly modeling trans-ancestry 12 

genetic architecture and accounting for cellular heterogeneity in bulk tissues, traceCB opti-13 

mizes information borrowing from well-powered European cohorts while robustly controlling 14 

for type I error. Simulation studies demonstrate that traceCB achieves superior statistical 15 

power compared to original ct-eQTL, particularly when leveraging tissue-level data. In an 16 

application to immune cells in East Asian and African cohorts, traceCB increased the ef-17 

fective sample size by up to 2.9-fold and identified approximately 40% more eGenes than 18 

single-ancestry analyses, with a replication rate exceeding 90% in independent datasets. Fur-19 

thermore, traceCB improved the colocalization of regulatory variants with GWAS signals for 20 

blood and immune-related traits, revealing cell-type-specific mechanisms underlying complex 21 

diseases. These findings establish traceCB as a powerful and scalable tool for leveraging global 22 

genomic resources to improve regulatory variant discovery at the cellular level across diverse 23 

populations. 24

Genome-wide association studies (GWASs) have successfully identified associations between 26 

hundreds of thousands of genomic variants and complex traits or diseases [1]. Despite these 27 

fruitful discoveries, the majority (over 90%) of identified risk variants are located in non-coding 28 

regions, posing a significant challenge for deciphering their underlying biological functions [2, 3]. 29 

A critical insight is that GWAS signals are enriched in gene regulatory elements, particularly 30 

expression quantitative trait loci (eQTLs)—genetic variants associated with gene expression 31 

levels [4]. This enrichment underscores the hypothesis that many GWAS hits exert their 32 

effects through the regulation of gene expression, as trait-associated SNPs are three times more 33 

likely to be eQTLs [5]. While large-scale eQTL studies based on bulk tissues have enhanced 34 

our understanding of GWAS signals [6], they rely on average expression strength across all 35 

constituent cells in a tissue, which masks the cellular heterogeneity of the eQTL landscape. 36 

Growing evidence indicates that eQTL effects can be highly cell-type-specific, with many only 37 

detectable in the relevant cell type for a given trait [2, 7, 8], highlighting the critical need to map 38 

cell-type-specific eQTLs (ct-eQTLs) that improves our understanding of disease mechanisms 39 

at cellular level [9]. Recently, expanding resources of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) 40 

data have enabled extensive ct-eQTL studies in various human tissues. Compared with tissue-41 

level bulk RNA-seq, scRNA-seq provides a higher resolution of gene expression profiles at the 42 

single-cell level, allowing the identification of ct-eQTLs [10]. 43 

Despite the promising insight offered by ct-eQTL mapping, its broad application has been 44 

restricted by the lack of generalizability in genetically diversified ancestries. So far, the sample 45 

composition of current eQTL studies is highly biased towards European ancestry (EUR) in 46 

both bulk and scRNA-seq eQTL data. In the tissue-level Genotype-Tissue Expression (GTEx) 47 

v10 dataset, 85% of the 981 donors are from European ancestry, while only 12% are from 48 

African descendants, which is the second largest ancestry in GTEx [11]. Similarly, across 11 49 

single-cell eQTL studies from the eQTL Catalogue, 88.6% are of European ancestry, 8.8% are 50 

of African ancestry, and only 0.7% are of East Asian ancestry (EAS) [12]. Given the strongly 51 

unbalanced sample makeup, most of the eQTL studies have been based on the European 52 

subjects. These discoveries may not be extended to non-European populations, limiting their 53 

values in interpreting GWAS variants. For example, the heritability of schizophrenia in EAS 54 

was found less enriched in the brain eQTLs when they were identified from EUR samples [13]. 55

Therefore, trans-ancestry ct-eQTL mapping, aiming to pinpoint ct-eQTL SNPs across global 56 

ancestries, especially for the underrepresented ones, is a critical step toward global health 57 

equity. 58 

The challenges of trans-ancestry ct-eQTL mapping arise from two aspects. First, the 59 

genetic architecture of eQTLs can vary across ancestries. Recent studies have reported that 60 

allele frequency differences account for a substantial proportion of population differences in 61 

eQTLs [13]. The eQTL effects and linkage disequilibrium (LD) also display heterogeneous 62 

patterns in different ancestries. Second, the ct-eQTL mapping with scRNA-seq data has been 63 

restricted by technical constraints. The prohibitive cost of scRNA-seq has greatly restricted 64 

the scale of single-cell eQTL studies, limiting the power in ct-eQTL mapping. Furthermore, 65 

due to the lower sequencing depth and higher dropout rate, the gene expression levels measured 66 

with scRNA-seq are much noisier than the bulk RNA-seq, which can also lead to potential loss 67 

of power [14–16]. 68 

Unfortunately, existing ct-eQTL methods have only partially addressed the above chal-69 

lenges. To circumvent the noisy nature of scRNA-seq data, interaction-based methods take 70 

bulk RNA-seq data as input and detect the ct-eQTLs by modeling the interaction effects 71 

between the genetic variants and a proxy marker of cell type, which can be derived from estab-72 

lished cell type deconvolution methods [17]. However, these methods can have limited power 73 

when working with rare cell types [2, 17]. To better leverage both data types, recent pioneering 74 

methods, such as IBSEP [16] and JOBS [18], integrate eQTL summary statistics from both 75 

bulk and scRNA-seq data. Although the integrative analysis can improve the statistical power 76 

for ct-eQTL prioritization, these methods were designed for single-ancestry analysis and can 77 

not account for the heterogeneous genetic architectures across diverse populations. To inte-78 

grate genetic data from the heterogeneous resources, a number of meta-analysis methods have 79 

been developed to combine trans-ancestry genetic studies. The classical fixed-effect (FE) meta 80 

analysis model assumes the effect sizes are homogeneous across studies, limiting its applicabil-81 

ity in trans-ancestry setting. The random-effects model (RE) [19] and its extension, RE2 [20], 82 

relaxes this assumption by allowing the effect sizes to vary. While these methods have been 83 

applied to meta analyze trans-ancestry studies, they have not adequately accounted for critical 84 

heterogeneity across ancestries, such as the LD and allele frequencies, leading to inflated type-I 85 

errors. 86

In this paper, we propose traceCB, a novel statistical framework designed to enhance the 87 

statistical power for ct-eQTL mapping in underrepresented ancestries. Our method achieves 88 

this by integrating eQTL summary statistics from scRNA-seq and bulk RNA-seq data across 89 

diverse ancestries, thereby leveraging information from a well-powered auxiliary population 90 

(Fig. 1). Our framework offers three key advantages over existing methods. First, it explic-91 

itly models the local genetic architectures of cis -SNPs across ancestries, optimally integrating 92 

scRNA-seq data from different populations. Second, it can further utilize high-quality bulk 93 

RNA-seq data from European populations to substantially improve the statistical power of 94 

ct-eQTL mapping in the target population. Third, the efficient moment-based estimation 95 

framework of traceCB operates with summary-level eQTL data, enabling genome-scale analy-96 

sis. Under this framework, we also introduce a special version, called traceC, that can integrate 97 

trans-ancestry ct-eQTL data when bulk data are not available. Through extensive simulations, 98 

we demonstrated that traceCB substantially boosts statistical power by borrowing information 99 

from trans-ancestry single-cell and bulk eQTL data, while maintaining well-calibrated type I 100 

error rates. As a real-world application, we applied traceCB to improve ct-eQTL mapping of 5 101 

major blood cell types in East Asian individuals by integrating BioBank Japan (BBJ) [21] 102 

ct-eQTL summary data with 10 European scRNA-seq studies from eQTL Catalogue [12] 103 

and whole blood eQTL data from eQTLGen [6]. For genes with significant trans-ancestry 104 

co-heritability, traceC increased the effective sample size by 1.82-fold (from 103 to 187.84), 105 

whereas traceCB achieved a 2.32-fold increase (from 103 to 239.24, Supplementary Table 8). 106 

Averaged across the 10 auxiliary studies (Supplementary Table 9), traceC identified 28% more 107 

eGenes across cell types (from 1,929 to 2,463), and traceCB further increased this gain to 38% 108 

(from 1,929 to 2,669) as compared to the original BBJ study. We replicated these discoveries 109 

in both internal and external validation datasets [22, 23]. High external replication rates of 110 

93% for traceC and 92% for traceCB support the robustness and reliability of our findings 111 

(Supplementary Table 9). With colocalization analysis, we showed that ct-eQTLs prioritized 112 

by traceCB colocalized with GWAS signals, revealing cell-type-specific gene-regulatory mech-113 

anisms underlying blood-related traits and immune diseases. Furthermore, we demonstrated 114 

the generalizability of our framework to other ancestries by applying traceCB to integrate 115 

African scRNA-seq eQTL data with European studies. Collectively, these results indicate 116 

that traceCB substantially enhances the statistical power of trans-ancestry ct-eQTL mapping, 117

## Results 120 

Method overview 121 

We present traceCB, a statistical method to prioritize ct-eQTLs in an underrepresented target 122 

ancestry by leveraging bulk RNA-seq and scRNA-seq eQTLs from a larger auxiliary ances-123 

try. With the summary statistics from trans-ancestry scRNA-seq eQTLs, traceCB leverages 124 

the cell-type-specific cis -heritabilities and co-heritabilities of gene expression levels to borrow 125 

information across ancestries. Built upon the cell type deconvolution model, traceCB can 126 

further leverage the cell-type proportions in the large bulk tissue samples to boost its power. 127 

traceCB performs eQTL analysis in a generalized method of moments (GMM) framework, mak-128 

ing it adaptive to complex genetic architectures and computationally efficient. The resulting 129 

traceCB estimator increases the effective sample size of ct-eQTLs and identifies more eGenes 130 

in the target ancestry, facilitating downstream analyses such as colocalization with GWAS or 131 

eGene discovery. For scenarios where bulk tissue data is unavailable, we additionally intro-132 

duce traceC, a simplified version of traceCB that prioritizes eQTLs using only scRNA-seq data. 133 

Both methods are computationally efficient and scalable to genome-wide analysis. For instance, 134 

in our real data analysis, the processing of chromosome 1 took approximately 432 seconds, cor-135 

responding to an average of only 0.3 seconds per gene for both traceC and traceCB. The code 136 

of software and analysis process can be found at https://github.com/LucaJiang/traceCB ,137 

and the documentation is available at https://lucajiang.github.io/traceCB/ .138

We next considered whether a same-ancestry scRNA-seq-plus-bulk integration strategy 212 

could be applied ad hoc in the trans-ancestry setting. Across simulation settings with vary-213 

ing causal-SNP proportions, trans-ancestry genetic correlations and target cell-type propor-214 

tions, traceC and traceCB maintained type I error control near the nominal level of 0.05. In 215 

contrast, the ad hoc same-ancestry strategies showed substantial type I error inflation, with 216 

stronger inflation at higher target cell-type proportions and causal-SNP proportions (Supple-217 

mentary Fig. 11). These results show that trans-ancestry integration requires explicit mod-218 

eling of ancestry-specific LD and trans-ancestry co-heritability, rather than direct reuse of 219 

same-ancestry bulk-plus-single-cell models. 220 

To assess performance when causal variants are not fully shared across ancestries, we 221 

partitioned the same 2,000-SNP cis window into a target-null auxiliary-specific region and 222 

a shared-effect region. In the auxiliary-specific region, where no target-population causal 223 

effect was present, traceC and traceCB maintained well-calibrated target-population type I 224 

error, whereas RE2-based strategies were more prone to inflation, particularly when auxiliary 225 

or bulk-tissue signals were stronger (Supplementary Fig. 5, Supplementary Fig. 6). These 226 

results indicate that covariance-based borrowing in traceC/traceCB does not spuriously convert 227

## Discussion 505 

In this paper, we present traceCB, a statistical framework that addresses the challenge of 506 

underpowered ct-eQTL mapping in underrepresented ancestries. By integrating summary 507 

statistics from single-cell and bulk-tissue eQTL studies across diverse populations, traceCB 508 

leverages the statistical power of large European ancestry cohorts to enhance discovery in 509 

target populations such as East Asian and African. In contrast to conventional meta-analysis 510 

approaches (for example, RE2) that often assume homogeneous effects across studies, traceCB 511

Our traceCB framework needs further investigation in the following directions. First, our 518 

analyses have been mainly focused on the blood cell types. Although the single-cell eQTL 519 

data have been collected in other human tissues, such as brain [44, 45] and liver [46] for 520 

the European ancestry, they remain unavailable in the under-represented populations. This 521 

data unavailability restricted the application of traceCB to a more comprehensive analyses of 522 

disease-related cell types. With the accumulating single-cell eQTL data resources, we expect 523 

that traceCB will be important for translating these resources into cellular-level biological 524 

insight and more equitable applications in human genetics. 525 

Second, the performance of our method depends on the availability and quality of auxil-526 

iary data. The eQTLGen study includes a fraction of non-European samples; for example, 527 

African ancestry samples account for 8.8% of the overall samples [6]. Despite this, we found 528 

that traceCB is robust to this contamination in our applications, as evidenced by the high 529 

replication rate of traceCB’s output. Besides, its effectiveness naturally scales with the sample 530 

size, technical quality and methodological consistency of the contributing studies. Continued 531 

generation of large, well-characterized single-cell and bulk eQTL resources from ancestrally 532 

homogeneous and admixed cohorts will be important for realizing the full potential of this 533 

type of integrative approach. 534 

Third, traceCB requires accurate estimation of gene-level heritability and co-heritability 535 

parameters, which can be difficult in settings with limited sample sizes and few SNPs at indi-536 

vidual loci. We also conducted additional simulations where the true heritabilities and genetic 537 

correlation parameters were known (Supplementary Fig. 2). With the true parameters, traceC 538 

achieved a statistical power nearly identical to RE2 while maintaining strict type I error control. 539 

Notably, the power advantage of traceCB over traceC was even more pronounced when the 540 

true parameters were used, underscoring the potential for further performance improvements 541 

through more accurate parameter estimation techniques in future work. Developing more ro-542

cell-type effects {βk2 : k̸ = c} and their proportions are not separately identifiable without ad-609 

ditional assumptions or external cell-type-specific reference data. Therefore, traceCB focuses 610 

on estimating the target cell-type effect while treating the combined non-target contribution 611 

as a background component. 612 

In the summary-level GMM formulation, the bulk-tissue association statistic is informative 613 

for the target ct-eQTL only through the target-cell component, scaled by the mean target cell-614 

type proportion and linked to the target-population effect through the trans-ancestry covari-615 

ance between βc1 and βc2. The aggregate non-target component contributes to the variability 616 

of the tissue-level statistic but is not separately identified or used as direct evidence for the 617 

target cell-type effect. This modeling choice keeps the summary-level estimator identifiable 618 

and scalable, while acknowledging that unresolved non-target cell-type effects may reduce the 619 

amount of useful information that can be borrowed from bulk tissue. 620
