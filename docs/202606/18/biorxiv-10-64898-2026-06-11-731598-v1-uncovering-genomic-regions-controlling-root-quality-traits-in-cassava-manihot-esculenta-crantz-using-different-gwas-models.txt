Abstract 11 

Cassava is a major staple crop in tropical regions, and improving its root nutritional quality, 12 particularly carotenoid and dry matter content (DMC), remains a central breeding goal. To 13 elucidate the genetic basis of these traits by locating genomic regions associated with them, we 14 analyzed 3,043 cassava clones from the Brazilian Agricultural Research Corporation (Embrapa) 15 breeding program, phenotyped across 188 multi-environment trials conducted from 2011 to 2022 16 in Brazil. All clones were genotyped using Genotyping-by-Sequencing (27,045 Single Nucleotide 17 Polymorphism - SNPs) and Diversity Arrays Technology - DArTseq (25,923 SNPs). Trait values 18 were estimated using a two-stage mixed model to obtain deregressed BLUPs (Best Linear 19 Unbiased Predictions), and genome-wide association analyses were performed using both the 20 Mixed Linear Model (MLM) and Multi-Locus Mixed Model (MLMM). We detected six 21 significant SNPs consistently associated with carotenoid content and DMC after Bonferroni 22 correction. These SNPs mapped to six candidate genes involved in pathways relevant to root 23 physiology, including Abscisic Acid ABA-related signaling, hydrolase activity affecting 24 carotenoid conversion, fatty-acid biosynthesis within plastids, cell-wall remodeling, and glycolytic 25 energy metabolism. The loci jointly explained 75.56 % of the phenotypic variance for carotenoids 26 and 76.23 % for DMC, with individual SNP effects ranging from ~17 % to ~42 % PVE (Proportion 27 of Variance Explained). Broad-sense heritability was H2 = 0.78 for carotenoids and H² = 0.34 for 28 DMC, confirming substantial genetic control and suitability for molecular breeding. Haplotype 29 analyses revealed four superior haplotypes for carotenoids and one key haplotype for DMC, each 30 showing significantly higher trait values compared with other allelic combinations. These 31 haplotypes represent promising targets for marker-assisted selection and genomic selection, with 32 direct applicability for accelerating genetic gain in elite breeding populations. The results provide 33 actionable genomic resources for breeding programs aiming to develop biofortified and high-root 34 quality cultivars and establish a foundation for future multi-omics and functional validation 35 studies. 36

Cassava is recognized as the fourth most important staple food worldwide, providing 42 carbohydrates, calories, calcium, and vitamins A, B and C (Kim & Iida, 2023). It serves as a dietary 43 foundation for approximately 1 billion people across different continents. This adaptable crop 44 demonstrates resilience to climate variability and the ability to thrive in marginal soils, making it 45 a significant contributor to food security, industry, and economic development, particularly for 46 rural communities (Forkum et al., 2025). Approximately 50 % of the world's cassava production 47 is concentrated in Africa, while Asia accounts for 38%, and Brazil ranks fifth with 5 % of the 48 global output (Borku et al., 2025). Fresh cassava roots contain a high-water content of 70 %, along 49 with 24 % starch, 2 % fiber, and 1 % protein. Other minerals such as iron, zinc, and provitamin A 50 make up another 3 % of the roots. Starch is the predominant metabolite in cassava roots, accounting 51 for 85 % of their dry weight, and it serves as a valuable raw material for human consumption, 52 animal products, and industries producing biofuels and other derived products (Forkum et al., 53 2025). 54 55 Cassava is a great source of carbohydrates, but it has a low content of micronutrients. In human 56 nutrition, vitamin A is crucial for vision and for the synthesis and differentiation of epithelial cells 57 (Forkum et al., 2025). In developing countries, cassava is a staple food, making it difficult for 58 people to meet their vitamin A requirements, particularly for pregnant women and children. This 59 has become a public health issue (Qi et al., 2025). Increasing the carotenoid content in cassava 60 roots is a key strategy for enhancing the biofortification of cassava, which aligns with the goals of 61 both public health initiatives in developing countries and breeding programs (Saba et al., 2024). 62 Carotenoid synthesis in plants is regulated by various hormones, such as jasmonic acid, salicylic 63 acid, and abscisic acid (ABA). These hormones increase the accumulation of these pigments in the 64 roots. Additionally, plastid division plays a significant role in the synthesis and accumulation of 65 carotenoids (Sun et al., 2022). Respectively, with dry matter content, this is more strongly related 66 to starch accumulation; the physiological processes that are important parts are the enzymes 67 involved in the conversion of glucose, protein, and starch synthesis through sucrose synthase and 68 cell wall-bound enzymes (Jia et al., 2025). 69 70 Considerable efforts have been made to develop cassava cultivars with yellow-orange flesh that 71 are rich in β-carotene and provitamin A, as well as to enhance the dry matter content in elite 72 cultivars. However, achieving genetic gains in these traits is challenging due to several factors, 73 including high environmental variability, negative correlations between traits, polygenic 74 inheritance, and complex physiological pathways. This highlights the necessity of complementing 75 traditional breeding methods with genomic-assisted breeding tools to expedite the selection 76 process and create cultivars that exhibit high dry matter content and elevated carotenoid levels. 77 Recent studies in cassava, including research in proteomics and metabolomics (Olayide et al., 78 2023), transcriptomics (Xiao et al., 2021), and genomics of African cultivars (Villwock et al., 79 2025) (Rabbi et al., 2017), as well as investigations into phenotypic diversity in Brazilian 80 germplasm (de Carvalho et al., 2022), have provided the cassava community with a deeper 81 understanding of the genetic foundations of these traits. Furthermore, these studies elucidate the 82 interactions among different metabolic pathways that influence the enhancement of these traits in 83 specific cassava populations. Currently, there are no efficient markers for the Brazilian population 84 that can be applied in routine breeding programs, and most of the GWAS (Genome-Wide 85

While these studies have identified significant regions on chromosomes one and ten, 87 limitations regarding population structure have hindered the application of these markers within 88 Brazilian germplasm. The integration of high carotenoid content and dry matter into breeding 89 pipelines entails considerable costs and time investments associated with morphological 90 evaluations and methods for determining dry matter content. Furthermore, the chemical procedures 91 required to assess carotenoid content impose significant financial burdens. This situation presents 92 an opportunity to analyze this information and select traits for molecular breeding, with the aim of 93 enhancing carotenoid content in cassava roots. By pursuing this strategy, it is possible to accelerate 94 processes, reduce costs, and improve efficiency within breeding program pipelines through the 95 application of molecular markers. 96 97 GWAS leverages various natural and breeding populations, eliminating the need for a segregating 98 population as seen in linkage mapping. GWAS can simultaneously detect multi-allelic variations 99 and rare, small genetic effects, providing high-resolution insights into historical recombination 100 events within the studied populations (Cantor et al., 2010). These models incorporate population 101 structure through principal components and kinship matrices, along with covariates (Yu et al., 102 2006) . This helps reduce false positives and allows the detection of major quantitative SNPs 103 (single nucleotide polymorphisms) that might explain a significant portion of the phenotypic 104 variation of traits (Briollais et al., 2016). Combining different GWAS models with haplotype-105 based analysis boosts the power of SNP detection and candidate gene identification. 106 107 In this study, we systematically investigated the genetic architecture of dry matter and carotenoids 108 in cassava by locating genomic regions associated with them. We analyzed a population of 3,043 109 cassava clones, using a historical dataset to evaluate both dry matter content and carotenoid 110 content. A GWAS was conducted using 27,045 SNPs from genotyping by sequencing and 26,000 111 SNPs from Diversity Arrays Technology datasets. The objectives of this study were as follows: 112 (1) to characterize the phenotypic variation and population structure of the sample; (2) to determine 113 genetic parameters, such as linkage disequilibrium, heritability, and correlations between traits; 114 (3) to implement GWAS and haplotype analysis to gain insights into the genetic architecture of 115 carotenoids and dry matter within the panel; and (4) to identify significant polymorphisms 116 associated with candidate genes and superior haplotypes. The findings offer valuable tools for 117 accelerating the breeding process aimed at biofortification and enhanced productivity in cassava 118 roots, while also contributing to our understanding of the genetic architecture of these traits. 119

Genome-wide association study of root quality traits 229 

230 

A GWAS analysis was conducted using 27,045 SNPs from GBS and 25,923 SNPs from DArTseq 231 across 3,043 clones with DBLUP values for DMC and carotenoid content. Different statistical 232 models were employed to enhance power and reduce false positives, including the Multi-locus 233 Mixed Model (MLMM), which adjusts for cofactors associated with markers through forward and 234 backward stepwise regression in a mixed model. The Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) is used 235 to determine the optimal number of cofactors. In the forward step, SNPs are added as cofactors to 236 prove if they improve the model. Taking a backward step removes cofactors that are not significant 237 (Segura et al., 2012). Additionally, False Discovery Rate (FDR) was utilized to improve the control 238 of Type I and Type II errors, proving effective with family-based and structured association 239 samples (Yu et al., 2006). Both models were implemented in Genome Association and Prediction 240 Integrated Tool (GAPIT) version 3 R package (J. Wang & Zhang, 2021) taking into account both 241 the kinship K (VanRaden, 2008) and population structure Q matrices, For multiple testing 242 correction, the Bonferroni procedure was applied with a significance level of α = 0.05 was used to 243 determine the threshold p-value (P=0.05/n, where n = number of SNP), the markers were 244 considered significant when p-value was less than 1.848 x 10 -6 . The results were visualized using 245 Manhattan plots, with a nominal threshold set at −log10(p-value) = 5.5, and quantile-quantile (Q-246 Q) plots were created to evaluate statistical inflation and model fit. Linkage disequilibrium (LD) 247 was estimated through pairwise correlation coefficients (r²) to identify candidate regions, and loci 248 with −log10(p) > 3.6 (p < 2.51 × 10⁻⁴) were selected for further investigation. Results 268 

Phenotypic variation, descriptive statistics and correlation 269 

270 

We investigated phenotypic variation in a dataset that included dry matter content and carotenoid 271 content, evaluated using a visual scale. The dry matter content in the studied population ranged 272 from 16.11 to 50.98 %, with a mean of 34.65 %. Most clones displayed a rating of 1 (79.29 %), 273 while a smaller proportion received ratings of 2 (13.94 %), 3 (6.57 %), and 4 (0.1 %) for carotenoid 274 content. Overall, the population ranged between ratings of 1 and 4. Discussion 435 

436 

Dry matter and carotenoid content are key traits for root quality cassava, industrial processing, and 437 

biofortification initiatives (Saba et al., 2024). These two traits have certain limitations regarding 438 

their evaluation methods. For carotenoids, a visual scale is typically used because it is a quick and 439 

cost -effective method for analyzing a large number of samples (Jaramillo et al., 2018) . However, 440 

this approach is subjective and can vary depending on the evaluator and the specific portion of the 441 

sample being assessed. Conversely, the specific gravity method utilized to assess dry matter lacks 442 

accuracy when applied across diverse genotypes, varying evaluation timelines post -harvest, and 443 

differing environmental conditions (Silva et al., 2023) . Collectively, these limitations emphasize 444 

the need for a more comprehensive understanding of the genetic architecture that underlies these 445 

traits , particularly the genomic regions involved, the associated genes, and the allelic combinations 446 

that contribute to phenotypic expression. This is essential for designing more efficient selection 447 

strategies in cassava breeding. In this study, integrating multi -year phenotypic data with high -448 

density SNP genotyping enabled the identification of loci, candidate genes, and haplotype patterns 449 

associated with DMC and carotenoids in a large breeding panel representative of Brazilian 450 

germplasm. 451 

Phenotypic variation, genetic variance, and trait correlations 452 

453 

Substantial phenotypic variation and high genotypic variance were observed for both traits, 454 

demonstrating that the population holds sufficient diversity for effective genome -wide association 455 

analyses. Dry matter content exhibited a wide quantitative range, whereas carotenoid levels, 456 

evaluated via visual score, showed narrower phenotypic dispersion but high genotypic signal. The 457 

moderate negative correlation between DMC and carotenoids (r = – 0.10) aligns with prior findings 458 

in African germplasm (Villwock et al., 2025) , confirming that the relationship between starch 459 

accumulation and carotenoid biosynthesis is weak. Thus, simultaneous genetic improvement of 460 

both traits appears feasible, with minimal antagonistic pleiotropy. For breeding programs, this is 461 

an advantageous scenario, as it suggests that gene pyramiding for DMC and carotenoids can be 462 

pursued without substantial risk of unfavorable interactions between alleles influencing the two 463 

traits. 464 

465 

In the present study, we observed significant genotypic variance and a wide range of phenotypic 466 

variation for DMC . We found strong genetic variance for carotenoids associated with different 467 

alleles in the population. This indicates that the population possesses sufficient genetic diversity 468 

to generate robust GWAS results. These findings are in agreement with earlier studies involving 469 

African germplasm (Rabbi et al., 2017) and Brazilian germplasm (Sampaio et al., 2024), both of 470 

which similarly report substantial genetic variation within their evaluated populations. 471 

472 

Heritability estimates reinforce the potential for high genetic gain. Broad -sense heritability was 473 

moderate for DMC (H² = 0.34) and high for carotenoids content (H² = 0.78), reflecting a high 474 

genetic basis, particularly for carotenoids, and favorable prospects for selection. These values 475 

differ slightly from those described by Villwock et al. (2025), who reported H² = 0.49 for 476 

carotenoids and H² = 0.50 for DMC in African germplasm, but remain within the expected range 477 

for clonally propagated crops evaluated across multiple environments. 478 Linkage disequilibrium, population structure, and GWAS performance 479 

480 

LD decay is a key parameter influencing the resolution and statistical power of genome -wide 481 

association studies (Briollais et al., 2016) . LD patterns reflect underlying evolutionary and 482 

demographic forces —including recombination rates, selection, and population admixture, which 483 

collectively shape the extent to which alleles at different loci are inherited together (Cantor et al., 484 

2010) . In this study, LD decay occurred rapidly within ~1 kb, with r² declining from 0.58 to 485 

background levels. This rapid decay indicates a high degree of historical recombination and 486 

substantial genetic diversity within the breeding population, consistent with repeated cycles of 487 

selection and recombination incorporated into Embrapa’s cassava breeding pipeline. Such LD 488 

patterns provide favorable conditions for fine -mapping loci and increase the precision of GWAS 489 

interpretation. 490 

491 

Population structure analysis revealed two major subgroups within the panel; one consisting of 492 

historical Embrapa germplasm and the other representing clones derived from genomic selection 493 

cycles. Accounting for this structure was essential for reducing spurious associations, as reflected 494 

by the well -aligned Q –Q plots and clean Manhattan plot profiles. The genetic distinction between 495 

these two groups suggests that both historical diversity and more recently recombined backgrounds 496 

contribute to the observed variation, reinforcing the value of this panel for association mapping. 497 

498 

The GWAS analysis results provide a robust number of SNPs detected across the genome for both 499 

traits, although with a high value of PVE. These SNPs result from the different models used, 500 

suggesting that each model and the genetic algorithms implemented allow better results and a 501 

larger number of SNPs detected from the datasets. The MLMM recovered a greater number of 502 

associations than MLM, while both models effectively controlled inflation and reduced false 503 

positives. For dry matter content, both MLMM and MLM consistently detected significant SNPs 504 

on chromosomes 1 and 10. For carotenoids, MLMM identified four SNPs across chromosomes 1, 505 

4, and 10, whereas MLM detected three SNPs on the same chromosomes (Supplementary Table 506 

S1). These findings are consistent with earlier GWAS studies in African cassava germplasm 507 

(Rabbi et al., 2017; Villwock et al., 2025), which also reported significant associations on 508 

chromosomes 1 and 10 for both carotenoids and dry matter content. The convergence of results 509 

across diverse geographic backgrounds suggests that the genetic architecture of these traits may 510 

be conserved in global cassava populations. 511 

Candidate gene discovery 512 

513 

GWAS have become a powerful approach for identifying genomic regions and putative causal 514 

genes underlying key agronomic traits in cassava. In this study, GWAS proved highly effective 515 

for detecting loci associated with two physiologically complex traits, carotenoid and dry matter 516 

content, within a genetically diverse breeding population. Consistent with the observed rapid LD 517 

decay, the high recombination rate and diversity within the panel enabled high -resolution mapping. 518 

Overall, six significant SNPs and six candidate genes were identified as strongly associated with 519 

variation in carotenoids and dry matter content. 520 

521 For carotenoids, four genes were associated with SNPs that together explained 75.56 % of the 522 

phenotypic variance. On chromosome 1, the SNP S01_22503611 mapped to Manes.01G102700 ,523 

annotated as a phosphatidylinositol -specific phospholipase C. Enzymes in this class participate in 524 

lipid -associated signaling cascades and are connected to transcriptional regulation involving ABA 525 

and Ca²⁺ —pathways broadly implicated in carotenoid biosynthesis and plastid development 526 

(Drapal et al., 2024) . Elevated ABA signaling in high -carotenoid cassava genotypes was also 527 

reported by Olayide et al. (2023), further supporting the importance of this regulatory network. 528 

Another SNP on chromosome 1, S01_23979768, was located near Manes.01G121900 , a gene 529 

annotated with protein kinase activity. Protein kinases are known regulators of ABA -mediated 530 

signaling processes and have been linked to metabolic adjustments associated with carotenoid 531 

accumulation (Bender & Zipfel, 2023) . Together, these two genes work to activate and regulate 532 

ABA functions during the accumulation of carotenoids. This interaction is also documented by 533 

(Xiao et al., 2021) , who emphasize the relationship between kinase activity and the ABA signaling 534 

pathway, which influences β-carotene accumulation in cassava roots. 535 

536 

The SNP S01_25281512 mapped to Manes.01G139400 , annotated with hydrolase activity. 537 

Hydrolases, along with desaturases and cyclases, participate in enzymatic steps that influence flux 538 

through the carotenoid pathway, particularly those related to the conversion of lycopene to β-539 

carotene, a relationship also emphasized by transcriptomic evidence in Olayide et al. (2023). On 540 

chromosome 10, SNP S10_25598238 was found within Manes.10G144300 , which encodes D -3-541 

hydroxyoctanoyl -[acyl carrier protein] dehydratase, a key enzyme in the fatty acid synthase II 542 

complex (Drapal et al., 2024). Fatty -acid biosynthesis is essential for the formation and 543 

maintenance of plastid membranes and lipid -rich structures, which serve as sites for carotenoid 544 

sequestration. Genes associated with this enzymatic complex were also reported to be highly 545 

expressed in high -carotenoid cassava genotypes (Olayide et al., 2023) .546 

547 

For dry matter content, two candidate genes were identified, collectively explaining 76.23 % of 548 

the phenotypic variation. The SNP S01_31462045 on chromosome 1 was located within 549 

Manes.01G231200 , a gene involved in homogalacturonan degradation and cell wall modification. 550 

This gene has been previously linked to cell wall remodeling processes associated with starch 551 

accumulation and root development (Jia et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2016). The second SNP 552 

associated with dry matter, S10_7304197 on chromosome 10, mapped to Manes.10G058150 ,553 

which encodes a protein annotated with iron ion binding and ATP/GTP binding activity. Such 554 

proteins participate in glycolytic pathways and energy mobilization and have been reported as 555 

highly expressed during tuberous root formation in cassava (Wang et al., 2016) supporting their 556 

role in processes associated with biomass and starch deposition. Together, these candidate genes 557 

represent biologically relevant pathways connected to carotenoid metabolism, plastid function, 558 

carbohydrate deposition, and root development. Their identification provides a strong foundation 559 

for future functional validation and incorporation into marker -assisted and genomic selection 560 

pipelines. 561 

Haplotype analysis and implications for cassava breeding 562 

563 

Haplotype analysis provided additional resolution into the allelic variation and functional 564 

polymorphisms underlying carotenoid accumulation and dry matter content in Brazilian cassava 565 

germplasm. For carotenoids, the gene Manes.01G102700 exhibited a nonsynonymous substitution 566 [T/C] that changes methionine to isoleucine. Individuals carrying the C allele showed higher 567 

carotenoid content (Figure 4F). Although the biochemical implications of this substitution require 568 

validation, isoleucine substitutions have previously been associated with altered enzyme activity 569 

in lipid -associated signaling pathways (Ohmura et al., 2001), which are known to influence 570 

carotenoid regulation. For Manes.01G139400 , located in a noncoding region, the [T/C] allelic 571 

variation revealed that clones carrying the T allele displayed higher carotenoid accumulation 572 

(Figure 4F), suggesting potential effects on regulatory or cis -acting elements. The SNP in 573 

Manes.01G092900 the [G/T] variant also produced a nonsynonymous substitution, replacing 574 

methionine with isoleucine. Clones carrying the T allele exhibited higher carotenoid levels (Figure 575 

4F), consistent with the hypothesis that allelic variation at this locus may affect kinase -mediated 576 

signaling cascades associated with carotenoid biosynthesis (Ohmura et al., 2001). On chromosome 577 

10, the SNP within Manes.10G117500 resulted in a synonymous change. Although this 578 

substitution does not alter the encoded amino acid, individuals carrying the C allele consistently 579 

showed higher carotenoid content (Fig ure . 4F). This pattern suggests that regulatory or structural 580 

factors, rather than coding variation, may influence trait expression at this locus. 581 

582 

For dry matter content, Manes.01G231200 carried a nonsynonymous substitution [T/C] that 583 

replaces leucine with lysine, and individuals with the C allele exhibited higher DMC values (Figure 584 

5D). Lysine -bearing variants have been associated with altered protein structural interactions, 585 

particularly in cell -wall modification processes that contribute to root developmental dynamics 586 

(Yang et al., 2020) . On the other hand, Manes.10G055500 has a synonymous mutation [T/C] 587 

affecting Leucine and the reference allele (T) was associated with increased dry matter content 588 

(Figure 5E). 589 

590 

Across the genome, favorable allelic combinations were organized into distinct haplotypes. For 591 

carotenoids, the haplotypes Hap 9 [TTCC], Hap 11 [ATCC], and Hap 12 [AGCC] exhibited 592 

superior accumulation. For dry matter content, the haplotype Hap 3 [CT], combining favorable 593 

alleles from both identified loci, showed the most advantageous performance. These results 594 

indicate that multi -locus haplotypes capturing combinations of favorable alleles provide more 595 

reliable markers for trait improvement than individual SNPs. It is essential to note that certain 596 

SNPs (single nucleotide polymorphisms) have a low minor allele frequency (MAF) in the 597 

population. Even in the case of superior haplotypes, rare and low -frequency variants contribute 598 

significantly to genetic variance. Some studies indicate that non -synonymous variants tend to have 599 

lower MAF, and these kinds of variants can have important positive or negative effects on trait 600 

expression (Floriani & Lipka, 2025). Including these variants in genomic prediction enhances the 601 

reliability of the predictions. This is a crucial consideration because rare alleles are often associated 602 

with significant phenotypic variation. If the model misinterprets these alleles and their effects as 603 

not statistically significant, it could lose valuable information for genomic prediction. Therefore, 604 

it is essential to validate these SNPs in either the training or validation populations in future studies. 605 

The identification of these haplotypes represents an important step toward deploying haplotype -606 

based selection strategies in cassava breeding. Because these allelic combinations integrate 607 

information across tightly linked loci, they offer improved predictive accuracy for both marker -608 

assisted selection and genomic selection. In addition, these superior haplotypes can guide the 609 

selection of parental genotypes for hybridization and backcrossing, facilitating the introgression 610 

of favorable alleles into future breeding populations. Incorporating haplotype -level information 611 may therefore accelerate genetic gain for carotenoid biofortification and improved dry matter 612 

content in cassava. 613 

Functional validation and future research directions 614 

615 

GWAS are now widely recognized as an effective approach for dissecting complex quantitative 616 

traits in plants, particularly when complemented by haplotype -based analyses. Haplotype 617 

information captures historical recombination patterns, including insertions, deletions, and copy 618 

number variation, thereby improving the resolution of genomic signals and enhancing the 619 

predictive ability of genomic selection models (Briollais et al., 2016; Hamazaki & Iwata, 2020). 620 

This makes haplotype -based discovery especially valuable for breeding programs that rely on 621 

tagging and tracking favorable genetic variants to accelerate the development of elite cultivars. In 622 

this study, integrating GWAS with haplotype analysis using a large, multi -year dataset from the 623 

Embrapa breeding program enabled the identification of six candidate genes and their associated 624 

SNPs for carotenoid accumulation and dry matter content in cassava. The characterization of 625 

favorable haplotypes, specifically [TTCC], [ATCC], [AGCC] for carotenoids and [CT] for dry 626 

matter, provides a set of high -value genomic targets that can enhance the accuracy of GS models 627 

and inform strategic allelic pyramiding. Haplotype -based genomic selection represents a628 

significant advancement in plant breeding, enhancing the prediction of complex traits by utilizing 629 

haplotype blocks. This approach could be a promising strategy for the cassava breeding program 630 

in Brazil. The next steps to implement this method include developing Kompetitive Allele -Specific 631 

PCR (KASP SNP ) markers for the candidate SNPs and validating these markers in independent 632 

populations. Once validated, these SNPs could serve as fixed predictors in the next genomic 633 

selection cycle. 634 

635 

Using the haplotype blocks to build G_SNP and G_HAP matrices for Bayesian or weighted 636 

GBLUP models. In previous research, Lin et al.,(2024) applied haplotype blocks in genomic 637 

selection models for yield traits in maize and found that using haplotypes improved prediction 638 

accuracy for (GS). Similarly, Difabachew et al., (2023) implemented models using haplotype 639 

blocks for 11 traits in winter wheat and explained the advantages of using haplotype blocks instead 640 

of individual SNPs, highlighting how this strategy can enhance prediction accuracy in this cultivar. 641 

Furthermore, Yu et al., (2006) utilized three LD blocks with KASP molecular markers. The 642 

incorporation of these superior haplotypes enabled the breeding program to address low -643 

temperature stress in the new genomic selection population derived from haplotype -based GS. 644 

Translating these findings into operational breeding can be facilitated through the development of 645 

allele -specific, high -throughput SNP assays, enabling rapid and cost -efficient screening of 646 

segregating families. Incorporating these markers into selection pipelines may significantly 647 

accelerate genetic gain for carotenoid and dry matter content. 648 

649 

These discoveries also open opportunities for deeper functional investigation. Experimental 650 

validation of the identified candidate genes will be essential to confirm their roles in trait 651 

expression and to elucidate how specific allelic variants influence transcript and protein behavior. 652 

Genome editing technologies such as CRISPR –Cas 9 systems offer a compelling toolset for 653 

generating targeted knockouts, allelic replacements, or overexpression lines to validate causality 654 

and characterize genotype –phenotype relationships ( Lin & Musunuru, 2018) .655 

656 Post -GWAS analyses utilizing omics data represent a compelling approach for future research 657 

aimed at accurately identifying causal variants associated with complex traits. By integrating 658 

multidimensional datasets, such as transcriptome -wide association studies (TWAS), researchers 659 

are able to provide evidence of the relationship between quantitative trait locus (QTL) expression 660 

and phenotypic outcomes. Additionally, metabolome (mGWAS) and proteome -wide association 661 

studies (PWAS) present further valuable opportunities for exploration. These methodologies yield 662 

a more comprehensive understanding of the molecular networks that underpin these traits, 663 

highlighting how protein products interact within superior genotypes to enhance carotenoid levels 664 

and improve dry matter production. Research focusing on gene -metabolite -trait associations has 665 

been pursued by many scientists studying cassava and other crops. For instance, Ding et al., (2023) 666 

combined metabolic and phenotypic genome -wide association studies to investigate the genetic 667 

architecture of metabolites produced in cassava storage roots. Luo et al., (2023) employed 668 

mGWAS and multi -omic functional analyses to explore the mechanisms involved in anthocyanin 669 

biosynthesis in cassava leaves. Siriwan et al., (2023) used proteomic and metabolomic analyses to 670 

elucidate the mechanisms of disease resistance to mosaic virus disease, uncovering nine proteins 671 

and metabolites associated with plant resistance. Parallel transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses 672 

of edited or contrasting haplotype carriers would provide further insight into the regulatory 673 

networks and biochemical pathways governing carotenoid accumulation and dry matter 674 

deposition. Such integrative functional genomics approaches will be crucial to refine gene targets, 675 

verify biological mechanisms, and optimize the deployment of favorable alleles within cassava 676 

improvement programs. 677 

Conclusion 678 

679 

This study provides a comprehensive characterization of the genetic architecture underlying 680 

carotenoid accumulation and dry matter content in cassava by integrating GWAS with haplotype 681 

analysis using an extensive panel of clones from the Embrapa breeding program. This integrative 682 

approach led to the identification of six trait -associated SNPs and six biologically compelling 683 

candidate genes. Collectively, these loci account for a large proportion of the phenotypic 684 

variance —75.56 % for carotenoid content and 76.23 % for dry matter content —demonstrating the 685 

high genetic control and clear marker –trait relationships present within the Brazilian germplasm 686 

and highlighting their strong potential as robust targets for marker development and genomic -687 

enabled breeding. 688 

689 

The annotated candidate genes encode products central to the biochemical pathways governing 690 

these traits. For carotenoids, the implicated genes participate in ABA -related signaling, hydrolase 691 

activity essential for lycopene –β-carotene conversion, and fatty -acid biosynthesis, which 692 

collectively support plastid development and carotenoid deposition. For dry matter content, the 693 

candidate genes are associated with cell wall modification, a process linked to starch accessibility 694 

and storage, and glycolytic activity, which contributes to root swelling and biomass accumulation. 695 

These functional roles are consistent with the GWAS signals, and the haplotype -phenotype 696 

patterns observed across the population. 697 

698 

The combined use of GWAS and haplotype -based analysis enabled the identification of four 699 

superior haplotypes carrying favorable alleles for carotenoids and one optimal haplotype for dry 700 

matter. These haplotypes represent valuable genetic assets for cassava improvement, providing 701 breeders with high -confidence, trait -linked markers that can be integrated into genomic selection 702 

and marker -assisted selection pipelines. Their deployment is expected to enhance prediction 703 

accuracy, accelerate allele pyramiding, and increase the efficiency of selecting high -carotenoid 704 

and high -dry -matter genotypes. Overall, the results provide a n empirical framework for the 705 

development of biofortified and high -quality cassava cultivars. They also establish a foundation 706 

for future functional studies aimed at validating the roles of the identified genes and clarifying the 707 

molecular mechanisms that drive superior performance in specific haplotype classes. Future 708 

research will prioritize the development of marker assays utilizing KASP SNPs technology, 709 

followed by validation in independent populations. Furthermore, it is essential to perform 710 

biological validation of these traits through advanced technologies such as CRISPR -Cas9, 711 

transcriptomics, metabolomics, and proteomics. This comprehensive approach will enhance our 712 

understanding of the interplay between genes and their protein products, leading to a more 713 

profound insight into carotenoid and dry matter production in elite genotypes . This work therefore 714 

contributes both actionable breeding tools and new genetic insights that will support long -term 715 

genetic gains in cassava improvement programs. 716
