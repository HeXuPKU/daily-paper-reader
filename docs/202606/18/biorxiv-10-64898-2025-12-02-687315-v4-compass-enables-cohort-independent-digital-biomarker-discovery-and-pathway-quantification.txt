Abstract 13 

Reproducible and clinically transferable quantification of pathway activity remains a major barrier in 14 

precision medicine, where biomarker performance often depends on cohort composition and 15 

normalization strategies. Here, we introduce COMPASS (COMPosite Activity Scoring System), a16 

deterministic threshold-based framework that converts gene expression into quantitative pathway 17 

activity scores without reliance on reference cohorts. COMPASS derives gene-specific activation 18 

thresholds directly from data, standardizes deviations from thresholds, and integrates directionally 19 

opposing genes into a single composite score. This enabled transparent activity scoring, statistical 20 

comparisons, and survival analyses without coding. Across diverse biological and clinical datasets, 21 

COMPASS robustly quantified cellular states, benchmarked the humanness and disease relevance of 22 

new approach methodologies, and stratified outcomes. Compared to GSVA and ssGSEA, COMPASS 23 

demonstrated greater consistency across datasets and improved robustness in bootstrap analyses, 24 

particularly for bidirectional programs, including regulatory-approved sepsis gene signatures. 25 

COMPASS therefore addresses a critical unmet need for exact, interpretable, and clinically transferable 26 

biomarker discovery and outcome modeling across diverse biological and clinical settings. 27

Introduction 28 

The rapid expansion of transcriptomic profiling, artificial intelligence/machine learning (AI/ML), and large-scale 29 

biomarker discovery efforts has led to an explosion of gene signatures proposed to define cellular states, predict 30 

outcomes, and guide therapeutic decisions. Yet, translating these signatures into clinically reliable biomarkers 31 

remains challenging because most current pathway-scoring approaches quantify relative enrichment rather than 32 

absolute biological activity. Widely used enrichment-based methods (e.g., GSEA 1, GSVA 2, ssGSEA 3, PLAGE 4,33 

AUCell 5, and VAM 6) rely on evolving ontologies, cohort-dependent normalization, and permutation-based 34 

statistics, introducing variability across datasets, platforms, and populations and limiting reproducibility, 35 

interpretability, and clinical transferability 7-9 (Fig. 1a -left ). These limitations become particularly important for 36 

bidirectional gene programs, where simultaneous induction and repression of distinct genes encode biologically 37 

meaningful states that may be obscured when treated as undirected gene sets. 38 

Here, we introduce COMPASS (COMPosite Activity Scoring System) , a deterministic, ontology-free, 39 

and web-based framework that quantifies gene-set activity directly from expression data ( Fig. 1a -middle ). 40 

COMPASS derives gene-specific activation thresholds, standardizes deviations from these boundaries, and 41 

integrates oppositely regulated genes into a single composite score using closed-form logic, enabling stable, 42 

interpretable, and clinically transferable digital biomarkers at single-sample resolution. 43

Results 45 

A deterministic, threshold-based framework for pathway activity quantification 46 

COMPASS operationalizes a threshold-based view of biological systems, in which cellular states emerge from 47 

transitions (high vs low) across expression boundaries [expanded here 10 ]. Modeled for long using the Bo olean 48 

Network Explorer (BoNE 11 ) and formalized recently as AXIOM-Bio 10 (Abstract eXpression Inference 49 

and Ontology-free Modeling for Biological Logic and Disease ), this framework for creating foundational models 50 

of disease continuum states has demonstrated reproducibility across species and diseases 10, 12 . However, 51 

BoNE’s computational complexity has limited accessibility. By embedding this logic in an accessible and intuitive 52 

web platform (https://compass.precsn.com/), COMPASS enables users to interpret raw expression data using 53 

gene sets as deterministic digital biomarkers with minimal technical overhead, bridging biological reasoning, 54 

computational rigor, and translational relevance ( Fig. 1a -right ). 55 

Unlike enrichment-based methods (GSEA 1, GSVA 2, ssGSEA 3, PLAGE 4, AUCell 5, and VAM 6) that depend 56 

on ranked gene lists and random permutations, COMPASS deterministically measures biological activity without 57 

reference to predefined ontologies. In contrast to matrix-factorization or deep-learning models such as PCA, ICA, 58 

NMF, MOFA ⁺, or variational autoencoders 13, 14 ), COMPASS avoids latent dimensions, retaining interpretability 59 

while eliminating stochastic variation. 60 

Beyond classification and pathway quantification, translational applications require linking molecular 61 

measurements to clinical outcomes. Because COMPASS generates continuous, direction-aware composite 62 

scores of any gene set at the sample level, these scores can be directly used to stratify samples into high and 63 

low groups for survival analyses, including Kaplan–Meier analysis and Cox proportional hazards modeling. This 64 

enables a single digital biomarker to support classification, cross-cohort benchmarking, and outcome prediction 65 

within a unified deterministic framework. Importantly, because thresholds are derived directly from the data rather 66 

than from evolving ontologies, COMPASS outputs are reproducible over time for a given dataset and gene 67 

signature, and are transferable across platforms, tissues, cohorts, and species. 68 

Mathematical formulation of COMPASS: thresholding, standardization, and aggregation 69 

The COMPASS workflow transforms a raw gene-expression matrix into deterministic, quantitative 70 

measures of pathway activity through a three-step process: (i) thresholding, (ii) standardization, and (iii) 71 

aggregation ( Fig. 1b ; see Methods ). Each step corresponds to a concrete, interpretable biological operation 72 

rather than an abstract statistical transformation. 73 

Briefly, in the thresholding step, COMPASS identifies for every gene its intrinsic expression boundary 74 

based on the given sample set and its inflection point where its expression pattern transitions dramatically from 75 

“off” to “on”. This data-driven threshold, determined automatically by adaptive segmentation algorithms, pinpoints 76 

where biological probability collapses into commitment. The standardization step aligns genes of varying 77

A web-based platform for reproducible, code-free pathway activity scoring 41 

COMPASS is implemented as a web-based analytical platform (https://compass.precsn.com/) with an 42 

intuitive graphical interface ( Fig. 1c; Figure Supplement S1 ). Users upload a tab- or comma-delimited gene 43 

expression matrix (txt, tsv, or csv), optionally include time-to-event metadata, define gene signatures with 44 

directionality, and initiate analysis through a point-and-click workflow. All computations, including thresholding, 45 

standardization, aggregation, and statistical evaluation, are executed automatically in the background, without 46 

the need for pathway databases or coding. Gene-set definitions are fully customizable, with flexibility to assign 47 

directionality weights (+/−). 48 

The platform generates publication-ready outputs, including boxplots for group comparisons 49 

(accompanied by pairwise t-test statistics), ROC–AUC analyses (with associated F and p values), composite 50 

activity tables (containing standardized Z-scores and composite scores for each pathway and sample), and 51 

survival analyses (with hazard ratios and log-rank statistics). Results are downloadable in standard formats 52 

(CSV, PDF). 53 

In terms of accessibility, unlike conventional computational frameworks, COMPASS is designed to be 54 

intuitive and accessible to both computational and non-computational users. Users can define and compare any 55 

number of sample groups directly from their uploaded data (e.g., WT vs KO, responders vs non-responders, 56 

multiple treatment conditions). The interface enables point-and-click selection of genes, pathways, and groups, 57 

generating publication-ready figures without manual scripting ( Fig. S1a-b ). All statistical analyses, such as 58 

composite score calculation, group-wise comparisons, ROC–AUC, survival analyses are performed 59 

automatically. By integrating accessibility with mathematical transparency, COMPASS enables consistent, 60 

sample-level quantification of pathway activity across biological and clinical contexts. 61

resampling. 88 

Table 1 highlights key differences between COMPASS and other enrichment-based gene set scoring 89 

methods. Unlike other approaches, which compute relative, cohort-dependent scores, COMPASS derives gene-90 

specific thresholds and generates absolute, direction-aware activity scores at the sample level. This deterministic 91 

formulation avoids permutations, improves reproducibility, and enables stable integration of opposing gene 92 

signals. In addition, COMPASS supports multi-class and continuous analyses, facilitating consistent pathway 93 

quantification across datasets and biological contexts. 94

Outcome prediction and survival stratification using COMPASS-derived scores 23 

To assess clinical relevance, we analyzed a large multi-cohort sepsis dataset ( GSE310929 ; n = 593 patients) 24 

with time to death annotation using a validated 12-gene sepsis-severity signature 30, 31 (Fig. 5a-b ). As in the case 25 

of the 11-gene diagnostic signature we used earlier ( Fig. 3a ), this 12-gene severity signature was also rigorously 26 

validated in the SEPSIS-SHIELD clinical study 30 before receiving regulatory approval for marketing. Enrichment-27 

based methods, as well as COMPASS showed comparable performance in sample classification ( Fig. 5c-e ); all 28 

underperformed to similar extent. Importantly, COMPASS-derived scores enabled direct stratification of patients 29 

by survival outcome ( Fig. 5f ). Patients with higher composite scores exhibited significantly worse survival 30 

(Hazard ratio [H.R] = 2.32), demonstrating that deterministic, direction-aware activity scores can be extended 31 

from group discrimination to time-to-event analysis. 32 

These results establish that COMPASS provides a unified framework in which pathway activity scores 33 

function as outcome-linked digital biomarkers, enabling consistent transition from classification to survival 34 

modeling within the same analytical system. 35

Discussion 36 

Translation frequently fails when biological signals identified in discovery cohorts cannot be reproducibly 37 

anchored across validation cohorts, preclinical model systems, and patient populations. COMPASS was 38 

designed to address this gap. By explicitly modeling gene directionality and anchoring expression to intrinsic 39 

activation thresholds, COMPASS provides a deterministic framework that improves interpretability, reduces 40 

cohort dependency, and preserves biological meaning across datasets. The benchmarking analyses presented 41 

here position COMPASS alongside existing single-sample scoring methods rather than replacing them. Methods 42 

such as GSVA and ssGSEA remain valuable for relative enrichment analysis. However, as AI/ML-driven 43 

biomarker discovery accelerates and transcriptomic signatures proliferate across biomedical research, a growing 44 

challenge is ensuring that molecular measurements remain stable, allow multi-group comparison, reproducible, 45 

interpretable, and transferable across cohorts, platforms, and clinical settings. By explicitly modeling gene 46 

directionality and anchoring expression to intrinsic thresholds, COMPASS provides a deterministic framework 47 

that improves interpretability and stability. Beyond that, a conceptual advance that is operationalized through 48 

COMPASS, reduces cohort dependency, and preserves biological meaning across datasets. 49 

A key conceptual advance of COMPASS is its ability to integrate opposing biological signals within 50 

composite signatures. This is particularly important for clinically relevant programs in which simultaneous 51 

induction and repression of distinct genes encode disease state, therapeutic response, or risk. Bootstrap 52 

analyses demonstrated reduced variability and greater consistency across cohorts, including superiority for 53 

bidirectional programs and regulatory-approved sepsis gene signatures. 54 

Another key advance of COMPASS is its ability to directly connect pathway activity with clinically 55 

meaningful outcomes. By generating continuous, direction-aware scores at single-sample resolution, COMPASS 56 

enables objective evaluation of biomarkers and therapeutic perturbations across independent cohorts before 57 

prospective testing. The same digital biomarker can support classification, benchmarking, longitudinal 58 

monitoring, survival analyses, and outcome modeling without stochastic inference or intermediate model fitting. 59 

Such portability and reproducibility are increasingly critical as transcriptomic biomarkers move toward regulatory 60 

qualification, decentralized testing, and AI-enabled clinical decision support. 61 

More broadly, COMPASS establishes a generalizable framework for quantitative biomarker science. Its 62 

deterministic design ensures that identical inputs yield identical outputs, enabling reproducibility across datasets, 63 

platforms, and settings, including regulatory contexts. By linking expression, threshold, deviation, and 64 

directionality within a transparent, closed-form system, COMPASS converts gene-expression data into 65 

interpretable and transferable measures of biological state, providing a foundation for consistent and scalable 66 

analysis across model systems and clinical cohorts. 67 

This study has limitations. Because COMPASS derives thresholds directly from input data, its 68 

performance depends on data quality and preprocessing (e.g., normalization) and may be affected by limited 69

Online Methods 94 

Gene expression datasets and preprocessing: Previously published bulk gene-expression datasets used for 95 

benchmarking COMPASS are described in the main text. For each study, normalized expression matrices were 96 

obtained from the original publication, GEO/ArrayExpress, or were reprocessed from raw counts using standard 97 

RNA-seq pipelines (alignment, quantification, and normalization). When raw data were available, counts were 98 

converted to transcripts per million (TPM) or counts-per-million (CPM) with log ₂ transformation. When only 99 

processed data were available (e.g., log ₂ intensity values from microarrays), those matrices were used directly 00 

after confirming that sample-wise distributions were unimodal and approximately comparable across groups. 01 

Genes with zero or near-zero variance across samples were excluded because they do not contribute 02 

meaningfully to composite scores. All COMPASS analyses were performed on gene-by-sample matrices in 03 

which rows correspond to genes and columns correspond to samples, with samples annotated by user-defined 04 

biological or clinical group labels. 05 

Gene signatures: For all analyses in this study, gene lists and direction assignments were curated from 06 

prior experimental work, literature-derived pathways, or Boolean implication networks, as detailed in the main 07 

text. 08 

COMPASS composite activity scoring: Composite activity scores were calculated using COMPASS 09 

as defined in Equations (1–5) in the main text. Briefly, log ₂-normalized gene expression matrices (e.g., TPM or 10 

counts-per-million) were used as input. For each gene, COMPASS estimated a data-driven activation threshold 11 

by fitting a step function to the ordered expression values and identifying the position that minimized the within-12 

group variance, thereby classifying samples as “low” or “high” expressors. Expression deviations from this 13 

threshold were then standardized by the across-sample standard deviation to generate direction-aware z-scores. 14 

For each predefined gene set or signature, per-sample composite activity scores were obtained as the weighted 15 

average of these z-scores, with +1 weights assigned to genes expected to increase and −1 weights to genes 16 

expected to decrease with pathway activation. The resulting scores are deterministic (no permutations) and were 17 

used to generate boxplots, group-wise comparisons, and receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves for user-18 

specified biological or clinical contrasts. All analyses were performed using the COMPASS web application 19 

(https://compass.precsn.com ). 20 

Survival analysis and outcome modeling : Survival analysis was performed using COMPASS-derived 21 

scores as continuous predictors. Patients’ metadata with time to event annotation were used as input. Patients 22 

were stratified into high and low groups using a StepMiner -derived cutoff applied to the distribution of composite 23 

scores within each cohort. Kaplan–Meier curves were used to estimate time-to-event (30-day mortality) 24 

probabilities, with significance assessed by log-rank test. Hazard ratios (HRs) and corresponding confidence 25 

intervals were estimated using Cox proportional hazards models, treating COMPASS scores as either 26 

continuous or dichotomized variables. In datasets where samples are annotated for the presence or absence of 27

Quantification and Statistical Analysis : For each gene module or signature, COMPASS computed a 30 

single continuous composite activity score per sample as described in the main text. These scores served as 31

assigned to user-defined biological or clinical groups, and composite scores were compared using two-sided 33 

Welch’s t-tests (unequal variances). Raw p-values were assembled into a group × group matrix and adjusted for 34 

multiple testing using both Bonferroni and Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) procedures; both raw 35 

and adjusted values were exported for interpretation. For classification performance, a designated reference 36 

group was treated as the negative class and each other group as the positive class. Receiver operating 37 

characteristic (ROC) curves and corresponding areas under the curve (AUCs) were computed from composite 38 

scores using standard empirical estimators, and AUC p-values were obtained from asymptotic normal 39 

approximations. All tests were two-sided and based on complete-case data. For survival analysis log rank test, 40 

hazard ratio was also calculated. Analyses were implemented in Python within the COMPASS web application 41 

using standard scientific libraries (numpy, pandas, scipy, scikit-learn), with visualization modules generating 42 

boxplots, ROC curves, KM plot for export. 43 

Head-to-head comparison with GSVA and ssGSEA : Gene set activity was quantified using two widely 44 

used single-sample enrichment methods, Gene Set Variation Analysis (GSVA) and single-sample Gene Set 45 

Enrichment Analysis (ssGSEA), implemented via the GSEApy package (v1.1.13) with default parameters unless 46 

otherwise specified (including kernel type, normalization, and gene set size thresholds). GSVA estimates 47 

pathway activity by modeling the distribution of gene expression values across samples using a non-parametric 48 

kernel transformation, whereas ssGSEA computes a rank-based enrichment score independently within each 49 

sample. In parallel, a deterministic composite score was computed using the COMPASS framework. In this 50 

approach, gene expression values were transformed into standardized deviations from StepMiner -derived 51 

thresholds with noise margin (T*), scaled by gene-specific variance to generate dimensionless Z-scores. These 52 

normalized values were then aggregated using predefined gene-specific weights to generate a directionality-53 

aware composite activity score at the sample level. Directionality weights (+1 for activation, −1 for repression) 54 

were assigned based on prior biological annotation of each gene and held constant across all analyses. 55

to discriminate sepsis from healthy controls using ROC analysis and the corresponding AUC. Score orientation 57

The conceptual foundation (a), computational workflow (b), and workflow for translational integration of digital biomarkers for analyzing gene expression data to derive logic-based pathway activation scores and digital biomarkers. 

(a) Conceptual Foundation : COMPASS addresses limitations of traditional logic modeling by offering a deterministic, reproducible, and accessible framework for translational precision medicine. 

(b) Computational Workflow : Workflow schematic showing user inputs (expression matrix, gene signature, logic), automated backend computations, and outputs including box plots, ROC-AUC analyses and survival analyses. 

(c) User Interface: Example of an intuitive interface and downloadable outputs demonstrating COMPASS’s ability to stratify groups and assess pathway activation with statistical significance, while remaining accessible to all biologists. 

(d-f) Translational Workflow: Workflow schematic illustrates a three-part framework for aligning diverse biological models with cohort-level measurements through digital biomarkers and the COMPASS system. ( d) Cohort-Level Discovery :Disease driver events, clinical endpoints, and regulatory endpoints are identified from large-n human trial datasets. These serve as anchors for downstream modeling and validation. ( e) Model-Level Quantification : Diverse experimental platforms—including cell lines, patient-derived organoids (PDOs), ex vivo tissues (e.g., precision-cut tissue slices, live or fixed), organs-on-chip, and animal models—are evaluated using digital biomarkers. COMPASS objectively quantifies the humanness and relevance of each model’s response, enabling cross-comparison and translational alignment. ( f) Cohort-Level Benchmarking and Simulation : Validated digital biomarkers are used to simulate cohort-level responses with high objectivity and precision. In silico trials (with survival analysis) further extend predictive capacity, supporting regulatory decision-making and personalized medicine. Central to this framework, COMPASS acts as a translational bridge linking small-n mechanistic studies with large-n population insights and enabling iterative refinement of digital biomarkers across biological scales.
