Background 47 Maize (Zea mays L.) is one of the world’s most important cereal crops. It carries extensive 48 genetic diversity that reflects complex genetic control of many traits and that was shaped by 49 domestication from wild teosinte followed by intensive modern breeding across diverse 50 environments [1, 2]. This diversity provides broad opportunities for crop improvement; however, 51 translating accumulated genetic knowledge into tangible breeding progress requires a clearer 52 understanding of how the genomic regions controlling key agronomic traits are organized and 53 distributed across diverse genetic backgrounds and environmental conditions. 54 Over the past three decades, traditional linkage mapping in biparental populations has identified 55 thousands of quantitative trait loci (QTLs) affecting important maize traits [3], and genome-wide 56 association studies (GWAS) using diverse germplasm panels have subsequently provided 57 enhanced mapping resolution and broader genetic coverage across the species’ diversity [4–6]. 58 Despite this wealth of genetic information, several challenges limit the efficient practical 59 application of mapping results in breeding programs. Many QTLs are detected only in specific 60 genetic backgrounds or environmental conditions, whereas others span broad genomic intervals 61 containing hundreds of candidate genes that are difficult to prioritize without additional 62 functional information [3, 4]. Initial QTL effect-size estimates can be inflated owing to sampling 63 variation and publication bias, a pattern formalized as the Beavis effect [7, 8], whereby QTLs 64 reaching statistical significance in small mapping populations systematically overestimate true 65

effect sizes, creating expectations that are often not replicated when loci are deployed in 66 breeding. Extensive genotype-by-environment interactions further complicate the identification 67 of broadly relevant loci versus population- or environment-specific artifacts [9]. Consequently, 68 despite decades of intensive mapping research, relatively few QTLs have been fine-mapped to 69 causal genes or effectively deployed in operational plant breeding programs [10–12]. 70 Meta-QTL analysis was designed to address these challenges by synthesizing QTL information 71 across multiple independent studies, distinguishing consistent genetic signals from study-specific 72 variation, and substantially reducing confidence intervals through precision-weighted averaging 73 across independent populations and genetic backgrounds [13, 14]. This approach has 74 demonstrated meaningful confidence-interval reductions across diverse crop species while 75 revealing patterns of genetic architecture not apparent from individual studies conducted in 76 isolation [15–17]. In maize specifically, meta-analyses have provided valuable insights for 77 drought tolerance [19, 20], disease resistance [21, 22], yield components [23], grain quality [24, 78 25], and physiological traits [26]. However, previous maize meta-analyses carry noteworthy 79 limitations that restrict their practical utility for breeding programs targeting multiple traits 80 simultaneously. Their predominantly single-trait scope omits critical information on co-81 localizations among traits that is needed for understanding genetic correlations, anticipating 82 unintended selection consequences across traits, and identifying loci capable of simultaneously 83 improving multiple breeding objectives [27, 28]. A genomic region influencing drought 84 tolerance may, for example, also substantially affect yield potential under optimal conditions, 85 creating trade-offs that single-trait analysis cannot detect or quantify [29, 30]. Systematically integrating both 96 approaches through dual-platform verification could improve MQTL positional confidence and 97 resolution simultaneously, facilitating efficient marker development, selection, and candidate 98 gene identification [4, 6]. A further aspect absent from most existing maize meta-analyses is the 99 systematic differentiation between broadly adaptive loci conferring consistent effects across 100 environments and those providing environment-specific advantages (under drought, heat, or low-101 nitrogen stress). This information could have critical implications for deploying MQTLs 102 strategically across diverse production systems and breeding programs targeting contrasting 103 target environments [9, 34, 35]. We present a comprehensive multi-114 trait meta-analysis spanning 1995–2025 that integrates 2,701 QTLs from 304 independent 115 studies across five trait categories: grain yield and components; plant development and 116 architecture; plant physiology and stress adaptation; grain quality and nutritional composition; 117 and disease and pest resistance. Our specific objectives were to: (i) consolidate QTLs into 118 refined high-confidence meta-QTLs through dual-platform verification integrating linkage and 119 association mapping signals; (ii) identify multi-trait genomic hotspots and classify them into 120 functionally relevant categories that directly inform breeding strategy; (iii) classify the 121 environmental context of MQTLs based on the environmental distribution of contributing QTLs 122 to provide deployment guidance; (iv) systematically prioritize candidate genes within hotspot 123 intervals using network-based analysis; and (v) assess cross-cereal syntenic conservation across 124 rice, sorghum, wheat, and barley to enable translational breeding applications and cross-species 125 marker transfer. 126

Consensus map construction and QTL projection 162 All QTLs were projected onto the IBM2 2008 Neighbors consensus genetic map, accessed 163 through MaizeGDB. This map was selected because it has the broadest marker overlap with the 164 populations, marker systems, and genetic backgrounds represented across three decades of 165 published maize QTL studies, and because it has been used consistently throughout the published 166 crop meta-QTL literature [15, 17, 18, 19, 20, 23, 50]. SNP physical positions were converted to 167 genetic coordinates using MaizeGDB marker correspondence tables. Where studies reported 168 only flanking markers without explicit peak positions, those flanking markers were placed on the 169 consensus framework to define CI boundaries, and peak positions were estimated by midpoint 170 interpolation. Of the 2,701 compiled QTLs, 2,518 (93.2%) were successfully projected onto the 171 consensus map; 184 were excluded because of insufficient or ambiguous positional information. 172 The excluded QTLs were proportionally distributed across trait categories and chromosomes, 173 confirming the absence of systematic projection bias that could skew trait-category 174 representation in downstream analyses. 175

Hotspot identification and candidate gene prioritization 199 A genomic region was treated as a hotspot when it met any of three predefined criteria: (i) three 200 or more MQTLs from distinct trait categories within a 5 Mb window; (ii) a single MQTL 201 supported by five or more independent mapping studies from different genetic backgrounds; or 202 (iii) an MQTL with PVE >20% (the top 5% by effect size across the full MQTL catalog). QTL effect sizes vary among trait categories 269 PVE differed substantially among trait categories (Additional file 1: Table S1). Grain quality and 270 nutritional composition QTLs showed the largest individual effects, with 18.6% exceeding 20% 271 PVE, including starch-biosynthesis genes (wx1, ae1, su1; PVE range 26.9–34.2%) [63, 64], the 272 endosperm protein regulator opaque2 (34.2% PVE) [65], and the carotenoid-biosynthesis gene 273 crtRB1 (28.4% PVE) [66]. These large effects reflect the biochemical nature of grain-quality 274 traits, in which individual pathway genes can exert substantial quantitative effects on 275 composition. Plant development and architecture QTLs included several major-effect loci: vgt1 276 (up to 54% PVE for flowering time) [67], Dwarf3 (28.6% PVE for plant height) [68], and barren 277 stalk1 (24.3% PVE) [69]. In contrast, grain yield and components QTLs exhibited smaller 278 individual effects, with only 5.8% exceeding 20% PVE, consistent with the highly polygenic 279 architecture of yield traits, which reflects many small-effect loci distributed across the genome 280 [3, 70]. 281 Physiological and stress-adaptation QTLs showed conditional effect amplification under stress 282 relative to optimal conditions. For drought-responsive QTLs evaluated under contrasting water 283 regimes within the same mapping populations, average PVE increased from 3.8% under well-284 watered conditions to 13.4% under severe drought, a 3.5-fold amplification (paired t-test, P < 285 0.001) [19, 29]. This conditional amplification has important implications for the choice of 286 genomic-selection training environments and for the strategic deployment of stress-specific 287 versus broadly adaptive loci in breeding programs. 288

Enhanced resolution through integrative meta-analysis 486 The dual-platform approach, leveraging the complementary strengths of biparental linkage 487 mapping and association mapping in diverse germplasm panels, produced high-confidence, well-488 resolved meta-QTLs that substantially exceed the resolution achievable by either approach 489 independently. GWAS co-localization was achieved for 68.4% of MQTLs at the primary ±1 Mb 490 threshold, providing strong evidence that the majority of identified meta-QTLs represent genuine 491 genetic signals rather than positional artifacts of individual mapping populations. Grain quality 492 and nutritional composition achieved the highest dual-platform co-localization rate (85.2%), 493 likely because large-effect biochemical pathway genes such as wx1, opaque2, and crtRB1 494 produce strong, consistent signals detectable across both mapping frameworks and diverse 495 germplasm [24, 25]. For GWAS-supported MQTLs, association mapping narrowed intervals 496 well below the linkage-only median CI of 12.6 Mb, which typically contains hundreds of 497 candidate genes. The 31.6% of MQTLs lacking GWAS co-localization support represent lower-498 confidence targets that warrant additional mapping effort before deployment; the reduced 499 disease-resistance co-localization rate (57.9%) likely reflects the relative scarcity of published 500 diversity-panel GWAS studies for resistance traits and the inherent difficulty of detecting race-501 specific resistance alleles in diverse panels where resistance-breaking pathogen races may be 502 present [21, 22]. The Beavis effect systematically inflates PVE estimates compiled across diverse 503 studies and mapping populations [7, 8]; meta-analytic precision-weighted averaging provides 504 partial mitigation through down-weighting of QTLs from smaller populations, but absolute PVE 505 values should be interpreted with caution, and comparative rankings among MQTLs are more 506 reliable indicators of relative effect-size magnitude than absolute values. 507

Hotspot biology and candidate gene mechanisms 508 The 23 hotspots harbor 70.6% of all MQTLs within only 30.6% of the euchromatic genome, a 509 pattern that is robust to category subsampling and reflects genuine, non-random organization of 510 the maize genetic architecture for agronomically important traits. The biological basis of 511 synergistic multi-trait co-localization is illustrated most clearly by the stay-green hub on 512 chromosome 3, where senescence-delaying alleles at nac7 and ZmNAC126 simultaneously 513 extend photosynthetic duration, enhance nitrogen-remobilization efficiency, and improve grain 514 nutritional quality through prolonged nitrogen accumulation [75, 95], three agronomically 515 beneficial effects achievable through selection at a single genomic region, representing a strong 516 opportunity for efficient multi-trait improvement. The 75.0% concordance of favorable allele 517 directions across co-localizing MQTL pairs confirms predominantly synergistic relationships 518 that breeders can exploit efficiently through coupling-phase selection, whereas the 25.0% 519 showing discordant directions identify regions where favorable alleles for one trait create 520 unfavorable effects on others, requiring target-environment-specific prioritization, exploitation of 521 tissue-specific expression variation to functionally uncouple antagonistic effects, or precision 522 editing of regulatory sequences to achieve desired combinations [85, 86]. The HS-C6-II multi-523 trait hub on chromosome 6 exemplifies the scale of potential simultaneous gains achievable at a 524 single region: DGAT1-2 [73] driving oil-content improvement, ZmMATE1 [74] conferring 525 aluminium tolerance on acid soils covering approximately 40–50% of tropical arable land, and 526 Fusarium resistance loci offering pathogen protection, all within a 40 Mb interval that could in 527 principle be targeted through a coordinated marker-assisted introgression. 528 The 13 instances of cross-category MQTL overlap within single refined intervals of <500 kb, 529 across 7 of the 12 Class I hubs, provide stronger circumstantial evidence for biological 530
